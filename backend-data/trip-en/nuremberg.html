Weimar

The name of the city of Weimar is known to everyone thanks to the Weimar Republic. Nowadays it is a small city, but it was once the capital of Thuringia and the most important cultural center. Weimar is included in the World Heritage List twice - for classical architecture and for Bauhaus architecture.

Weimar train station. It's a 15 minute ride from Erfurt.

Along the road to the city center there are portraits of victims of the Nazi regime.

The Buchenwald concentration camp was near Weimar; if I had more time, I would have visited it. But in general, I have been to other camps (first, second, third).

This square was originally built during the Third Reich for party meetings. Now there is a shopping center here.

Weimar theater. The German Constitution of 1919 was signed here, thanks to which the republic is called Weimar. Opposite the theater stands a double monument to the most famous German poets - Johann Goethe and Friedrich Schiller, who both lived in Weimar.

Weimar is also the birthplace of the Bauhaus school of art, which gives its name to the architectural style. It was in Weimar from 1919 to 1925, then moved to the city of Dessau.

Erfurt

Erfurt is the capital of the state of Thuringia. This region was part of East Germany, which I know much worse than the western part (I've been only to Dresden as a child and to Berlin and its surroundings).

Erfurt is not the most famous city in Germany, but it is approximately in the middle, and the main lines of high-speed trains cross here, so the local station is the main interchange hub.

During the Napoleonic Wars, Erfurt was annexed directly to France. In 1808, Napoleon met with Alexander I here, and at the same time Napoleon and the poet Johann Goethe met. Napoleon's base was the Petersberg fortress.
