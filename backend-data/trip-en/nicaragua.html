Nicaragua: Leon, Matagalpa, Managua, Masaya, Granada, Rivas

27-30 October 2021

Leon

The first acquaintance with Nicaragua was not very successful. It started with the fact that I sat at the border for three and a half hours - it was the worst border after Uzbekistan (where I spent 5 hours). The border guards decided to test me with a complete list of questions for travelers; they carefully wrote down how much money I had, which countries I entered on what day, and the address of my hotel. When the chief border guard finally approved me, even stamping took about 10 minutes - while they filled out and stamped a dozen pieces of paper in different copies. I went through the gate, where the customs officers stopped me - it turned out that EVERYONE needs to fill out a customs declaration, and to get it, you have to go back to the bridge, where the man sits and hands them out, then to the room with the customs officers, where they will stamp it. Whoops, another kilometer or two of walking in the terrible heat, and I’m in Nicaragua.

After the border, I was unable to buy a SIM card or change money. Luckily, on the Honduran side I had already exchanged the rest of my Honduran money for Nicaraguan Cordobas, which meant I had $10 to get to Leon. One cramped minibus and I'm in the city of Chinandega. Immediately changed to another one to Leon.

I compared Honduras with the Philippines and expected that Nicaragua would be something like Vietnam, but when I got off the minibus at the market-bus station of Leon, I saw something more like Bangladesh or India. This is clearly the poorest country in Central America, since it is the only place where you can see pedicabs.

It was very dirty around until I reached the historical center.

Then life began to get better: I checked into a hotel, bought a SIM card there, then with great difficulty, but was still able to withdraw money from an ATM and have dinner.

In the center everything was decent and did not feel dangerous, at least there were a lot of people in the square, which usually does not happen in dangerous countries. Nicaragua has a crime rate much lower than Honduras and El Salvador.

Managua

The capital of Nicaragua was destroyed by an earthquake in 1972. And in 1979, the Sandinista revolution occurred. It overthrew the dictator Somoza (about whom the US President said the catchphrase that he was “our son of a bitch”), after that Nicaragua began to focus on the USSR and build socialism. That is why this country is so different from its neighbors, where there is anarcho-capitalism and the power of mafia gangs. There is socialism in Nicaragua, which means there is only one gang - the state. There is no crime, but for protection from the state you have to pay, as usual, a price higher than for protection from bandits - as a result, the standard of living in Nicaragua is the lowest in the region (and prices too, which is a plus for tourists). Neighboring Costa Rica, not the richest country, even introduced visas for Nicaraguans, due to the fact that the flow of refugees had become very large. A few years ago, pension reform was carried out in Nicaragua, protest rallies were cracked on, and the dissatisfaction of the dissatisfied and the flight from the country accelerated, in general, the picture is familiar to us. In urban planning, Nicaragua apparently also decided to take inspiration from the USSR; as a result, wide empty avenues appeared in the capital.

Masaya

Near Managua there is Masaya Volcano - one of the most easily accessible active volcanoes in the world, which is even easier to get to than Bromo.

The volcano is located right next to the main highway passing through all the main cities of Central America (Pan American), about 15 minutes drive from Managua.

Right next to the highway there is a ticket office where you can buy not only a ticket, but also a minibus transfer to the very top, and it costs pennies - about 5 dollars in total. The bus takes you to the top.

Along the way you can see a couple of buildings destroyed by the eruption.

And here is the top. A few years ago, as I understand it, the smoke was stronger - in "Heads and Tails" show they said that you should not stand at the edge for more than 5 minutes.

Granada

Perhaps the most beautiful colonial city in Central America is Granada (and there is also Granada in Spain). By the way, it is the oldest in this region (not counting Panama City, which was destroyed and built in a new place) and the second after Santo Domingo. This is the only large city where you can feel a relaxed atmosphere, that people are chilling here, going to a cafe in the evening, and not hiding from bandits. It is not clear why Granada is not included in the World Heritage List; the entire center appears to be well preserved.

I stayed in a luxury hotel in a mansion on the central square.

Rivas

Before going to Costa Rica, I decided to take a quick look at Lake Nicaragua, which gave the country its name. It is the largest lake in the region, connected through a river to the Caribbean Sea, making Granada a port on the Atlantic Ocean. There have been ideas in the past to build an alternative to the Panama Canal through Nicaragua, since only a fairly narrow strip needs to be dug between the lake and the Pacific Ocean. The last time the Chinese thought about this was in the 2010s, but abandoned the idea.

The nearest town is called Rivas. There, as usual, there is a cathedral in the central square.
