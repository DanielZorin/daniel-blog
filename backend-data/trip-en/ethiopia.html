Dire Dawa

Dire Dawa is one of the largest cities in Ethiopia, and one of three that have the status of a full-fledged region (like Addis Ababa and Harar). The city is young, founded in the early 20th century around the railway station. At that time, the road from Addis Ababa to Djibouti was being built, Harar was on the way, but it is on a high hill, so the station was built below. And now it has grown into a full-fledged city. However, there is nothing special to see here, except for the train station.

While looking for a place to stay in the center, at first I came across inns at bars. The rooms there were so-so, and I expected a lot of noise in the evening. In the end I wandered into a more respectable hotel, only the most expensive suite was available, although it cost only $25, so I was happy. Though, they hadn’t had time to clean it up after the mephedrone party of the previous guests.

More precisely, it was probably a party with khat. I saw other hotel guests with those brooms later. But it's funny anyway, we're used to the fact that when someone throws a party, there should be bottles of alcohol or beer cans left behind. And here we have only the purest drinking water.

While waiting for the room to be cleaned, I had lunch with a dish called "tibs" - fried pieces of meat with rice.

Train ride

There aren't many places in Africa where you can take a passenger train, so I wanted to try the Ethiopian railways. The line from Addis Ababa to Djibouti is the only one that works in Ethiopia. It was built by the French in the early 20th century (Djibouti was a French colony), and modernized by the Chinese in the 21st. The train takes two days, the first day from Addis Ababa to Dire Dawa, the second day from Dire Dawa to Djibouti (and back again, two days). As a result, departures in each direction are not every day, but every other day. A ticket to Adama costs about $11, half as much for locals. The ride is supposed to take about 7 hours...

Usually, when I travel, I eat one big meal a day - dinner. In the morning, 90% of restaurants are closed anyway, and it's easier to eat a couple of cookies or fruits - that's about all I had, and I had to stick to it until late in the evening, since nothing was sold on the train at all.

Adama

I learned about the city of Adama while researching what cities are within a day trip radius from Addis Ababa - Adama is essentially the only large one. Wikivoyage claims that Addis Ababa residents come here on weekends, but what they do here is not very clear.

I arrived by train late in the evening, randomly poked at a cluster of hotels on the map, and asked a tuk-tuk driver to take me there. Many hotels here, like in other cities, have no signs and are combined with clubs/bars. I checked into the first one I came across, because I had no strength at all. The hotel was just awful - the water didn't work at all, and in the first room they gave me, the sockets didn't work either. At least I had my own unlimited Wi-Fi.

Eating at 10 pm is also difficult, but I found girls who made hot sandwiches.

Addis Ababa

The capital of Ethiopia looks much more advanced than the rest of the country. There are expensive restaurants, branded shops and even a metro.

Let's start with the Piassa area (from the Italian Piazza - "square"). In this part of the city, the old architecture of the late 19th century was preserved the longest, but they say that by the beginning of 2024 it was finally demolished. They've already managed to re-lay some of the streets:

A memorial to the Battle of Adwa was built on a large square.

In this battle in 1896, Ethiopia unexpectedly defeated Italy and was able to maintain its independence.

Only in 1935 the second Italian invasion was successful, and Ethiopia briefly became a colony (until 1941).

Addis Ababa has a metro (officially Light rail, but it seems to go on overpasses everywhere, so it's not just a tram). The metro opened in 2015, initially there were two lines, but now the system has degraded, and only one line works, since the trains broke down and were not repaired in time. And on that one line, trains leave every 20-30 minutes. I went to the final station, and at first we stood in a long line in front of the platform. But when the train pulled up, everyone quickly ran inside.

At first it was more or less okay, but after a few stops there was a terrible crush in the carriage. I barely made it to the stop I needed.

After that I rode buses, they are also sometimes overcrowded, but not always.

By the way, it was downright cold for the locals, and I was wearing a T-shirt (I just didn't have anything warm).
