Serbia: Belgrade, Novi Sad, Subotica

28 November - 1 December 2013

Belgrade

Russia and Serbia are fraternal countries. Orthodoxy, collapsed empires, millions of compatriots in separated countries, lack of allies - these are all known facts. But what about at the everyday level? Here Russia and Serbia also have a lot in common. For example, in no other countries have I seen boards placed in puddles so that people could cross these puddles.

Belgrade bus station, the picture is very familiar thanks to the stalls. However, this can also be found in other places in Eastern Europe.

They are just beginning to learn English here.

Exhibitions of military equipment.

Unauthorized street trading.

Inscriptions on the walls in several layers, made in a clumsy way.

Here's more about Kosovo.

In general, there are few political inscriptions on the walls in relation to the total number. I found only one wall where they speak out.

Traditional values.

And there is even a hotel "Moscow"!

The first McDonald's in Yugoslavia. History does not tell us about how long the queues were on opening day. There is a suspicion that they are not the same as in Moscow, because... Yugoslav citizens could travel abroad.

Yes, Serbia does not yet reach Western Europe (and in some details, even Eastern Europe), but Russia has a lot to learn from Belgrade. For example, in Belgrade there are excellent timetables and diagrams at all stops.

Dedicated lanes for buses and taxis.

They also cook very well in Serbia, and food even in restaurants is cheap.

It’s very interesting that in Serbia they write the letters “t” and “p” in cursive font. In Russia, this is how some people write them in cursive.

Old signs, some painted on the wall.

New ones are in Cyrillic and Latin. In general, most of the inscriptions are in Cyrillic, but Latin ones are also found.

I went to the Yugoslav History Museum.

The man who ruled Yugoslavia for 35 years is buried here.

Many people here are nostalgic for it, apparently because dictatorship is better than civil war and occupation.

Someone may reproach me for not saying anything about the sights of Belgrade.

Well, don’t sit back, go and see for yourself!

Novi Sad

The hotel cost only 12 euros. I expected a catch, but there was none. Indeed, 12 euros for a double room, and this is not even a hostel, there are four floors of rooms. And in the basement there is also an excellent restaurant where you can eat for $6. Rostil is grilled meat. Serbia historically does not like Muslims, so the majority of dishes are made from pork. A shot of brandy costs about $1, which is even cheaper than in the cheapest Russian bar.

Novi Sad is nice. It is the capital of the only autonomy that has not separated from Serbia.

In these places, the border between Austria and Turkey passed along the Danube, and the Novi Sad fortress was at the forefront.

The number and multi-level bastions are impressive.

Night illumination.

There is also a small old part of the city around the fortress.

Most of the city is modern.

Sometimes even too much.

For some reason, Seselj is very popular in Vojvodina (it's that guy who promised to gouge out the eyes of the Croats with a rusty spoon, and was repeatedly sent to the Hague Tribunal). It seems that in his biography nothing is directly connected with Vojvodina.

The writing on the walls is random, just like in Belgrade. By the way, white pride.

Subotica

Subotica is a Serbian town near the Hungarian border. The architecture here resembles provincial Hungary.

City Hall.
