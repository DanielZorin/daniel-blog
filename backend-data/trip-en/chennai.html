Thiruvananthapuram

The city with the long name Thiruvananthapuram (formerly called Trivandrum) would be completely unremarkable if not for one recent event. In 2011, the largest treasure in history was found here - more than one hundred thousand items made of gold and precious stones, worth at least a trillion dollars. These treasures were collected for many centuries by the rulers of these lands and accumulated in the basements of the Padmanabhaswamy Temple.

In 2011, five basements were opened, and one, the largest, has not yet been opened, but they found out that there are two more basements below. So there's probably a lot more treasure out there than we know.

From the outside you wouldn’t be able to tell that in the basement you could literally swim in gold.

Security measures are, of course, enhanced - foreigners are not allowed inside, and Indians cannot even bring a mobile phone.

Nagercoil

Judging by the guidebooks, the main attraction of Nagercoil is the Nagaraja Temple, after which the city is named.

Yes, this is a temple in honor of the naga (the monster from Heroes of might and magic), statues of which can so often be found in temples in some Southeast Asian countries.

The Nagercoil area is the end of the Western Ghats, the mountains that run along the southwest coast of India, separating the very wet coastal regions from the fairly dry areas in the east.

Madurai

I remember as a child I often read through a book about world landmarks, and there were photographs of an Indian temple that looked like a pyramid, filled to the top with multi-colored statues of gods. And here I am in the city of Madurai at the gates of the Meenakshi Aman Temple.

The case when in reality it looks no worse than in the photographs. Although in the southern part of India most of the temples are similar in shape, but this one is much larger than the rest.

At night it looks mysterious and a little creepy.

This temple occupies a whole rather large block and has the shape of a square. There are four high towers with statues - one on each side.

Tourists are allowed into this temple, with the exception of a few secret rooms, but you cannot take a camera with you. A mobile phone is allowed, and taking photographs with a mobile phone is not prohibited!

So I took some pictures with my phone inside. This is what the courtyard looks like.

I paid so much attention to the Meenakshi Temple not only because it is cool (which it is), but also because apart from this temple there is absolutely nothing to see or do in the million-strong Madurai. This city looks like all the others, there is not a single beautiful street, there is nowhere to walk, the eateries are the same as everywhere else.

A big contrast with China, where in every no-name city like Guiyang I can remember many interesting places.

Pondicherry

Not only Portugal, but also France had their own piece of India, and it was also scattered throughout the country. Now these four cities (Pondicherry, Karaikal, Yanaon and Mahe) are a single union territory of Pondicherry (or Puducherry).

Pondicherry has been French for almost as long as Goa - until the 1950s. There are traces of colonization here, although for some reason there are fewer of them than in the Portuguese colonies: these are mainly churches and monuments. The churches here are like that, there is something in common with Vietnam and New Orleans.

Chennai

Chennai (formerly Madras) is the largest city in southern India. Travelers rarely stay here for long. I allocated two days to explore this metropolis, in principle, as in most cities in India, this is more or less enough.

Since Madras was an important colonial port, pompous Victorian-style buildings remain, but fewer than in Mumbai and Calcutta. First of all, this is the railway station:

Besides Thomas, the relics of only two apostles are known: St. Peter (in the Vatican Cathedral of St. Peter) and St. James (city of Santiago de Compostela, Spain). I traveled around Madras mainly by city trains. The system is similar to Mumbai - they go very often, they never checked my tickets either. It seems that travel around the city costs 5-10 rupees, I have never paid more.

Many train stations look like abandoned buildings. When I first arrived at the station closest to my hotel (and it’s not far from the center), at first I didn’t believe that I was in the right place: there wasn’t a soul around. But the cash register still worked.

On the platform.

Several times I traveled around the city by buses, fortunately they have numbers and the routes are plotted on Google maps.

In addition to trains, Chennai has a new metro. At that time, it was the most pointless metro in the world: the line went from nowhere to nowhere, carried air, and was also organized crookedly: although there seemed to be one line, some of the trains did not go to the final station! Everyone was kicked out three stations before the end, and we had to get on another train, which was on the other side of the platform (where the train in the opposite direction should be). In the near future, it is planned to complete the metro so that it runs from the airport to the city center to the railway station.

The reserve for the future is clearly large: so many turnstiles for the sake of the only passenger at the station (me).
