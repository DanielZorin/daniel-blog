Sri Lanka: Kandy, Dambulla, Sigiriya, Vavuniya, Mihintale, Kurunegala, Negombo, Colombo, Hikkaduwa, Galle

30 June - 8 July 2016

Kandy

The first city in a new country is the place where you absorb the most information. In Sri Lanka, the first city for me was Kandy, if we don't count the village near the airport where I spent the night after arriving. It is a large and fairly typical city for the country.

Sri Lanka is a former British colony, this is immediately apparent from the clock towers in the central squares.

There is a single numbering of buses throughout the country, and there are no differences between local and intercity ones. The design of the sign above the windshield is uniform.

Everything is fine with fruit. There are even local durians and mangosteens. The strange fruit “elephant apple,” which I tried once in Cambodia and once in Thailand, comes from these lands and is sold here in every stall.

Soursop is also common.

Surprisingly, they sell “brick” bread here, although I was sure that this was a typical Russian delicacy! The crust is just as crispy.

In the city there are confectionery shops with a non-trivial assortment.

The fleet has been decommissioned from all over the world. On the truck there is an inscription in Japanese “Natural gas vehicle, environmentally friendly”.

Copy center. Very inexpensive - for one dollar you can photocopy as many as 70 pages! For some reason, printing is twice as expensive. In Russia, as far as I know, you cannot find cheaper than 2 rubles (3 cents) per page, even in the provinces, unless we are talking about printing several thousand pages. And it’s very difficult to print one page for less than 10 rubles.

The poorer the country, the more elite the fast food chains there will be. Sri Lanka is a very poor country, here KFC prices are at the level of an expensive restaurant.

Sigiriya

The top attraction of Sri Lanka is Sigiriya (although I would not call it widely known, even I had not heard of this place before I was planning to visit Sri Lanka). This is a huge stone on which a royal palace stood a thousand years ago. Now, it seems, only the foundation remains of it, and the entrance ticket costs as much as 30 dollars - until recently, even Angkor was cheaper.

I did not go inside, but headed around Sigiriya along the perimeter of the square moat.

On the other side are the remains of the wall.

In many places the moat is interrupted, you can get closer to the rock, but tickets are still checked at the entrance.

They say there are wild elephants walking here.

A kilometer north of Sigiriya there is another huge rock where you can also climb.

Entrance here costs only 3 dollars.

Under the stone is a Buddhist temple with a Buddha statue.

Arrows indicate where to go. There were no tourists except me.

From the top you can see Sigiriya.

If you look closely, you can see quite a few people on the stairs.

The scenery at the top looks cool. On a bare rock there is a mound of earth from which trees grow.

There is a very strong wind from above.

The area around is completely flat.

At the entrance there is a map of the world painted on the wall. It looks like it was drawn a long time ago - the USSR and Yugoslavia still exist on it.

Vavuniya

For decades Sri Lanka was ravaged by the civil war that ended relatively recently. In the north of the island, the Tamils tried to secede. Tamils came to Sri Lanka from southern India during the British colonization. The indigenous Sinhalese Buddhists did not really like the Hindu Tamils, and the confrontation resulted in a war. Although the Tamils are in a clear minority, they managed to keep the north of the island under control for quite a long time. Now the Tamil separatists have been defeated. The front line passed near the city of Vavuniya. Vavuniya can be seen as an example of an average Sri Lankan city without tourists.

Near the bus station there is a classic clock tower.

Tamil Hindu temples from southern India are characterized by a lot of statues in the shape of a pyramid.

Negombo

Due to some misunderstanding, this city is considered a resort. There is absolutely no beach here, and it’s also a bit expensive.

I didn't swim here.

Prices in local restaurants are never low. They don’t even offer a vegetarian dish for less than two dollars.

Budget establishments here are called “hotels”.

It is curious to know that in Sri Lanka, many hotels do not allow Sri Lankans themselves; they say that some of them do not know how to behave. In China and India, on the contrary, many hotels do not allow foreigners.

Negombo was once a Dutch settlement.

What remains from the Dutch is a canal that has turned into a dirty, stinking slurry.

However, package tourists are given boat rides here.

Christianity still remains here. The main cathedral is a bit gothic.

Cemetery.

On the corners, instead of Buddha statues, there are statues of Jesus and Mary.

And here comes Buddha.

All types of transport in Sri Lanka, except cars, are prohibited from entering the highway.

Sale of lottery tickets.

The watermelons here are the size of pineapples. Very convenient - no need for a refrigerator to store the uneaten half.

View from above.

I'm unlikely to return here.

Colombo

The capital, Colombo, is perhaps the most interesting city in Sri Lanka, but it is rarely visited by tourists, because there is an airport in Negombo, beaches in the south, and there is somehow no cheap housing in Colombo. However, it is full of majestic English architecture, cheap food, and a wide selection of markets.

The center of Colombo in some places resembles Yangon.

The most important streets have been restored, but further away, not so much.

Hikkaduwa

Hikkaduwa is a resort where tourists from Russia like to settle. The sea here is nicer than in Negombo, I didn’t even disdain to swim, although the waves are strong and the water is muddy, it’s better to surf here than swim.

There are a lot of people.

Everywhere there are inscriptions in Russian.

Apart from a seashore, there is not much here. After sunset everything dies out. This is what the main street looks like, compare with Pattaya.

With food, the rule is simple - the closer to the station, the cheaper, further south is more expensive.

I don't know what else can be said about this city. From here you can drive to Galle in about 30 minutes.

Galle

Galle is a walled city in the south of Sri Lanka. Built by the Dutch, a very interesting place.

It’s not often in Asia that you come across an almost complete piece of Europe.

In Galle this is exactly the case - no dirty markets, tuk-tuks or slums within the walls. Only quiet hotels and restaurants for tourists.
