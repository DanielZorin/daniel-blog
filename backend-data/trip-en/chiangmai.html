The very next day I left there. This hostel had literally every fault you could think of. Mae Salong

Lost in the mountains of Chiang Rai province is the Chinese village of Santikhiri, better known by its old name Mae Salong. After the defeat of the “whites” in the civil war in China, the division located in Yunnan province was unable to evacuate to Taiwan due to the distance from seas. The soldiers were relocated to Myanmar, and after another 10 years they were given refuge in northern Thailand in exchange for help maintaining stability in the area so that the communists would not get through.

To get to Mae Salong, you need to take a bus towards the junction of the three borders and get off in the middle in the town of Mae Chan.

From there the songthaews go to Mae Salong.

Mae Salong is essentially a village along the highway winding through the mountains.

Chiang Mai

I didn't like the city right away. To begin with, I spent three hours looking for a hotel, and not even because it was expensive, but because there was simply no room anywhere - despite the fact that there is a hotel in every second house, there are dozens of them. Then it took another two hours to find a place to eat. Everywhere was very expensive for tourists. No city in Thailand is as crowded with tourists as Chiang Mai, even Khao San in Bangkok doesn't have that ghetto feel. However, in the following days I came to the conclusion that it was very possible to live here. The city center is a square surrounded by a moat. The eastern half of the square and the adjacent area behind the moat are a tourist area. There is no need to go there, and then there are almost no problems, everything around is cheap, and it is not as hot as in the center and south. If we talk about a tourist visit, then Chiang Mai is incredibly overrated. In reality, this is a transit city that can be explored in less than a day; then you can safely leave for Chiang Rai, because there is nothing outstanding here, and you need to somehow get out of the ghetto.

The good old connections with Khaosan work here. How can it be that a ticket to Bangkok costs 600 baht, and to Pattaya - 1350, if a Bangkok-Pattaya ticket costs less than 100?

In Chiang Mai you can explore several temples (just enough for a few hours), one of them is truly unusual.

The rest are about the same as in all other cities.

Chiang Mai has a million population, but it is not even close to Bangkok; rather, it can be put on a par with other provincial low-rise cities.

By the way, everything is very bad with city transport. There are as many as TWO bus routes for such a rather large city, which also run at huge intervals of 30 minutes. The owners of the minibuses that don’t even have numbers are dissatisfied. Due to the influx of tourists, drivers have already become insolent and will most likely refuse to take you at the standard rate of 20 baht (why, by the way, in Bangkok the fare is 6.50 without air conditioning and 13 with air conditioning, and in Chiang Mai 20 without air conditioning? Sukhothai

Sukhothai was the first capital of Thailand in the 13th to 15th centuries, before the rise of Ayutthaya. The remains of the ancient city are included in the UNESCO list.

The modern city of Sukhothai was built 10 kilometers from the ancient one. I spent three days there, and for the first two I didn’t go anywhere, but sat in the room under a blanket in my jacket, trying not to get cold. Thailand was hit by a cyclone, causing the temperature to drop to a terrible level - 12 degrees. It even snowed somewhere in the mountains, but in Sukhothai it was just very cold and rainy. Like that:

A bit gloomy.

There is nothing to see in the new city, everything interesting is in the old one.

Even beautiful signs are only in the old town.

The central part of the ancient city is square in shape and surrounded by a moat. Entrance costs 100 baht.

There are no residential buildings there, only a neat park with paths, ponds, and temples.

If you leave the southern gate, you can walk along the former outskirts of the city. Now they are ruins of temples in the middle of fields and vegetable gardens.
