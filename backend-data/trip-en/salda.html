Afrodisias

Instead of going to Denizli, I turned southwest towards the small town of Tavas. Here is probably the most unusual monument to Ataturk that I have seen - here he dances the Lezginka (more precisely, the local dance Tavas Zeybegi).

Behind, as usual, stands the second Ataturk.

City logo.

The main street.

Monument at the entrance.

Turning from Tavas onto a country road, after some time I crossed the border of Aydin province. In Turkey, at the borders of provinces there are at most small blue signs, and not huge steles, as happens in some countries.

Here are the ruins of the ancient city of Aphrodisias - one of the least known and difficult to access World Heritage Sites in Turkey. It seems that you can get here by minibus Tavas-Nazilli, but how often they run is unclear (I didn’t see one on the way).

Sarcophagi were made in this city, and now many of them are on display here.

In general, I have been to many ancient Greek and Roman cities, but here there are especially many sculptures and bas-reliefs on display. Perhaps they are better preserved due to the city’s distance from civilization.

Pamukkale

I was already in Pamukkale just six months ago in winter, now I saw this beautiful place in summer.

Since I had a car, I stayed at a hotel in the neighboring town of Karahayıt, a couple of kilometers north of the far entrance to Pamukkale. A room at the height of the summer season cost just over 10 euros. The town is very lively, it seems that tourists who come for a two-day excursion often settle here.

In six months, entrance to Pamukkale has doubled in price - from 120 liras to 250! The lira also fell by half. It’s good that I have free entry with a museum membership, so I went twice - at sunset and at dawn.

In summer, travertines look more dazzling white.

The disadvantage of summer is obvious - there are a lot of people, even before closing. I was hoping that tour groups arrive mostly during the day (for a one-day excursion) in order to have time to return before evening. Maybe there are even more people here during the day, I don’t know.

Çardak

After leaving Pamukkale, at first there is nothing particularly interesting; a major highway goes towards Ankara. I turned off from it in the city of Çardak, where Denizli airport is located (almost 50 kilometers from the city). In the central square there is an object of modern art.

Looking at the map, I would not have guessed that the city center is a narrow strip of development to the south of the highway, and not a wide one to the north.

Adana Kebab at the diner cost 43 liras, cheap (at that time the regular price was around 55).

Salda

Lake Salda is called “Turkish Maldives” in advertising brochures (this is also the name of Suluada Island near Kemer, where I have not yet visited). All thanks to the white beach and blue water, although it is not the same sand as in Maldives.

The nearest town is called Yeşilova.
