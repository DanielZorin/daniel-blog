Singapore

7-11 April 2016

Singapore

Singapore is famous for its prohibitions, but besides them there are many interesting signs and public service announcements. Here are the most well-known rules of conduct on the subway. For smoking, the fine is $1000 (Singaporean, that's about 700 American), but for some reason the penalty for bringing in durians is not specified. By the way, Hong Kong has the same rules and comparable fines, but no one talks about it.

Rules of conduct in the botanical garden. In general, nothing special: do not smoke, do not touch animals and plants, do not litter.

Rules of behavior on a windowsill in some shopping center: do not sleep, do not eat.

It says right on the emergency call button that the fine for pressing it is $5,000.

An entire comic was drawn to explain that there is no need to feed the monkeys.

Singapore is literally the birthplace of Captain Obvious. Nowhere else have I seen so many clear explanations of obvious things. Here, for example, are the rules of behavior in the elevator. In Russia there are also rules of conduct in elevators/on escalators/in subway cars, but they are written in small print somewhere in the corner, but here it’s really interesting to look at them.

How not to litter and spread infections.

How to dry your hands after washing.

Give up your seats to disabled people, elderly people, passengers with children and pregnant women.

It's obvious: a branch can fall from a tree, so be careful in the park.

It seems that in Singapore there is danger waiting for you literally on every corner. If a branch falls on your head, your phone will be stolen.

(It says "no smoking" in Chinese at the bottom right).

Or they’ll grope you in the subway. In fact, the likelihood of such troubles in Singapore is much lower than in 99.9% of cities in the world.

The creators here show truly parental care, treating everyone little children. Did you forget your suitcase in the airport toilet?

During rush hour on a weekday there will be a crowd here, go to the farthest carriage.

Don't lose the balloons, otherwise they will stop the train.

Sorry, we're building a new metro station here.

Buy your child a ticket.

Park at your own risk.

On the streets there are boards with a counter of crimes solved recently in the area.

Don't be fooled - Singapore is not a concentration camp, it's no tougher here than in any other normal city. It's basically forbidden to act like a pig.

Singapore's downtown is very reminiscent of city centers in the USA. Unlike Asian cities with skyscrapers, here the business district is separated from residential areas and shopping areas, only offices, only hardcore.

And cars.

View from Chinatown.

Looks like San Antonio.

Further east is Marina Bay, one of the most recognizable urban panoramas in the world.

The famous Marina Bay Sands hotel with a rooftop pool. The pool is only for guests who fork out at least 300 dollars for a room, but some cunning people made their way there by tagging along.

A beautiful bridge in the form of molecules.

Giant trees with light show.

In fact, in 15 years Singaporeans created a bunch of attractions from scratch, increasing the tourist attractiveness of the city.

By the way, about the rules of behavior. Someone is spending the night right on the Marina Bay embankment, no one even chased them away.

This is what the center of Singapore was like once upon a time under the British.

The old bridge, which is prohibited for livestock, is now surrounded by skyscrapers.

Opposite the town hall is an exhibition of contemporary art.

It's not uncommon to see some of the coolest vertical gardening in Singapore.

The parking lot immediately became much nicer.

Surprisingly, Singapore is not a very pedestrian-friendly city. There are a lot of giant junctions with a bunch of traffic lights across each lane. It is not uncommon to find intersections where there is only a pedestrian crossing on three of the four sides, meaning you may need to make three crossings just to cross the road. It is not recommended to jaywalk here, and not only because there is also a fine for this, but simply because in Singapore the traffic is busy and consists almost exclusively of cars, and not of mopeds, as in neighboring countries.

This is despite the fact that car usage here is discouraged as much as possible. For the first time I see that in addition to paid parking, there is also a toll for travel on some streets: these structures above the road read the license plates and automatically deduct 50 cents from all those passing by.

This, by the way, is Orchard road, an area with shopping centers.

A little away from the business center in Singapore, many old colonial quarters have been preserved. They are kept here in exemplary condition: everything is restored, painted, and a design code is applied that prohibits huge flashy signs.

Chinese restaurant with a small sign!

Mosque.

Similar houses can be found in the cities of neighboring Malaysia.

This is the area where I stayed in a hostel (Lavender metro station).

Muslim cemetery.

Not far from the hostel there was a public swimming pool, where entry even for tourists cost around a dollar (and for citizens of Singapore it was even half the price). For comparison, in St. Petersburg I have not found a pool cheaper than 250 rubles ($4) for a 30-40 minute session, but here you can swim all day.

Indian Quarter. Incredible - even the Indian quarter is clean here!

There's a bit of color.

Indian temple.

In Singapore, housing is expensive; I paid $12 per bed in a hostel. A month in the Indian quarter costs at least $200 - also not budget friendly; in Malaysia and Thailand you can rent an entire apartment for this money.

Street signs in Singapore are the same colors as in the USA.

In the Indian quarter, the text is dubbed in Tamil.

Not far away there is also the dirtiest place I have found in Singapore - the flea market.

Here they even throw garbage into the canal, oh wow!

Chinese and Indian food is widely available in Singapore, as is the case in Malaysia. It's cheaper than a "European" restaurant, especially if you find a food court where office workers go. For example.

Near the Indian quarter, I managed to go into a residential building and take the elevator to some upper floor, where there was an open balcony. Not a bad observation deck.

The center is there.

Low-rise Indian quarter.

Unlike Hong Kong, where vast hilly areas are given over to natural parks, Singapore is small and fairly flat, so almost all of it is built up. But there are still a few pieces of nature. The first one is the botanical garden included in the UNESCO list.

Here you can look at dozens of different fruit trees, some even with fruits. Unripe durian:

There are copies of different landscapes, for example a steppe with a cactus, like in Mexico.

Lizards that can be found in different cities of Southeast Asia live here too.

The second large park is Southern Ridges Walk - a chain of parks along the southern coast in the hills.

The entire trek follows paths that at times rise almost to the treetops.

The most elaborate bridge.

From here you can look at the residential areas of Singapore.

Cable car to Sentosa Island.

You can get there on foot for free.

Sentosa has the only beach in Singapore with a view of cargo ships - well, that's what it is. If you want to go to the beach, there are ferries from Singapore to the nearest Indonesian islands.

There are also several amusement parks in Sentosa.

And the second statue of a mermaid-lion.

Singapore Airport can also be considered a kind of landmark - according to many ratings it is the best airport in the world.

Everything here is really polished to a shine.
