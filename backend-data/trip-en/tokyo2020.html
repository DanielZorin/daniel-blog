Chiba

From Narita I quickly went to the regional capital, Chiba.

It's a big city with a giant train station.

The main entrance to the station.

The main attraction of Chiba is the suspended tram. This is the largest system of such rare transport in the world. Excluding small systems at airports and amusement parks, there are only three suspended railways operating as public transport in the whole world - in Chiba, between Kamakura and Enoshima Island, and in German city ​​of Wuppertal.

It's like a monorail, but the rail is on top instead of underneath.

There are two tram lines in Chiba; they do not run very often - about once every 10 minutes.

Parking spaces are marked near the residential building. In Japan they like to install such wheel stops, apparently so that no one accidentally backs into the car when parking.

Since there is little space in Japan, you can often find cars with an unusual square shape - in order to make maximum use of the space, the trunk is made the same height as the interior, or in other models there is simply no trunk in the back, there is glass immediately behind the rear seats. The complete opposite of the USA, where the most popular cars are generally pickups that instead of rear seats have a huge body with an open top.

The AirAsia flight from Honolulu lands in Osaka, not Tokyo, so I had almost a full day of transit in that city.

I had to check out from the hotel at 10, and the plane to Tokyo was only at 5 o'clock, so I went for a walk around the city center. The hotel was not far from the cheap Shin-Imamiya area, same as last time.

Tower in the "New World" area. It’s interesting that in Japan the opening hours are not the same as in Europe - at 10 am almost everything is closed, it’s quite normal for a store to open at 11. In Russia and Europe, for some reason, all stores torture employees making them arrive at work at 9 or even 8 , although in the morning there are approximately zero visitors. Near Harajuku Station, I noticed a series of posters. If you don't translate the text from Japanese, you'll never guess what it's about. It looks like stills from a manga for girls about the romantic relationships of schoolchildren, playing with the cliches of this genre, the drawing style is also recognizable and characteristic of girls' manga. But in fact, this is social advertising calling... to set strong passwords on social networks. The boy hugs the girl with the words “I will hold you tightly... until you change the password!”

He supports her in his arms with the words “Be careful, your password is not strong!”

"How cruel! To reuse the password..."
