Georgia: Ninotsminda, Akhaltsikhe, Khertvisi, Vardzia, Borjomi, Kutaisi, Batumi, Stepantsminda

6-10 May 2014

Ninotsminda

The border between Armenia and Georgia (Bavra-Ninotsminda) was in complete wilderness. Outside the gates of the fashionable Georgian border terminal, an enchanting sight awaited: a complete absence of taxis, although I wouldn't mind one. However, quite quickly we reached relative civilization - the city of Ninotsminda, formerly Bogdanovka. Villages in Georgia are in complete ruin, in Ninotsminda it is no better. I was too lazy to photograph everything there in detail, but this shot should give an idea of Ninotsminda.

This is what this village looks like.

Here we were picked up by a truck (for the first time on the trip!), and the driver did not speak Russian (also for the first time), since the truck was Turkish and was heading to Batumi.

Vardzia

There are sometimes direct minibuses from Akhaltsikhe to Vardzia, but it was too slow for our schedule. Therefore, the plan was this: hitchhike about 40 kilometers along the Akhaltsikhe-Akhalkalaki highway, get off near the turn to Vardzia and take a taxi for the last 16 kilometers. This plan completely failed: in the village of Khertvisi, located at the fork, there was no taxi. Why is it that in those rare cases when a taxi is really needed, there isn’t one, and at airports and train stations, they cost ten grand each? But there was an excellent castle there.

And also the confluence of two rivers and two mountain valleys. One of the rivers is Mtkvari, also known as the Kura.

Children walk to school across a suspension bridge.

Village.

A funicular is being built near the castle.

After walking near the castle, we decided that we should hitchhike further.

This turned out to be no easy task. On Google maps the area generally looks like a desert, but in reality there are small villages along the way. Something like this.

In total, the road to Vardzia from the highway took almost two hours.

Since the distance there is only 16 kilometers, we can estimate that we walked 5-6 kilometers while waiting for cars to appear on the horizon.

But it was worth it.

Kutaisi

I just arrived in Kutaisi and took photo of the local Mac...

...when the rain poured down.

Since the roads here are not at a high level, everything began to flow with terrible force, and my feet got wet.

We barely managed to hide in the main attraction of Kutaisi - the Bagrati Temple (they are threatening to exclude it from the World Heritage list because Saakashvili restored it from ruins).

It was getting dark, and after a look at the local architecture...

We set off on the final leg of the trip to Batumi.

Batumi

Mikheil Saakashvili wanted to make a European-level resort out of Batumi. After his departure, these Napoleonic plans were curtailed, but a lot had already been done, so we can safely say that Batumi became the most civilized place on the Black Sea coast of the USSR (probably, Sochi can now compete for this title, but I haven't been there). There are no resorts on the Black Sea in Turkey; there are resorts in Bulgaria and Romania, but I can’t say anything about them. In the city center it is like Europe.

They made bike lanes.

Stepantsminda

Until recently, Stepantsminda was called Kazbegi, and they still write that on minibuses. Although Mount Kazbek is located nearby, it has no relation to the old name of the city, which came from the name of a Georgian writer.

Stepantsminda is a village.

But there are a lot of tourists here, because this is the largest settlement on the Georgian military road, and probably the most accessible place in mountainous Georgia (it’s a long drive to Svaneti).
