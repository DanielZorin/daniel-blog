Canada: Montreal, Quebec

20-22 May 2018

Montreal

My acquaintance with the second largest country in the world began in Quebec, a region where French is spoken. French colonies in Canada appeared in the 16th century and were taken over by the British after the Seven Years' War in the mid-18th century, however, Quebecers managed to preserve their language even after 250 years. Unlike Louisiana, whose population in the United States was a clear minority and was practically assimilated into the English-speaking environment, in sparsely populated Canada they made up a significant proportion of the population. And today, most people here speak French, and the signs are not even all in English.

Getting around Montreal is quite convenient thanks to four metro lines. The fare is $3.50, or $10 per day. The stations look quite stylish in some places.

It is curious that Montreal carriages run on rubber wheels. Such cars are most often used in the world for small systems, such as trains connecting terminals at an airport (for example in Hong Kong, Kuala Lumpur and San Francisco), but there are also large systems, in particular, almost all metro systems in France, including part of the Paris metro, as well as the metro in Mexico City.

Quebec

The capital of Quebec is called Québec. It is interesting that in English the province and the city are spelled differently - the city is written Québec with an accent mark, as in French (and in speech they simply say Quebec City). In French, they are distinguished by the presence of an article with the name of the province (but not with the name of the city). It may be the most "European" city north of Mexico.

Nice train station, accessible from Montreal (but cheaper by bus).
