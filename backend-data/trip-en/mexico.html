Mexico City is one of the largest cities in the world. It’s good that it has many convenient metro lines; you don’t need to understand the bus system and stand in traffic jams. The fare is inexpensive - 5 pesos ($0.40). For those who can't count, it is written at the box office how much 1,2,3...27...50 tickets cost.

At rush hour there is quite a crowd in the metro, just like in Moscow.

In particularly difficult situations, women are sent to a separate carriage.

As in Guadalajara, stations are indicated by unique pictograms.

The interchanges are very long, you have to walk for 10 minutes. In the passages they organize exhibitions and put up instructive stands.

We study astronomy here.

Cuernavaca

In 1910 there was Stability(tm) in Mexico. 34 years earlier, in 1876, Porfirio Diaz became president. When he served out his term, for the first time he put someone in his place, and the next time he did not bother to comply with the constitution, each time receiving 146% in the elections. The people approved of the National Leader, who saved the country from the turbulent 1860s, raised it from its knees, so that all Mexicans began to dress better and eat rye and vegetables, that’s all. The National Leader was opposed by a bunch of liberal creaks who lived on grants from the State Department, but they did not enjoy popular support and always lost in the elections. In 1910, Mexico held the Olympics celebration of its centenary of independence. But in 1910, something went wrong. (This is irony, I am drawing parallels with the history of Putin's reign in early 2000's and using the popular Russian memes)

The main problem in Mexico was the lack of land among the peasants. At the beginning of the 20th century, Mexico was an agrarian country with communal farming (just like the Russian Empire), but during the reign of Diaz, most of the land ended up in the hands of local landowners (latifundistas). This happened because the Indian peasants did not have any documents for the land where they had farmed for many generations, so the tricksters were able to quietly privatize it. Mexico needed at least something like Stolypin's reform to solve the agrarian question, but the government did nothing, and discontent accumulated. The reason was easy to find: in 1910, Diaz once again won 146% in the elections, and sent his opponent Francisco Madero to prison right on election day. Everyone was stunned by such impudence, and an uprising broke out in several regions.

At this time, the country first learned about two popular leaders: Pancho Villa in the north in the state of Chihuahua and Emiliano Zapata in the state of Morelos south of Mexico City (where Cuernavaca is located). They advocated radical land reform with the forced taking of land from landowners. Francisco Madero himself was more restrained in this regard, because he understood that, having come to power, he would have to negotiate. Diaz's army lost to the rebels, and in the spring of 1911 the National Leader resigned and went on vacation to France, and Madero became the president.

As usual, the treason began immediately. Madero was in no hurry to distribute land to peasants and factories to workers; in fact, he did not really do anything during his presidency. Within a couple of months after Madero was elected president, Zapata declared him a traitor to the revolution and resumed guerrilla activities. Madero also did not carry out any lustrations; on the contrary, half of the government remained the same, and he appointed Victoriano Huerta, a former army general of Diaz, as commander of the army of the north. This turned out to be a very reckless decision: when Diaz’s nephew decided to start an uprising in 1913, Huerta, sent to fight with him, suddenly changed his color and staged a coup in Mexico City. Madero was overthrown and killed, and a military junta led by President Huerta came to power. Though, Diaz’s nephew was immediately thrown out of the country as the ambassador to Japan.

The revolutionaries of the first wave did not like this turn of events, since they perceived what happened as a kind of Thermidor, the return of the old order, although in words Huerta promised all the same reforms (in particular, he wanted to privatize land using vouchers). Huerta's main opponent was Venustiano Carranza, governor of the northern state of Coahuila, who called for respect for the constitution and holding elections. Villa and Zapata united in the fight against the junta. As a result, Huerta lasted only a year in power, and already in 1914 he himself went into exile.

After another victory in the revolution, a Convention was created, at which the leaders of the revolutionary armies had to agree on who the main revolutionary was. It was decided that Carranza should resign as interim president; he did not agree with this and fled to Veracruz to gather an army. Mexico City remained under the control of Villa and Zapata.

For the peasant leaders, this was the peak of their success: almost the entire territory of the country was under their control, with the exception of the Gulf Coast. But then they lost everything.

Due to the lack of effective command and coordination between the detachments of Villa and Zapata, the war did not go according to plan. Instead of uniting the armies, they began to act individually, and individually they were defeated, and they had to return to the partisans.

Carranza played his trump card: he once again promised to distribute land to everyone. Meanwhile, in Morelos, Zapata had already begun to implement a similar plan, confiscating land from the landowners in favor of the peasants.

To this day, the coat of arms of the state of Morelos says “Tierra y Libertad”, which is translated as “Land and Freedom”. The Mexican revolutionaries borrowed the motto from the Russians.

Meanwhile, the ousted dictator Huerta received money from Germany to stage a coup and bring Mexico into World War I on the side of the Germans. However, the unlucky Mexican Lenin was tied up at the border by American border guards.

After the defeat of Villa and Zapata, the country came under the control of Carranza. In 1916, in the city of Queretaro a new constitution was adopted for Mexico. It annulled all the seizures of land by the latifundists, committed during the reign of Diaz, and the land began to be gradually given to peasants and communities. Along the way, the minerals were declared state property, which displeased the United States, who tried to invade, but were rebuffed.

The adoption of a new constitution in 1917 is traditionally considered the date of the end of the revolution, although the revolution continued further.

Let's look at the fate of the main characters. Venustiano Carranza became President of Mexico in 1917. In 1920 there was a coup d'etat, during which he was overthrown and killed. The coup was organized by Alvaro Obregon, who was the commander-in-chief of Carranza's army during the revolution. He became the new president, holding this post for 8 years, until he was killed by a student fanatic.

Overthrown during the revolution, Porfirio Diaz died in Paris in 1915.

Victoriano Huerta was arrested by the Americans while trying to cross into Mexico and died in prison in 1916.

Pancho Villa, after the defeat of his army by Carranza, continued guerrilla warfare in northern Mexico. For a long time, the Americans supplied weapons to Mexico through the northern border, the area controlled by Villa. But when the United States decided to support the Carranza regime, the supply of weapons stopped. Villa organized a raid on US territory in order to loot weapons. This was used by the Americans to attempt a punitive operation in northern Mexico, but Villa remained elusive thanks to the support of the local population. However, his army melted away, and he could no longer capture any major city.

After the murder of Carranza in 1920, Villa was able to negotiate an amnesty with the new government. He was given a large estate, and the soldiers who remained with him were given a good pension. However, the prosperous life was short-lived. In 1923, Pancho Villa was killed and his car was shot at by an armed detachment. There is no clear answer as to what exactly caused the murder. Perhaps the authorities did not want Villa to return to politics. One version of the song La Cucaracha is dedicated specifically to the murder of Villa.

Emiliano Zapata was assassinated in 1919. He was going to negotiate peace with the general of Carranza's troops, but instead he was simply shot.

The memory of Zapata is still alive. This is practically the Mexican Che Guevara, a personality who has become almost legendary. And today in the south of Mexico there are guerrillas who call themselves the Zapatista Army of National Liberation.
