Indonesia: Bali

26 April - 9 May 2019

Denpasar

After Australia, I flew to Bali - this is the closest and cheapest arrival point. I decided to give the island a second chance and explore it more carefully than in 2016, fortunately this time I knew what to expect. Last time I was in two main locations (Kuta and Ubud) and a little on the east coast. The western part of the island is not particularly interesting; I passed through it on the way from Java. There is the southern part (south of Kuta), where I also visited a little, and where more elite housing is located. I set my eyes primarily on the northern coast and the mountainous central part. Usually, everyone visits all sorts of temples and other point of interest without spending the night, since in a day you can get anywhere, albeit spending a lot of time in traffic jams. I chose Lovina as a base in the north, but in the south there is still nothing better than Kuta - it is the most centrally located.

One day I decided to travel from Kuta to Denpasar, the capital and largest city of Bali. This is a city where tourists usually don’t go, because there is neither the sea nor any famous attractions. You can get to Denpasar for pennies on the fairly frequent Trans Sarbagita bus - an attempt to create civilized transport in Bali based on the model of Jakarta. We drove about 5 kilometers to Denpasar for about 40 minutes, however, it took me about the same long time to get back by taxi. This is Bali, baby, they don't like to drive fast here.

Lovina

To explore northern and central Bali, I decided to base myself in the coastal town of Lovina. There is a direct bus service from the Perama bus company, so getting there was no problem. Later I found out that you can get there much cheaper, since local minibuses run from the northern station of Denpasar to the large city of Singaraja, and they run not once a week, as in other places in Bali, but often, about every 30 minutes. From Singaraja to Lovina you can take a taxi for a few dollars, or wait for the more rare Singaraja - Gilimanuk bus (ferry to Java), which runs along the northern coast of the island.

Lovina is considered a quiet beach place. Unlike Kuta, there are no waves on the northern coast, so there is no surfing here. As a beach place, in my humble opinion, Lovina is not even a competitor to Pattaya - the beach is nothing, visibility in the water is five centimeters from algae and silt, and it’s downright unpleasant to swim. At the same time, there is no Pattaya breadth of entertainment here, it is just a small town with a limited choice of restaurants, in this regard Kuta is head and shoulders better.

Munduk

The most mountainous part of Bali is just south of Lovina and Singaraja. When you drive from the south of the island, you first climb a high plateau to Lake Beratan, then you drive to the top of the pass, from where you sharply descend along a serpentine road down to the shore of the sea. From Lovina I drove through the mountains exactly south of it, along lakes Buyan and Tamblingan.

There are surprisingly many roads here, not only one main highway, but also small narrow roads with a very large slope, on which it is difficult for two cars to pass each other.

Bedugul

Before heading back south from Lovina, I decided to stop for a day in the town of Bedugul, home to the famous Lake Beratan Temple, featured on the 50,000 Indonesian Rupiah note. When I got there, I was very surprised: it was as if I had teleported to Sulawesi or Sumatra - this is a completely non-tourist place, it looks like the rest of Indonesia. All tourists are brought to the temple by bus, there are souvenir shops at the entrance, and 200 meters away there are regular markets and noodle shops. There aren’t even really hotels on booking.com, only a couple of expensive ones. In fact, I found a hotel for locals, where I practiced my Indonesian language skills.

Nusa Dua

One day, to complete the picture, I went to the very south of Bali - the Bukit Peninsula, which looks like a wide ledge adjacent to the rest of the island. The Bali airport is located on the isthmus. In 2016, I was in that area on the beaches of the western half of the peninsula, and this time I started from the eastern half. The only civilian bus route in Bali runs from Denpasar there via Kuta, the final stop is Nusa Dua, a place where expensive hotels are concentrated.

The bus arrives at the only street in Nusa Dua that can be called an open-air shopping mall. There are restaurants and shops with perfumes (for some reason, rich tourists love to buy perfumes everywhere, so half of the airports are also packed with them) and souvenirs, and on the sides of this street there are high fences, behind which are the territories of hotels.

There don't seem to be many visitors.

Food is expensive: fried rice, for example, costs $5. In Kuta, even in an above-average class establishment it will cost 2, well, maximum 3. And in an ordinary warung it’s no more than a dollar, maybe even 70 cents.

Off the coast of Nusa Dua there is a place with so-called "blowholes" - these are when waves make passages in volcanic rocks and create fountains.

Entrance there costs a couple of dollars, the fee was introduced literally a couple of weeks before my arrival.
