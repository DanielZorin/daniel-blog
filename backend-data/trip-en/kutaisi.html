Georgia: Chiatura, Kutaisi, Tskaltubo

23-25 June 2023

Chiatura

The city of Chiatura in central Georgia is located somewhat away from the main routes, but tourists often come there to look at the local cable cars and scenic views. From Tbilisi you first need to get to the neighboring city of Sachkhere, and from there 10 kilometers by local minibus to Chiatura.

Previously there was an intercity trolleybus line between Sachkhere and Chiatura, now there are only minibuses. Chiatura was originally a mining town, as manganese was mined here.

Arriving in Chiatura. The city is small and located in a river valley. The center is below, residential areas are on the surrounding hills.

I read somewhere that in Soviet times there were 17 cable cars in Chiatura. On the Yandex map I counted 21. Almost like in La Paz, although they were of course much shorter in length. For a long time, the cable cars were barely repaired, but still partially operational. Thanks to that people came to Chiatura for the experience of riding in a creaky, rusty cabin, similar to the pepelats from the “Kin-dza-dza” movie. It seems that this was possible back in 2019, but then renovations started, and now in the center of the city there is this brand new station:

From here, cabins depart in three directions: to the south to the sanatorium and two to the west to residential areas.

There is another cable car very close by, which goes in approximately the same direction as the sanatorium, but lower. It was not operating that day, although overall it looks new.

If you cross the bridge over the river, you can see another cable car station, with two lines. This station is notable for the fact that it was the very first cable car in the entire USSR! Those who came to Chiatura before 2019 usually rode from here. Now this station is being revamped. The portraits of Lenin and Stalin are covered with plastic.

Kutaisi

In 2014, I passed through Imereti (the central part of Georgia), without really seeing Kutaisi. This time I came here for a few days to fix that. Kutaisi has always been the second largest city in Georgia, but just recently it was overtaken by the ever-growing seaside resort Batumi.

Kutaisi has a fairly large old quarter, somewhat less hilly than Tbilisi.

Across the river on a steep cliff stands the Bagrati Temple - the only place I really saw in Kutaisi last time.

Since then, it has been removed from the World Heritage List.

Because of the reconstruction - before, it was essentially ruins, only part of the walls without a dome. But now it has been fully completed.

Nearby there are still ruins of a fortress, which are overgrown with grass.

Initially, the Bagrati Temple and the Gelati Monastery were included in the UNESCO list, now only the monastery remains on the list. It is located near Kutaisi in the mountains, about 20 minutes drive.

Tskaltubo

Near Kutaisi there is a resort town called Tskhaltubo (or Tskaltubo), famous for its abandoned sanatoriums. In Soviet times, people from all over the country came here because they got tour vouchers from their jobs. After independence they became empty, but now they are gradually coming back to life.

There is a huge park in the middle of Tskaltubo, and sanatoriums along its perimeter.

The most famous tourist cave in Georgia, Prometheus Cave, is near Tskaltubo.

On a summer weekend there were a lot of people. It was a wise decision to lag behind the group (they go in every 15 minutes).

Sledgehammer.

The cave is quite long, I spent about an hour there just to get to the end.

Very cool lighting here.

Huge stalactites.

The largest hall.

The last part of the cave can be navigated by boat. A ticket with a boat ride is about 50% more expensive. From the map hanging at the entrance, I thought that the footpath ran parallel to the boat's path, but in fact, on foot you immediately exit through a tunnel. So it makes sense to take a ticket with a boat ride.
