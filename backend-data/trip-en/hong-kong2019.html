Once I went deep into Kowloon to look at the place where Kowloon Walled City was located. The nearest metro station is Lok Fu.

Kowloon Walled City is a legendary place, somewhere between Chungking mansions and Brazilian favelas. 50 thousand people lived on an area of ​​26 thousand square meters! Even taking into account 10 floors, this is 5 square meters for each. The city was controlled by criminal triads, but most likely, as in the favelas, most of the residents were just ordinary poor people, and not masters of the underworld.

In 1993, the city was completely demolished, and now in its place is this park.

A trip to Hong Kong would be incomplete without an expedition to the New Territories or distant islands. This time I went to look at the abandoned villages of Hong Kong. Yes, in Hong Kong, where real estate is astronomically expensive, where people live in apartments of 10 square meters, there are entire abandoned villages with empty houses where no one lives.

We start from Fanling metro station.

This is a residential area of ​​Hong Kong.

Here you need to take bus 56K and get to the final stop.

This is what the bus stop looks like here.

Near the civilization stop, there are a couple of eateries and a number of residential buildings.

We need to walk along the shore of the bay on the footpath.

The sign to the abandoned village of Fung Hang has become overgrown.

On the other side of the bay it is already China, the city of Yantian.

At some point the road rises sharply to go over a hill.

There is untouched nature all around.

And here is the village of Fung Hang. The building in the foreground is a public toilet, it works and is kept clean.

But most of the remaining buildings are abandoned.

Some are boarded up and clearly uninhabited, but they are still standing firmly; apparently, someone monitors them periodically and prevents them from collapsing.

And here someone may live, even have an umbrella.

You can look into an abandoned house.

The Hakka people lived in this area. It seems that most of them left back in the 1960s - 1970s, when, along with a large influx of refugees from China, farming in Hong Kong became unprofitable. Some moved to the city center, others even further, to the UK, to which Hong Kong belonged until 1997.

We go further along the coast to the next village.
