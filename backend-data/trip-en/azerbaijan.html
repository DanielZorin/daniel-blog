Azerbaijan: Baku, Gobustan, Ganja

28 April - 1 May 2014

Baku

Azerbaijan is the most expensive place in all the territories of the former USSR, more expensive than Moscow and Sakhalin.

* * *

Azerbaijan is the first absolute monarchy on the territory of the late Soviet Union. Power is unlimited and is passed on from father to son. This was not achieved even in Turkmenistan, where the son of the Turkmenbashi betrayed his homeland and emigrated to Russia.

The current president, before his father’s death, was not very active in politics, so there were assumptions that he would be a nominal ruler, but Ilham Aliyev turned out to be no timid person and quickly took all the power for himself.

It was under Ilham that the personality cult of Heydar Aliyev reached unprecedented heights. The peculiarity of this cult is that Azerbaijanis do not limit themselves to installing monuments at home, but strive to erect them all over the world, causing bewilderment among local residents.

Heydar Aliyev successfully replaced Lenin in Azerbaijan. His statements are already being quoted everywhere. To educate the people, quotes are placed at the entrance to each underground passage.

The son is not far behind and is saving up his own collection of quotes.

Of course, at the entrance to the school, a statement from you know who.

Inside the school... well, you get the idea.

With all this, the dictatorship in Azerbaijan does not look scary. While in Tashkent I felt like I was in 1937 (despite the fact that there is not a single portrait of the president there), Azerbaijan rather reminded of a comical Latin American republic from the book “Kings and Cabbages”. It's hard to take it seriously when the president is addressed as "His Excellency."

Even taxi drivers in Azerbaijan are not afraid to openly admit that all oil revenues go primarily to enrich the royal family. Considering how much oil per capita there is in Azerbaijan, the average salary even in Baku does not exceed about 25,000 rubles ($800). For comparison, the global price standard, a Big Mac at McDonald's, costs 150 rubles in Azerbaijan versus 90 in Russia.

So people leave Azerbaijan to work in Russia for a reason.

Gobustan

Not far from Baku there is the Gobustan nature reserve, famous for its mud volcanoes and rock paintings. You can get there by local bus, but it’s easier to go by intercity bus. To do this, you need to stand at the exit from the bus station and get on any bus that goes south, for example, to the city of Lankaran. For one and a half manats you can get to the town of Gobustan. To go back it’s easy to catch a local bus to Baku for 60 kopecks, since there is only one. The bus arrives at the outskirts, from which you can take any bus to the center for 20 kopecks.

In general, this is far from the only place in Azerbaijan where there are mud volcanoes, but it is the most famous and easily accessible.

You can only get to the volcanoes themselves by taxi, the standard price is 25 manats (if you don’t bargain, they’ll ask for 30), but thanks to Alena’s talents we managed to negotiate for 20.

There are not very many tourists, so it is almost impossible to find travel companions on the spot. There was also a group of Chinese, but they arrived in their own minibus.

Mud volcanoes are small and make squelching sounds.

You can touch them, the liquid inside is cold.

The liquid is not at all viscous, similar to ordinary muddy water.

The leaking liquid mixes with the clay and hardens. Figures can be sculpted from pieces of clay lying around the perimeter of the volcano.

And if you sit down and point the camera from bottom to top, you can take shots from the series “The World After Nuclear War” or “Hell on Earth.”

The road to the volcano is a dirt road, along which huge piles of garbage lie.

Rock paintings (petroglyphs) are 15 minutes away in the rocks.

You can't make kebabs.

Ganja

Ganja is the second largest city in Azerbaijan. Oil money still flows here, but foreign tourists usually do not, so here you can look at life without excessive gloss.

The first thing that catches your eye is that here you can find Soviet artifacts that were carefully cleaned out in Baku.

In general, Ganja is not a very 'eastern' city; it looks more like a Russian province. Previously, by the way, it was called Elisavetpol and Kirovabad.
