Germany: Düsseldorf, Cologne, Bonn, Trier

24-27 June 2012

Düsseldorf

Düsseldorf is a gray and gloomy city.

Even the obelisks here seem to have been taken... well, you understand where.

A typical street in the center of Dusseldorf.

The European Football Championship was going on, so there was an outpouring of patriotism unprecedented for Germany: flags were hanging in the windows.

In normal times this would be considered a sign of extreme nationalism.

Tram tracks dispel the melancholy.

Stops are indicated by cubes with the letter H (Halte).

Street signs with house numbers for the nearest block, like in Stockholm.

Cologne

One of the most uninteresting cities I've ever been to. There is absolutely nothing to see here except the cathedral. You can simply arrive by train, go into the cathedral, which is directly opposite the station, and leave. In a city where there are two red men at the traffic lights, things cannot be good, as we have already seen in the example of Oslo .

The city looks like this.

Traffic lights say "Please wait" when they are red.

Only occasionally does something stand out.

Like this balcony.

Over the river.

And one more.

And then it’s rough again.

Bonn

This is where the emergency communication poles in the Moscow metro come from.

Bonn stands in stark contrast to Cologne. The streets are much more lively.

Overall beautiful houses instead of gray boxes.

Bells are hung on the facade. For some reason, in every city in Germany there is one house decorated like this.

Beethoven was born in Bonn. This was also the capital of Germany before the fall of the Berlin Wall.

Trier

Of course, everyone wants to know what the farthest outskirts of a run-of-the-mill German city look like. It looks something like this. Overall very good, the main problem is that there is nowhere to go in the evening because nothing works. However, this is also typical for large cities.

Antique cars sometimes drive around the outskirts.

Trier, like Bonn, is pleasant to look at.

There are several typical German houses.

Castle.

Trier was on the northeastern outskirts of the Roman Empire; many buildings remained from the Romans, which is unusual for Germany.
