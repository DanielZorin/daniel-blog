* * *

I have long wanted to review a supermarket in Turkey. But due to the depreciation of the lira, prices were constantly changing. At the end of December 2023, when I took photographs, the exchange rate had settled at around 28-29 liras per dollar for six months. In general, many complain that Turkey has become expensive compared to 2022. But this is not entirely correct; rather, spring-summer 2022 was abnormally cheap, since then the lira had just fallen, and prices in dollars were gradually rising. As a result, prices in dollars have now returned more or less to the level of 2019, which is still much cheaper than it was in the golden times around 2013, when I was in Turkey for the first time. I will review the Migros supermarket closest to my home. This is not the cheapest supermarket, there are cheaper Bim and Sok, but in general it’s not that the prices are very different; in Migros there are more different options for each product.

It is not always easy to understand what is what based on price tags. Especially in the vegetable department, even if you speak Turkish. In general, I rarely buy vegetables and fruits in the store; as a rule, I buy them at the market, since they are fresher, cheaper, and there is more choice. For example, there are no tangerines here, and in the market they cost only 10-15 liras. Tomatoes and cucumbers at the market cost 25-30 liras (in this store 35), and in the summer they were 15. Mushrooms cost 80 liras. Pepper 40-50.

Potatoes cost 18 liras, garlic 95, onions only 7.50.

Meat. Chicken fillet costs approximately 150 liras ($5) per kilogram. In this store there are actually fillets from different manufacturers, so you have to be careful, otherwise you may end up paying 200 instead of 150.

A whole chicken costs 50-70 liras per kilo (about $2).

Beef minimum 400 per kilogram ($13).

For barbecue, it’s good to get ribs (pirzola) for 560.

In this refrigerator there are all sorts of minced meats, you can take minced meat or preparation for kebab or raw cutlets (kofte), for about 3-4 dollars per box (300-400 grams). There are also steaks that cost ~600 per kilo, but they are not available now.

Four sausages cost 45 liras.

On the left is a pack of the cheapest sausages - they cost 95 liras for a pack weighing a whole kilogram. On the right is the egg department, in which the last pack remains. In Turkey, eggs are often sold not by 10 or 12 (as in the USA), but by 15 or 30. 15 eggs cost 60 liras ($2).

Pork is not popular in a Muslim country, so a pack of regular cheap bacon costs $15 ($80 per kilo), twice as much as a steak.

Milk is quite cheap, a pack costs less than a dollar (24 liras).

For 50 liras you can get a delicious salep drink. It also comes in powder form, which is probably cheaper, but not every store has it.

Cheese in Turkey is mainly of local varieties, salty and similar to feta cheese.

The cheapest cheese on the market costs 120 liras per kilo, but here it’s a little more expensive.

More familiar yellow cheeses (kasar peynir in Turkish). Cheese from Migros costs 108 per 400 grams, usually used in salads and pasta.

The butter costs about 80 liras per 250 grams.

Activia yoghurts - 43 for 4 pieces, Danette without a promotion is about the same. By the way, to buy a product on sale, you need to have a discount card, which is sold for a couple of liras. On the right, Quark for 20 liras is something between cottage cheese and sour cream, you can add it to soup.

My favorite Kinder slices recently went up to 7.50 (was 6.50, but I still remember them for 2.75). Well, it's still very cheap.

Bread. In Turkey there is a standard bread, the price of which is regulated, it looks like this:

This loaf costs 7.50. There are special social kiosks where it is sold at half the price (but not in my village).

Thicker bread already costs 24.50.

A kilogram of small rice costs 25 liras, large ones are closer to 50. For 56 liras you can buy lentils (mercimek).

Also common in Turkey is cereal such as bulgur. It is cheap, around 30 liras per kilo.

Pasta is cheap, half a dollar per kilo.

The most expensive Barilla around one dollar.

For some reason, the instant noodles here are from the Indonesian brand IndoMie. One bag costs 7.75, five - 25.

Opposite the cereals there are different types of canned tuna, quite cheap - three cans for 70-80 liras.

Sunflower oil costs 42 liras, olive oil - around 350 per liter.

All kinds of seasonings for 20-30 liras. There is sumac, coriander, basil, ginger, oregano.

A jar of soy sauce costs about 75. The more popular pomegranate sauce here is cheaper.

A kilogram of sugar is 35 liras.

Sometimes the store has fresh fish (they put it directly on ice), but usually the seafood is frozen. Shrimp and squid cost around 500 liras per kilogram. On the market, fish costs around 250-300 per kilogram (regular dorado type), salmon around 400.

You can buy pizza for a couple of dollars.

Frozen vegetables 40-50 each.

Ice cream. Half a kilo costs about 70 liras, inexpensive. There is both regular ice cream and sticky Turkish ice cream.

A large cake also costs 80 liras, beautiful.

Sweets were cheap at one time, but now well-known brands like Twix and Toblerone have become much more expensive. Twix costs 19, and Snickers costs 25.

Toblerone costs some absolutely unimaginable money - more than $3, although I bought it at one time for about $1. The freebie time is over. Milka is also a bit expensive. But sometimes there are promotions for a dollar.

I often buy KitKat chocolates for 9.50.

Haribo marmalade costs 42 lira for a large package.

Oreo cookies are also very expensive, but local cookies are cheap, less than 10 liras.

A large pack of chips costs 40 liras.

Beverages. A large cola here is 2.5 liters and costs 45 liras ($1.5).

Iced tea only 25 liras.

Juice is also 25.

Iced coffee 20 liras ($0.70). There is a cheap one for 15, but I don’t like it.

They sell lemonade from Georgia, a small bottle for $1.6. I love Georgian lemonades, so I take them regularly. Previously, I also ordered large bottles of a different brand (but also pear) for $1.5 per liter (I looked now, they are back on sale again). Though, they once brought me tarragon instead of pear. Dr. Pepper can also be ordered for delivery at approximately 50 liras per can.

Regular water costs 25 liras for 5 liters.

Alcohol is expensive in Turkey (and is only available in the largest stores or special ones called Tekel). A can of Efes beer costs 42 liras ($1.5).

The cheapest wine starts from 150 liras ($5).

Turkish raki (aniseed vodka) costs 529 liras ($18) for 0.7 liters.
