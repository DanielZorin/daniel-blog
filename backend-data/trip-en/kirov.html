Russia: Naberezhnye Chelny, Yelabuga, Izhevsk, Kirov

23-25 December 2018

Naberezhnye Chelny

Naberezhnye Chelny is a city in Tatarstan, built from scratch in the 1970s around the KamAZ truck factory. We can say that this is a monument to the Brezhnev era: the architects designed an ideal city, as they saw it at that time. It turned out something like this: a city with square neighborhoods and wide avenues.

There is an airport in Chelny where Pobeda and many other airlines fly. This is the only Russian airport I have been to that does not have regular public transport. There are only special buses that run only a few times a day.

Panel houses are illuminated along the contour in different colors.

Sometimes there are constructivist monuments. Interestingly, in Naberezhnye Chelny there is no monument to Lenin in the city center.

A tall round tower in the middle of the city.

The central square looks like an airfield. From one end to the other, it’s probably 2 kilometers, and there’s almost nothing on it.

This is me still walking across the square.

I reached the next street. There is a tram in the city.

In Naberezhnye Chelny the streets are sooooo wide.

I wonder why such streets were designed in the USSR, if according to the plans it was calculated that a maximum of 5% of the population would have a car?

In Chelny, all blocks are square and have numbers. Although streets have names, addresses are usually called by block number house number, as in Zelenograd near Moscow.

Street signs in two languages.

While traveling on a tram, I noticed the Eiffel Tower.

Yelabuga

Yelabuga is the complete opposite of neighboring Naberezhnye Chelny, a small ancient city included in the list of historical cities of Russia.

Level streets lined with 19th-century mansions, the city is perfectly preserved.

Due to the heavy snowfall, there were clearly fewer people on the streets than there could have been.

The small streets are quiet and calm.

Izhevsk

The study of Russian regions took me to Izhevsk, the capital of the Republic of Udmurtia. The day was treacherously short, so I arrived in Izhevsk after dark.

Izhevsk is mainly known for its arms factory, which also produces Kalashnikov assault rifles.

The city stands on the shore of a lake into which the Izh River temporarily floods. At the same time, there is a highway along the shore of the lake, and the embankment is built on a hill, along with the main part of the city.

Kirov

Kirov a.k.a. Vyatka, another new region for me. The city is known for Dymkovo toys, Vyatka kvass, the trial of Navalny and the fact that one of the few large cities retained its Soviet name, although it is not immediately clear that it was derived from a surname; the city could have just received such a name. And before Vyatka the city was also called Khlynov.
