Mannheim

Mannheim is an industrial city in western Germany. It is interesting primarily for its unusual layout - the historical center consists of an even square grid of streets, as if it were a city in the USA or Mexico, and not in old Europe. Moreover, this is not the fruit of experiments of the 20th century; the city was built exactly like this back in the 18th century, along with the palace of the electors, which stands on the southern edge of the square. The palace now houses the local university.

You can count 10 blocks in a straight line from the palace, and to the right and left their number varies from 5 to 7, because a ring road is built around the grid. In the historical center, the streets do not have names; all addresses go by blocks that are named, like on a chessboard, by a letter and a number.

This is what the signs look like. The blocks to the left of the palace are called letters from A to K (the letter J is omitted), to the right - from L to U. The numbers go from the center of the palace to the edge, starting from 1. That is, the blocks closest to the palace are A1 and L1, then to the left there will be A2, to the right - L2, forward - B1 and M1.

Moreover, the architecture of the city is far from medieval; apparently, it was thoroughly bombed during the war.

One of the most beautiful cities in Germany is the university town of Heidelberg.
