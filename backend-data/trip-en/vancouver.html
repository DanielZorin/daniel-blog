Vancouver

Vancouver is the largest, and in fact the only city on the Pacific coast of Canada in the province of British Columbia. This is supposedly the most expensive city in Canada, and it has the most Chinese people, which is why it is also called Hongcouver. The city is growing rapidly, the entire center has long been covered in skyscrapers, but there is no easily recognizable landmark in this city, like the Space Needle in Seattle or the CN Tower in Toronto.

British Columbia is a beautiful region with many mountains and lakes. The most popular destination in the Vancouver area is Whistler Ski Resort, which hosted outdoor competitions during the 2010 Olympics. The prices there are such that Switzerland will seem cheap in comparison. However, beautiful places begin almost within the city limits, you don’t need to travel far, a regular bus will suffice.

It is convenient to travel around Vancouver by metro (SkyTrain), which has three large lines.

An all-day ticket costs $10 CAD and is also valid on buses. Vancouver has three fare zones (for example, North Vancouver is the second zone), but a day pass is valid for all.

The Vancouver Metro is the world's largest fully automated metro system.

Since there is no driver, you can stand in his place and observe (you can also do this in Kuala Lumpur)

It can be seen that apartment buildings with 15 floors are popular in Vancouver. A rare view in other cities in Canada and the USA.

I rode many stops, and along the routes I constantly saw high-rise buildings.

Looks like there's a one-story area in the distance.

But here is the city again.

This is New Westminster station in the city of the same name.

The city is notable for being the first in the region and the capital of British Columbia. In 1866, the territories on the mainland and the large Vancouver Island (not to be confused with the city of Vancouver) off the coast were united into a single colony, the capital of which was (and still remains) the city of Victoria on Vancouver Island. New Westminster lost its capital status, and later, when the Trans-Canada Railway was completed, it ceased to be the largest city, giving way to the expanding city of Vancouver, where the station was located.

Since the city is old, there are few traces of the late 19th and early 20th centuries.
