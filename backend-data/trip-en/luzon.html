Philippines: Manila, Mindoro, Banaue, Baguio, Angeles

19 June - 3 July 2015

Manila

Manila is a city where you can have a look but won't find any reason to stay long. You can look at the enchanting stinking garbage dump in the tropics. Manila is a city you will hate even if you are in transit there. I've been to Manila four times. By the fourth, I could no longer think of anything good to say about this city.

Immediately upon arrival in Manila, the visitor is greeted by the local incredibly convenient transport system. Well, at least it exists, which is not bad. But for a city that is twice the size of Moscow, it is a little lacking. The airport consists of several terminals that face each other with their backs. If you fly with different airlines from different terminals, you will have to take a taxi to travel between them. In traffic, yes. All the roads around the airport are simply clogged with traffic jams, more so than the roads in the city center. There is no transport at the airport itself, because the bus is such a tricky thing. You need to take a road without a sidewalk to a highway where jeepneys are driving in a traffic jam at a speed of 5 km/h. Oh jeepney!

All tourists in their reports talk about jeepneys with such aspiration: look, this is a unique transport! Look how brightly colored they are! Look, no two are alike! It’s good to admire jeepneys from the window of an air-conditioned taxi, but go try to use them for their intended purpose.

I understand that Filipinos are not usually very tall, but even they have to crawl into a jeepney while bending over. Russian gazelles and Thai songthaews will seem like a very humane form of transport.

The anarchy is so strong that the jeepneys don’t even have numbers, which is nonsense for such a big city with hundreds of different routes. At least the jeepneys have signs in English, so if you know the names of the main streets, you can figure out where to board.

Manila also has a subway. Rumors about terrible crowds are somewhat exaggerated - I have never been in such a situation that it was even close to what is happening in Moscow on Vykhino in the morning. The metro costs about twice as much as a jeepney (20-30 pesos versus 10-15), but it is much more convenient than being stuck in traffic. Perhaps the most reliable way to get to the airport is to take the metro to the terminal, and from there take a jeepney/taxi/bicycle taxi. If you need to go to the third terminal (from where Cebu Pacific domestic flights fly), you can take a bicycle taxi through the huge slums surrounding the metro station to the intersection with the highway, and from there on foot. It is better to get to the fourth (main terminal) by jeepney, but you still need to get to the jeepney parking lot. In short, this is not Rio de Janeiro.

Probably, if you have a short vacation, it’s easier to spend money and get stuck in traffic jams by taking a taxi. At least you can sleep in it. All taxis in Manila have "May reklamo ka?" written on them. There don't seem to be many people willing to advertise on taxis.

Since the vast majority of tourists in the Philippines do not go beyond their beach resort, located on a small island far from the cities, the tourism infrastructure here is, to put it mildly, not very good. There are problems with hotels - the cheapest room costs at least 700 pesos ($16), and that’s even without air conditioning. You still need to look for it, there are only two such places. For locals, there are dormitories at a budget price of around 100-200 pesos with rooms for 20 people, but this is if you want to not just feel the bottom, but dig even lower. On the other hand, since there are no tourists, the locals are not trying to get rich at your expense, so they won’t cheat you anywhere, they won’t charge you double the price in a jeepney, or they won’t try to scam you.

Despite extreme poverty, Manila cannot be called a dangerous city. The main danger here is stepping into a deep muddy puddle.

They are well familiar with the depth and dirt here.

The life of a wealthy person in Manila will consist of standing in traffic jams and going to large shopping centers, because this is the only way to more or less protect yourself from trash and frenzy. Yes, and don’t take intercity buses. By the way, in Manila they haven’t even reached the point of creating a bus station; there are a bunch of small offices of transport companies in different areas of the city. You can also play a leisurely round of golf under the walls of the old city.

Visit a UNESCO-listed church.

And look at the monument to a man named Lapu-Lapu, who fought the Spanish colonists when they first appeared. The Philippines was a Spanish colony for about 300 years, before Spain lost the war with the United States in 1898. On the main square, in addition to the Lapu-Lapu monument, there is a whole alley of busts of various rebels, each of which has a caption about “occupiers”. On the contrary, Americans in the Philippines are treated very well because they liberated them from Japanese occupation and maintain several military bases that boost the Philippine economy.

There are many destroyed buildings in the old city.

Street signs a la Spain. Almost all Filipinos still have Spanish names and surnames, that’s all that remains from the occupiers.

The phenomenon of guest workers in the Philippines is on a grand scale. The fact is that most Filipinos speak English well: the country was a colony of the United States, plus English is the language for communication between numerous peoples from different islands. Filipinos have a specific accent, in particular they like the sound [a] in place of the letter A instead of the American [e], but it is much more understandable than the Indian accent. It turns out that the Philippines is the cheapest of the English-speaking countries, so the demand for workers is high. By the way, citizens of the Philippines, in order to leave the country, need either a “permit to leave to work” or a return ticket (for tourists). If they suspect that you want to escape, they may not let you out.

Mindoro

All Philippine resorts are located very far from civilization (unlike, for example, in Russia or China, where the beaches are usually in the city). You need to fly somewhere, then take a bus, then a ferry, then another motorcycle taxi. On the one hand, tourists do not see the poverty and squalor that make up large cities. On the other hand, this means problems with infrastructure: an extremely poor selection of goods in stores, a lack of parties and nightlife, you won’t be able to go anywhere much from the island; in less advanced places, even electricity may not be available around the clock. Suitable for retirees and those who want to escape from civilization for a week and snorkel, but these places are not comfortable for living. The closest resort to Manila is called Puerto Galera and is located on the island of Mindoro. You first need to take a bus to Batangas city (about two hours), then an hour by ferry.

I was never afraid of flying on airplanes, but sailing on this boat was really scary, it felt like a roller coaster when it was jumping on the waves. I arrived to Mindoro all green from the motions.

While you are loading onto the ferry back in Batangas (Luzon Island), children come up in skiffs to collect alms.

Mindoro looks like a lost paradise as you approach.

Puerto Galera is not essentially a city, it is several villages on a fairly large coastal area, among which there is a main one, which the locals call “Poblacion” (in Spanish this means “settled area”), and tourists usually live in the village of San Isidro, better known as White Beach. Apart from this resort area, the rest of the island of Mindoro (and it is quite large) is little developed and practically uninhabited. Life exists almost only on the coast; inside there is an impenetrable jungle. The road around the island is interrupted for a couple of tens of kilometers just at Puerto Galera, so you can only go in one direction.

Port. Upon entry, tourists are charged a so-called environmental fee of approximately two dollars. A common practice is - don’t feed the Filipinos bread, give them some nice pieces of paper, the more the better.

We initially settled in the "town", it is within walking distance of the ferry terminal. The city has a more or less large supermarket and several food outlets with typical Filipino food like fried chicken. Few hotels - the only one with reasonable price costs more than $17, and the quality is very low - wooden walls with holes, geckos, no shower (you can only shower with cold water from a ladle), no internet. But there is air conditioning.

Baguio

If you don’t want to leave Banaue at night, then the only alternative is a morning minibus to the city of Baguio, halfway to Manila. Six hours on hellish serpentines is a serious ordeal. Hotels in Baguio are bad again. The cheapest one cost almost $20, the room was normal (only there was no outlet, we had to run an extension cord from the hallway), the Internet worked well for once, but the shower/toilet on the floor was crap, like at a train station. The bonus included an animal corner where two snakes lived.

For two days, the green snake did not move a millimeter from its place.

When you approach Baguio, the city looks very picturesque - colorful houses on the hills, just like in Mexico. Disappointment awaits you inside, especially if the weather lets you down.

Angeles

The crystal dream of many Filipinos is to get out of the Philippines. It is traditionally easier for women to do this, because for them there is the option of emigration through marriage. To bring the dreams of ordinary Filipino girls closer to reality, there is the city of Angeles, the purpose of which was originally to serve the nearby American military base. Now, aging soldiers continue to hang out in the familiar places in pleasant company.
