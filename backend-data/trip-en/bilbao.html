Spain: Zaragoza, Pamplona, San Sebastian, Bilbao, Barcelona

12-19 February 2014

Zaragoza

The name "Zaragoza" came about as a result of a gradual modification of the Roman "Caesaraugusta". Still, the Spaniards are far from the French, who changed the Roman "augustus" into "aout", consisting of pretty much one sound [u]. Now Zaragoza is a fairly large city with a population of about 700,000, located on the periphery of tourist attention, but still has a certain flow due to the fact that it is located halfway between Barcelona and Madrid.

During the Napoleonic Wars, Zaragoza became a symbol of Spanish resistance to the French occupiers. The French fought for every house and suffered huge losses, killing most of the city's population. There is only one reminder left from those times: the gate where the fortress wall, which has not been preserved, once passed.

Pamplona

Once a year in the city of Pamplona, bulls are released from this pen.

There is nothing inside the pen during the off-season.

Bulls run past the town hall.

Along the main street.

And to the bullring.

A huge number of tourists come to Pamplona to watch the running bulls, and maybe even go for a run themselves. This action is called Encierro.

The rest of the time it is a quiet small town.

Here you can rent a luxurious room for 25 euros, with an anti-hacking Wi-fi password.

The architecture is lovely and varied.

In the center of the city there is a well-preserved star-shaped fortress, which has now been converted into a city park and picnic area.

Animals live in a nearby park.

Residential area.

Sometimes the city is revived by political struggles for autonomy. There are many Basques living in Navarre, but they have less autonomy than the neighboring Basque Country. There is a movement to unite Navarre with the Basque Country.

In Basque, Pamplona is called Iruña. Both names are often written on public transport schedules.

The most beautiful street signs here are in two languages.

Bilbao

Bilbao is the largest city in the Basque Country (but not the capital, the capital is Vitoria). There are no popular beaches in the immediate vicinity, so tourists come here mainly to see the Guggenheim Museum and other examples of modern architecture.

Since I am not much of a connoisseur of contemporary art, I did not go to the Guggenheim Museum.

I limited myself to exploring the surrounding area, which is printed on souvenirs from Bilbao.

The second attraction of Bilbao after the Guggenheim Museum is the Biscay Bridge - the world's first "flying ferry", built at the end of the 19th century. The bridge is located outside of Bilbao and connects two neighboring cities - Portugalete and Getxo. It’s easy to get there by metro; it has two lines that run together in the center and then diverge along the two banks of the river. The Bilbao metro was designed by Norman Foster.
