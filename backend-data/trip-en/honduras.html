Honduras: Tegucigalpa, Comayagua, San Pedro Sula

25-27 October 2021

Border

Honduras, Honduras, in the heart of each of us. The name of the country has become a kind of meme in Russian language, but in general Honduras does not stand out that much among other countries in the region - in crime it is lower than El Salvador, in poverty it is lower than Nicaragua. The country lags somewhat behind in terms of tourism, because backpackers often transit through it without stopping (the direct road from Guatemala to Nicaragua/Costa Rica passes along the southern edge of Honduras, past the capital). Tourism is concentrated on the island of Roatan off the Caribbean coast, which is separated from the rest of Honduras - cruise ships call there and it has its own airport. I never found direct buses, so I crossed the border both at the entry and exit on foot, taking a chicken bus to the border crossing. The border with El Salvador is called El Amatillo.

Tegucigalpa

Tegucigalpa is the capital of Honduras, a city of a million people in the mountains. This city is not as terrible as I imagined: as it turned out, there are quite decent areas, and not only with villas behind high walls. And it even has its own small skyline, unlike most other capitals in this region.

Buses come to the poor part across the river, which is technically a separate city called Comayagüela.

A typical street in Comayagüela, note there is also a Chinese canteen here.

Somewhere around here I got out and hailed a taxi, since Uber cars don’t hang out in this area, it was a long wait.

I needed to take a coronavirus test to enter Nicaragua, and the most convenient way to do this is in the capital, there are several options, one of which was not even super expensive, but just expensive ($80). The lab was in a good area, but it turned out that Covid tests were taken at another department in the same part of the city, but several kilometers away, so I had to take an Uber, but in the end I made it before the end of working hours.

San Pedro Sula

After Comayagua, I went to San Pedro Sula, a city famous for having the highest murder rate in the world in the first half of the 2010s, until Caracas took the lead. In 2019, according to Wikipedia, San Pedro Sula fell back to 15th place, however, it is still the most dangerous city in Central America without taking into account Mexico (which seems to be experiencing a crime wave right now).

The main road of Honduras is of good quality, even on a cheap public bus I got there faster than I expected.

There are quite a few restaurants with verandas and picnic areas along the shore, it seems that locals like to come here to chill.

And for some reason banana chips.

The San Pedro Sula bus station is located at the exit of the city near the ring road. The station is quite new and colossal in size.

Food court inside.

This is a very rare case in Central America for a large city to have a bus station where all the bus companies are gathered, although, unfortunately, it did not reach the idea of a general ticket office, each company has its own ticket office.

I traveled back with the most expensive company, and the bus was extremely disappointing. It looks like it's only expensive because it closed half of its seats due to the corona virus.

I took a taxi and drove to the central square. I didn’t take any photographs along the way, but the views of the outskirts of the city (at least from the south side) were a pleasant surprise - I expected solid favelas and trash, but there were many new shopping centers and decent cars.
