Gibraltar

23 July 2016

Gibraltar is a small peninsula in southern Spain, which became a British possession following the War of the Spanish Succession (1701-1714). The Spaniards still demand the rock back, but the locals don’t want it. Previously, the rock was of great strategic importance, now it is more of an offshore, and military bases have been turned into museums. In 2016, Gibraltar began to admit Russian citizens with a Schengen visa. Makes sense - anyway, 99% of people visit Gibraltar for one day from Spain. It’s still impossible to get to England through Gibraltar; staying there illegally is extremely unlikely - the country is too small to hide. Most likely, previously nobody cared to update the visa rules, because the vast majority of tourists are from the European Union, Canada, the USA, Australia, Japan and Korea anyway; they do not need visas. I can assume that entry with a Schengen visa was made primarily for the Chinese, who visit Europe more and more.

In one respect only, Gibraltar is not Britain: here the overwhelming majority voted against Brexit in the referendum.

Under Franco, the border with Spain was closed for some time, so the British built an airport in Gibraltar, so you have to cross the runway on the way from the border. Now there are several flights a day from the airport, including low-cost flights to London.

The standard program in Gibraltar is to walk along the main street and climb to the top of the cliff by cable car. The price of 20 pounds seemed ridiculous to me (on the other hand, this is the only expense most tourists in this country have, so the desire to get the most is understandable), so I walked up. After Taishan, Zhangjiajie, Sugarloaf, Monte Alban, I climbed Gibraltar. The inside of the rock is all dug up with tunnels, some of them are abandoned, some have been turned into a museum, and some are still in use.

I also walked back through the airport. In Gibraltar, like in the UK, there is no exit passport control, and the Spanish border guard also did not even look at my passport. Here you can slip into the Schengen zone without a Schengen visa, however, to do this you need to get a much more problematic British visa in order to fly to Gibraltar via London. In the end, I never supported Gibraltar's economy even with one penny: although it is a pleasant country, the prices there are steep.
