Kokhma

In Russia, there is a very strange custom: if someone dies in a Soviet building, then a coffin lid is placed near the entrance, while you can say goodbye to the deceased in his apartment. This doesn’t happen in large cities, because the population there is an order of magnitude larger, and not everyone knows all the residents of the house, and it’s hard to imagine that a deceased person would be left lying in an apartment.

I was in Kokhma twice: in 2008 and 2011, but then my acquaintance with the city was limited to the kiosk "Products. Tobacco. 24 hours. Cheap." It must be said that in small towns, stores that operate 24 hours a day, at night, as a rule, sell through a small window, even if the store itself is a large self-service supermarket. This is done for security reasons.

Kokhma is a revolutionary city. Previously, there was a monument to Lenin in this place, but in the 90s it was blown up. Then, in the 90s, Kokhma separated from Ivanovo and became a proper city, however, life did not improve from this.

The city has an obligatory attribute of modernity - a brand new temple.

Plyos

Plyos is a resort on the Volga where Medvedev’s dacha is located. There was also a huge sanatorium here during the Soviet era, but it has been abandoned for twenty years, as a result it has turned into a gloomy Silent Hill in the middle of a rather positive city.

Houses that previously housed vacationers stand with broken windows and boards falling off.

Shuya

I was peacefully photographing a trash can near the Staraya Shuya restaurant, when a strange-looking dork came out of the restaurant and began to explain to me that the law is that even on the territory near the restaurant you cannot take photographs without permission. I was polite and calm, and it paid off! Although I deleted the photo of the urn (I didn’t even restore it because I took it out of boredom), the guy asked to take a photo of him, so I still had a great photo of a colorful character.
