South Korea: Jeju, Busan, Gyeongju, Daegu

23 May - 5 June 2015

Jeju

So, after almost three months in China, I flew to Korea on a Shanghai-Jeju flight. Staying in one country for such a long time was my first experience of this kind. Previously, I rarely spent more than a week at a time in one country, especially in small European countries, and this is usually not enough to get to know how the country works. When you book hotels on the Internet (because you arrive late in the evening and don’t want to search on the spot), you don’t see the entire market. With a busy schedule, you don’t always find the best prices in supermarkets, and the cheapest restaurants pass you by. When you have little time, you take the fastest transport, not the cheapest. If you are visiting for a few days, there is no point in buying a local SIM card or learning the language. I know how the system works in China: how to read the menu in a canteen, how to find out the right bus route, how to bargain in a hotel. Now I had to figure out how to do all this in Korea.

Korea is considered a high-tech country, almost like Japan: LG, Samsung and so on. The biggest technological innovation I found there was the Jeju Airport toilet with remote control. Then they never met again. Internet in Korea is a little worse than in China, and more expensive, on the other hand, nothing is blocked.

There was no cultural revolution here, but the Japanese occupation from the beginning of the 20th century, the careless attitude of the occupiers towards the culture of Korea, and the Korean War of 1950-1953 were merciless, so the antiquities are represented mainly by individual temples or palaces, and one can only guess about the historical layout of cities , seeing the ring road on the map.

English in Korea is better than in China, but still not very good. Korean language has no relatives. It has the same complex politeness system as Japanese, and a lot of borrowings from Chinese. Previously, Koreans wrote in hieroglyphs, so knowing Chinese, one could understand a lot, but now the alphabet is used, and it is difficult to guess by sound. For example, "Jeju", written in hieroglyphs, would be read in Chinese as "Jizhou", and the last character on this tablet ("hall") is "guan" in Chinese, and "kwan" in Korean.

The Korean alphabet is the easiest to remember. It has fewer letters than Latin, but similar sounds correspond to similar letters. Thus, simple and aspirated consonants (in English transliteration they correspond to voiceless/voiced ones) always differ in that aspirates have an additional horizontal line. Some signs still use the old version of the English transliteration (in the new one it will be written as Sam Seong Hyeol)

* * *

Jeju Island is interesting primarily for its volcanic rocks and caves. For the Koreans themselves, this is also the beach part of the country. But the climate in Korea is not very warm, you can only relax on the beach for a couple of months a year, and it’s cold in May-June. The island is small, you can go around it in a circle on a local bus, which stops in every village, in about three hours. There are two cities - Jeju on the northern coast of the island and Seogwipo on the southern coast. Jeju has government, airport, shops and motels, Seogwipo has a football stadium (used for the 2002 World Cup) and beach resorts. Traveling by bus is quite convenient: there are two routes from Jeju to Seogwipo along the coast, one along the eastern half of the island, the second along the western, as well as several routes through the center, on which you can get from one city to another much faster, and you can also get off in the middle near Mount Halla.

Daegu

Daegu is the fourth largest city in Korea, after Seoul, Busan and Incheon (a suburb of Seoul). The city is not touristic at all, there is no sea or any famous places. I spent two days in Daegu.

An ordinary street consists of low houses with a huge number of signs.

A little further there will be areas with multi-storey buildings, similar to Moscow. In Korea, house numbers are always written in huge numbers on the side.

Looking at the map, you can guess that Daegu was once a medieval city surrounded by a fortress wall, but now the center looks like this.

The street layout has been preserved; in some places there are replica gates at the entrances.

One antique area was still found. I didn’t find any information about this place, so most likely these are former slums that have now become civilized one-story houses, but they didn’t have time to replace them with apartment buildings.

I settled near a relatively small bus station, where the bus arrived from Gyeongju. Across the street was the giant Daegu East Train Station, where the high-speed trains from Seoul to Busan stop. The station area is packed to capacity with motels.

There are so many motels not because Daegu is a tourism center, but because Koreans need somewhere to meet in private.

Motels have more expensive rooms with round beds and mirrors on the ceiling, but this is too much luxury. A cozy room with a cooler for 25,000 won ($23) was quite enough for me. The TV had porn channels.

The porn they showed there was very funny. Like the Japanese porn, it was censored, but not with the blurred squares, but by the composition of the frame itself: the angle was always such that the genitals were not visible.

Because of this, actors mostly have sex with their clothes on. At the same time, they moan so picturesquely (men are even louder than women) that doubt arises whether the process really takes place, or is it also imitated?
