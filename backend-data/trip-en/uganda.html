Kampala

The capital of Uganda is quite an interesting city, fortunately it is located on picturesque hills, and Lake Victoria is not far away (in the neighboring city of Entebbe). But the main feature is the traffic jams, the whole city is one continuous traffic jam, since for a population of 7 million there is not a single road wider than two lanes. Even taxi apps offer mostly motorcycles, not cars. Cars are only available in Uber, and the price is much higher than usual in Africa.

Luckily, the bus dropped me off in the very center, I found a hotel in the nearest block, so I didn’t have to go far, I walked and rode longer distances by motorcycle taxi. First - to the independence monument.

It’s somehow really quiet and calm here, there are some big offices and government buildings around.

The ad offers work in marketing and sales, salary 50-80 dollars a week.

Someone hung a portrait of Idi Amin on the minibus. He was the president of Uganda from 1971 to 1979, one of the most odious rulers in world history. He did many eccentric things, for example, he wanted to put up a monument to Hitler. They also say that he declared war on the USA, and the next day he declared himself the winner, but I have not found any proof of this story in English-language sources. Of course, he personally shot the dissatisfied, and then ate them. Amin's rule ended with him deciding to attack Tanzania, but something went wrong, and the Tanzanian army occupied Uganda and overthrew him.

Jinja

Uganda borders the northeastern shore of Lake Victoria, the largest lake in Africa (Kenya and Tanzania also border this lake). The two main cities on the coast are Entebbe (a suburb of Kampala) and Jinja, 100 km to the east. Jinja is a pleasant town, even a bit of a resort.

The hotel for $14 (50 thousand shillings) was quite good.

The city center, as usual, is a shopping area. What's strange is that it is far from the lake, although one might expect some kind of embankment here.

It is forbidden to litter.

There is one high-rise here.

And there is quite a bit of colonial architecture on the main street.

Shopping arcades.

The end of the main street.

Post office.

An abandoned building with columns.

On the right you can see the roof of a Hindu temple. In Uganda and Kenya there are still many people of Indian origin, they moved here during colonial times.

A cheap snack bar.

On the left is the same mashed bananas matooke, the main local side dish.

I took a motorcycle taxi and went to the river bank. Jinja is located at the place where the Nile River flows out of Lake Victoria. More precisely, the White Nile, there is also the Blue Nile, which begins in Ethiopia. They merge in Sudan (the capital of Sudan, Khartoum, is there) and the Nile flows through Egypt to the Mediterranean Sea as a single river.

On the map I saw a road leading across the bridge to the other side.

As it turned out, the road is quite passable, the locals walked there and rode motorcycles to get to the area on the other side of the river.

This bridge is actually a railway.

Cabbage grows under the bridge.

Here it is - the source of the Nile. Lake Victoria is visible in the distance. Strictly speaking, this is not quite the source, because water also flows into the lake from somewhere. It is believed that the real source is in Burundi near Mount Kikizi.

Cable-stayed bridge across the Nile.

Apart from this place, there is nowhere to really go to the river bank from this side. There is another place where you need to go around, and there seems to be an official observation deck with a paid entrance.

Another Hindu temple.

I still wanted to stand on the shore of the lake, and took another motorcycle taxi. It's not easy to get to the lake shore either. After driving along the gullies of this road, I found myself in a fishing village.

And here it is, Lake Victoria.

I can safely return to the center.
