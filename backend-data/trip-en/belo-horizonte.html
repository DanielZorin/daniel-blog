Brazil: Foz do Iguacu, Belo Horizonte, Congonhas, Ouro Preto

16-21 January 2013

Foz do Iguacu

Brazil is very lax about border controls. I entered Brazil by land twice, from Paraguay and from Argentina, and not once did the bus even stop at passport control. The first time I had to run back to the border to get my passport stamped. This is not very surprising, because for residents of South America it is almost like the Schengen zone, passports are not needed, they only look at ID cards, there are no restrictions on the time of stay in the country. And for illegal immigrants, South America is not very attractive. The city of Foz do Iguaçu is quite boring. In the evening, nothing works; on the main street, only a store near a gas station is open.

Belo Horizonte

Belo Horizonte can't boast a rich history. The city was founded about 100 years ago. Unlike all the other Brazilian cities I've been to, Belo Horizonte is built on a grid with two diagonals, like La Plata in Argentina. And there are also many works by Niemeyer. More, probably, only in the capital of Brazil.

Niemeyer's creations are concentrated in the wealthy region of Pampulha in the north. The main one is the church, the sight of which would make the hierarchs of the Russian Orthodox Church begin to convulse.

Congonhas

People come to the state of Minas Gerais to look at Brazilian antiquity, of which a lot has been preserved here. I chose the cities of Congonhas and Ouro Preto. From Ouro Preto you can go to neighboring Mariana (half an hour by bus), but I liked Ouro Preto so much that I didn’t want to go anywhere. If you really want to, you can visit Congonhas and Sao Joao del Rei (and neighboring Tiradentes) in one day: most buses to Congonhas from Belo Horizonte continue on to Sao Joao del Rei (1 and 3 hours ride respectively) .

The path goes to the top of the mountain.

The main attraction of the city is this church. The place is quite touristy by Brazilian standards. Where else can you see a whole row of souvenir shops?

There are even people walking around here with large cameras around their necks, complete nonsense anywhere in Rio de Janeiro.

The Basilica of Bom Jesus de Matosinhos is so famous because Aleijadinho worked on it. It may be a legend, but it is believed that when this church was built, Aleijadinho (who also worked on many of the churches in Ouro Preto) had leprosy and lost his fingers, so he worked with a hammer strapped to his forearm. On the road down there are houses with wooden statues depicting the Passion of Christ.

City view.

There is an old-world charm on the hillsides.

Central square.

The way back was strange. It seems that there is a bus schedule, but in fact there are more of them. As soon as a new bus arrives, tickets for it begin to be sold at the ticket office.

Ouro Preto

Ouro Preto is the most beautiful city in the world. Here time stands still in the nineteenth century. Where else can you meet ladies in ancient dresses on the street?

The shops here are called "republics".

Socialist Republic of Coffee.

All the store signs are beautiful. "Jewelry of the State of Minas."

"Cut".

"Miners".

Railroad crossing.

A tourist train runs from Ouro Preto to the neighboring town of Mariana. Here it is. But that day it did not work, and the station was closed.

I never went to Mariana, although you can go by bus. It was too good in Ouro Preto. I watched football players practice, passing the ball to each other across the field.

In the middle of the city there is a completely abandoned hill on which there is no one, and only a structure similar to an altar stands. This is the best place to see the entire panorama of the city around.

Garbage collection.

They like to store garbage here in the corners, near the signs “Please do not throw garbage here.”

But even this does not spoil the impression when there is so much beauty around.
