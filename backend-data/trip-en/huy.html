Brussels

Compared to last time I liked Brussels much more.

There are still bags of garbage lying along the streets here, but this time I saw them being removed.

Everything is expensive in Belgium, food is twice as expensive as in neighboring Germany. Hotels are also expensive, but airbnb is better - for $30 I rented a small studio apartment not far from the notorious northern train station. Formally, this is not Brussels, but a suburb of Saint-Jos-Ten-Noode, judging by the sign.

White people in Brussels clearly do not live in the areas where I have been. In the center and north there are only tourists from other European countries. However, the Saint-Jos-Ten-Noode area looks quite decent, not dirty.

It turns out that there are two super avenues in Brussels, one running from west to east north of the historical center, the second from north to south east of the center. They intersect at right angles, and in the distance on each one you can see a huge church with a dome. Last time I was just living near that church that is visible in the distance.

Charleroi

I flew from Brussels early in the morning through Charleroi airport - one of the main Ryanair hubs. The price of a bus to the airport is high - 17 euros, although the ride is no more than an hour. An alternative is to get to the city of Charleroi for 10 euros, and from there for 5 euros on a local bus. Not so much savings. There were no Blablacars, so I came up with a plan to travel for 7 euros to the Courcelles train station, from where, judging by the map, it was a 4-kilometer walk to the airport. The first failure occurred when the train passed Courcelles without stopping, although the timetables on the Belgian railway website and in the ticket machine stated that there would be a stop. I had to get off at the Charleroi train station, from where the airport was not 4, but at least 8 kilometers away. However, there was more than enough time to spend 2 hours on the hike.

Charleroi used to be the poorest and most depressive city in Belgium, but now this is not noticeable, it's like any other city.

Bread vending machine.

The second failure was that this airport is not designed for pedestrians at all. Around the terminal there is a huge labyrinth of parking lots surrounded by high fences. As a result, we had to make a giant detour around the entire airport to the entrance for cars.

Inside the terminal is an interesting sight. Sleeping people lie along all the walls, and soldiers with machine guns at the ready slowly walk along the corridors.

This is not a concentration camp, no. It’s just that, apparently, many people don’t want to pay for an extra night in a hotel in Brussels.
