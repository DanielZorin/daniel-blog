Kobe

Kobe is a large city very close to Osaka and Kyoto. If you have an unlimited travel pass, you can use it as a base in Kansai, because the ride to Osaka is about 30 minutes maximum, and housing is cheaper here. Kobe is famous for its ultra-expensive marbled Kobe beef, but first of all I went to see the Kitano district, where many mansions from the late 19th and early 20th centuries are well preserved.

In Japan they are called "Ijinkan" - houses of foreigners.

Like Yokohama, Kobe was a major port, so visitors from Europe settled here.

Now many of them are museums.

Marugame

Okunoshima

The small island of Okunoshima has become famous due to the sky-high population of rabbits that inhabit it. And once it was a gloomy military base with a chemical plant.

To get to Okunoshima, you need to transfer from Mihara Station on the main line to a local train along the coast to Tadanoumi Station (in the city of Takehara).

There is a ferry from there, quite inexpensive - 620 yen round trip. You can also buy rabbit food at the ferry terminal.

The ferry is large and car-carrying; after calling at Okunoshima, it goes further to larger islands where non-tourist passengers go. The view from the water is like this:

There are no cars on Okunoshima, but there is one bus that runs from the ferry to the only building on the island - the hotel. But I easily walked around the entire island on foot in a circle, visiting the mountain in the center.

If you go in the opposite direction from the hotel, a destroyed factory building will soon appear.

During World War II, chemical weapons used in China were produced here. The location was chosen due to its remoteness and isolation.

The building is in poor condition, there are signs everywhere asking people not to climb.

After the war, the island was turned into a park; numerous buildings besides the plant were also abandoned.

Hiroshima

Of course, everyone knows about the city of Hiroshima, because it was destroyed by an atomic bomb in 1945. The bomb exploded over the very center of the city and destroyed everything around almost completely, because almost all the houses were small and wooden.

This famous building with a dome (now a UNESCO monument), which was left as a memorial, was located a couple of hundred meters from the epicenter, but since it was not wooden, the walls held up.

Interestingly, there are two other pre-war buildings in Hiroshima that survived the bombing. One of them is a bank:

The second one is now not so easy to find inside a shopping gallery.

A sign at the site of the epicenter on an inconspicuous street not far from the memorial.

On the site of the former city center at the junction of two rivers there is now a peace memorial park.

Paper cranes. The story of the girl who died 10 years after the war from leukemia caused by radiation, and folded 1000 cranes in the hospital, is widely known. Now Hiroshima is absolutely safe - only those who were there that day and received a dose of radiation were harmed. By modern standards, the bomb was not very strong and exploded high in the air, so a week after the explosion in Hiroshima it was quite possible to walk without a chemical protective suit. In Chernobyl, the power was thousands of times greater and the radiation contaminated the earth, so it is not entirely safe to travel there even now.

The museum has an interactive visualization of the explosion.

The second bomb was dropped three days later on the city of Nagasaki on the island of Kyushu. The diagram shows that due to the hilly terrain of Nagasaki, only a narrow strip between two hills was destroyed there, while the city center (down by the bay) was not affected so much. Hiroshima on the plain was completely demolished.

Now Hiroshima is similar to other large Japanese cities with a large business center.

There is also a reconstruction of the old castle here. This castle was destroyed precisely by bombing.

The main attraction of Hiroshima that is not related to the war is, of course, the island of Miyajima and the famous gate in the water, depicted on the cover of half of the guidebooks to Japan.

As a child, I looked at a photograph of this gate in a book about the wonders of the world and could not understand how it was possible to fit an entire temple into two columns and crossbars above them? I thought there was something inside the columns, but in reality it's just a gate in the shallow water.

And the temple itself is located on the shore opposite it.
