Heihe

First steps on Chinese soil.

By Chinese standards, Heihe is a complete wilderness, and also very cold. The population is a measly 200 thousand. I was sure that everything here would be tailored exclusively for Russian shopping tourists, but this turned out to be far from the case. In Heihe, no one speaks Russian, except for a few salespeople in the central supermarket who know a few words and numbers. Complete adaptation - you check into a hotel, explaining yourself with gestures, in a restaurant you choose dishes at random from pictures or a list of Chinese names. Perspicacious Chinese: why tie the entire life of a city to trade with Russia if the economy there could collapse at any moment and work would be lost? Harbin

Harbin is one of many Chinese cities founded by Europeans who grabbed parts of the weakening country in the 19th century. Following the successful annexation of the modern Far East after the second Opium War (1862), plans were prepared in Russia to squeeze out even more territories in northern China. A fleet base was built in Port Arthur and a railway was constructed to Vladivostok directly through the territory of China (it was easier to build through China than through the wild territories north of the Amur). Harbin appeared as a station where a branch began from the railway from Chita to Vladivostok to the south to Port Arthur. The railway has long been transferred to China, and Harbin is now a city with a population 10 times larger than Vladivostok.

After the 1917 revolution, many Russian emigrants lived in Harbin, but after the advent of communism in China, the community came to an end. All that remains is the Church of St. Sophia, which houses an architecture museum.

Changchun

On September 18, 1931, the Japanese blew up a railway on the outskirts of Shenyang (the road passed to the Japanese after the Russian-Japanese War of 1904-1905), blamed the Chinese for this, after which "polite" Japanese soldiers appeared in the territory of northeast China, supporting the desire of the local Manchus to federalize from China (It's a reference to the similar invasion of Russia into Ukraine that happened back then in 2014). The capital of the Manchu People's Republic Manchukuo became the city of Changchun, and the ruler was the former last Chinese emperor Pu Yi. Now, by the way, the Manchus, who conquered China in the mid-17th century and founded the Qing Empire, have almost completely assimilated, Manchu language practically disappeared from use.

How do Chinese museums differ from Russian ones? In Russia, in such places they usually lay out all sorts of objects that have survived from those times, but the Chinese instead make entire sculptural compositions illustrating the events described on posters and photographs.

In format, this is most similar to a panorama of the Battle of Borodino.

Since this museum is dedicated to tragic events, it also contains many epic compositions symbolizing suffering under the yoke of the occupiers...

Jilin

There is one reason to come to Jilin - here you can observe one unusual natural phenomenon.

The Songhua River does not freeze in winter. On a cold winter morning, steam rises from it, covering the trees on the shore with frost.

The Chinese consider this place one of the four most beautiful natural attractions in China, along with Stone Forest of Kunming, the Three Gorges of the Yangtze and the mountains in Yangshuo .

Either I arrived too late (although it seems that despite the beginning of March it was very cold, around -20), or it wasn’t humid enough, but it didn’t look nearly as cool as in the photographs.

Shenyang

Dandong

The city of Dandong, small by Chinese standards, is notable primarily for the fact that it is located on the border with one of the most incomprehensible countries in the world - North Korea. China and the DPRK are connected by a railway-road bridge and complex relations. On the one hand, North Korea still exists mainly thanks to China, who saved the North Koreans during the 1953-1956 war, helped them avoid starvation in the 1990s, and generally largely saves the DPRK from complete collapse, all for the sake of geopolitics. On the other hand, crazy neighbors who spend all the country’s resources on creating nuclear bombs are not very pleasant for the Chinese either, and the North Koreans do not consider China, which has slipped into capitalism, as friends in official propaganda.

There is also an old bridge next to the existing bridge. Half of it was destroyed by bombing during the Korean War, and it was left as a monument.

Border terminal building. Tourists not from Russia who travel to North Korea travel in transit through China, because it is much more convenient than through Vladivostok. You can fly from Beijing to Pyongyang or take a train via Dandong.

You cannot leave North Korea freely, but it seems the system now is roughly the same as it was in the USSR, that is, if you are close to the party and Kim Jong-un, then you can. There are a lot of Korean signs and restaurants in Dandong, and I doubt that defectors hang out there, nostalgic for their homeland. The homeland is clearly visible from here. In summer, there are even pleasure boats for tourists, from which you can take a closer look at that shore.

Besides the border with North Korea, there is plenty to do in Dandong. For example, here you can visit the Great Wall of China, its most eastern section.

Do you see the tourists in the photo? Neither do I. There are no people here at all.

Once upon a time, I read in children's books that the Great Wall was built by order of the first emperor Qin Shihuang (the same one who made the Terracotta Army). In reality, what was built then can only be seen in excavations. The current wall, where it survives, mainly dates from the Ming Dynasty (14th to 17th centuries). And those areas where tourists go (mainly near Beijing) were generally rebuilt in the 20th century. The site in Dandong is no better or worse than the ones in Beijing in this regard.

Dalian

I really wanted to escape the winter frosts, and finally, the temperature in Dalian became positive! The city immediately began to sparkle with new colors.

Finding a hotel took a very long time. There were problems in Dandong too, but I attributed them to the proximity of the border. However, in Dalian I was not accommodated in any cheap hotel near the station, which is actually par for the course for big cities in China. However, on the other side of the station there was a very pretentious-looking hotel, where a room cost only 78 yuan ($12). In terms of price-quality ratio, this is one of the best results in China, and simply outstanding for such a metropolis (usually the best hotels at low prices are in small towns near national parks).

All Chinese hotels have rubber slippers, which are very convenient. In Russia and Europe I always carried my own slippers, but in China I never needed them. Almost every room has a kettle, and often a desktop computer too.

Another undoubted advantage of this hotel is the enchanting view from the window.

In most Chinese megacities, the development is quite chaotic, there is no such thing as a high-rise block proudly rising somewhere in the middle. What is called a skyline in America exists in every big city. There is one in Dalian too. Another exception is Chongqing and Shanghai.

Dalian (Dalny) was founded in 1898 by the Russians, who leased the surrounding area for a naval base known as Port Arthur. After the Russo-Japanese War, it passed to the Japanese, who, however, continued to build it according to the Russian plan. Some buildings from those times have been preserved in Dalian.

They are mainly concentrated in the Russian quarter north of the station, while the modern center is to the south.
