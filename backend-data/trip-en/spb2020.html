27 July - 1 August 2020

Saint Petersburg

I haven’t been to St. Petersburg for more than two years, since transit in February 2018. It was simply necessary to visit good old St. Petersburg.

Excluding the distant suburbs, in the main part of St. Petersburg it is no longer easy to find places where I have not been, but one important blank spot still remained - Lakhta. The very place where the Olgino-Lakhta trolls are based, as well as the infamous Zenit Gazprom Arena stadium, which was destroyed by cormorants. For the 2018 FIFA World Cup, two new metro stations were opened on the green line: Novokrestovskaya and Begovaya. The first one is right next to the stadium. Apparently, they were in such a hurry that they did it poorly, and already in 2020 Novokrestovskaya was closed for repairs, so the trains didn’t even stop there. It’s good that you can go to Begovaya.

The station, the namesake of the Moscow metro station, is located next to the park of the 300th anniversary of St. Petersburg. It has also become more convenient from here to travel west to Kronstadt and Sestroretsk, so as not to be stuck in a minibus to the Chernaya Rechka.

I lived on Vasilievsky Island, the advantage of this location is the opportunity to look at the bridges from the less popular northern side (and not remain cut off from the house).

This particular bridge is usually printed on all postcards - the Palace Bridge.

It is opened first at 1:10, and both neighboring bridges are opened 10-15 minutes later, so ships would not be able to pass under it anyway. I think this was done so that tourist ships would see the opening of the Palace Bridge at 1:10, and in 20 minutes the path would be cleared for large ships. Zelenogorsk

St. Petersburg is divided into 18 districts (rayon in Russian). Central areas with dense buildings are divided into sub-districts (okrug in Russian). (Interestingly, in Moscow it’s the other way around - okrugs are divided into districts). But remote areas like Kurortny are divided into cities and towns. St. Petersburg thus includes 9 cities: Lomonosov, Peterhof, Krasnoye Selo, Pushkin, Pavlovsk, Kolpino, Kronstadt, Sestroretsk and Zelenogorsk. I was in Lomonosov (aka Oranienbaum) and Pavlovsk, but a long time ago (2001-2003), so there were two left: Zelenogorsk and Krasnoye Selo. Zelenogorsk is the furthest city from the center, and at the same time the fastest in terms of transport, since it only takes half an hour to get there by Lastochka train St Petersburg-Vyborg.

Krasnoye Selo

The last city within the boundaries of St. Petersburg where I hadn't been was Krasnoye Selo. I mistakenly believed that I visited the Krasnoselsky district back in 2013 via of Pulkovo airport, but the airport is in the Moskovsky district. I first came to that district only in 2017 - at the "Yunona" market. In reality, the area is such that you can’t just get there by accident. Along the highways there are all giant shopping centers, several new neighborhoods with questionable transport accessibility, and the notorious Kadyrov Bridge. And in the farthest corner, on a ledge surrounded by the Leningrad region, stands the city of Krasnoye Selo.

Station ceiling.

From the station you need to walk about a kilometer and a half through a forest park to the central square.

Krasnoye Selo is the most uninteresting of the nine cities within St. Petersburg; it can even be called a city with a stretch - rather an urban-type settlement in scale. Almost all the houses are late Soviet.

The only attraction is the 18th century church, standing proudly in the central square.

Gatchina

Gatchina is the only major imperial residence that is located on the territory of the Leningrad region, and not St. Petersburg. It takes no more than an hour to get to Gatchina from Moskovskaya Square or the Baltic Station. Gatchina is the largest city in the Leningrad region (but still less than 100k population).
