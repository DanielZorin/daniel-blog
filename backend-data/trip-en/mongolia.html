Mongolia: Ulaanbaatar, Darkhan, Sukhbaatar

8-12 January 2015

Ulaanbaatar

Let's start our Mongolian lesson. Saturday = byamba. Sunday = nyam.

Cold beer = huiten pivo (in Russian it sounds obscene).

Xerox = canon. Canon appeared here earlier.

Bra = levchik. Briefs = trusik.

Pies = piroshki.

Oligarch = oligarch.

Computer pawnshop = computer lombard (from Russian).

Russia = ohuls (attention to the leftmost portrait).

In the evening we went to the cinema to see the third part of The Hobbit. As is usually the case in countries with small populations, the film was shown without dubbing with Mongolian subtitles. So my vocabulary was replenished with the word “armor” = “huyag” (sounds similar to "penis" in Russian). Opposite the cinema is an abandoned building with Lenin.

The hotel has a Chinese toilet brand Huida.

Money - tugriks (more precisely, tögrögs). Becoming a millionaire is relatively easy - you only need to exchange a little more than $500.

Zorig is the Mongolian Yeltsin. He was the leader of the revolution at 28 years old, what have you achieved at that age?

Khuitsaa is a national Mongolian dish (the name also sounds obscene in Russian).

I couldn't help but try. It looks like this. Very tasty and filling. Costs three dollars.

In general, Mongolian cuisine is not for everyone, just like Chechen. All dishes here contain a lot of fatty lamb, so vegetarians and lovers of light snacks will have a hard time here. On the left in the photo is the most popular dish, called tsuiwan. In the background on the left is Mongolian tea. It tastes even worse than Kyrgyz jamat, that is, it is absolutely impossible to drink. It is better to take tea or coffee in bags.

And these are buuz (dumplings), about the same as in neighboring Buryatia.

In no other city in the world have I seen so many hotels as in Ulaanbaatar. Near the station there is a hotel ("buudal") in every house. Mostly the rooms there are rented out by the hour, and the condition is appropriate.

On the first day, at six in the morning, we didn’t have the strength to choose for a long time; some of the hotels were completely booked, so we stopped in a very wretched establishment with a creaky bed and armchairs from a century ago for 35,000 tugriks (less than $20). The next day, moving a little further from the station, for exactly the same amount we found a decent place with fresh renovations and Internet access.

As the classics would say, in the county town of U.B. there were so many hotels for an hour and pawn shops that it seemed as if all its inhabitants were born only to immediately pawn their belongings and finally hire a prostitute.

Darkhan

Mongolia boasts the world's lowest population density. Half the country lives in the capital. In vast territories, only occasionally there are towns with a population of a couple of tens of thousands, the rest is steppe with rare nomads. Instead of roads, there are directions everywhere, except for the highway from the Russian border to the Chinese border. Naturally, it is impossible to see places very remote from civilization, such as Lake Uvs-Nuur, the Mongolian Altai or the Gobi Desert, without a well-equipped jeep. I had to limit myself to getting to know the cities.

Mongolia has one railway line running from Russia to China, with small branches. There is also a second dead-end line from Russia in the east, but there is no passenger service there. Trains in Mongolia are very old and slow; in Russia such trains have almost been phased out. But tickets are cheap. A night in a sleeper from the border to Ulaanbaatar (9 hours and 300 km) costs only $9. Linen and tea are included in the ticket price. In Mongolia, all trains have "general" carriages (with unreserved seats), which are almost never seen in Russia anymore. They are very popular, since trains are still more convenient than the rusty minibuses.

Recently, the ticketing system has been computerized, so you can take a ticket from an intermediate station at a train station in any city. The first fast train has also appeared, which so far only runs on the route Ulaanbaatar - Darkhan with a stop in Zuunkharaa. They promise to soon launch the same trains in a southern direction to the city of Sainshand.

Inside it's like Europe. A reserved seat is much more expensive - $11.

Sukhbaatar

Sukhbaatar is the largest city near the Russian border. If you travel to Mongolia by train, the border controls are here. The city is named after the founder of communist Mongolia, who some years ago lay in a mausoleum on the main square of Ulaanbaatar.

Now Mongolia has another hero - Genghis Khan. Even the station cafe in Sukhbaatar is named after him.

The rest is modest here.

Photos of shock workers still hang on the honor board.

The station welcomes travelers.

It is easier to cross the border by car 30 kilometers to the east than to spend five hours at border control by train.
