Iran: Tehran, Mashhad, Bandar Abbas, Hormuz, Qeshm

19-22 August 2022

Tehran

The journey through Iran begins from the capital, Tehran. This is a very large city with a population of about 10 million, but visually it is not very different from other Iranian cities, since the historical architecture has been preserved only in isolated cases. Except that Tehran is richer than the province, so there are more restaurants and shopping centers.

Since foreign bank cards are not accepted in Iran, tourists can only use cash. Upon arrival, I exchanged a hundred dollars at the airport; oddly enough, the rate there was better than anywhere else later. I received 28 million and 600 thousand rials, which were given to me mostly in 100 thousand bills (these are two packs tied with bank tape), and the rest in larger 500 thousand bills. Later, they mostly gave me packs of 500k bills, which are not so inconvenient to carry around, since the prices for almost everything are very low.

Tehran has a fairly developed metro, although it goes to the international airport once every hour and a half.

A metro ride cost 25 thousand rials, that is, for 1 dollar you can travel 11 times. Probably the cheapest metro in the world.

Everywhere on the streets hang portraits of “martyrs” - soldiers who died for their homeland.

There are enough martyrs in Iran; half a million died in the Iran-Iraq War (1980-1988), and the Iranian military is still involved in various conflicts in the Middle East (Yemen, Iraq, Lebanon, etc.).

The war against Iraq in Iran is called the "Eight Year Sacred Defense". Although after a couple of years it ceased to be a defense - the Iranians recaptured all the territories occupied at the beginning of the war and invaded Iraq itself, trying to overthrow Saddam Hussein and his pro-Sunni regime.

Iran has had very bad relations with America since the Islamic Revolution of 1979. In the same year a crowd seized the American embassy, ​​and Khomeini approved it, like that’s what they deserve. An attack on an embassy is, by diplomatic standards, a very strong insult (once in the 19th century, the Iranians already had to apologize for the attack on the Russian embassy and give up the largest diamond to the Russia tsar). The Americans, though, also played against the rules - they tried to free the embassy employees captured in prison by force, landing special forces in Iran. The operation ended in failure; in the end, they were released only two years later, but the relationship was completely ruined. Sanctions against Iran have been in effect since 1979, they prohibit American companies from any activity in Iran, I cannot even work remotely from there or read work email (even in Russia this is still not prohibited, except in the territory of Crimea and Donetsk). It’s interesting that EU sanctions were introduced already in the 2000s, when Iran tried to create nuclear weapons, and before that Mastercard operated in Iran (unlike Visa). The former US Embassy has been turned into a museum.

It’s officially called a “den of espionage,” but it’s not particularly clear what’s so espionage about it. The usual telecommunications equipment, as in any embassy of that time.

There is no clearly defined center in Tehran, rather several central districts, like in Tokyo. Probably the area most similar to the “center” is the area around the Golestan Palace and the central market nearby. There was an old city there from the Qajar dynasty (18th-19th century), although the fortress walls have not survived. The Shahs of the Pahlavi dynasty (who ruled in the 20th century) moved to a new palace on the outskirts of the city in the hills, and Golestan was used only for ceremonies.

Golestan Palace is included in the World Heritage List.

Mashhad

Mashhad is located in the northeast near the borders with Turkmenistan and Afghanistan, far from the main routes, so tourists from non-Muslim countries rarely appear there. I decided that the city was important enough to fly there, since there are flights to Mashhad from many Iranian cities.

In mosques you can find stands like these with round stones. The point is that the Prophet himself prayed on bare ground. Therefore, it is also correct to touch the ground when praying, and not a soft carpet. For this purpose, these pebbles exist, made from the soil of holy places (Karbala, Najaf, Mashhad, etc.), and it must be touched with the forehead. This is a Shiite tradition; I have never seen such stones in Sunni mosques.

I go inside the mausoleum. What is the essence of Shiites? After the death of the Prophet Muhammad, the question arose as to who should be his heir. Some (future Shiites) believed that the heir (imam) should be the closest relative (as in royal families), in the case of Muhammad this is Ali - the husband of his only daughter. Others (future Sunnis) believed that it was not so important and supported the influential Umayyad clan from Syria. As a result, first Ali himself was killed, and later, in the battle of the Iraqi city of Karbala, his son Hussein, which became the most tragic day for the Shiites; on the anniversary of the murder of Hussein, the Shiites come to Karbala and flagellate themselves.

In total, the Shiites had twelve imams, starting with Ali. The last of them disappeared from the world in 872, and the Shiites believe that he, as the messiah (Mahdi), will return and do everything justly when Allah wishes. Of the 11 imams, four (2, 4, 5 and 6) are buried in Medina in Saudi Arabia, six are buried in Iraq (Ali in Najaf, Hussein in Karbala, 7 and 9 in Baghdad, 10 and 11 in Samarra), and the eighth Imam Reza - in Mashhad. You can communicate with the twelfth Imam in Qom, the second holy city of Iran, where the mosque has a well for messages.

Bandar Abbas

Bandar Abbas is a large city in the south of Iran, on the shores of the Strait of Hormuz (approximately opposite Dubai). The city is not rich in attractions, but is a good base for a trip to the neighboring islands in the Persian Gulf. The climate here, due to its proximity to the sea, differs from other cities in Iran on the hot side; in August it was a real steam room.

Hormuz

Hormuz is a small island with a rich history, the center of trade in the Middle East with India and China, and a fortress controlling the entrance to the Persian Gulf. Marco Polo and Afanasy Nikitin visited this island. At the beginning of the 16th century, Hormuz was captured by the Portuguese and held until 1622, when the Persians recaptured it, after that they began to develop Bandar Abbas, and the island fell into disrepair. Now Hormuz has only one small town on the northern coast, near the ruins of a Portuguese fortress, but there is a lot of natural beauty around the island.

Qeshm

Qeshm is a large island near Hormuz and Bandar Abbas. This is a free economic zone, and it seems that you can enter here without a visa (if you fly to the local airport). I don’t know how it is controlled that you won’t go to the mainland - when buying a ferry ticket, they didn’t look at my passport carefully, they just wrote down my name. The nature and architecture of Qeshm are in many ways similar to neighboring Hormuz - if you look at the program of a standard excursion, there will be salt caves, mangrove forests, and a Portuguese fortress.

The city of Qeshm is quite large and there are high-rise hotels there.

The streets were very empty, my driver said that everyone comes out in the evening and it is too hot during the day.
