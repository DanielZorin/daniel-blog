Iceland

18-20 May 2018

Reykjavik

After the trip to the Balkans in the distant summer of 2014, I had only two unvisited countries in Europe. Getting to Ireland was difficult because of visa, but in Iceland there was no such difficulty - it’s in the Schengen zone. It’s quite possible to fly there inexpensively, but I put off the trip because I realized that it would be a shame to purposefully go there without a car. And finally, in 2018, I visited Iceland while passing through, because it was just halfway between Europe and Canada, and the now deceased airline WOW Air sold cheap tickets.

Two thirds of the 300 thousand Icelanders live in Reykjavik.

There is not much unusual architecture here, I would say that it looks like a province in Finland.

Cold winds often blow here, and it seems that this ribbed surface is better resistant to it.

But there are also many smooth facades.

There is a cheaper "Bonus" store, which I have not been to. Prices are indicated in Icelandic krona. At that time, the rate was at its peak - 100 crowns per 1 US dollar, but now the rate has dropped to 120-125, that is, Iceland has fallen in price by 20%. Golden Ring

I devoted my second day in Iceland to a trip around the “Golden Circle” - the famous sights of Iceland. The full day excursion cost less than 50 euros - surprisingly cheap, less than the trip from the airport to Reykjavik and back.

Immediately after leaving Reykjavik, the landscape begins to look something like this.

Interestingly, 1000 years ago there were many forests in Iceland, as the climate was warmer. Cold weather and deforestation led to the destruction of almost all forests by the first Viking colonists.

The first destination is Thingvellir National Park.

The European and North American lithospheric plates meet here.

Below is a valley with lakes.

Iceland's first parliament historically met here.

Small waterfall.

We drive further, the landscape is about the same.

From time to time there are areas where trees have been planted; they look like islands of forest.

Next stop is Gullfoss waterfall.

The weather in Iceland is very harsh, especially in the interior of the island. There was a terrible wind blowing, so strong that it was physically difficult to walk against it, and I took off my glasses because I was afraid that they would blow off. At the same time, it began to rain periodically, which felt like a lot of mosquito bites from all sides. An umbrella in Iceland is completely useless because the wind will blow it apart 5 seconds after you open it.

And this is at the end of May, almost the beginning of tourist season. It seems like the weather in Iceland is always like this, including in summer, but in winter it’s only much colder.

Still, I managed to get to both observation platforms, below and above, despite the wind.

View from above. By this time the rain had stopped and the weather looked generally fine, but the wind had not disappeared anywhere.

And the third point is the Valley of Geysers.

Here is the Geyser geyser, which gave its name to the rest of the geysers.

The geyser erupts once every 20 years, but nearby the Strokkur geyser shoots every 10 minutes.

There are also smaller hot springs around.
