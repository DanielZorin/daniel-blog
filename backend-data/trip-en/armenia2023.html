Armenia: Yerevan, Tatev, Garni, Spitak, Alaverdi

14-20 June 2023

Yerevan

I went to Armenia for a few days on business. I haven’t been to Armenia since my first trip back in 2014. Recently, this country has changed a lot: in 2018 there was a revolution, in 2020 there was a war with Azerbaijan, which led to the de facto loss of most of Nagorno-Karabakh, and in 2022 there was an influx of migrants from Russia, so now Armenia is not at all that cheap and carefree country as I saw it almost 10 years ago. In Yerevan it is much more expensive than in Turkey and Georgia, I had difficulty finding an apartment for $40 not in the center, while in Tbilisi for $20-25 there are many options.

In Yerevan, in residential areas, houses made of pink tuff are well recognizable.

I live north of the center - less than 2 kilometers in a straight line, but this area is on a plateau that is much higher than the center, like El Alto and La Paz

There are many ordinary small streets here.

But there are also quite luxurious mansions; apparently some of Yerevan’s rich people live here.

There is also Victory Park and a statue of the Motherland with a sword, like in Tbilisi.

Tours in Armenia. The maps are old, including all of Karabakh (although Armenia never officially considered it part of itself). De facto, it is now almost impossible to get to Karabakh; the Azerbaijanis are blocking the road, which is why there are big problems with supplies there. Russian peacekeepers essentially cannot do anything; at the latest they will be expelled from there in 2025, when the mandate of the peacekeeping mission ends, but most likely some kind of agreement between Armenia and Azerbaijan will be concluded earlier, negotiations are currently underway.

Yerevan has cheap public transport - a bus ride costs only 100 drams ($0.25), for comparison in Tbilisi 1 lari ($0.40), and on old trolleybuses generally 50 drams.

But food in Yerevan is not particularly cheap. In the Karas chain, which seemed super cheap to me in 2014 (compared to Russia, which was then an expensive country), a kebab/shashlyk costs about 3,500 drams - $9. In Georgia, kebab costs 18 lari - $7.

Khashlama costs about the same.

But tabbouleh salad costs a couple of dollars, although the taste here is somewhat different than in Arab countries.

In stores, almost all goods are from Russia with labels in Russian, as if you were in Pyaterochka. The prices seemed high to me, although I apparently compared this with what it was a year and a half ago, and now the difference is not so big.

Yes, we are cringe, but we are free.

Khor Virap - Noravank

Last time I explored Armenia by hitchhiking. Now I’m older and have traveled along a similar route on a tour bus. Last time I hitchhiked along the same (only) road to Karabakh, but did not visit some famous monasteries and cities along the way. The first region immediately south of Yerevan is the Ararat region, from where Mount Ararat is best visible (better than from Turkey and Iran) .

This area is quite flat, with many fields and farms.

The capital of the region is the city of Artashat, one of the ancient capitals of Armenia. However, the modern city was built in the 20th century in a new location. Near Artashat there is one of the most famous monasteries in Armenia - Khor Virap.

Khor Virap means "deep dungeon". It is believed that at the beginning of the 4th century, the Armenian king imprisoned Saint Gregory here, but later, when Gregory healed him, he converted to Christianity himself and adopted it as a religion for all of Armenia. The oldest church is this one, without a dome.

That same dungeon, there are many who want to go down.

Garni

One day I went to the town of Garni near Yerevan - the only place (besides Yerevan itself) from my last trip that I visited again.

In Garni there is a temple listed as a World Heritage Site. Somehow at that time I did not fully realize that it is extremely rare to see a temple from the times of the Roman Empire that is not in a state of “a foundation and a couple of columns.” There are at most 10 such temples in the world, the largest are the Pantheon in Rome and the temple in Baalbek in Lebanon.

Like Baalbek, here you can see patterns on the roof.

But there is almost nothing inside.

Nearby is the foundation of the palace.

There are quite a lot of visitors.

Views of the canyon.

If you go down, you can look up close at the basalt columns (like Giants' Causeway), but I couldn't because of bad weather.

There are a lot of cherry trees growing near the temple.

Descent into the canyon.

The town of Garni.

These big round pies with plums, nuts and honey are sold everywhere here.

It's called "Gata".

Spitak

The only region of Armenia that I didn't visit on my first trip, not even just passing through, was the Lori region, through which the main road from Yerevan to Tbilisi goes (back then I went along alternative roads through Lake Sevan and through Gyumri). First you need to drive through the Aragatsotn region (north of Yerevan).

The first more or less large city on the way to Tbilisi is called Aparan. When I was traveling from Tbilisi to Yerevan, there wasn't enough space in the cabin, so I sat in the luggage compartment (just like in Indonesia), and got off at Aparan to change to a more convenient local minibus for the last leg of the journey.

Later it turned out that in Aparan excursion buses stop near the food court in the center.

The food court is good and inexpensive.
