Munich

Munich played a huge role in the history of the Third Reich. Adolf Hitler moved here in 1913, dodging conscription into the Austrian army, which he considered unworthy of his presence. After World War I, Hitler again settled in Munich and began his political career here. During Nazi rule, commemorative gatherings took place in the city, although the most important parade, shown in the film Triumph of the Will, took place in Nuremberg. The most important event that happened in Munich at that time was, of course, the Beer Hall Putsch. It began in the Bürgerbräukeller beer hall, which has not survived to this day, but was located somewhere between the current center of contemporary art and the Hilton Hotel.

From here, the stormtroopers, who had drunk too much beer and demanded power, headed to the city center, specifically to the War Ministry. The road went through this bridge...

Dachau

In Russia, despite massive propaganda every year on May 9, in general people talk very little about the Second World War. The TV picture makes it look like some kind of Hollywood superhero movie with a happy ending. At the same time, 99% hardly understand why exactly the Nazis were bad, except that they lost and were then collectively condemned. Words such as “Auschwitz” and “Dachau” are mentioned in passing in the school curriculum, solely as names and nothing more, apparently, so as not to traumatize the child’s psyche. So, let's take a closer look at Dachau.

The Nazis wanted to build an “ideal society” of superhumans with maximum Aryan spirit. Anyone who did not fit into this concept was not entitled to a place in the Reich, so almost immediately after Hitler came to power and the adoption of the law “On Eliminating the Plight of the People and the Reich,” the first concentration camp was opened in Dachau. All those who disagreed with the regime and other alien elements were supposed to be re-educated there through occupational therapy. That is why on the gates of this camp, as well as all subsequent ones, it was written “Work makes you free.”

With the outbreak of the World War, prisoners were especially actively used as free labor. They were fed just enough to keep working. They lived in these barracks.

These barracks are a reconstruction; only ruins remain of the original ones, their places are simply marked by piles of stones.

Over time, more and more people began to be accommodated in double-decker bunks. At the peak of "total war" in 1944, the number of prisoners was in the tens of thousands, in a fairly small area.

The morning formation took place on the central alley; here the prisoners could exchange a few phrases with each other in the interval between hard labor.

Hitler did not like Jews very much; he saw everywhere a “Jewish conspiracy” and “the machinations of global Zionism.” In principle, this is not unique, anti-Semitism has existed in Europe since ancient times, and even now in our God-saved fatherland, every second person blames the “Jewish-Masonic conspiracy” for all their troubles. But even having seized power, the crazy corporal could not give vent to his thirst for revenge without the passive support of the majority. The majority did not care about the Jews, the minority wanted to curry favor, this is how the “final solution to the Jewish question” began, and this is the most important lesson to be learned from history and not to repeat such mistakes. Initially, the Nazis stripped Jews of their civil rights and essentially turned them into free slave labor. Later, around 1942, for some reasons that are still debated, it was decided to kill them. Practice has shown that mass executions are not the most effective way when you need to kill several million people, since the executioners quickly lose their nerve. This is how the gas chamber concept came into use. There was also one gas chamber in Dachau, although it was never used. It is necessary to separate concentration camps, where prisoners of war and dissenters were kept and forced to work, and death camps, built for the purposeful extermination of people. The death camps (Auschwitz, Majdanek, Treblinka, Sobibor, Chelmno) were located mainly in the occupied territories of Poland, in order to avoid, if possible, publicity of what was happening there among the civilian population of Germany. For some reason, there is often a misconception that the Nazis burned people alive en masse. This is not true; cremation ovens were used to burn bodies.

This is what the entrance to the gas chamber looks like. Since there were hundreds of prisoners, and only a few guards, to avoid a riot, the guards said that everyone had to work, and first they needed to take a shower. That's why it says "shower room" above the entrance. An additional benefit for the executioners is that there is no need to remove clothes from the bodies, they are already collected in one place where they can search them and take anything valuable.

Gas chamber from the inside. Sprinklers are attached to the top to make it seem like this is a shower room.

It's all about this window with bars. Behind it is a container that slides out and in from the outside.

Outside view. Capsules with poisonous gas Zyklon-B were placed in the container, which killed everyone in the room within a few minutes. The cell was cleared of bodies and disinfected by other prisoners, the Sonderkommando, who sooner or later were also sent to the cells.
