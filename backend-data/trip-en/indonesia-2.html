Indonesia: Yogyakarta, Surakarta, Bandung

26 November - 3 December 2011

Yogyakarta

Yogyakarta is the most touristic city on Java. Here in the city center you can sometimes meet Europeans, and the population knows the standard scams involving luring them into various stores. In addition, this is a city with an ancient history, which for a long time was the capital of an independent state, and is now the center of the autonomous region of the same name within Indonesia. Unlike Jakarta, here you can walk along the streets, which, in fact, will be discussed further.

Yogyakarta is much neater than Jakarta. The sidewalks are paved with tiles that do not fall apart before your eyes.

In some places there are even traffic lights that count down the time, and they are not even ignored.

There is a palace in the city where the Sultan still lives, even if he has no real power. Around the palace there is a vast area with beautiful houses, surrounded by white stone walls.

Historically, close associates of the Sultan used to live there, but now it's just wealthy people. There is no crazy traffic flow here and there is real silence.

Typical fence.

An atypical fence and a sudden statue.

The lanes, as in Jakarta, are narrow, but do not sparkle with desperate poverty; on the contrary, everything is very civilized.

A bit reminiscent of courtyards in southern Europe.

The most magnificent building in the city is the Bank of Indonesia. There is something like this in Jakarta too, but I only saw it from the bus window.

On the main street there is no crowding between the rows of merchants and passers-by.

At the entrance there is a breathtaking sculpture.

Even the channels are not very dirty.

As for public transport, everything here is also very diverse. There are no bajajs, but since the traffic is not so busy, pedicabs are used. There are also buses on dedicated lanes, horse-drawn transport for tourists and taxis. A taxi is the most convenient, but you can’t always catch one. You have to bargain desperately with a rickshaw driver, and they only agree to prices comparable to the price of a taxi ride.

Local aristocrats do not disdain bicycles with three working and one passenger seats.

The graffiti culture has also reached Yogyakarta.

In Jakarta, most people's native language is Indonesian, a very simple language - the lingua franca of Indonesia. The provinces have their own languages, in particular Javanese. Javanese has its own script, which is used to duplicate many street signs.

The easiest way to guess about the city's great imperial past is by looking at the majestic lanterns.

And at the most beautiful pillar pedestals in Indonesia.

Fabulous.

Before the trip, I imagined what fantastic nature and enchantingly beautiful landscapes would be in Indonesia: palm groves, mountains, volcanoes - everything that lovers of nature hikes value so much. My disappointment knew no bounds. I didn't see anything supernatural. Well, fields, well, mountains, well, forests, they all look the same. There are these in Russia too, I’ve seen them many times from the train window. Interest in natural attractions seems to have been lost.

Except that there are palm trees instead of birches, and strange mountains of stones.

The Indonesian village lives poorly, mainly on subsistence farming. People are gradually moving to cities, although the rural population still makes up a significant percentage compared to more developed countries.

A characteristic feature of the village landscape are huge rice fields flooded with water.

Compared to their size, it is striking how few people work on them, and how much work everyone has to do.

Parangtritis

While in Yogyakarta, I couldn’t miss the opportunity to get my feet wet in the Indian Ocean, because Jakarta had the Java Sea, which is not quite the same. To get to the ocean, you need to get to the final bus stop and change to a minibus. At first it seemed that there would be no one except us, but it turned out that local residents use the minibus to travel between villages, and not to go from the city to the beach. After some time, all the seats were taken by schoolgirls on their way home from school. And then suddenly the minibus’s wheel broke. At first the driver worked magic on it for a long time, but then he finally took up the jack.

Passengers watch the process.

It was in some rather rich village. Maybe this is not a village, but a holiday village.

And here we are, finally, in Parangtritis. Another tropical downpour has just passed through.

The long-awaited Indian Ocean.

After the rain there were no people on the beach. Swimming, however, is not recommended in any weather, as a very strong current sucks you into the depths.

Exposure to the Indian Ocean had a detrimental effect on the condition of my boots.

In Parangtritis, at every step, in almost every house, there are public toilets called mandi. In general, the word mandi means “to wash,” but don’t let this mislead you: kamar mandi does not mean a bath or a shower. This is a floor-level sink with a faucet above it. It is assumed that with the help of this device you can somehow wash yourself.

Borobudur

Borobudur is a gigantic Buddhist temple that is actually larger than it appears in pictures. It was once lost in the jungle and filled with volcanic ash, but was found and gradually restored in the 19th century.

Now Borobudur is cared for with thin brushes.

Upon entry, everyone is given a sarong to wear. For an attraction of this scale, there are very few visitors; you can walk around absolutely freely.

The essence of Borobudur is that at each level the walls are decorated with bas-reliefs, and if you go in the right direction, they form the entire story of the life of Buddha.

Buddha statues are placed in the corners.

On the upper level there are stupas. Each stupa has the same Buddha statue as in the previous photo. For some reason, all the postcards print a photograph of the only stupa that has no roof and the Buddha statue is visible.

And around the temple there are endless fields where poor locals grow rice.

Surakarta

Surakarta (abbreviated Solo) is another imperial capital. On the one hand, Solo was not destroyed several times due to volcanic eruptions, on the other hand, Borobudur and Prambanan are closer to Yogya, so the city is not spoiled by the attention of tourists. In addition, the Solo Sultanate has not retained autonomy to this day, and Solo is not even a provincial capital (the capital of Central Java is Semarang).

As a result, you can sometimes find mountains of garbage on the streets here.

Garbage is removed on an industrial scale.

Clothes are dried directly on the street.

Foreigners become superstars, so every schoolgirl wants to take a photo with you.

There are two Sultan's palaces in Surakarta. The fact is that once (in the mid-18th century) the sultan and the prince did not divide their spheres of influence, as a result, one half of the city remained with the prince, and the other with the sultan. It’s interesting that two hours before me, Russians were in the prince’s palace and left an entry in the guest book. To people who have been to Peterhof, this palace will not seem pompous.

Although independence is long gone, the Surakarta dynasty still exists. Their portraits are hung throughout the rooms of the palace.

Usually the prince lives in Jakarta, but from time to time he visits his historical homeland. Part of the palace is reserved for such occasions.

The Sultan's palace is behind several rows of blank white walls. In some places the wall goes on both sides of the street, there are no houses.

This palace is also distinguished by its area, not its height.

Without any jokes or sarcasm, the city is quite beautiful, there are no slums in the center and many majestic buildings.

Everything was done with love, from the fence to the handwritten sign.

More beauty. Particular attention should be paid to the lantern in front of this house.

The lanterns are simply incredible in their grace and grandeur. These are the kind of lanterns I would expect to see in Paris, not in a provincial Indonesian city.

Surakarta is the only city in Indonesia where trees grow around the canals, and in the water there is not garbage, but grass and stones.

Another notable feature of Solo is the love for beautiful arcs.

There are arches near palaces...

... and above the entrances to the gateways.

An old railway runs right through the city center. It seems that steam locomotives even run on it sometimes.

In terms of convenience, regular trains in Indonesia are approximately on the same level as Russian Railways electric trains, with the only difference being that we have fewer people. During peak hours in Jakarta, you can see crowds of people on the roof. Local trains are sometimes painted pink.

Intercity trains do not have sleeper cars. The longest route Jakarta-Surabaya takes about 13 hours. There are also night trains, but they still have only seats. There are three classes of carriages. First class has air conditioning and in the third class you have a chance to sit on the floor. A first class ticket from Jakarta to Yogyakarta (8 hours) costs 700 rubles ($20). In third class it is 10 times less.

Bandung

Welcome to Bandung, white man!

Welcome to Bandung, the city of good traffic! This one intersection has more traffic lights than the whole of Jakarta.

Joke. Here, too, people mostly drive as they please, with the exception of central streets with traffic lights. You can cross the road only by maneuvering in the traffic. After a while, a slight feeling of confidence in one’s own invulnerability arises.

Bandung is called the Paris of Java. There were no similarities, except for a couple of signs.

And a model of the Eiffel Tower.

It also doesn’t look like Holland at all, except for the name.

Not surprisingly, it doesn't look like Paris or Amsterdam. This is not an imperial capital, but a center of serious business. Everything is decorated in a harsh modern style, even the street lamps.

Even in the slums the houses have harsh concrete walls.

The main feature of the city is that the shops are grouped along the streets. There is a street where they sell auto parts, a street with furniture stores, and here is a street where they sell license plates.

It smoothly flows into the street, where they sell rarities like typewriters.

And here is a street with furniture shops.

The water in the canals is not stagnant, but rather turbulent. It didn't make it look any cleaner.

The main type of public transport is minibuses (Angkot). The streets are too narrow for buses and too busy for pedicabs (although there are some, albeit in smaller numbers). When minibuses get stuck in traffic, people with guitars get on them and start singing songs.

As elsewhere, the Bank of Indonesia building is striking in its luxury.

In Gedung Sate (this is one of the main attractions, a building in Art Nouveau style), in one of the wings in the basement there was a museum of the Indonesian post. Inside you can take photos with local schoolchildren and look at stamps and mailboxes.

And here is the Gedung Sate itself.

There are a lot of interesting things on the streets. After two weeks in Indonesia, the eye became accustomed to typicalities and began to notice funny little things. For example, there is a fantastic lump of barbed wire on a pole. Apparently, there are countless treasures hidden at the top of the pillar.

To protect spotlights from theft, barbed wire is not enough; you need to put the spotlight in a cage.

The street names are better than in Jakarta, but it’s impossible to navigate a little further from the highways. For example, house 44 fraction 11 point B on an unknown street.

By the way, the writing in the Sundanese language does not look like Javanese.

Restaurant. Here you can have a hearty lunch for about $2. For two.

If you don’t have enough money for a restaurant, you can use the services of a noodle brewer.

The city has a lot of bright, daring, high-quality graffiti. One wall near the train station is worth something.

This is what I especially liked.

Near the Technological University. Apparently, elections of local authorities are taking place there, much more democratic than ours.

The apotheosis of a broken sidewalk: a collapsing staircase.

An aerial view of a part of the city that has not been explored.

Eating durian turned out to be a tricky business. The first durian, apparently, was unripe, and there was almost no pulp in it.

The second time I was successful and it was tasty. There was no smell at all. Perhaps there is a different variety of durian here than in Thailand.

Farewell, hospitable Bandung! From here I had a 3-hour train journey to Jakarta, an hour by car to the airport, 8 hours by plane to Doha, 6 hours by plane to Moscow and another two hours from the airport home.
