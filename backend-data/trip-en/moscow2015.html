Russia: Moscow

8-23 September 2015

Moscow

Many times I thought about writing about Moscow in the same style as about all other cities, but I didn’t know how to approach this, where to start. I saw too much of everything (although I photographed much less). But in September 2015, I had the opportunity to be a tourist in Moscow and record what I saw in a short period of time.

The most important discovery was this: if you are a tourist, then it is quite possible to live in Russia. If you are not pressed around the clock by Crimea, and Kiselev’s propaganda, you do not communicate with government institutions and officials, if you don’t care how deep the country will slide in a year or five years, you feel very cheerful. A simple comparison: many people really like to live in Thailand on the shores of the warm sea and eat cheap fruits, but for the locals everything is not so rosy, since revolutions occur there with the same frequency as in Ukraine, and in the very south several times a week something explodes. After the fall of the ruble, Russia became almost cheaper than Thailand in many respects. There are, of course, large numbers of Putin’s fans, but it’s not written on anyone’s forehead, and in real life (not on the Internet) Moscow residents are polite and friendly, although residents of other cities like to claim the opposite.

It’s best, of course, not to live with a Russian passport, so that the KGB doesn’t bother you at all. By the way, did you know that holders of blue passports of the United States of America are in a privileged position in Russia: they can stay on a tourist visa for 180 days, and not 30 or 90, like everyone else, and do not have a stay limit of 90 days every six months? If I were an American, I would go to Russia for a few months one summer. The only thing that is still not cheap in Moscow is restaurants. Previously, a dish in an average restaurant cost 10-20 dollars, now it’s 5-10. I went to a Chinese restaurant to compare. Tasty, but not at all like what they cook in China. I found the website of another Chinese restaurant, where the menu looked as authentic as possible, but the prices there did not please me at all - three times more expensive than in China.

The caramelized fruit was a success, although the most popular small apples were missing.

In Moscow there is noticeably less advertising and huge signs in the center; the city no longer looks like Seoul or Hong Kong.

Who wants to see how it was before, welcome to the residential areas.

The area of the three railway stations has been polished to a shine.

Behind the Kursky railway station, it turns out, they made a fashionable office area out of former factories.

Many walls where giant advertisements used to hang are now covered with graffiti.

In this nook in Polyanka, where nothing had ever happened, they painted the heroes of Aqua Teen Hunger Force and a burger.

A burger man walked nearby.

There are also a lot of taxis, usually bright yellow. Most have Yandex.taxi or GetTaxi logos. They say that prices have dropped significantly - previously, for less than 500 rubles, a taxi wouldn’t even go to the next door. However, the point of such a large number of taxis in Moscow, where there is an inexpensive metro, is still not very clear.

A round pavilion with QR codes was erected near the Kremlin.

The fountain in GUM was filled with watermelons.

From time to time there are jokes about sanctions.

And about Obama.

I didn’t see Nemtsov Bridge (the folk memorial on the place where Boris Nemtsov was killed) in March; I left just then. But it survived until September.

In some places in the center there were machines that print photos from Instagram.

The Children's World has undergone renovations.

Now you can go out onto the roof - one of the very few observation platforms in the center, and probably the only one publicly accessible and free.

* * *

Some things don't change in Moscow.

I mean the stupid fences that surround Red Square 90% of the time. This fence is completely meaningless, you can go to the other side, but you need to make a detour, otherwise Putin in the Kremlin will be unhappy.

This building opposite the Kremlin has been under construction for 20 years now.

In a Spanish shop they sell jamon, probably now Belarusian.

What definitely hasn’t changed one percent in ten years is Moscow State University.

In 2006, I walked exactly along this corridor. Two floors below, however, were renovated several years ago.

It took 10 years to build this library, which is not actually used.

They still sell "rubber pizza" on the ground floor. When I ate it regularly in 2007-2008, it cost 40-45 rubles. It was a golden time before the inflation spree caused by the 2008 crisis.

Once, in our first year, we were given a cleanup day to clean up leaves, it’s unclear why, because it’s beautiful with leaves.

My workplace is still the same creative chaos.

Only in Auchan, which I remember when it was called Ramstore, the design is constantly changing. This legendary place is the shopping center closest to Moscow State University. The only alternative is the fountains near the circus (Called "Ja", the short of "Jawaharlal Nehru Square"), where it is customary to drink strong drinks.

A visit to Moscow would be incomplete without a walk in the Kitay-gorod metro area - the only place with old narrow streets “like in Europe”.

Zabelina Street has been improved; previously there was a roadway and a parking lot.

The nearby park has been restored and the hedges have become more luxuriant.

I went into the artists’ house, everything was in place.

I wonder if this picture has been here since 2004? I had never been here at that time.

I also visited the house where I lived until 1997. With a carpet on the wall, yeah.

The area around is unrecognizable. Then there was an almost wild yard, where ice slides were flooded in winter. Now it's a sports ground.

School.

On the other side of the house, Moscow just ends.

Mytishchi is already there.

I remember that as a child I walked here to the river through the fields, and now here are Leroy Merlin, Auchan and a bunch of other things.

View down.

The back staircase reminded me of Hong Kong.

All these panel houses do not look very presentable, of course.

Classic yellow-green fences dominate here.

But the area around has been greatly improved.

Just 10 years ago there was nothing here, just forest and paths. And the Yauza River.

And now the embankment looks like this!

Full park.

In general, it has become very nice in Medvedkovo.

I visited a place called Technopolis, which used to be a car factory.

Here is now the office of 3DYA - a company that makes three-dimensional figures from volumetric scans.

Lenin is there too.

Scanner.

The computer-processed scans are printed on a three-dimensional printer.

The territory of the plant is not without its charm.

I was short on time, and I didn’t have time to go anywhere around the country. But I did visit one city outside the Moscow Ring Road - Korolyov, which starts immediately north of Moscow. There is a monument to Lenin in the center.

I was in Korolyov when I was 9 years old, at a chess tournament. There was no Internet then, I didn’t see a map of the Moscow region and didn’t know where I was going, so it seemed to me that this was some distant and unknown place.

The sign “chilled meat” gave a whiff of the Motherland. Next time I should definitely take a trip to Tambov or Saratov.
