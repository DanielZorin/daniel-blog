2-4 February 2024

Lusaka

Zambia is a fairly large country in the interior of the African continent. There were no big wars or economic disasters here, like in Zimbabwe, everything is calm and quiet. The capital of Zambia is called Lusaka, it is a large city with a population of one and a half million. But from the inside it doesn’t feel big: there is only a small commercial and administrative area, and a huge amount of one-story buildings, in general, much like in Harare - I began to notice a trend in African urban planning.

I checked into the hotel called Lusaka backpackers. Costs about $14, inexpensive (although in golden times in Thailand I would consider it expensive).

According to the map, it seems that it is very close to the center, but in fact it is 2 kilometers away. This street is also a dead end, if there were a passage it would be closer.

Livingstone

The city of Livingstone is located near Victoria Falls on the Zambian side. Unlike the small purely tourist town on the Zimbabwean side, Livingstone is not that small a city, with a population of almost 200 thousand. Until 2011 it was the capital of the province, and until 1935 it was the capital of all of colonial Zambia (Northern Rhodesia).

Victoria Falls

Victoria Falls is one of the most famous attractions in all of Africa. It is curious that the three most famous waterfalls are all located on the borders of two countries: Iguazu in Brazil and Argentina, Niagara in USA and Canada, and Victoria on the border of Zambia and Zimbabwe. It seems that the vast majority of tourists in both countries only visit the falls, fortunately there are airports on both sides of the border, and there are quite a few flights there from South Africa. Well, I passed a long road of 1000 kilometers through both capitals to get here.

The first European to visit this waterfall was Livingstone (after whom the city is named). Near the entrance there is a monument to Henry Stanley, who found the missing Livingstone. Though, it was not here, but in Tanzania.

Entry from Zambia costs $20. I arrived at the opening, and at first I explored the waterfall alone; later tourists began to arrive, including those from across the border from Zimbabwe.

This is what the Zambezi River looks like from above. There is a cliff on the left - this is the waterfall.

The river forms a deep gorge, which meanders for a long time after the waterfall. Gradually the waterfall moves further, lengthening the gorge.

Unlike Iguazu, which can be viewed both from above and from below, here all the viewpoints are only from above, approximately at the same level from where the water falls.

That is, you are standing on the other side of the gorge, and the waterfall is right in front of you. It’s not like in Niagara, where you can see the entire waterfall from afar from the other side. In addition, Victoria is much wider than Niagara.

The Zambian lookout is located on a rock, where this bridge leads.

Since the waterfall is very close, a cloud of spray flies from it. I even took a raincoat with me, but overall it wasn’t that bad. As a result, of course, I got soaked wet, but then I quickly dried out in the sun, and it turned out to be quite normal to photograph.
