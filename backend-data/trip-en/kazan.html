Sviyazhsk

Sviyazhsk is a village for 100 people, located on a picturesque island.

There are branded lights here.

Some houses are still old.

Sviyazhsk people.

Near a very ancient wooden church stood a man who had clearly read “12 Chairs” and decided to trade for a view of the malachite puddle In the novel, the protagonist sold tickets to a malachite cave that was free to enter, pretending to be an official ticket seller..

During Soviet times there was a concentration camp with mass executions here.

The guides tell an interesting story that Trotsky, being here in 1918 during the storming of Kazan, erected a monument to Judas Iscariot in Sviyazhsk as the first revolutionary in human history. It was approximately in this square. The story is very cool, although there are considerable doubts about its reliability: the episode is described only in two written sources, one of which no one has seen, and the second is unreliable.

The monument to Judas is almost as cool as the monument to Hitler, which the great friend of the Soviet Union, Doctor of all Sciences, Idi Amin, wanted to erect in the center of Kampala.

Yoshkar-Ola

Yoshkar-Ola is a revolution of colors and a break of all patterns. A city that has no analogues in Europe. Of course, highbrow intellectuals, snorting and raising their noses, will say that this is all a tacky remake that has no artistic value, pandering to the tastes of the masses. But we will answer this in unison - isn't it better for people to be happy that they have the entire city center as an amusement park, and not a bunch of gray commieblocks and rickety shacks. In America, where there are almost no ancient buildings, they are not ashamed to build Disneyland and Las Vegas, but why are we worse?

First things first. At first Yoshkar-Ola was an unremarkable provincial town. There were no special attractions, except for one 18th century church. There were only broken roads.

Yes, extracting money from the Vasyukinites is an almost impossible task. But once a year, during the Benderiad holiday, Kozmodemyansk transforms and the fun begins. Kozmodemyansk was the prototype of the fictional city of Vasyuki from the novel "The 12 chairs". The main character Bender is a genius swindler. In that chapter, he comes to Vasyuki and pretends to be a chess grandmaster, he promises to organize an international tournament in Vasyuki, which will bring an influx of tourists and money to the city, and eventually Vasyuki will be renamed New Moscow and become capital of the world.

There are surprisingly few references to The 12 Chairs in the city. Vasyuki residents are not realizing their tourism potential. Where is the "Four Knights Club", the "Passed Pawn" hotel, the chessboards on the streets and the monument to Bender? There is only a small museum.
