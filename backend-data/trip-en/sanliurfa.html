Diyarbakir

Diyarbakir is the largest city in Turkey with a predominantly Kurdish population. Because of this, it became the center of an interethnic conflict, the last round of which was not so long ago - in 2016. Then there was a real war going on here, the city center behind the ancient fortress walls was under siege for several months and was subjected to artillery shelling, as a result of this a significant part of it was destroyed, and now the city is being rebuilt almost anew. It seems that this is the first time I have found myself in a city where the war was so recent.

Diyarbakir has direct flights from Antalya twice a day, making it a convenient starting point for a trip to the southeast. I arrived in the evening already dark, but walked around the city.

The fortress walls of Diyarbakir, as well as the Hevsel gardens located behind them, are included in the UNESCO list.

These gardens have been here for several thousand years, and they still grow something there.

A river is visible between the gardens - this is the Tigris River (Dicle in Turkish), one of the two rivers of Mesopotamia - the interfluve of the Tigris and Euphrates, where ancient civilizations arose.

Iraq's two main cities, Mosul and Baghdad, are also located on the Tigris River.

It is claimed that the wall around Diyarbakir is the second longest wall in the world, after The Great Wall of China.

Mount Nemrut

Of the most famous places in Turkey, Mount Nemrut is probably the most difficult to get to. It's at least 10 kilometers from a place where minibuses theoretically go once an hour, I am not sure if there is a taxi available there at all. And even to the nearest major city buses from neighboring cities go literally a couple of times a day. I considered it justified to spend money on renting a car, which, thanks to the increased level of my knowledge of the Turkish language, I managed to do in Sanliurfa for 1000 liras (not without a problem - many rental companies do not allow driving to the neighboring region, and not everyone has cars with an automatic transmission).

Harran

Harran is a small town near the border with Syria, famous for its beehive-shaped mud houses.

The structure of the city is unusual - in the middle there is a huge wasteland where there once was an ancient city, to the south of it there is an old district with beehive houses, and to the west there is a modern city. In the south there are still ruins of a castle that were closed to the public.

Gaziantep

Gaziantep is one of the largest cities in Turkey, located in the southeast near Aleppo in Syria. Originally called simply Antep (Aintab), it was renamed "Veteran Antep" after the Turkish War of Independence due to its long resistance to intervention.

In the center of the city there is a castle on a hill.

For some reason, they stopped accepting museum cards, but entry costs only 10 lira.

While you are climbing, there are numerous statues on the sides depicting the heroes of the defense of Antep.

On the right are the fourteen boys killed at the mill.

Inside the castle there is an exhibition with bas-reliefs describing the events of the war. Antep was under siege by French troops for almost a year, from April 1920 to February 1921. It held the enemy forces for a long time, allowing the army to be used on other sectors of the front. In the text comments it was mentioned several times that local Armenians were on the side of the French, but somehow modestly it was not indicated why.
