Russia: Vladivostok, Artyom, Birobidzhan, Khabarovsk

1-6 April 2014

Artyom

From Vladivostok airport you can get to the city by train for 200 rubles or by bus for about 70. Both do not run very often. The trip to the end of the world, which I had wanted to do for so long, started lightly, since my luggage was promised to be delivered only the next day, so I took the bus, which runs every 20 minutes to the center of the city of Artyom, where the airport is located. From there it is not difficult to go to Vladivostok, since all intercity buses in Primorye pass through Artyom. Artyom turned out to be an ordinary Russian provincial town, so I immediately felt the feeling of traveling around the Motherland that had been forgotten for six months.

Apart from the funny name (and it was named after a communist figure who had the pseudonym “Comrade Artyom”), there is nothing special in the city. Monument to Artyom.

Although no, there are two monuments to Lenin here, for such a small city! One is white.

The other is grey. Two cheerful Lenins.

Not far from one of the monuments to Lenin, they tried to offer me a book of Mormons in a purely American accent.

I discovered a couple of new glass buildings in the city.

New positive buses.

There are also guest workers in the Far East. Then, when I flew between Khabarovsk and Sakhalin, I was amazed that half of the passengers there were from Central Asia. In Artem there is friendship of peoples - Russia, Korea, Tatarstan, Tajikistan, Germany and Armenia.

Well, the Caucasus, of course.

Vladivostok

Russian lurkmore claims the following: “The main and practically the only attraction of Vladivostok is Zelenka - the car market on the Green (nee Rotten) corner.” That's where I went. I don’t know enough about cars that I can’t even distinguish companies by their logos (only if the name is signed with words), and I’ve never been to car markets in Moscow.

Green Corner is located on one of the most distant residential outskirts of Vladivostok.

To get there, you need to overcome certain obstacles.

There is a road sign to the market, and a pipe serves as the gate.

Car trading goes something like this: a car is parked, and on the windshield the price, characteristics and telephone number (not always) are written in white putty.

There are two main characteristics of cars: year of manufacture and mileage. Most used cars are from Japan, where cars are written off quickly, after 2-3 years maximum. If it says "without mileage", it means that there is no mileage in Russia.

The vast majority of sellers are not random people, but professional car importers from Japan to Russia who sell many cars at once.

Apparently, the most convenient places on the sites are behind the fences, and those who have one car park it by the side of the road.

Main street of the market.

In addition to cars, here you can buy goods brought from Japan: alcohol, all sorts of Japanese snacks and candies, small household items. They sell from folding trays.

There is a whole department with special equipment where you can buy an asphalt paver or a tow truck.

The market is like a labyrinth; the transitions between its parts can be quite original.

At the very top there was an abandoned toilet.

And a couple of broken cars. And also a pack of dogs that I didn’t dare pass by.

I wonder if I want to buy a car that is parked here in the middle, how will they get it from there? Will they pave the path with the help of tow trucks?

Usually the transitions are wider.

For tourists, Green Corner is valuable primarily for its stunning views of the hills filled with cars.

So this place can rightfully be considered one of the sights of Vladivostok.

* * *

Vladivostok is epic, first of all, because it is more than 9,000 kilometers from Moscow (if you travel by rail) and for most residents of western Russia it symbolizes, together with Kamchatka and Chukotka, the end of the world. People usually don’t go here on tours, so the vast majority of people have never been to these parts and know about them only by hearsay.

The southern edge of Vladivostok, where the land ends and the sea begins, is located on the Egersheld Peninsula, some distance from the historical center. Lenin shows the way there (and further to Japan).

The railway runs parallel to the road for a long time, ending at a freight train station located next to the port and docks.

The area here is not the most prestigious.

On one of the streets there is a destroyed tower.

It burned out from the inside. I won’t show you what’s on the floor because there’s a huge pile of garbage there. Judging by Yandex panoramas, the tower has been standing in this form for several years.

Original installation on the house.

From here you can see all the splendor and poverty of Vladivostok, and Russia as a whole. In the background is a grandiose bridge to Russky Island, in the foreground is some kind of shack.

This is the end of the world.

Around this bend is a lighthouse, which is so small (next to it is a radio tower 10 times higher) that I didn’t even take a picture of it. A street with the wonderful name Lukomorye goes in the other direction, which in fact turned out to be a cottage village behind a barrier.

Lukomorye is a place from fairy tales, so the artists drew the knights that are associated with this tale.

The drawings on the walls in Vladivostok are on point.

Smoking cigarettes means harming yourself.

A wall of stars on which you can immediately identify Lennon, Jagger, Marilyn Monroe, Shevchuk, Kobzon, Tsoi and Dima Bilan.

Sometimes the roofs are also painted.

And the walls of houses.

The second contender for the title of the edge of the world is the notorious Russky Island, where the APEC summit took place. APEC is some kind of leftist organization, the significance of which is very doubtful, but Putin decided to coincide the summit with a general reconstruction of Vladivostok and the creation of bridges. The plans include turning Russky Island into New Vasyuki with skyscrapers, but so far there are only a few villages where in the summer residents of Vladivostok go to lie on the beach on the seashore.

In the spring the forest looks like this, but in the summer, when it is green, the landscape is completely atypical for Russia and is more reminiscent of the tropics.

The only large object on the island at the moment is the building where the summit took place. Now the Far Eastern Federal University has moved there. According to Russian tradition, you can’t get inside without a pass, and you can’t even walk around the territory here, because the guards are stationed at the outer gate.

You can only gaze at the bridge from afar. The names of the local bays are romantic - Ulysses, Patroclus, Diomede, Ajax, Paris.

By the way, not two bridges were built, but three. The third, not as high as the two most famous, is located on the outskirts and shortens the detour of the Amur Bay. You can see it if you arrive to Vladivostok by train.

* * *

Probably the most important stereotype about the Far East is that only Chinese live there, and will soon hold a referendum and separate from Russia. In fact, I saw the Chinese only in the market near the bus station, however, you can also see the Chinese in Moscow markets. This is not the flavor of Vladivostok.

According to statistics, Vladivostok has the largest number of cars per capita in Russia. The easiest way to understand this is to try walking around the city. The traffic lights here are set up roughly like this: pedestrians are on red for two minutes, green for 15 seconds. The city's topography is also not conducive to walking.

The roads are in much better condition than the sidewalks.

And in some places there are no sidewalks at all.

Analogue of Southern Butovo, Rybatsky, Troeshchina and similar areas in Vladivostok is called Cape Churkin. Now this area is practically in the center of the city, since the Golden Bridge was built exactly there.

Previously, you had to go there around the Golden Horn Bay. Or take a ferry.

Vladivostok is indeed somewhat similar to Istanbul, both on the map and on the panorama, so the names “Golden Horn” and "Bosphorus" are here for a reason.

There is even a multi-colored staircase, just like in Istanbul!

Here you understand well what the words “Where the rails came out of the country’s pocket” meant.

The station, by the way, was built similar to the Yaroslavl station, located at the other end of the road.

Public transport in Vladivostok is represented by a tram, which is more dead than alive, buses and a funicular. A characteristic feature of the buses is that they are slightly smaller than all those written off from Korea, and you have to pay at the exit, as in Odessa.

The funicular costs a ridiculous 9 rubles. It’s also interesting that in the 90s, public transport in Vladivostok was completely free.

Old Soviet sign.

Signs in English have been posted in the city center. By the way, there are foreign tourists, even organized groups.

Monument to a hitchhiker.

Monument to sister cities.

Funny pharmacy.

Vladivostok does not have many brands popular in Moscow, such as McDonald's or KFC. Coffee House and Shokoladnitsa are present. But there is cheap Pirate coffee and CFC - Country Fried Chicken, an Australian chain competitor to KFC.

Local flavor also includes everything related to the sea.

The Russian Pacific Fleet is partially parked on Korabelnaya Embankment. You can also rent a yacht there for walks, banquets and fishing.

On the shore there is a submarine turned into a museum.

There is a special fishing university.

The most popular Far Eastern dish is called "pyan-se". This is a big dumpling with spices. Sold hot from mobile carts and costs 50 rubles. Very satisfying.

In general, Vladivostok is a distant city, but ours (as Lenin said).

Birobidzhan

Birobidzhan is the capital of the Jewish Autonomous Region, a godforsaken little region in the Far East where regions tend to be huge. There are no Jews here (1% of the population), since everyone left for Israel a long time ago, there has been no practical sense in “autonomy” for a long time, but the region was not abolished, although it was planned at some point. Probably, we could leave it as a historical curiosity.

Anyone arriving at the station is greeted by an inscription in Yiddish.

There is a menorah near the station.

By the way, it is a cultural heritage site. It’s unexpected that this city thought about tourists and put up signs for them.

For the sake of humor, the Jewish flavor is maintained here.

There are monuments to Jewish musicians.

And Jewish immigrants.

The names of government agencies are duplicated in Yiddish.

Overall, the city makes a pleasant impression, much better than one would expect from a city of this size and distance from civilization. In general, in the Far East, even in small towns, the tension and aggression that is characteristic of the cities of central Russia is not felt. In my opinion, it is not dangerous to walk the streets here.

There are old signs here, clearly preserved from Soviet times.

Khabarovsk

I don’t know about you, but for me Khabarovsk was associated primarily with this view, which is placed on the 5,000 ruble banknote.

To see the Amur Bridge (built before the 1917 revolution, at that time it was an outstanding engineering feat), you need to take a bus ride to the far northern outskirts and walk another 20-30 minutes. For such an occasion, I even grabbed a current banknote.

There is a museum near the bridge that is open one day a week and only in the summer.

Motorcyclists raced wildly on the neighboring hills, so I immediately remembered the legendary game "Elastomania".

But to be honest, Khabarovsk is a fairly clean city, unless it rains.

Khabarovsk is much friendlier to pedestrians than Vladivostok.

There are wide and fairly new sidewalks everywhere.
