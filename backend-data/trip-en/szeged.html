Hungary: Budapest, Siofok, Szeged, Kecskemet

27 November, 1-3 December 2013

Budapest

I was in Budapest a year ago and didn’t think that I would return there soon, but then Wizz Air began flying to Moscow for 20 euro, and Budapest became my main European destination. Budapest greeted me with a fraudulent ATM: when withdrawing, they offer a choice of debiting in forints and dollars; if you choose dollars, then the rate is super unfavorable. At the same time, when you choose forints, he asks again, do you really want to withdraw at our fixed rate? Scam exchangers in Budapest have not gone away either, pay attention to the difference between buying and selling.

Since I spent quite a lot of time in Budapest last time, I decided not to stay in the city for long, but to immediately rush from the airport to the southern station, and from there to Siófok.

In the evening I took a train to Belgrade. European trains have a slightly different system of class gradations than in Russia. There is no analogue to platskart/cheapest class (where the compartments are not separated from the corridor). There is a Sleeping car and a Couchette car, both of which are coupes by our standards. Some have two levels of shelves, others have three. At the same time, there are compartments where there are shelves on both sides, and others where there are only one. In total there are compartments with 2, 3, 4 or 6 seats. When you buy a ticket, you indicate your gender, i.e. apparently men and women are not seated in the same compartment unless they are traveling together. I took the cheapest tickets for a six-seater compartment, but the train was almost empty, I was traveling alone, and there was no one in some compartments at all. An overnight transfer costs about $30, roughly similar to Russian prices.

Siófok

Siófok is one of the main resort towns near Lake Balaton. I expected to see something similar to a typical communist sanatorium resort.

But everything turned out better than I thought. Siófok is most similar to Svetlogorsk or Jurmala, that is, is a modernized and tidied up Soviet resort.

However, the main feature of the Hungarian province, the inscription “for sale” on every house, is preserved here.

On the lake shore, apparently, are old sanatoriums.

The disgusting Soviet tradition of fencing off pieces of the shore for oneself is also present here. It's quite a long walk to get to the shore of the lake.

It was the end of November, so the whole city was immersed in sleep. Even McDonalds was not working, at least I did not notice any cashiers working there.

Lake Balaton itself is a common place for swimming and walking.

The popularity is apparently due to the fact that it is not far from many neighboring countries, and everything is also very cheap.

Szeged

Deja vu. This is the name of the feeling that I experienced when I looked at the map of the city of Szeged hanging at the bus stop and moved towards the hotel. The layout of the city is exactly the same as in Kostroma: rays extend from the center near the river bank in different directions, and the rings have the shape of a polygon (and are not rounded, as, for example, in Moscow). There is a similar layout in Amsterdam, where there are rings of canals instead of roads, but Szeged is more like Kostroma. I think no one would believe that this photograph was not taken in Russia:
