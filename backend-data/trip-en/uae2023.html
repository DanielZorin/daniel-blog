Dubai

I have a tradition - to come to Dubai once every five years: 2013, 2018, 2023. Dubai is interesting because it is constantly growing and changing. Yes, there are many questions regarding the implementation of megaprojects, but in general the city and the country as a whole are moving in an interesting direction - not just selling oil, not just selling real estate, but making a place where you can do business and store money, alternative to the “Western world” (that is, approximately what you-know-who dreamed of achieving by forcing himself on neighboring countries). For me, of course, the minus of the UAE is that this is a country for people with more money than I have - even nomads are given a residence permit with an income of $5,000 or more, which is higher than in Europe. Although if I found a local job, I think I would happily live there, especially since I’m not bothered by areas with Indian food, but quite the opposite.

We were able to see a little bit of Dubai from the plane. This is the palm tree island.

It turned out that there was another larger palm tree even further from the center, and a third one was being built near the old city. But two additional palm trees were not completed, since it turned out that living on a palm tree is not particularly convenient: the water between the “leaves” stagnates, and a traffic jam is expected to form at the exit.

Ras al-Khaimah

I dedicated one day to visiting three emirates that I had never been to before. The first was Ras al-Khaimah (abbreviated RAK) - the northeasternmost emirate. Interestingly, the UAE initially were formed by six emirates, and the ruler of Ras al-Khaimah was unhappy that Abu Dhabi and Dubai had too much power. But immediately after the withdrawal of British colonial troops, Iran occupied two islands in the Persian Gulf that belonged to Ras al-Khaimah, and a couple of months later the sheikh changed his mind and joined the UAE. The islands are still under Iranian control.

Dibba

One day I went on a day trip by boat to Musandam, an exclave of Oman. You can drive there from two sides - along the shore of the Persian Gulf and along the shore of the Gulf of Oman. As I understand it, excursions are mainly from the east (Gulf of Oman), and we were allowed through the border in a simplified manner, without stamps and visas. The city of Dibba is divided by the border, the Omani part is called Dibba al-Baya, and the Emirati also consists of two parts: most of Dibba al-Fujairah belongs to the emirate of Fujairah, and a small piece near the border is called Dibba al-Hisn and is part of the emirate Sharjah.
