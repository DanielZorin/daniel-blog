Russia: Galich, Sudislavl, Kostroma, Ivanovo, Kineshma

11-13 August 2019

Galich

A couple of hours drive from Kostroma is the ancient city of Galich. It is convenient to travel from Moscow due to the fact that some trains to Siberia (along the northern route of the Trans-Siberian Railway) stop here. This is further than Kostroma, and tourists clearly get here infrequently.

Railway station Galich.

It seems that the building was the victim of a ridiculous reconstruction from Russian Railways.

Of all the historical small towns in Russia where I have been, Galich looks like one of the saddest. The roads are almost all broken; these days, contrary to stereotypes about Russia, such a picture is not so easy to see.

Even the churches are almost all dilapidated.

Damaged houses.

The main square has clearly not been touched since the 19th century, except that the asphalt was laid somewhere in the mid-20th.

As in many Russian cities, instead of fortress walls, earthen ramparts along the perimeter have been preserved.

A second fortress was built north of the city center on Balchug Hill in the 14th century, but was soon abandoned after being devastated during the civil war in the 15th century. I first tried to go up the seemingly short path, but the path there went through such tall and wet grass that I turned back.

But I made a great shot at the top.

Sudislavl

On the way to Kostroma, the bus stopped for about 30 minutes in an urban village called Sudislavl. The village turned out to be very picturesque, everything in it seemed to have been preserved untouched since the 19th century. And the most picturesque place is the church on the hill and the road to it.

The pavement also appears to be in excellent condition. It was Sunday that day and many people were walking to or from church, a rare sight.

The church offers slightly panoramic views.

The main gate was overgrown with weeds.

Among the relatively new artifacts, there is a monument to Lenin.

Outside the center, many houses are decaying.

Ivanovo

I’ve been to Ivanovo several times, but each time I was passing through, I never really explored the city. Ivanovo is formally part of the Golden Ring, but usually everyone passes by, because there are no ancient monasteries here - the city is just over 100 years old. But in fact, there are interesting places here even without monasteries.

The last time I walked around the city was in 2011. A lot has changed in 8 years. For example, the river embankment has now been developed.

On the other side is the factory building. In Ivanovo, a lot of industrial architecture from the early 20th century has been preserved in the city center.

Children's playground with a castle.

The Big Ivanovo Manufactory is right on the main street.

The layout of the city is unusual: there is not one main square as such, but rather two separate pieces, between which there is a manufactory and a river and several more blocks. To the north of the river there will be Lenin Square.

To the south is Revolution Square.

There is a monument with very expressive faces. Reminiscent of monuments in East Timor.

The city is very car-heavy; for example, in this square there is an unregulated crossing with 6 lanes! I drove around Ivanovo a little by car, it was quite stressful - everyone was driving fast and honking their horns often.

The oldest building in Ivanovo, preserved from the times when it was a village.

Monument to the "city of brides" in the shape of a shoe.

It's nice to see that some things don't change. This poster hung here on my last visit in 2011 (the right side is dedicated to the 140th anniversary of the city in 2011). At this rate, it can live up to the 150th anniversary in 2021.

The tent that I liked so much “Tobacco products 24 hours cheap” is still standing, although the inscription has almost disappeared.

Right opposite is now the juicy new Pyaterochka.

The new bus station is located on the southern outskirts of the city.

Ivanovo railway station is one of the most unusual train stations that I have seen, not only in Russia, but throughout the world. I don’t know by what miracle this old building was preserved in the form of a huge wooden palace.

Now this is an administrative building, partially rented out for shops, and the passenger station is next door.

The modern train station is on the right.

Incredibly elegant and colorful station.

On the station square there is a monument to the revolutionary Olga Genkina, who was killed by the Black Hundreds in Ivanovo in 1905.

There are generally many extraordinary Soviet monuments in Ivanovo - not just the eternal flame or the monument to Lenin, but for example the monument to the first Soviet of Workers and Peasants in Russia, created here in 1905.

Also known as "Red Talka" after the name of the neighboring river.

Kineshma

In the Ivanovo region there are three cities from the historical cities list. I visited Shuya and Plyos in 2013, and now I’ve reached Kineshma (emphasis on the letter I).

There are trains from Ivanovo to Kineshma a couple of times a day, direct night trains from Moscow, and buses from Ivanovo very often.

An old friend - the Volga River. How many cities have I already visited on it, from Tver to Astrakhan?
