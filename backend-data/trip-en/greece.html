25-27 October, 29 October - 4 November 2016

Corfu

I was in Greece in 1997, at the age of 7. But this doesn’t count, so I traveled like the first time. Wishing to see different parts of the country, I started the route from the western edge - the island of Corfu. This is the most “non-Greek” part of Greece, because while Greece was under Turkish rule for 400 years (from the 15th to the 19th centuries), Corfu belonged to Venice from the 13th century until its division between Napoleonic France and Austria, and after the war for another 40 years managed to be a possession of England. Therefore, Corfu in terms of architecture has more in common with the coastal cities of Croatia and Slovenia, although the main language here has always been Greek.

Ioannina

Greece is primarily a country of beach holidays; cities on the mainland (except Athens and Thessaloniki) are quite rarely visited, and especially the Epirus region on the border with Albania. The ancient Greeks almost did not explore it due to the colder climate. The city of Ioannina appeared on the borders of Byzantium only at the end of the 9th century, but Epirus was soon captured by the Crusaders, then the Venetians, and returned to Byzantium only briefly in the 14th century, soon passing to the Serbs, and then to the Turks, who held it until the Balkan Wars of 1913 of the year.

Athens

Athens sounds interesting, but in reality it is one of the most uninteresting and unattractive capitals in Europe. If in Rome in addition to the Colosseum and other ruins of Ancient Rome there is a lot of stuff built in later eras, then in Athens there is nothing to see except the ruins of the Acropolis: since ancient Greece times, the city fell into disrepair, and in the 19th century it was a village of 4,000 people, where the capital of newly independent Greece was moved as a sign of continuity. As a result, most of the city is built up with monotonous white cubes covered with ugly graffiti. One day is enough to explore this capital.

The ancient city center was located on a high cliff, dramatic. Now it is a museum. The Parthenon can be seen from afar.

Rhodes

The trip began on the western edge in Corfu and ended on the eastern edge - on the island of Rhodes. This island became part of modern Greece later than all others: since it is near the coast of Turkey, it had the largest Muslim population and a naval base, the war for Greek independence did not reach here. In 1912, Rhodes passed to Italy after Turkey's defeat in the Italo-Turkish War, and was Italian until the end of World War II, after that the British handed it over to Greece.

Before the capture of the island by the Turks at the beginning of the 16th century, the Order of Hospitallers was based here, which later moved to Malta and became Maltese. Fortifications have been here since those times, and the city of Rhodes is probably one of the largest and best preserved fortresses I have seen in Europe.
