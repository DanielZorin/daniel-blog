Spain: Madrid

16-25 February 2012

Madrid

One of the main attractions of Madrid is the street name signs, which are made here with great love. Each street in the central part of the city, even small alleys, has its own unique design. The image illustrates the name of the street.

Some houses have the inscription "Manzana", this word means not only "apple", but also "block of houses".

They also often indicate here that the house is insured against fire (asegurada de incendios)

In the literary quarter, where the houses of Cervantes and Lope de Vega are located, road signs from the old series, in relief, have been preserved.

Here, quotes from literary works are written on the asphalt.

Sometimes the text is essentially equivalent to a plaque, like the one at Lope de Vega's house.

At Cervantes's house - both text and a quote from Don Quixote

There are signs like this further from the center.

In Madrid they like to paint houses on neighboring walls. I counted four such compositions.

It turns out that in Spain football players also have certificates. I wonder who and when they present them to? To be allowed into the locker room if suddenly the local guard doesn’t recognize them? Here is Zidane's ID.

Real Madrid is even in chess.

Near the stadium on the square there are round benches, in the middle of which trees from different countries are planted. There is no Russia, but there is the USSR, the birch tree is growing.

In Madrid you immediately understand that you are in the capital of the empire. Although the king does not live here, the palace is used for ceremonies.

There are sculptures on the roofs.

Even the urns feature crowns.

Rose garden.

In the same park, in the pavilion, there was an installation with a starry sky.

Alien.

Area with a ping pong table.

There is a corner with a world map, and in the corner there is a house made of cardboard.

There is always a detailed route map at bus stops. The rooms are arranged in a semicircle.

When I get to Spain in the summer, and not in the early spring, I will definitely go to a bullfight. In Madrid I visited the main bullring and the adjacent museum.

Around the arena there are monuments to famous matadors, and at the entrance there is a board of honor where the names of the most outstanding ones are listed.

Of course, the city cannot do without contemporary art.

The food in Madrid, as in all of Spain, is simply excellent. You can eat for 200 rubles ($7) in the central square. In Madrid, there is the oldest restaurant in the world, where you can order a whole roasted pig for a ridiculous amount of around 20 euros.

Tangerines for 0.33 euros per kilogram - only at Don Fruta in the Rastro area.

In Spain, it turns out, calculus is taught using the same books as in Russia. Even the cover design is the same.

Madrid airport is jaw-dropping.

The train station too.

Inside the station there is practically a whole park, and even a lake with turtles.

But the main thing is that Madrid has a special atmosphere. There is always some kind of movement in the central square. Here are the residents of the Democratic Republic of the Congo demanding the overthrow of the bloody dictator. Another time there was a huge crowd of students protesting against the crackdown on a demonstration in Valencia.

Some Christian sectarians usually handed out postcards with links to the site damemasinfo.com (“Give me information”), but one day a preacher stood up in a cowboy hat, shouting to the entire square that they needed to repent. The man standing to his left was translating from English to Spanish.

In general, be sure to come and see this city.
