Russia: Vologda, Belozersk, Cherepovets, Kirillov, Ferapontovo

18-20 July 2014

Vologda

In 2011, I was in Kostroma and had to admit that no one pronounces the unstressed “o” there. Well, in Vologda they definitely have to speak “o”! But no, as it turned out, the speech of a resident of Vologda is no different from the speech of a resident of Moscow. Although there is still a monument to the letter O in Vologda.

From a tourism point of view, Vologda is a very advanced city by Russian standards. There is even a tourist information center here (previously I saw it only in Moscow, St. Petersburg, Perm and Sergiev Posad).

Although it seemed to me that Vologda was away from the main tourist routes, both from Moscow and St. Petersburg it's an overnight train. At the hotel they gave me a free map of the city, just like in Europe! For some reason the map is 16+.

For some reason, the souvenir row near the local Kremlin was closed.

Belozersk

Belozersk is one of the historical cities of Russia. There is a medieval fortress and quite a small population, but it is far from the level of Suzdal because it has much fewer churches-monasteries. It’s a bit far away, even from Vologda it takes at least 3 and a half hours to drive.

Belozersk is one of the most ancient cities in Russia. 1152 years - exactly the same age as Russia itself, according to generally accepted history, i.e. The first mention of Belozersk coincides with the first mention of Rurik. Actually, this is the same reference, it says that Rurik became the ruler of Novgorod, and his brothers - of Belozersk and Izborsk (although other interpretations are possible!)

Cherepovets

People like to add photographs of the city of Cherepovets to demotivators as a symbol of industrial trash (on a par with Norilsk). I expected to see something terrible, but I didn’t.

In fact, Cherepovets is a completely ordinary Russian industrial city. Well, yes, this is not Suzdal and not Plyos, there are factories here. And there is no special historical center for tourists; in the center there is mainly Soviet monumental art.

Maybe if you go to the Severstal plant, the main enterprise of the city, you can take photos there about the hellish blast furnaces, but on the streets it’s okay.

Kirillov

I discovered a very interesting building in the city of Kirillov. This looks like a regular commieblock. But for some reason the architects decided to decorate their creation with the year of construction, as they liked to do on buildings of the late 19th century.

But of course, people don’t come here for this, but because the Kirillo-Belozersky Monastery is located here.

Near the monastery there is a lake. By the way, Belozersk is only 40 kilometers from here, but driving directly is problematic because there is no bridge across one of the rivers, only a ferry, and public transport runs very rarely.

If you arrive by early bus from Vologda, it seems that this place is not very popular, but around 11 o’clock excursion groups arrive here. There are a lot of groups, it’s amazing how many groups travel around the Vologda region, although it would seem that it is quite far from Moscow and St. Petersburg. Moreover, many groups are with foreigners: they speak English, French, Italian.

There is a fee for photography.

Ferapontovo

Quite close to Kirillov is the village of Ferapontovo. The bus runs here once a day, and if you leave for Kirillov from Vologda in the morning, you won’t have time to catch it; you need to spend the night in Kirillov. The alternative is to ask to get on one of the many tourist buses, the main thing here is not miss them, otherwise they quickly leave Kirillov. Or you can hitchhike.

Ferapontovo is a good Russian village. Not abandoned and hopeless, like 99% of other villages, but sincere and dear.

This is because the Ferapontov Monastery is located here, where frescoes from the early 16th century have been preserved. This is one of the world heritage sites in Russia.
