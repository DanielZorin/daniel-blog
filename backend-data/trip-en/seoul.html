Gwangju

Near the city of Gwangju there is a place included in the UNESCO list - the Hwasun Dolmens. There are a lot of dolmens in Korea, but Hwasun is the most convenient to get to, and at the same time you can visit Gwangju, the largest city in the southwest of the country.

Dolmens are stone tombstones of ancient people (1st millennium BC). There are dolmens in both Europe and Russia (mainly in the Caucasus), but for unknown reasons, half of the world's dolmens are located in small Korea.

By and large, these are huge boulders that stand on supports made of smaller stones.

Of course, this place will not blow your mind.

But it’s very pleasant to go here, because the dolmens are located in a very picturesque area.

The stones were mined here.

Getting here from Gwangju is not that easy. The public transport system is organized strangely: there are several bus routes that go to Hwasun, and then they go along different roads, but all the route numbers are the same, so it’s difficult to understand whether the bus you need has arrived or not. In several buses, the driver immediately said that he was going the wrong way, somewhere the third or fourth arrived, but he, too, only took us to the village of Neunju.

From Neunju to the dolmens you need to walk another 5-6 kilometers to another village. At the same time, buses also go along this road; we caught a bus back only about a kilometer and a half from the dolmens, right at the turn.

There are farms along the road.

Here is a typical Korean countryside.

Apparently, more people come here on weekends, but on weekdays there were almost no tourists.

The village near the dolmens is called Hyosan (indicated on the map as Hyosan-ri)

Daejeon

The city of Daejeon (million of people, the fifth most populous in Korea) impressed me with its hotel. I planned to stay there for one night, but stayed for the second just to enjoy the divine hotel. Called Motel CF, located next to the bus station. It's a bit far from the center, but an e-mart supermarket is nearby.

What's so special about this hotel? The fact is that it is relatively inexpensive - 25,000 won ($23), but at the same time it has everything you can dream of. I have been to both 4 and 5 stars, but they were not all close to this modest Korean hotel. Sokcho

Seoraksan is the most popular national park in mainland Korea. To get there, you first need to get to the city of Sokcho and check into a hotel there (by the way, the cheapest of all where I stayed in Korea - 20,000 won or $18). You can get to the park by city bus, where for the first time I was unable to pay with the t-money pass, which works in all major cities, and even in Jeju and Gyeongju. Entrance to the park costs about $3, and another $10 for the cable car ride.

If you don’t have the time or energy for a long hike, then the smartest strategy is to first take the cable car to the top, walk around everything there, and use the rest of the time on the tracks below.

There are two routes from the cable car station. One leads up here.

Seoul

Korea usually begins with Seoul, but my trip ended there instead. Seoul is a truly enormous city, it seems that it never ends. There are as many as five World Heritage sites within the reach of the Seoul metro: I think, this is a record (on par with Beijing). The first place is the Changdeokgung Royal Palace.

It is more convenient to go there on Tuesday, when tourists are allowed in at any time. On other days it's only with excursions, which go often, but, unfortunately, almost everything is in Korean.

All palaces are quite similar.

There are so many buildings that it’s easy to find a corner where no one will interfere with your photo shoot.

A stove pipe, which, according to Korean tradition, heats the floor.

But there is one trick in this palace.

Cherry trees grow in backyards.

There is a lot of it, and no one needs it. At the same time, the cherries are quite juicy and tasty. There were also apricots growing in one of the courtyards, but they were not very ripe yet.

Another more popular palace is Gyeongbokgung.

At the entrance you can take pictures with the guards. The last Korean king was overthrown by the Japanese more than 100 years ago.

It's quite similar from the inside.

The throne rooms and chambers are quite modest by European standards.

There are plenty of secluded corners here too.

And the backyards are larger.

Next to the first palace is Jongmyo Temple, the place where kings performed rituals to commemorate their ancestors. The spirit of each king after his death was enclosed in a special tablet, which was kept in a special room. It is interesting that a special path was equipped for the spirits, which led directly to the temple, while everyone else entered from the side. This is still observed now - you cannot step on the path.

Entrance here is also only with a guided tour, but here I was lucky to arrive just in time for the start of the tour in English.

Royal burials have the same special path.

There are quite a few of these burials, they are scattered across different parts of Seoul. They look more like Viking burial mounds than Chinese imperial burial grounds.

Another interesting place, however, not popular among tourists is Namhansanseong. This is a fortress on the hills in the corner of Seoul. Mostly Korean weekend hikers come here. You need to get there first by metro, then by bus.

There is a good view of Seoul from the walls.

There would be, if not for the haze.

This fortress is so huge that it was originally designed as a place where in case of problems the entire population of the capital could be evacuated.

Now there are several villages here.

And vegetable gardens. Yes, in the capital of Korea.

Apart from these attractions, most of Seoul was built in the second half of the 20th century. There is only one small block on the hill, made to look like an antique.
