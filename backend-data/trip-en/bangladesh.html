Bangladesh: Khulna, Bagerhat, Jessore, Rajshahi, Puthiya, Natore, Bogra, Mahastan, Paharpur, Rangpur, Sonargaon, Chittagong

1-3, 9-10 June 2017

Khulna

The first stop on my journey through the provinces of Bangladesh was the city of Khulna - one of the eight provincial capitals.

I traveled from Dhaka by an overnight train which arrived in Khulna around 5 am. I was traveling in the most expensive class - a compartment with air conditioning, the ticket cost as much as 20 dollars. Long-distance trains in Bangladesh are quite comfortable (which cannot be said about suburban trains), and they also run on time, unlike Indian ones.

There are compartments for four and two. Tickets can be purchased on the website and printed at the station ticket office.

Before dawn I had to sit in a diner near the station.

As soon as the sun rose, I went for a walk around the city. It was still too early, and besides the pedicabs near the station there was almost no one on the streets.

Selling bananas at the station.

A shopping area where shops have not yet opened.

Typical Khulna. There are no attractions in the city. The region has two World Heritage Sites - the Bagerhat Mosque and the Sundarbans (mangrove forests). It is almost impossible to see the Sundarbans from Bangladesh; there are no excursions there; at most you can drive to the edge of the reserve and go to the zoo there. It is better to see this park from the other side of the border in India, where there is some kind of infrastructure. But it’s quite possible to go to Bagerhat, it’s only 20 kilometers from Khulna.

Bagerhat

In the city of Bagerhat, several mosques and mausoleums from the 15th century have been preserved. They are not located in the modern city of Bagerhat, but along the highway to Khulna, you need to get off the bus early.

The main building is a mosque with 60 domes. Entrance to the territory costs 2.5 dollars; the inside is closed, but you can look through the windows and doors.

There are a number of prohibitory signs along the fence.

Behind the mosque there is a nice pond with ducks.

The rest of the objects are still a couple of kilometers along the road to the south, you can walk. For a long time this city was abandoned, but now it’s a rural area, people are growing something in the fields.

There are ponds overgrown with mud along the route.

Mahastan

Just north of Bogra are the ruins of the Mahastangarh fort, which is about 2000 years old. Now all that remains of it are the walls, which seem to have been well restored recently.

You can walk around the fortress wall, after a while the restored part will end.

Inside the wall are fields where rice grows and cows graze.

Gradually the wall goes into the ground.

And turns into a shaft.

Local residents collect mangoes. I didn’t notice any tourists in this area, although the area at the entrance is well landscaped.

Entry.

There are still remains of a temple nearby.

What’s good about Bangladesh is that even in such a seemingly rural area there is plenty of transport. You just stand next to the highway and after 5 minutes you get into a passing tuk-tuk, which will take you to the next village in 10-20 taka. The main thing is to wait for a tuk-tuk that still has room to fit into.

The next village to the north is called Shibganj. Administratively the fort is on its territory.

Well, a village... with a population of 400 thousand.

Central square.

In all Bangladeshi villages I aroused a lot of interest, people came up and looked from a distance of several meters to see who had come to them. Those who were more advanced in English asked what country I was from.

Paharpur

The second World Heritage Site, the Paharpur Temple, was not easy to reach: it is deep in the countryside and some distance from the main roads.

The nearest town is called Joypurhat, I got there by bus from the village of Shibganj - it is at the turn off from the main highway leading north, and I easily found a bus there. From Joypurhat you need to go completely along a country road. On the central street of Joypurhat, the police took an interest in me, they fiddled with my passport for a long time, looking at visas and stamps, and in the end they put me on the right bus, although it seems to me that I missed a couple of also suitable buses while hanging out in their booth.

Joypurhat looks something like this.

Among the attractions there is an impressive monument to freedom fighters.

The bus on the country road was shaking terribly.

And it was covered with stone pavement, like on the roads of Ancient Rome. It looks like this road hasn't been improved in the last fifteen hundred years.

Finally, after another short rickshaw ride, I reached a huge Buddhist temple, one of the largest in South Asia.

Immediately I got on Bangladeshi TV, reporters were filming something, and they asked me on camera a few questions about whether I liked this temple.

Considering the fascinating nature of the road here, the place is definitely worth a visit.

Sonargaon

On the road from Dhaka to Chittagong in the city of Sonargaon there is an abandoned area called Panam Nagar. Once upon a time, rich Indian traders lived here and built luxurious villas for themselves, but after the declaration of independence of India and Pakistan and the exchange of populations, they were all expelled because they were not Muslims. By some miracle, the houses were not occupied by other people, like the palace in Natore, but were left empty, and now they have completely fenced them off and turned them into a museum.

The facades of the houses are already in poor condition, but once there was Baroque here.

Chittagong

Chittagong is the second largest city in Bangladesh. It is of interest mainly to journalists trying to get into the shipyards where old ships are dismantled. There's not much to see in the city. I ended up here for an unusual reason - the flight Chittagong - Dhaka - Kuala Lumpur with the national airline Biman was cheaper than just Dhaka - Kuala Lumpur.
