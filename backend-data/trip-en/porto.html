Portugal: Porto, Braga, Aveiro, Coimbra, Caldas da Rainha, Obidos

25-29 September 2014

Porto

Once upon a time, the city of Porto changed my ideas about the world. Having come here for the second time, I do not give up my opinion - to see Porto and die.

To be honest, I don’t even know what to write about Porto.

Every square centimeter is beautiful here.

Instead of a thousand words.

Showcases.

Lawyer signs.

Students celebrate the beginning of the school year.

Railway station.

If you want to see Europe, come to Porto, after that there is little that will impress you in Europe.

Braga

It is clearly visible how similar Portugal and its former colony Brazil are in appearance. In Braga, I could not get rid of the feeling of deja vu, the local church and the church in the city of Congonhas are so similar.

They even have the same name: Bom Jesus.

Both here and there along the stairs to the top there are chapels with wooden statues illustrating the Bible.

The interior also has a lot in common.

This church is located quite far from the city center.

There is a funicular going up, with inscriptions using the spelling of a hundred years ago (now they write saida).

There is also a cave with stalactites nearby.

Actually Braga.

This is what residential areas look like in Portugal. Visually somewhat different from neighboring Spain.

In the city center, gates are integrated into houses, just like in the Middle Ages.

Modern Art.

Ancient art.

In all cities of Portugal, the streets are lined with yellow and black paving stones with all kinds of patterns.

Street signs.

The economic situation in Portugal is not very good. On the walls, people are actively protesting, first of all, against the owners of the debt, which in Portuguese is called with the Russian word troika: IMF, European Commission, ECB.

Get rid of the troika, get rid of the government, get rid of the economy, everyone goes to the Maidan on September 22.

As sad as it is, against this background the communists are becoming popular: “Get out of the euro! Down with the government of national betrayal! Let’s bring back the escudo! Long live the united democratic patriotic government!”

Aveiro

This town is called the Portuguese Venice because there are a couple of canals along which boats float.

But it still doesn’t reach Venice.

It's still a nice town though.

Paving stones are present.

A steep bridge was built for the 2004 European Football Championship.

Central square.

All cities in Portugal have unique street signs, just like Spanish cities. Here they are mainly made from Portuguese tiles.

More tiles.

The old station building is interestingly decorated.

On the other side, there is a public service announcement on the asphalt: “Only by feeling the problems with our skin, we begin to take them seriously.”

The print on the wall "Em cada esquina um amigo" ("A friend on every corner") is a quote from the revolutionary song "Grandola", the broadcast of which on the radio became the signal for the 1974 revolution.

Coimbra

In what other Western European country can you stay on the central square for 25 euros, except Portugal?

It is immediately clear that Coimbra is a student city, the walls are covered with emotional verses.

"All the time you spent without sex and love was wasted. Coimbra is passion!"

“We women reject exploration of our bodies.”

The university itself.

And here are the students. Students in Portugal wear cool raincoats, like in the Harry Potter films. Of course, not all year.

Lace was hung on one of the streets.

And somewhere they hang it on balconies.

"The euro is drowning the country. Down with the privileges of politicians. To prison those who robbed the country and drove it into debt. Let's unite the left front and throw off the troika!"

As in other Portuguese cities, the views here are a sight to behold.

Posting advertisements is prohibited.

Unexpectedly, there are sheets with obituaries, like in the Balkan countries.

In the evening there was a concert of Portuguese fado romances on the main square; I listened to it right from the balcony, sitting on an easy chair.

Leiria

Portuguese public transport is not very frequent on weekends. Long distance buses depart daily, but local buses stop. Due to these I could not visit the towns of Alcobaca and Batalha, so I did not stay in Leiria and went south. But I saw the castle on the hill.

Caldas da Rainha

Let's see what a small, not very touristy city in Portugal looks like.

Caldas da Rainha ("Hot Springs of the Queen"), population 30 thousand. This, of course, is not exactly a backwater, but tourists usually pass by while exploring neighboring Obidos, Leiria or Nazaré. I stayed in this town because the hotel was cheaper and there was a local bus to Óbidos.

The paving stones are in place. Although the city is small, it is quite lively by European standards; shops and restaurants were open on Sunday. Apparently, the Portuguese have tightened their belts due to the crisis.

The bus station is right in the historical center, but it doesn’t really spoil the view because it’s hidden inside the building. The same is done in Porto.

There is also a train station here; the station is located on the main Lisbon-Porto line.

The station is decorated with tiles, and like other stations, there are antique Paul Garnier clocks.

Around the station there is a residential area.

More paving stones.

There is a hospital here, which is considered the oldest mineral water hospital in the world, founded in the 15th century.

The church built on the side has been standing since approximately the same time.

The windows are, as usual, gorgeous.

There is also a souvenir shop (even two, next to each other opposite the hospital) with a very unexpected assortment.

This is what the paving stones look like during construction. So cute that you want to pick it up and take it home.

This small town has two unique types of street signs.

Even two and a half.

Another typically Portuguese vintage feature is a pay phone for calling a taxi.

And finally, the craziest place in this town is the playground.

By the way, in Caldas da Rainha I heard Russian spoken several times. For some reason, there are a lot of Russian and Ukrainian emigrants in Portugal.

Óbidos

Óbidos is a museum city, one of the most popular in Portugal. It's an hour and a half to two hours drive from Lisbon. It is easier to get from Lisbon than from other large cities, because there are direct buses, but Óbidos is a little off the main road, and buses heading towards Porto do not stop there.

True, there is also a railway station, it is located on the outskirts, on the back side of the castle. Not all trains stop here, so the bus is more convenient.

Obidos is wonderful.

The whole place is covered in flowers.

Lock.

Around the city you can walk along the fortress wall, like in Dubrovnik. Here the passage is free.

There isn't even a fence.

The views from the top are excellent.

There is a children's playground under the castle walls.

On the other side is a cemetery.

Gate of the city.

The church here has been converted into a bookstore.

Aqueduct.

Tourists are entertained with costumed performances.

The story about Obidos would be incomplete without the ginjinha. This is a Portuguese cherry liqueur. In Obidos there is a special tradition - here it is poured into glasses made of chocolate, and the glass must be eaten after drinking the liqueur.
