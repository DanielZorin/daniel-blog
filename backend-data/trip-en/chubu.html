Fuji

Mount Fuji is a symbol of Japan. It is located not far from Tokyo, but it is quite rare to see the peak even from a close distance due to clouds and fog. Winter is the best time for this, and I was incredibly lucky: that day there was not a cloud in the sky, Fuji was visible even from the center of Tokyo, and I drove even closer - to Lake Kawaguchi on the northern slopes.

The easiest and fastest way to get to the city of Fuji-Kawaguchiko is by direct bus from Tokyo, although there is also a railway.

Buses in Japan have an interesting payment system. When entering, you need to take a piece of paper on which the number of the current stop is printed. When exiting, you need to pay the driver; the price, depending on the number on the piece of paper, is indicated on the board above.

Karaoke. There are a lot of such establishments in Japan. Unlike Russia, where karaoke is just a restaurant where someone can sing in front of everyone, in Japan there are private rooms with good walls for rent. Half an hour here costs 180 yen during the day or 350 yen in the evening (the top price is a discount for schoolchildren). You can also take unlimited (bottom line) - 800 yen during the day until 8 pm, or 1680 yen per night. In principle, it is quite possible to spend the night there if you wish.

The castle in Matsumoto is one of the most beautiful places I have seen in Japan.

The reflection in the water is directly adjacent to the castle, so that it seems to be floating in the air.

And absolute silence all around.

Only fish swim in the pond.

Matsumoto town is small and quiet in the evenings.

Historical buildings with black tiled roofs have been preserved here.

Water sources are quite common.

Closer to the station is a modern area with multi-storey shops and restaurants.

Matsumoto is apparently a popular city among local tourists, so hotels here are slightly above the national average. Here is the price tag of the inexpensive Toyoko Inn chain, a single room is 6-7 thousand yen (more expensive on weekends).

Tsumago - Magome

At the end of the 17th century, two main roads were built and equipped, connecting the main cities of Edo (Tokyo) and Kyoto. One of them ran along the seashore - now it is the main highway and the route for high-speed trains. The second went inside the island through the mountains, and was called nakasendo - “middle mountain road.” Its remains can still be seen in the picturesque Kiso Valley.

The non-high-speed railway line here runs roughly parallel to Nakasendo.

Mountains in the distance.

Small towns along the road.

It is curious that the names of stations on the signs are written large not in hieroglyphs, but in hiragana, so that they can be easily read (reading hieroglyphs is not always obvious, especially in geographical names).

River.

I got off at Nagiso Station and immediately took a local bus to Tsumago.

Tsumago is a post station on the old road. In those distant times, such stations were needed to provide places for lodging and food. In Japan, people traveled mostly on foot because horses were scarce and only wealthy people could afford them.

The appearance of this town was reconstructed in the 1960s, and now nothing is being built or sold here.

Old Japan looked something like this. Everything is made of wood, which is why large cities mostly did not survive the war.

Now Tsumago mainly has shops for tourists.

Water source.

From Tsumago to the next station, Magome, the old road has been preserved in some places. The distance is about 17-18 kilometers, so it’s quite possible to walk it in a few hours, even with my backpack.

Altar in front of the road.

Since the closest bullet train station is Nagoya, people usually come to this region from the south, and almost everyone takes the Nakasendo in the opposite direction, ending in Tsumago and going back to Nagoya from there. I came from the north and walked in opposite direction.

Hikone

Between Nagoya and Kyoto there is another city with an old castle - Hikone, right on the main railway line, although without a stop for high-speed trains.

Station square with an equestrian monument.

Hikone Castle.

As usual, it is surrounded by a large moat of complex shape, but certainly with right angles (in Europe, castles and fortresses are usually round).

Entry.

The colorful building of the local museum.

Its backyard with statues.

The castle has a fairly large garden.

If you walk around the castle and reach the farthest point from the entrance, then just a couple of hundred meters away you will find Lake Biwa - a fairly large lake that stretches almost all the way to Kyoto.

Residential area.

On one of the fences hung a series of posters with advice on how to use smartphones, the Internet, etc. Here they advise not to save credit card data on children’s phones so that they cannot buy applications and goods in games.

Tips for older people: ask to set up your phone when purchasing and don't rush to switch to fiber optics.

Standard Japanese toilet with control panel under the right hand. It is unusual here that the washbasin is located on the lid of the toilet tank, so water after washing hands goes into the tank - economically.
