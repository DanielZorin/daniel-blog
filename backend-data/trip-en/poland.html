Poland: Terespol, Warsaw, Torun, Gdansk, Gdynia, Sopot, Malbork, Elblag

8-12 August 2013

Terespol

For a long time Poland remained a blank spot of Europe for me. Getting there directly, either by plane or by train, although not incredibly expensive, is clearly more expensive than to Germany, which is further away, and quite comparable to Spain. Most people come to Poland in transit on their way to Western Europe by car or during a bus tour, and relatively few people travel here specifically. But the country is no worse than others in Eastern Europe, and is really underrated. I got to Poland in the most budget-friendly way possible. First I got to Brest on a seated night train. It cost about 1,400 rubles, although if you buy a ticket directly on the Russian Railways website, it will be 800 rubles more expensive. You can save money by buying Moscow-Orsha tickets from Russian Railways and Orsha-Brest on the Belarusian Railways website, where prices are 10 times lower. Belarus recently introduced electronic registration, so I was able to travel to Brest without printing tickets. True, in Orsha I had to move to another seat in the carriage, although the train was half-empty in Belarus, and there was no need to change seats. There is an local train from Brest to Terespol for 150 rubles, and here I am in Poland.

The Brest-Terespol train is very popular among Chechen refugees. The fact is that the presence of a Schengen visa is not checked upon landing; you can go to Poland, and only after that you will be deported. The ticket is cheap, so you can live in Brest and from time to time try to travel to Poland with your whole large Caucasian family. At the Polish border control, the Chechens are trying to pity the Poles with the sad story of their lives, so that they give them temporary political asylum, having received which, 99% of the “refugees” flock to Germany. You can get from Terespol to Warsaw for $13. Trains run rarely and are not always well matched with trains, so I had three hours to explore the town. Poland welcomes you with apartment buildings.

But in general Terespol is more of a village.

Gdansk

Gdansk is the place of the beginning of the end of communism. Lech Walesa worked here at the Lenin shipyard. A strike began at this shipyard, which led to the creation of Solidarity. A significant part of the shipyard is open to the public, which provides a unique chance to walk around the historical industrial area.

The strike movement in Poland existed before 1980. Unlike the USSR, which survived by trading hydrocarbons, in Poland communist power led the country to absolute poverty and degradation. In the 70s, that some in Russia remember with nostalgia as a time of satiety and abundance, in Poland everything was issued on ration cards. Near the entrance to the shipyard there is a monument in the shape of three crosses, dedicated to those killed during the strike in 1970. The monument was erected during the “thaw” of 1980-1981. In 1981, the Polish government imposed martial law, Solidarity was banned, and Lech Walesa was sent to prison. Communism was overthrown in Poland in 1989.
