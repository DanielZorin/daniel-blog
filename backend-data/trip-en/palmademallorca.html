Spain: Barcelona, Palma de Mallorca, Soller, Girona

24-30 March 2018

Barcelona

Four years have passed since my last visit to Barcelona and 7 whole years since the first (and by far the longest) visit. I got a ticket for some charter for 2,500 rubles ($40), I quickly found tickets from Girona to Lithuania in a week for some ridiculously small money, and together with the cheap Kaliningrad train, the total two-way fare came out to much less than $100.

The most noticeable thing that has changed in Barcelona during this time is that housing has become more expensive. In 2013-2014, I went to booking and booked a room near the center in a hotel for less than 20 euros, and a place in a hostel where I once stayed cost 5 or 6. In 2018, a place in a hostel cost more than 20 euros , and the hotel is closer to 60, although the end of March is far from peak time. You could find it cheaper for one day, but you can’t move for 6 days in a row every day, right? Even on airbnb, although I found housing for $35, I still had to move on the last day, since it was not possible to book for all the days. Most of the time I lived near the Sagrada Familia. It's not that close to the old town, so I've only been to this area before on my very first trip, and never made it here while transiting through Barcelona.

The Sagrada Familia is slowly being finished, it’s not clear when it’ll be ready. They promise for Gaudí's centenary in 2026.

The main slogan was “Freedom for political prisoners!”

It was the most massive event I have ever seen; not a single music festival came close, not to mention the rallies in Moscow even in 2011.

And on the far side of Montjuic from the center there is a large cemetery. I came across it by chance, I didn’t know about it before, and this became the main discovery of this trip to Barcelona.

From a distance it may seem that its contours resemble a Soviet residential area, but in fact these are multi-story columbariums.

Because it is on the slopes of a hill, it adds drama to the terrain.

All blocks have six floors.

We can see similar places in many cemeteries in Europe, but usually it is one columbarium corner with several blocks, but here it’s practically an entire city. Incredible place.

Up close.

Palma de Mallorca

My plans at that time did not include moving within Spain; I wanted to base myself in Barcelona. Thinking about where I could go for one day without an overnight stay, I chose an unexpected option - the island of Mallorca. The flight took less than an hour, and round-trip tickets cost only about 40 euros - no more expensive than going to Andorra. Mallorca is another region of Spain, the Balearic Islands (the other two large islands are Menorca and Ibiza).

The bus from the airport arrives at a bus station deep underground; on the floor above there is a metro, which I did not take because it goes to the outskirts.

Station square, if you can call it that.

It looks like an ordinary Spanish city, the architecture is reminiscent of Barcelona or Valencia.

There are examples of Art Nouveau from the early 20th century, with the influence of Gaudi.

Girona

I stayed in Girona for one night to catch an early flight from the airport (some low-cost airlines fly here as an alternative to Barcelona). I was already passing through this city in 2013.

That time, however, I did not reach the city walls, mainly due to bad weather. This time there was no rain; on the contrary, the weather was beautiful.
