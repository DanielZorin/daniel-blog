Australia: Sydney, Wollongong, Palm Beach, Newcastle

25-31 January 2020

Sydney

Since the first trip I wanted to return to Australia and Sydney in particular, so during my second trip to Oceania I allocated more days for transit through this beautiful country. Since I had visited all the states and their capitals, traveling to a new place was not so easy: although I could already rent a car, the distances in Australia are enormous, and it takes about two weeks to travel beyond the immediate vicinity of a big city. So I decided to just spend seven days in and around Sydney, since there are more than enough interesting places there, and at the same time well-developed public transport. As a result, it turns out that Sydney is among my leaders in terms of time spent there among all the cities in the world, and in general this is absolutely deserved; I would spend another 10 days there with interest.

And now, 8 months later, I'm back in Sydney. The opera is still out there.

On the last day I managed to go to the Royal National Park. It is Australia's oldest national park and the second oldest in the world after Yellowstone in the USA. The park is located in the southern suburbs of Sydney and includes a piece of ocean coastline. You can enter from three sides. The closest station to the center is Loftus, from where you can walk to the western edge of the park in 5 minutes, although you will have to cross the highway. From the next couple of stops you can also walk to the park, but longer. Otford station is near the coast on the southern boundary of the park, and the Cronulla terminus is near the northern boundary, served by ferries across the river from Cronulla. From Otford to Bundeena the road goes through the entire park, but it is not easy - more than 30 kilometers, and if anything, after half the way there are no roads, you can’t even get there by taxi. I visited the northwestern part of the park.

The Sydney Tram Museum is located outside Loftus Station. There used to be a regular tram line running inside the park, but since the 1980s it was turned into a tourist line. Now the tram (Like the entire museum) operates only twice a week - on Sunday and for some reason on Wednesday.

Having crossed the highway, I immediately went onto a path through the forest. In Australia, the forest is called bush, and respectively, hiking = bushwalking.

This park appears to have been undamaged by the January 2020 wildfires, but there are remains from the fires of several years earlier. Fires in the forests of Australia occur regularly (in 2020 they were stronger than usual), and the local flora is adapted to this - although the trunks are scorched, the tree continues to live.

Some trees have buds coated with a special resin so that they open when exposed to fire.

Gradually the paths become wider.
