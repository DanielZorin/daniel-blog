Finland: Helsinki, Turku

8-9 October 2016

Helsinki

Helsinki is one of the last European capitals that remained unvisited by me for a long time. Many consider this city boring, but it is quite interesting, I don’t understand why it is worse than Oslo or Copenhagen. Of the Scandinavian capitals, probably only Stockholm will undoubtedly be more interesting.

The center of Helsinki in some places, for obvious reasons, subtly resembles Russia.

A monument to Emperor Alexander II still stands opposite the main cathedral. Until 1917, his monuments stood in most major cities of the empire, just like there are monuments to Lenin now, but the Bolsheviks removed them all.

These streets are more reminiscent of German cities.

Finland inherited a wider track than in Europe from the Russian Empire.

Probably the main attraction of Helsinki is the Suomenlinna Fortress ("Finnish Castle"). Under the Swedes it was called Sveaborg - "Swedish Castle".

Suomenlinna is located on a separate island near Helsinki, and ferries go there very often for about 5 euros round trip. Although it is primarily an open-air museum, several hundred people still live on the grounds of the fortress. There is even an underground tunnel from the mainland that even a car can drive through, but it is for official use only.

Turku

In Turku I stayed in an unusual hotel - it was a moored cruise ship.

A single room cost 35 euros, which is cheap by Finnish standards. Breakfast is also included - this is generally an exceptional rarity in inexpensive hotels.

On this giant ferry I went towards Sweden.

I sailed first to the Åland Islands, a territory with a special status. It takes about 5 hours to sail there from Turku, and then another 2 hours to Sweden. I did not have a cabin, since I sailed during the day.

Locals use ferries to have fun and buy alcohol, which is cheaper here than in the cities of Finland and Sweden.

You can also play slot machines.

I looked from the deck to the shores. The path to Åland goes through countless small islands off the coast of Finland.

On many islands, someone lives or maintains cottages with piers.

The islands are rocky and covered with forest.

Upon arrival in Åland, many passengers immediately boarded the ferry in the opposite direction, without even leaving the city.
