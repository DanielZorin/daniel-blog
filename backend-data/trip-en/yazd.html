Iran: Shiraz, Persepolis, Yazd, Isfahan, Qom

23-27 August 2022

Shiraz

Shiraz is the center of the Fars region (from which the name Persia comes) and the cultural capital of Iran. It was also the capital of all of Iran for a short time in the 18th century.

Shiraz Airport.

Persepolis

In the vicinity of Shiraz there are many ruins of ancient cities from different eras of Persian history. The most famous ones date back to the Achaemenid era - the first Persian Empire, which existed from 550 to 329 BC. In terms of area, it was the largest Persia; it included the territories of modern Turkey, Egypt, Syria, Israel, Iraq, Pakistan, Afghanistan, Turkmenistan and Uzbekistan. The capital of the Achaemenid Empire was Persepolis, the ruins of which are located near the modern city of Mervdasht, an hour's drive from Shiraz.

City of Mervdasht.

What we see now is the very central part of the ancient city, where there were several palaces and state halls adjacent to each other. It was all on a platform higher than the rest of the city.

The steps are specially low so that it is convenient to climb, and you can even ride a chariot.

The famous "gate of all nations".

What remained of the palaces were mostly columns and foundations.

Persepolis was destroyed by fire in 329 BC, when it was taken by the troops of Alexander the Great.

From Pasargadae I wanted to go to Yazd. I had already traveled about 100 kilometers in the direction of Yazd, and I didn’t want to go back to Shiraz to catch a bus there. So I tried to catch it on the highway near the nearest town of Saadet Shahr. There were a lot of buses passing there, most heading towards Tehran and Isfahan, some towards Yazd, they all stopped there to check in at the police checkpoint, but they were all full and did not take passengers. After standing for more than an hour, I decided to go somewhere and took a bus towards Tehran to get to the town of Surmak at the turn. In Surmak, I was advised to go first to the neighboring city of Abarkuh, and then look for a collective taxi to Yazd. In the end, this happened, at the same time I found out how much a taxi between cities costs and realized that it is so cheap that you can even take a taxi alone.

Abarkuh looks curious, as I later realized, similar to Yazd.

There are pyramids like these that were used as huge refrigerators.

Further towards Yazd the landscape turns into a real desert.

Yazd

Yazd is probably the most interesting city in Iran in terms of architecture. It preserves an extensive historical center, listed as a World Heritage Site.

I settled in a hotel - caravanserai. You can imagine what local houses look like from the inside, since it is not clear from the outside.

Typical Iranian hotel breakfast, similar to Turkish.

The historical center consists of a maze of streets with concrete walls around the houses.

Often only the arches are thrown over the passages.

Apparently, this is what cities in the Middle East looked like 100 years ago, now there are only a few of them left - Yazd, Urfa, Bukhara.

Badgir (“windcatcher”) is a structure with towers used to create cool cellars.

There are quite a lot of them preserved in Yazd.

Also preserved here is “Qanat” - an ancient Persian water supply and irrigation system, which consists of many kilometers of underground channels with water.

Isfahan

Isfahan is the third largest city in Iran and the former capital. There are also a lot of historical attractions here, but by this time I, it seems, had already seen the most interesting things in Shiraz, Mashhad, and Tehran, so I looked around Isfahan more lazily.

Isfahan also has a metro, one line, but the second one is almost completed. They let me in for free, since payment was only made using Iranian cards.

The station in the center where I wanted to get off turned out to be closed, apparently they are preparing to open a second line to which there will be a transfer, I had to go back one station.

There are not only carriages only for women, but also only for men.

The main attraction of Isfahan is Naqshe Jahan Square, a World Heritage Site.

The square is huge, on one side is the Imam Mosque, on the other - the palace, on the third - the market.

Imam Mosque (formerly Shah Mosque).

It is very unusual to see the mosque so empty, only rare tourists walk through it, since it works mainly as a museum (you need to buy a ticket at the entrance). There are also no carpets on the floors. Usually mosques in Iran are very crowded, but here it was like walking into a cathedral somewhere in Europe.

You can come here to pray during one of the five prayers; especially for this purpose, carpets are laid in the center of the courtyard.

I got to Isfahan on Friday, the market was closed, like in Tehran.

After walking more than a kilometer through the galleries of the market, I came to the second square, about the size of Naqshe Jahan, but without palaces.

Not far from here is the Friday (Jami) Mosque, another World Heritage Site.

The decor here is much more modest, but this is one of the most ancient mosques in Iran.

Qom

Qom is the second holy city in Iran after Mashhad. Foreign tourists also come here quite often, since it is located on the route between Tehran and Isfahan. As in Mashhad, the entire city center is oriented toward pilgrims who come to visit the mausoleum of Fatima Masumeh, the sister of Imam Reza (who is buried in Mashhad).

In the city center, most people wear black or white clothes.
