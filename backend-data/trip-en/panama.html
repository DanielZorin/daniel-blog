Panama: David, Panama City, Colon, Portobelo

6-10 November 2021

David

Panama is the richest Central American country with a history separate from the rest. While the previous five countries originally made up the United Provinces of Central America, Panama was part of Colombia (and for a time, Panama, Colombia, Venezuela and Ecuador were one big country). This is surprising considering that there is still no road from Panama to Colombia (“Darién Gap”). At the end of the 19th century, the Americans decided to build a canal in Panama and supported the fight for independence from Colombia. Since then, Panama has been inextricably linked with the canal, the territory of which was for a long time part of the United States, and was completely transferred to Panama only at the end of the 20th century. Panama grew around the canal, and was not particularly colonized elsewhere, so there are almost no large cities in this country, except for the capital. David is the third largest city (about 200 thousand inhabitants), and the first city on the road from Costa Rica. I stayed there overnight to break up a very long drive.

Panama City

Panama City, the capital of Panama, is the richest and most developed major city in Central America. Thanks to the canal and offshore companies, there is a lot of money in Panama, people come here to live and do business. Such a Latin American version of Dubai. If other capitals of the region are at best ordinary, and at worst extremely dangerous, the capital of Panama has a lot to see and is quite safe. I rented a hotel room in a new area, not far from skyscrapers - it’s a little more expensive than the most budget hostels in the historical center, but the location is more prosperous.

* * *

Of course, while in Panama, I went to see the Panama Canal. Not far from the capital, near the Miraflores locks, there is an observation deck. The ticket price in 2021 has been halved - from $20 to $10. On the official website it was written about strict protocols that they allow groups in strictly according to time, but in fact this is all profanation - you come, buy a ticket, join the nearest group. No one makes sure that you spend no more than half an hour at the observation point, and this is impossible, because the groups do not differ from each other in any way (they should have handed out colored helmets, like in Costa Rica).

The Panama Canal passes most of the way through a large lake on an isthmus; a series of locks have been completed to connect the lake with both oceans, and for this it is necessary to raise and lower the ship 26 meters to the level of the lake.

Colon

Colon, the second largest city in Panama, stands at the northern entrance to the Panama Canal. The guidebooks say that this is a dangerous city, but I thought it would be like in San Jose, that is, dangerous compared to Japan, but overall normal. What is there to be afraid of after Honduras and El Salvador? Once in Colon, I was simply disgusted by what a dump it was - truly the worst city of the entire trip.

The weather, of course, added to the infernal appearance of the city.

Even on the main street, some of the buildings are abandoned. One block away, almost all the buildings are like this, there are continuous squats and slums. It was scary to even go there, I saw them from the taxi window when I was driving back.

It’s also very dirty here, like on the border of El Salvador and Honduras.

Portobelo

The fortresses were built in the 17th century. In principle, they are not very different from similar fortresses along the entire coast of Brazil or on the Caribbean islands.
