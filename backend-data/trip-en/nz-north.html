1-9 April 2019

Wellington

Wellington is the capital of New Zealand, but not the largest city. The capital was moved here so that it would be in the middle of the country, and not on the northern edge.

Wellington is known for its extreme climate with very strong winds. The local airport is one of the most difficult to land. Luckily the wind was moderate when I was in town.

The city center is quite densely built up, something I have never seen on the South Island.

Taupo

The town of Taupo stands on the shores of the lake of the same name, which is actually the mouth of a huge volcano. Its last eruption was around 180 AD, and it is one of the largest eruptions in the last 2000 years.

The city center consists of several streets with shops and restaurants, and no residential buildings at all, and then there are residential areas.

Tongariro National Park

Tongariro National Park is the main park of the North Island, popularly best known as the prototype of Mordor in the movies "The Lord of the Rings". This is a high mountain area in the center of the island with several active volcanoes. Basically, everyone walks along the Tongariro Alpine Crossing track, which is about 18 kilometers long, possible to complete in one day. The track starts and ends in different places, so even if you drive your own car to the start, you will still need a shuttle from the end point. I took the bus from Taupo (70 NZD round trip), there are also shuttles from the closer town of Turangi for 55 NZD.

The shuttle arrived at the entrance site just before dawn. There seemed to be quite a lot of people, but after the start of the mountain sections everyone dispersed, there was no longer a crowd.

Mount Ruapehu is visible in the distance.

The first 4 kilometers the road goes through a volcanic valley with a very small elevation difference.

Streams with orange water.

Then the climb begins to about 400 meters.

The climb seemed easy to me compared to Mount Taishan and Nepal.

Here a flat valley begins, after that there is another climb of 200 meters.

Ngauruhoe a.k.a. Mount Doom.

A few years ago you could go up to its crater (and throw a ring there), but now this is prohibited under the pretext that it is sacred. Too many people threw rings?

The second climb is shorter, but technically more difficult. If it had rained, climbing these rocks would have been a bit daunting.

But the weather was so perfect that with such a clear sky it didn’t look much like Mordor.

This is the top point of the route, from here the road is mostly downhill. Quite a lot of people relax here.

From the side you can look at the red crater.

Then the most fun part is the sharp descent down into the valley on the other side of the crater. There is no path here, only loose volcanic ash, and you slide down like on sand. In some places the ground is really hot, you can cook food by burying it.

In principle, you can walk the track in the opposite direction, it is more difficult because the end point is lower than the starting point, which means the climb will take longer.

The last few kilometers are through typical New Zealand dense forest with ferns.

Auckland has double-decker buses. Travel is expensive - for cash 3.50 only in the first zone, and my house was in the second (although it was possible to get out 700 meters earlier, still the first zone). With a travel card, one zone costs a little less than $2, although the card itself costs $10 and the deposit is not refundable. It still pays off quickly.

There is also a system of commuter trains, which is useful for tourists mainly to get to the airport (you still have to travel a short distance by bus). The metro is under construction.

Bay of Islands

The last trip in New Zealand was to the very north of the North Island, to the Bay of Islands. The site has some historical significance as it is where Captain James Cook first met the Maori and where the Treaty of Waitangi was signed, under which New Zealand became a British colony.

Bay cruises depart from Paihia. You can get there either by regular bus or by excursion bus. I went there on a regular route and back on a tour, so I had time to walk around the city before the cruise.

The village of Waitangi, where the treaty was signed, is located across the river, about 30 minutes’ walk from Paihia.

Unexpectedly, it turned out that the site of the signing of the Treaty of Waitangi is almost the most expensive attraction in New Zealand; admission costs more than $50! For the first time I see that the price does not correspond so much to the interestingness of the place. To be honest, I was sure that admission was free, as is usually the case in such places of historical events.

I ended up walking around the perimeter. Judging by the photographs, the inside is just a field and one long Maori house with a triangular roof.
