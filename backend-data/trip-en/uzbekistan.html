Uzbekistan: Andijan, Tashkent, Bukhara, Samarkand, Shahrisabz

4-11 May 2013

Andijan

The taxi driver who took me to Andijan did not speak Russian at all. After four and a half hours on the Kyrgyz-Uzbek border, I stopped being surprised by anything. Communication took place through an interpreter, whom the taxi driver called on his mobile phone.

Andijan is a provincial city, there is not much to see there. Nothing reminds us of the mass shootings in 2005. I took a taxi to Tashkent, it cost only about $10 for a six-hour car ride. As usual in such taxis, there are four passengers and a driver. The silent old lady slept most of the way. The man in the front seat didn’t know a single word of Russian, and I never learned anything about him. My neighbor could explain himself a little, as he said, he learned it when he worked on construction sites in the city of Kursk. The driver turned out to be the same age as me, he managed to work in a beauty salon in Moscow. They treated me kindly, asked where I wanted to go, and whether I liked Andijan. After the crowd of gloomy faces in line at customs, it was very pleasant.

The stop at the bazaar in Kokand is unremarkable. It's time to explain about money. There are three banknotes in Uzbekistan - 200, 500 and 1000 sum. $1 is approximately 2700 sum. When you exchange $100, you get three packs of banknotes. This is Uzbekistan. Everyone's skills in quickly counting the number of pieces of paper in thick wads of money are excellent. Change for 100 sum is given in the form of candy. It feels like you are in Zimbabwe during the times of hyperinflation, when everyone went to buy bread with suitcases of money.

The road after Kokand is suddenly quite beautiful, hills, mountains on the horizon.

The man-made landscape outside the window is depressing. Collapsing houses, barracks, herds of donkeys and sheep wandering along the roadway along with cars, and many posters about a bright future, but not Soviet ones, but modern ones, written in Uzbek.

The next stop is near the city of Angren. Angren is a typical Soviet industrial city. Smoking factories and rows of five-story buildings with Soviet patterns on the ends. In Angren we had dinner at a diner. The menu is only in Uzbek, the waitresses also speak only the state language. In Kyrgyzstan, I studied many names of local dishes, so I was able to recognize the excellent lagman in “logmon”.

The secret of how cheap taxis are over longer distances is that the cars run on “gas fuel”, and gas is produced in Uzbekistan itself. During refueling, all passengers must get out of the car and wait for it at the exit of the gas station.

My phone was blowing up with text messages. Beeline greeted me four times: three times in Uzbekistan and once in Tajikistan, informed me that I was roaming, and kindly gave me the phone number of the Russian Embassy in Uzbekistan.

Tashkent

When we arrived in Tashkent, it was already dark (and I expected to get there by five o’clock). Tashkent at night looks like some kind of poor Dubai. It seems like you are driving along a lit street, surrounded by some pretentious buildings with columns, palaces of the local elite. Then you leave the main street, and the ugly part begins. The road is broken down, the sidewalk-path runs along a long fence, there is no light. In Moscow or Rio de Janeiro it’s not particularly scary to walk at night, but here I wouldn’t bother myself.

Using almost my last sum, I took a taxi to the cheapest hotel from Booking, which I did not have time to book due to lack of Internet. The prices are really good, especially in comparison with other hotels in Tashkent: only about $20 for a single room. But the administrator demanded payment immediately and refused to take a deposit in any currency until tomorrow. Well, that means we need to look for more adventures.

There was a cop hovering near the kiosk at the bus stop, so I moved in the other direction. Soon some women began to offer me... no, not what you thought, but to rent an apartment. But I could not, since then I would not get a registration, which threatens with a fine (read: robbery) when leaving the country. Another creepy old man was hanging out nearby, who said that he knew who would exchange 20 dollars for 56 thousand soums, but it was not clear what he wanted. The granny with the poker face says she doesn’t know anything about the black course. In the end, I had to agree to change money with those who rented apartments at the official rate of 40 thousand for 20 dollars. A car pulled up from somewhere, they gave me a wad of money, and I could go back to the hotel.

Hotel "Al-Khosilot" is a real museum of the USSR. If it cost 200 euros per night, it would be just like in Africa. On the floor, the eccentric attendant was playing solitaire. She gave me bed linen and shampoo, and as she left the door she said in such a flirting tone, “Would you like me to show you something?” I already thought that I had checked into the most natural brothel, but it turned out that it was just a lizard crawling in the corner of the corridor.

Cheerful morning view from the window.

Here I am a millionaire. An idiot's dreams come true!

Taxi drivers did not agree to take me to the market for the 4,000 soums that I had, although they write on the Internet that in the city they usually pay 2-3 thousand. I can’t imagine how you can bargain in such a situation. Bus travel costs 800 sum. The schedule style is familiar.

Buses in Tashkent are quite decent.

There is also a metro, but riding it is unpleasant. At the entrance they check passports (including those of locals, although Uzbek passports are not opened) and inspect bags. Inside the carriages are like in Moscow, where instead of advertising there are posters “Vigilance is the need of the times.” In Tashkent, the regime has tightened the screws to the maximum. There are more police on the streets than people. Documents are checked constantly. In the city center there is a dead zone, empty streets.

Bukhara

Several streets with well-restored madrassas, hotels, shops, and souvenirs have been made for tourists. For everyone else, there are slums all around. Here they are visible from above.
