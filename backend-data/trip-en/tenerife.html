Spain: Tenerife, Barcelona

1-7 November 2018

Puerto de la Cruz

On the island of Tenerife, I was based in the town of Puerto de la Cruz on the west coast - it was the most convenient place. There is a lot of accommodation there, since this is a tourist place, it is close to the airport, to the capital of the island, and there is a direct bus to the Teide volcano. Most tourists live on the south coast, where there are large hotels and sandy beaches, but I found it inconvenient - far from everything.

La Laguna is not on the coast, but slightly inland.

Teide

The juiciest place in Tenerife is the Teide volcano - the highest mountain in Spain. Teide is located in the middle of the island, on a high plateau. There is a public bus there once a day in the morning there and back in the evening, from Puerto de la Cruz, and from the south coast. The cable car takes you almost to the very top at a height of 3555 meters. You can’t just go higher; you have to get permission many months in advance.

Teide and the central part of Tenerife in general are almost always above the level of the clouds; at some point on the serpentine road you leave the cloud, and it remains below.

All that is behind the rocks are clouds; the sea is almost invisible.

Teide.

Cable car. We had to wait another hour and a half for our turn to go up.

There are no settlements in the highlands, only this parking lot near the cable car.

We quickly go up.

It's cold at the top and there's even some snow.

The path is laid among piles of volcanic stones.

Top of Teide.

View of the clouds from the top.

My plan was to go down on foot - to see more than just the top (there are not many paths there). Going down is, of course, easier than going up, but it still wasn’t that easy.

The fact is that the road is not a staircase, as in China or Nepal, but a path along stones, which means you need to always watch where you are stepping, carefully step between different stones.

For those who climb on foot, there is a shelter on the slope where you can also spend the night.

Towards the end, thorns appear on the slope, the road is now smoother.

The final, longest part of the route in terms of mileage (but taking up 20-30% of the actual time) goes almost flat, with a slight slope.

There are black stones lying along the road.

These are stones that were thrown out during a volcanic eruption (the last eruption was more than 100 years ago).

I still didn’t catch the return bus, so we hitchhiked back.
