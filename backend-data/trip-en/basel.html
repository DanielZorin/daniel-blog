Switzerland: Basel, Laufenburg

19 January 2018

Basel

For the second time in a row I am visiting Switzerland for one day without an overnight stay. The city of Basel is located right on the triple border with France and Germany; from Germany you can get here using German travel cards without additional payment. By the way, catch this life hack - there are many trains that travel from Germany deep into Switzerland: to Bern or even Geneva. If you buy a ticket in advance on the website, then for a specific train on a specific date you can usually get a promotional ticket for 20 euros, from Germany to, say, Bern ( 2 hours of travel). And then you can just board in Basel and go to Bern, which will be cheaper than just a Basel-Bern ticket, because according to Swiss tariffs such a trip will cost 50 euros.

As we know, Switzerland is an expensive country. Let's take a look at the prices at a local supermarket. This is a Coop store, not high-end but not the cheapest, in the center of Basel. Prices are quoted in Swiss francs, which are almost equal to US dollars. Apples, for example, are $4 per kilo - in the USA, it seems, it’s still a little more expensive.

Bottles of water from 0.5 to 1 franc - also not expensive, even in India a bottle of water costs $0.33.

Juices for 2-3 francs.

A liter of milk costs 1.5 francs.

Locally produced eggs are very expensive - 2.5 francs for 6 pieces or 4 for a dozen.

Yogurts are inexpensive - a large glass is less than a franc.

Cold cups of coffee are expensive - 2-3 francs each (usually in Europe they are from 0.7 to 1.5 euros).

All fruits are expensive. Oranges 4 francs per kilo and a half.

The cheapest bananas are 2.60.

Potatoes 3.40.

Broccoli 3.95, eggplant 4.60.

The cheapest noname brie cheese is 2 francs, in Germany it would be 1 euro.

More cheeses. Parmesan costs about 2 francs per 80 grams, local Gruyère costs 5 francs per 150 grams.

Mozzarella 3 francs. Bagels too 3.

Barilla pasta for 2 francs - normal price.

Seasonings are 1.70, dried mushrooms are about 4, ketchup is 3.70.

Sausages for 3, 3.50, there is even 750 grams for only 4.90.

Meat packs for 6-8 francs.

There is bread for 2 francs per kilo, and there is also Bio for 3.60 per 400 grams.

Ready-made food is sooooo expensive - 2.50 per 100 grams, that is, as much as 25 per kilo. It's usually cheaper even in a restaurant.

Semi-finished products for 5-8 francs.

Coca-Cola bottle 1.95.

Sushi is incredibly expensive - one box costs 12-14 francs.

Even rice triangles - onigiri here cost 3.80 francs! In Japan they usually cost 100 yen tax, some are 110-120, that is, no more than 1 franc.

Swiss chocolates from 4.50 to infinity.

In general, it is a very expensive country, but you can survive, some products, such as pasta and yoghurt, are reasonably priced.

Laufenburg

An hour's drive from Basel is the town of Laufenburg. It is interesting because it is divided by a river and the state border into two parts - German and Swiss.

Since I had an unlimited travel pass for Baden-Württemburg, I traveled to Laufenburg along the Rhine River line in Germany. The Swiss line from Basel runs literally parallel to it. The German station is located on a hill.

View of the river and the Swiss side. It is also unusual that the cities on both sides of the border have the same name, although the border here has existed for many centuries.
