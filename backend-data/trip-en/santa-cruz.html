Bolivia: Santa Cruz, Sucre, Potosi, Uyuni, Oruro

27 March - 5 April 2021

Santa Cruz de la Sierra

Bolivia, my first country after coronavirus! Usually all tourists come here by land from Peru or Chile, but since the land borders were closed, I arrived by plane, which is clearly rare for this country - there are few flights to Bolivia, and all of them are extremely expensive, except for the flight from Sao Paulo for ~$200. Interestingly, international flights do not fly to the capital La Paz, but to the second largest and richest city of Santa Cruz. Santa Cruz is located in the east of the country on a lowland that turns into the Amazon jungle and Pantanal swamps further towards the border with Brazil. This city has grown dramatically over the past 30 years; before that it was a regional center on the edge of the country. There are not many historical monuments in Santa Cruz, but it is the only city in Bolivia where there is a Starbucks, and not even one.

Sucre

I was sure that the capital of Bolivia is La Paz, but it turns out that the official capital is Sucre. This is written in the constitution, although in fact the government and the president are in La Paz (and this is, normally, the definition of the capital), and only the constitutional court was left in Sucre.

Panorama of the center of Sucre.

A crowd of people walked towards the center in the morning; at first I thought it was some kind of religious procession.

But when they started singing “El pueblo unido jamas sera vencido,” it became clear that this was a protest.

In 2019, a revolution occurred in Bolivia; socialist President Evo Morales, who tried to rig the elections, fled the country. The appointment of new elections dragged on, then was postponed due to the coronavirus; as a result, in the fall of 2020, a supporter of Morales won the new elections, and the exile triumphantly returned to his homeland.

Historically, Sucre was a wealthy city, home to an elite who owned mines in nearby Potosi. The altitude of Sucre is only 2800 meters, much lower than most Bolivian cities, so it is more comfortable here.

The white historical center is listed as a World Heritage Site.

Potosi

The city of Potosi (Villa Real de Potosi), like Sucre, is listed as a World Heritage Site. This is a silver mining center, which makes it similar to the Mexican city Guanajuato.

Potosi is located on the side of a mountain at altitudes from 3900 to 4100 meters, and after arriving there I was overcome by mountain sickness - breathing problems due to low air density, leading to a lack of oxygen in the blood. I have never had this disease before, because the maximum altitude where I went was about 3200 meters (in Nepal and in China). A couple of times I was around 3500 (Elbrus and Teide), but for a very short time. The first day in Potosí I had a severe headache and was out of breath from climbing one floor of stairs. Coca leaves and Sorojchi Pills, which are sold in pharmacies for 5 bolivianos, helped

The center of Potosi consists of two-story buildings with wooden balconies. It really looks like the Canary Islands or some cities in Mexico.

Uyuni

Uyuni is a small tourist town where people come to see the salt flat of the same name. This city seemed very interesting and atmospheric to me, a kind of stronghold of civilization in the middle of the desert, far from everything, with dusty roads and strange monuments. It reminded me of small towns in the western part of the USA, only it is not half-abandoned and dead, like its American counterparts, but very lively.

The city is really very dusty, probably because it is desert and dry, and many of the roads are dirt.

Initially, Uyuni appeared as a railway junction - trains ran from here towards Argentina and Chile and exported minerals. The station is in the city center.

Now there are passenger trains from here north towards Oruro, but not every day. There are many statues along the first street from the station.

Salar de Uyuni

The main attraction of Bolivia, for which people usually come here, is the Uyuni salt flat. Many people combine it with the Atacama Desert in Chile, but in 2021 this route was impossible due to closed borders. In general, there were few tourists, I was still on a day off, so there were Bolivians from other cities, of whom our entire group actually consisted. An excursion to the Uyuni salt flat costs 200 bolivianos (about $30), tourists are taken there in jeeps, since you actually need to drive along the salt flat, and not on a road.

The village closest to the salt flat is called Colchani. There is a regular road leading to it, along which you can meet llamas.

Oruro

In every country I try to visit a large and non-tourist city. In Bolivia, the largest such cities are Oruro and Cochabamba. Oruro is right on the way from Uyuni to La Paz.

On the way from Uyuni to Oruro, the bus stopped at the exit from the town of Challapata.

Further the road was blocked by an empty bus and police. I asked what happened, the police told me that the road was blocked because of the university, and they expected release at 6 o’clock (at that moment it was about 3). I thought I had misunderstood something, maybe in Spanish “rockfall” or “mudflow” sounds similar to “university”.

After waiting a little, I got into a taxi, whose driver promised to avoid the traffic jam on country roads. In fact, he drove around the police post along the dirt road, and then pulled out onto the main road, along which stones were chaotically scattered (but it didn’t look like a landslide, because the road went through a plain).

After a few kilometers we arrived at a blocked bridge, and it turned out that it was blocked by two cars with flags. It really turned out to be a protest because the university had some dispute with the government! In Russia or in the USA, the police would disperse such a protest in 5 minutes, and the participants would be jailed under criminal charges, but in Bolivia, it seems there is not even a fine for doing this, the police themselves protect them.

Oruro is the fourth largest city in Bolivia (fifth if you count La Paz and El Alto separately). A huge new bus station was built in the outskirts, but there are few normal hotels there, so I settled near the old station, where you can still catch a minibus to La Paz.
