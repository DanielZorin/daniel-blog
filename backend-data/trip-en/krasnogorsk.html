Russia: Krasnogorsk, Istra, Serpukhov, Podolsk, Mytishchi, Noginsk

8 August 2017 - 23 October 2018

Krasnogorsk

I lived in Krasnogorsk for more than a year, from August 2017 to October 2018. Although at first glance it is a residential suburb of Moscow, starting immediately when crossing the Moscow Ring Road in the northwestern direction, I perceive Krasnogorsk as a separate city. While living there, I noticed that I began to say “to the city” to mean “to the center of Moscow.” Now there are MCD trains running to Krasnogorsk, but back then they were ordinary trains with a treacherous break from about 11 to 13, the ride from Krasnogorskaya station to Tushino station was 12 minutes and the ticket cost 22 rubles. Noginsk

Estate of Savva Morozov. In the 19th century, the city of Bogorodsk was the most populated and industrially developed in the Moscow province due to the large number of enterprises.

Most of them are located a short distance from the center or on the other side of the river. Many industrial buildings have survived to this day.

Now some of them have shops or shopping centers, others continue to operate.

Industrial landscape of the 19th century.

Monument to the fighter.

Here, next to a nondescript, half-abandoned furniture store from the 90s, stands the most important monument of Noginsk.

What's important about it? It’s just that this is the very first monument to Lenin. Opened in January 1924, coincidentally the same day Lenin died.

More precisely, the first of those that have survived to this day. There is information that busts of Lenin were placed in public places before 1924, but they were short-lived. The first monument to Lenin in St. Petersburg was erected in 1926.

Not far from the monument is the Zakharovo station - the final and dead-end station on the branch leading to Noginsk.
