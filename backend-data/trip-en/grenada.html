Grenada

29-30 April 2022

On the new trip to the Caribbean, I started from Grenada and then moved along the chain of islands to the north, visiting all the countries that I had skipped on the first trip. Grenada was somewhat unique here, as it is surrounded on three sides by the countries that I visited: Trinidad and Tobago in the south, Saint Vincent and the Grenadines in the north, Barbados in the east. Nevertheless, I found a convenient direct flight from Grenada to St. Lucia, and overall the puzzle came together. I was a little confused about how they would react to a passport from the evil country (especially since when transiting through the USA at the border they questioned me for a very long time and in detail), but as it turned out, in Grenada, and on all the other Caribbean islands, there was no tension in this regard. Grenada was also the first Caribbean country to lift all coronavirus entry restrictions.

On the way from New York I took a couple of photos of the British Virgin Islands.

Grenada airport is exceptional: there are no ATMs or money changers at all. But in theory, the minibus should accept cash US dollars. The owner of my AirBnB kindly met me in a car.

First of all, I went to St. George's, the capital of Grenada. It turned out to be a really big city by Caribbean standards, very picturesquely located on the hills. The main cathedral is on the hill.

A memorial plaque stating that Maurice Bishop and his associates were shot here in 1983. In 1979, Bishop overthrew the previous right-wing dictator Gary and changed Grenada's political course to cooperation with Cuba and the USSR, which caused discontent among neighboring countries and, of course, the United States. At the same time, Bishop was not yet very radical and tried to somehow come to an agreement with the Americans, and in October 1983 he was overthrown and shot by representatives of the more radical wing of the Communist Party, after which the United States sent troops to Grenada and occupied it

In Grenada, I rented a small studio near the airport (the capital was 10 kilometers away). It was in a small village on the slopes of a hill. In general, all of Grenada outside the capital consists of approximately such villages on the slopes, since the entire island is so hilly.

The main street.

The decor is a bit retro, it was pretty much the same on other islands.

I covered the entire distance between the airport and the capital on foot.

On the southern coast of the island behind the airport there is a local university campus.

The campus is large and similar to American universities; I have not seen such large universities on other small Caribbean islands.

Panorama.

The seashore near the university.

The university has several more buildings on the other side in the village of Grand Anse, and branded buses go there. I also rode on it, I thought it was an ordinary bus, but it turned out it was free, but in theory only for students, luckily I was not discovered. By the way, there were quite a few foreign students on campus and on the bus, which is also atypical for Caribbean countries (usually I was the only white person on the street).

Grand Anse is also the most popular beach in Grenada, with several large hotels nearby.

Yachts are based in the neighboring bay.

Advertising along the road.

Bus stop and advertising of the operator Digicel, well known throughout the islands of Oceania. In the Caribbean it is also available in all countries.

The street is named after Kirani James, the only Olympic champion from Grenada who won the 400m at the 2012 Games.

Distances in Grenada are indicated in miles, just like in the UK. It is clear that many cities have French names, although France fought with England for the Caribbean islands already in the 18th century, since the 19th century the island was British.

The largest shopping center I found in Grenada.

There is a food court with two regular joints and one Chinese. A meal costs approximately 5-10 dollars.

There is also a large supermarket here. In all the Caribbean countries, I was shocked by the prices for fruits and vegetables, they are simply astronomical. Watermelon is almost three US dollars per kilogram (here, as in America, they weigh it in pounds).

Potatoes - two dollars per kilogram (1 USD = 2.7 local dollars).

I’m even scared to divide the prices for strawberries by 2.7.

But rice costs okay, 1.50 dollars per kilo.

Steak - about 30 dollars per kilo.

The more elite one is twice as expensive.
