Kanchanaburi

Everyone goes to Kanchanaburi to see the "Bridge on the River Kwai". In 1942, the Japanese attacked Myanmar (Burma) to block the only road through the mountains that the Allies could use to supply China. To supply the occupying army in the region, the Japanese had to transport everything along a long sea route around southern Thailand and Malaysia, risking being hit by British submarines along the way. Therefore, it was decided to build a railway from Bangkok to Mawlamyine, which was a very difficult task, since it was necessary to overcome vast mountainous terrain. Construction was carried out not only by Thai and Malay voluntary-forced workers, but also by English and Australian prisoners of war, which explains the increased interest of modern tourists from these countries in this episode of history. The road was built in a year at the cost of many thousands of lives, but it stood only until 1947, and the bridge was bombed back in 1945. In 1947 it was closed as unnecessary, in 1957 the section in Thailand was brought into working order, and it is still working. The River Kwai Bridge in its current form was built after the war, and the river it crosses was renamed (Khwayai in Thai) under the influence of the book and film, while the real River Kwai (Khwanoi) is further down the road. So it goes.
