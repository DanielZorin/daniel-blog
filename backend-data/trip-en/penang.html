Perhentian Islands

Beach holiday. What could be worse? A narrow strip of sand (and in worse cases, stones), where people walk over each other’s heads, muddy salt water, and prices that are significantly inflated. From cheap Crimea to the “elite” Côte d'Azur, you can spend more than 10 minutes on the beach either out of despair, or if you have never seen the sea in your life. Having spent months traveling around Southeast Asia, I mostly either avoided the resorts or took the best of them - a good selection of restaurants and entertainment, while living a kilometer from the sea and swimming in the pool. I didn’t know that it could be different: something beautiful, quiet, where you’d want to go into the sea, and not for $500 a day, like at Maldives.

The Perhentian Islands are located off the east coast of Malaysia. There are two of them - Small (Kecil) and Big (Besar) Perhentian. I was only at the Small. There is more infrastructure and lower prices, on Besar one a significant part of the territory has not yet been developed, the hotels are of a higher class. On Kecil there is also the only village with a permanent population. There are no cars or motorcycles in the Perhentians; you can only move around on foot along jungle paths or by taxi boats. There are numerous boats from the mainland for 70 ringit ($17) round trip. Tourists mainly land in two places - Coral Bay and White Beach, which are located on the west and east coasts, respectively; between them you can walk in about 15 minutes through the hill in the center of the island. I stayed in a hotel in the middle between these beaches for 60 ringit ($15) per day, without air conditioning. Electricity is understandably in short supply on the islands, so rooms with air conditioning start at around $30.

Both main beaches are built up with hotels and restaurants and are only suitable as a place to eat and hang out. This is Coral Bay.

* * *

The most heavenly and deserted beaches on Little Perhentian are located on the northern half of the island.

Firstly, north of Coral Bay, behind a couple of large hotels, there is a chain of beaches where there are very few people, no boats and coral reefs. If you don’t want to walk far or take a taxi, then this is the closest option to the hotel zone.

The farthest beach in this part of the island can only be reached by swimming; I couldn’t find a path. And then there is a dead end and rocks. To get to the northern end of the island, you need to find a path up the mountain at the back of White Beach and cross the hills in the center of the island.

There is a telecommunications tower and a platform with solar panels at the top. The Internet on the island is bad. My hotel only had Wi-Fi near the entrance, and it was incredibly slow. My U Mobile SIM card had no network connection at all in most of the island. To get the Internet in the evening I went to White Beach, where I could get 3G. And, of course, there was connection at the foot of the tower on the hill.

View from above of the rocky northeastern coast.

When you go down the other side of the hill, you find yourself in the most heavenly and undeveloped part of the island. There is only one hotel on one beach here, and even the jungle path to the next beach is sometimes difficult to find.

This beach is called Turtle Beach, but I was not lucky enough to see a turtle underwater.

A little about food in the Perhentians. Since everything is imported by sea, prices are approximately twice as high as on the mainland. There are almost no shops, there are a couple of miserable stalls with noodles, but the choice there is very small. The main problem with fruits is that they hardly grow on the island, so they are very expensive. A dish in a restaurant costs about 10-12 ringit ($2.5-3) versus 5-6 on the mainland, but the choice is decent and the food is delicious. It is better to eat seafood.

There is a good place on White Beach with burgers for 6-9 ringit.

In the evening it is best to come to the shore of Coral Bay. The tide floods the legs of the tables.

Penang

Penang Island (Penang in English or Pinang in Malay) is one of the most interesting places in Malaysia. This is the only region where the Chinese are the majority and there is no de facto positive discrimination of Muslims. The capital, George Town, retains British colonial architecture, complemented in recent years by the addition of famous Penang street art. Penang is also the culinary capital of Malaysia.

All three main types of food in Malaysia are well represented in Penang: Chinese, Indian and Malay. This is where those Indian eateries under the general name "Nasi Kandar" come from.

Actually, nasi kandar with lamb:

The most iconic Chinese dish is Char Kway Teow. Fried rice noodles in soy sauce with shrimp.

And another signature dish is laksa. Fish soup based on curry and coconut milk, with a strange sour-spicy taste.

The desserts "cendol" and "kacang" based on grated ice also originate from Penang.

Opposite my hotel on Chulia Street, freshly squeezed juices were poured out in the evening for 2-3 ringit ($0.5-0.7). I liked the carambola juice most of all - better than the fruit in its raw form.

To keep tourists from getting bored, a lot of street art has been created in various nooks and crannies of Georgetown, and there is even a special map where the paintings on the walls are marked, although, as I found out, not all of them.

Kangar

Three main facts about the state of Perlis: 1. It is the only state whose ruler bears the title of “raja” and not “sultan”, 2. It is the smallest state in Malaysia 3. The flag of Perlis = an inverted flag of Ukraine.

The capital of Perlis state is called Kangar.

The smallest and most provincial capital of the region.

The streets are cramped and one-lane.

The main shopping center is stuck in the 90s.
