22-23 October, 5-11 November 2015, 14-18 April, 9-29 June 2016

Kuala Lumpur

If Bangkok starts from Khaosan Road, then Kuala Lumpur starts from Petaling Street.

This is a Muslim country, so there are no discos with beer and loud music right on the street, just a market and many hotels.

I regularly stayed on Petaling Road at the Sasana Hotel, right at the gate. 45 ringgit for a single room, 60 ringgit for a double room, with air conditioning and shower. I haven't found better value for money in the area.

In this hotel you can go out onto the roof, where bed linen is usually dried.

Petaling Street itself is covered with a canopy on top.

In the evening, red Chinese lanterns light up there.

Trade ends much earlier than on Khao San - around midnight the stalls are closed and taken away, the street is empty. Some restaurants, however, are open 24 hours a day, as are 7-11.

The prices at the market are quite normal, I often bought fruit there (mostly in the stalls on the parallel Sultan Street).

Cheap second hand.

The area is sort of Chinese, so there are a lot of very authentic Chinese eateries here. Prices are lower than in China - $1-2 per dish. The peculiarity of canteens is that there are several different food outlets on the same area with seats, and drinks are served centrally. Usually, to sit, you need to order some kind of drink, for example, excellent Malay tea Teh tarik for 1.5-2 ringgit ($0.3-0.5).

The food is just like in China, amazing.

In addition to the Chinese, there are also Indians here (although in KL there is a special Indian quarter near the central station). You can eat here around the clock, either roti pancakes or chicken curry, washed down with the same tarik tea.

* * *

Kuala Lumpur is 100 years younger than Bangkok; this city was founded in the second half of the 19th century to develop tin mining in this region. The British built a number of ceremonial buildings here, most of which are concentrated on Freedom Square.

The clock tower does not stand alone, but is built into the ensemble.

Jamek Mosque.

You can also add Kuala Lumpur Central Station here (on the diagram it is called Kuala Lumpur).

Now it is used as a regular station where local trains and most long-distance trains stop. The main station is called KL Sentral.

Opposite the station is a gigantic railway office, which does not really correspond to the scale of the Malaysian network, consisting of two lines, on which trains run quite rarely - 2-3 times a day, except for the KL - Ipoh route.

Kuala Lumpur is built at the confluence of the Klang and Gombak rivers, but it’s hard to guess because both rivers flow in the backyards, and along them there are overpasses and metro lines built, instead of embankments with benches.

Only rare graffiti artists wander here.

Square in the old town.

Many other cities in Malaysia, founded at the same time, look similar - Ipoh, Seremban, Kuala Selangor.

Historically, Malaysia has three large communities. In addition to the Malays (and a very small number of indigenous people), there are the Chinese, who historically did business in this region on the east-west sea route, and Indians who came from another British colony, India.

Therefore, here you can see mosques, pagodas and Indian temples.

In Malaysia, there is legislatively enshrined positive discrimination of ethnic Malays - there are quotas for them in business, education, and management; Malays also receive a 7% discount when purchasing real estate. This policy was introduced to redistribute wealth from the Chinese, who predominantly lived in large cities and ran all serious business. The occasion was the massacre between Malays and Chinese in Kuala Lumpur in May 1969.

It is impossible to become a “Malay” (officially this is called the word “bumiputra” = Malays indigenous people of Borneo), the privilege is given at birth from Malay parents. Also, Malaysia has different laws for Muslims and non-Muslims. Every person has their religion written in their passport. To obtain privileges for Malays, in addition to having the right parents, it is imperative to profess Islam.

Monument to the heroes of the struggle for Malaysian independence.

It has a good view of the area around the station.

In addition to the colonial buildings in the center, Kuala Lumpur has another place founded in the 19th century - the Hindu temple in Batu Caves on the northern outskirts of the city.

This is a fairly popular and easily accessible place - located at the final train stop.

The temple was built in a karst cave, which is often found in Southeast Asia.

The climb up is easy.

The Petronas Towers can be seen from here too.

The most recognizable place in Kuala Lumpur is, of course, the Petronas Towers, the tallest building in the world from 1998 to 2004 (before the construction of Taipei 101). The area around is called KLCC = Kuala Lumpur City Center.

On my first visit to Kuala Lumpur there was terrible smog, the towers were blurry.

Subsequently, I walked near the towers many times - there are free bus stops and shopping centers nearby.

You can go upstairs for 20 dollars. Probably the best solution would be to go to the observation deck in the TV tower for $10 - from there the Petronas Towers will be visible. I haven’t gotten around to it in a month, I’ll have to go next time.

Inside there is a large shopping center, as well as the Petrosains science museum, and in the annex there is an aquarium, which is worthy of a separate discussion.

Park in front of the towers.

The new area looks nice and progressive.

You can walk along covered walkways from the Petronas Towers to the largest shopping center - Pavillion.

There are three free bus lines, including one to Petaling Road. In the Pavilion itself, shopping is at the highest level: Dolce & Gabbana, Michael Kors, Louboutins. On the ground floor there is a supermarket, it is more expensive there, but you can buy jamon, cheese, and even pork.

Just for shopping, I usually went to the supermarket of the main Malaysian chain Giant, near the Bukit Bintang monorail station. By the way, there in the evening you can buy packs of sushi on sale for 9 ringgit ($2.2) per box of a dozen pieces - more expensive than in Thailand, but still inexpensive.

Malay fashion. For girls, the main disadvantage is that there are no outerwear without sleeves; walking on the street in Asia will be quite hot. In general, there is a good selection there, and the prices are very attractive. Cheaper than Indonesia.

I've never been to an aquarium before. Until recently, there was no aquarium in Moscow, but in other cities I missed it too. In Southeast Asia, there are large aquariums in Bangkok, Kuala Lumpur and Singapore. There is no clear opinion about which one is better. I visited the Malaysian one.

The aquarium is located in a building adjacent to the Petronas Towers. I walked through it many times on the way somewhere, and often there was a long line even at the entrance, but on day X there were very few people, no one bothered each other. Entrance costs 60 ringgit ($15).

At the entrance you can pick up a brochure that shows feeding times for different fish. Most fish are fed twice a day, morning and evening, so in half a day you can see them all in action. I only missed the octopus, which spent the entire afternoon sleeping, huddled in a corner. There is an aquarium with piranhas right at the entrance.

* * *

Petaling Jaya is the closest suburb of Kuala Lumpur from the west. It's easy to get there using the pink metro line. This city was originally founded as a suburb to provide people with living space, so there is no center here, instead there are "sections" from 1 to 7 with a street grid.

Only the park around the lake near Taman Jaya station can claim to be the center.

When you take the metro, it feels like little China.

And you come somewhere to Taman Bahagia, and you see almost a little America there.

Only the houses are close together. America will really be in the next suburb of Subang Jaya, which is considered more expensive and elite.

Unlike real America, there are shops on large streets, and not one mall for 10 kilometers.

But few people really walk here.

And the streets are called numbers.

Besides idle curiosity, what brought me to Petaling Jaya was the desire to taste durians. In the SS2 area there are two main points of trade for these divine fruits - Donald's Durian and Durian SS2. They are located literally a five-minute walk from each other, the nearest metro station is a 30-minute walk, but there are free buses around Petaling Jaya, just like in Kuala Lumpur.

Donalds is a store, and SS2 is a street stall like a watermelon one. Both offer a buffet - for 25-30 ringgits per person you can eat as many durians as you like. Considering that the cheapest cost 12 ringgit per kilogram including peel, this is a very good deal.

Of course, you can’t eat the most elite varieties like Musang King, which costs 50 ringgit per kilo, at the buffet.

But regular durians are also good. Both establishments have plenty of different varieties.

Different varieties of durian differ quite greatly in taste. To become a connoisseur, you need to taste them for a long time in different seasons.

Tip - bring a bottle of water with you to refresh yourself after each durian.

It was experimentally determined that I can eat four durians at a time, and Alena can only eat two.

On the outskirts of Kuala Lumpur there is a place that is the complete opposite of the dense metropolis: Crab Island (Pulau Ketam). From the center you can get there in about an hour and a half: first by KTM commuter train to the final station of Port Klang, from there by ferry for 7 ringgit. And there is a fishing village on stilts.

The ferry, by the way, is very cool for such an unpretentious place - like the Comet. There it is parked in the distance.

The capital of the state of Selangor is the city of Shah Alam, and the residence of the Sultan is in the city of Klang (a common situation in other states). Kuala Selangor

Kuala Selangor can be called a suburb of Kuala Lumpur with a stretch - after all, it takes two hours to get there, albeit by direct commuter bus from the very center. There is no pretense here, like in Klang and Shah Alam. This is a real province.

After checking into the Zande Motel for 40 ringgit, I explored the city. The city is one square with these rows of houses, and several more streets around. There is another hotel on the main square, cheaper, but with worse rooms. There were, however, no staff there - it was as if everyone had died out.

There are also blocks around the bus station.

Путраджая

The city of Putrajaya is the artificially created new capital of Malaysia (more precisely, the seat of government). It has the main disadvantage of all such cities - the disproportion between the scale of architecture and the number of people. Putrajaya is good for taking pictures from a helicopter, since the central streets are quite empty.

I also chose the wrong time for the trip - a day off. Expensive trains to the airport stop in Putrajaya, but it is much cheaper to take the RM5 bus from Mydin Supermarket.

Midday. The heat is 40. All residents left, some to work, some to relax in Kuala Lumpur. There are almost no cars.

On weekends, buses rarely ran through the city. It’s not close to the center from the bus station, about two kilometers. During all this time I did not see a single store.
