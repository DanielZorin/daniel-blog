New Zealand: Christchurch, Mount Cook, Queenstown, Milford Sound, Dunedin, Oamaru

24-31 March 2019

Christchurch

New Zealand is the last major piece of land to be inhabited by people just 700 years ago. Unlike Australia, where prisoners were exiled, New Zealand was colonized in the 19th century by free people, and this was accompanied by a fierce struggle with the indigenous Maori inhabitants for territory. Colonization began from the South Island, since it was not as crowded as the North Island, so the oldest cities are in the south. Christchurch is currently the largest city in the South Island. In recent years, this city has been in the news twice due to sad events: first it was destroyed by an earthquake in 2011, and in 2019 there was a terrorist attack on the mosque, exactly a week before my arrival. Mount Cook

My original plan was to travel from Christchurch to Queenstown via the west coast. However, the weather ruined this plan, as both the railway across the island and the route along the west coast were closed due to the storm. I had to urgently change plans to get to Queenstown by a shorter route through Mount Cook. The journey took the whole day, but along the way I still saw a lot of characteristic New Zealand landscapes.

A chain of mountains (the Southern Alps) stretches along the western coast, at the tops of them there are numerous glaciers, which, when melting, create rivers that cross the island from west to east. In autumn they are widest and fullest.

Queenstown

Queenstown is the most touristic city in New Zealand. There are only 15 thousand people here, but almost 3 million tourists a year. If we take into account that the majority come in the summer, and not for 1 day, we can estimate that at any given time in the summer there are several times more tourists than local residents. Therefore, in fact, airbnb does not work well here, and hotels are more expensive than in other cities.

The city mainly consists of hotels, cafes and souvenir shops.

There is a clock tower at the central intersection.

What to do in Queenstown? Well, firstly, from there you can go to the fjords, which will be discussed separately. Secondly, there are many options for practicing all kinds of extreme sports. Interestingly, bungee jumping was invented in Queenstown, the first bungee was on a river 20 kilometers from the city. The prices seem inhumane, although I don't know how much it costs in other countries, perhaps just as expensive. This kind of entertainment is not for me.

Those who hate rides and jumping as much as I do can take a relaxing hike around the area. There are a lot of places around here, there are even shuttles to neighboring towns at reasonable prices (a little more expensive than buses). I chose the easiest option and walked through the immediate surroundings of Queenstown. It's funny that the first time I saw the fjord was in New Zealand, and not in nearby Norway. There are also similar fjords in southern Chile and Alaska, but New Zealand and Norway are the most accessible and closest to civilization. Judging by the map, besides Milford Sound, there are many other fjords in the same region, but no one goes there, and there are no roads there, at most there are walking paths, and even that not everywhere. I wonder why this happened?

A cruise along the fjord costs 45-50 New Zealand dollars (~35 US dollars) and lasts an hour and a half.

The scale of this place is easy to appreciate by comparing the size of the rocks with the size of the ship.

Since there had just been heavy rains recently, there were quite a few waterfalls along the banks of the fjord.

Some are very powerful.

Inside the fjord the water is calm, but as soon as the ship reached the end and the open sea appeared, it began to swell very strongly, fortunately this lasted about 10 minutes.

You can spot fur seals on the rocks.

Dunedin

Dunedin is New Zealand's oldest colonial city, and one of its most beautiful. It was founded by settlers from Scotland and named after Edinburgh (in Scottish it is called Dunedin). Who would have thought that the stress falls on the second syllable, it was very difficult to get used to it, especially since stressed E is rarely pronounced as [i]. For a long time Dunedin was the largest city in the country, now it is in sixth place, but is in the top four in terms of importance due to its large university and cultural heritage.

The symbol of the city is the railway station building, the appearance of which is a reference to Flemish and Dutch architecture.

Oamaru

The small town of Oamaru, an hour and a half north of Dunedin, is famous for its well-preserved Victorian architecture and steampunk festivals. Probably, it was precisely such a festival that Artemy Lebedev accidentally came to when he was in New Zealand in 2009 .

I didn't notice anyone wearing 19th century costumes, although you can actually buy them in souvenir shops. But it is a fact that the city looks amazing in retro style.

On the other side of the hill, yellow-eyed penguins live on the beach. Penguins wake up early in the morning and swim out to sea to hunt; in the evening they return to the shore to rest and sleep. In the evening you can notice them. Penguins are very shy, so access to the beach is closed several hours before sunset, and you can only watch from an observation deck high on the rocks.
