China: Xiamen, Nanchang, Jingdezhen, Huangshan, Nanjing

1-12 May 2015

Xiamen

I have already talked about Chinese cities that were partially built by Europeans: Dalian, Tianjin, Guangzhou, Hong Kong, Macau. Xiamen is also one of them. The city opened up after the First Opium War, mainly to the British. In terms of layout, this is perhaps the most European of all Chinese cities: here the historical and business centers are separated (unlike Tianjin).

Xiamen is very popular among Chinese tourists, but not so much among foreign tourists. It’s interesting that here on the streets there is navigation for tourists: signs, maps of the area, just like in Europe, but it’s not translated into English.

Map of the historical center.

Does anyone think that all Chinese cities are the same?

Typically, Chinese cities have a rectangular grid of streets (as in America), while the central (in ancient times, walled) part should be square, this is according to Feng Shui. Xiamen's layout is more reminiscent of Moscow. The relief is favorable - Xiamen is located on a round island, so the main streets run in arcs.

The tunnel itself was turned into a place for walking, all sorts of figures were placed.

There is no metro in Xiamen (probably, it is technically difficult to build one on an island?), instead they built a busway - high-speed buses. In most of the island the line runs along an overpass.

Just like in subways, there are turnstiles at the entrance to the station and payment is made using tokens. The price depends on the distance, and travel on the Xiamen metrobus is cheaper than most Chinese subways - starting from only 0.5 yuan, from the airport to the center (this is across the entire island with a transfer) it is 1.5 yuan.

In Xiamen I somehow had no luck with hotels - with great difficulty we managed to rent a single room the size of one in Hong Kong for 100 yuan. But we were lucky with the food - there was a Michelin-starred establishment nearby with abnormally low prices, worthy of a separate discussion.

The main attraction of Xiamen is Gulangyu Island. This is a small island located between Amoy Island and the mainland, directly opposite the historical center. In the 19th century, Europeans lived there in their own separate fenced little world. Here it is - so tempting and so inaccessible.

There is a ferry to Gulangyu from the pier near the center; it takes about five minutes to sail there. It is inexpensive - less than 10 yuan, but several years ago the Chinese decided that it would be nice to increase income from tourism, and now tickets for this ferry are sold only to the lucky owners of Xiamen registration. Everyone else is invited to make a big detour, driving 20 minutes north to the cruise terminal, and buy a ticket there for 35 yuan. Another interesting option is ferries from the mainland, which can be reached by bus across the bridge over the strait. They cost only a little less (30 yuan), but there was a bus there for 2 yuan right from my hotel, and a beautiful view from the bridge along the way. We were unlucky with the weather.

Nanchang

Another city I used for transit was Nanchang, the capital of Jiangxi Province. In some places here it seems that you are walking around Novosibirsk or Minsk.

On the main square there is a memorial to the Nanchang Uprising on August 1, 1927 - the first armed clash between communists and nationalists. August 1 is considered the day the Chinese Army was founded (officially called the People's Liberation Army).

A guy was break dancing near the monument.

Jingdezhen

In the city with the unpronounceable name Jingdezhen, I had a transfer from one train to another and three hours to walk around the city.

To my shame, I didn’t know anything about this city, but Jingdezhen is not some provincial regional center in Jiangxi Province. In former times, it was the main center for the production of porcelain, the most valuable Chinese export during the imperial era. European traders in the 17th and 18th centuries specifically came to Jingdezhen to make purchases. By the way, in English porcelain is sometimes simply called “china”.

Now China exports other things, but Jingdezhen remains a porcelain city, where vases are sold in the markets, and even the pillars are decorated accordingly.

And even trash cans.

Huangshan

Huangshan (Yellow Mountains) is one of the most popular landscapes in China. Located in the south of Anhui Province. Logistics here are not easy. First you need to get to the city of Huangshan (formerly called Tunxi), there is a railway and an airport, direct trains to Shanghai and Nanjing. From the train station you need to take a bus to the village of Tankou, which takes about an hour. From the Tankou bus station there are shuttles directly to the entrance to the mountain, from where you need to go up by cable car or on foot. From Nanchang via Jingdezhen you can only get to Huangshan in the evening, when buses to Tankou no longer go, so I had to spend the night in the city and get up early in the morning.

In Tankou we quickly checked into the first hotel we came across for 80 yuan and went upstairs.

We decided to walk to the entrance to see if there was a hole in the fence somewhere to bypass the ticket office.

Entrance to the park.

Rare monument to Deng Xiaoping. He went up in 1979.

There is a bus road and stairs up to the entrance. There are actually two entrances: eastern and western (there is also a northern one, but you can’t get there from Tankou). At the same time, the road first comes almost close to the western one, and then turns to the eastern one, where it’s another four kilometers to go.

There is no way around the western entrance; there are steep slopes or fences everywhere. It was already well past noon, so we decided not to waste it in search of adventure. A ticket to Huangshan is very expensive - 240 yuan ($40), but they very easily gave us a “student” discount of 50%, and 120 yuan no longer seems like a lot. The funicular (about another 100 yuan) on the eastern slope is closed for repairs, so you can only go up on foot.

The walk up is 2.5 kilometers, that is, approximately like the upper section of the climb to Taishan. This time we decided to follow the example of the Chinese and use crutches bought in the city for only three yuan. When you walk, the difference is not very noticeable, but in total it is huge: the climb to Huangshan did not seem particularly difficult to me, the next day my legs did not even hurt.

The mountains are, of course, beautiful.

No one went up on foot, everyone only walked down. Apparently, the Chinese are aware of the repair of the funicular at the western entrance, so they come to the eastern one, go up, follow the route along the peaks and go down to the western exit. I walked the same route in the opposite direction.

In addition to the main route from the eastern entrance to the western one, you can also walk to the northern entrance (quite far), and there are a couple of side treks, such as the climb to this peak.

We got to the top relatively late due to the long road, so there was no time for side routes.

There are very few tourists! Apparently, again, all the horror story tellers about crowds come here right around the New Year holidays. The weather was perfect (but it rained the whole next day).

There were no adventures at the entrance, but we had a lot of them on the way out. It turned out that Huangshan is not a 24-hour park at all. There are hotels at the top, but the entrance from below is closed in the evening. There is no lighting on the stairs. There was no lighting on Taishan either, but there were many people with lanterns walking in both directions, but here there was not a single soul and dead silence. Fortunately, the charge of the gadgets was quite enough for the descent. A new obstacle awaited below: a closed gate. At the same time, there weren’t even any guards there who could let us out. I had to find a way to climb over the fence, albeit in the opposite direction. On the side of the checkpoint there was a wide fence on which you could stand. The height is 2.5 meters, that is, I would not have climbed it from the outside, but fortunately from the inside there is a low fence nearby that can be used as an intermediate platform. They loosened the ground under the fence in a very timely manner to a very soft state; jumping was not so scary. Of course, there was no taxi at the exit, only 10 kilometers on foot to Tankou itself along the road. Fortunately, about 30 minutes later a park employee passed by and gave us a ride to Tankou, so by about 10 pm we were already at the hotel. The next day the plan was to rest from the climb, plus move from Tankou back to the city of Huangshan, where there is a train station and you can buy tickets to Nanjing. It rained all day. The hotel in Huangshan for the standard 80 yuan ($13) was incredible. Huge room, soft beds and armchairs, the fastest internet I have ever seen in a hotel (faster than at my home in Moscow).

Also, the room was on the 17th floor, and there was a gorgeous view from the window.

Morning of a sunny day. The train to Nanjing is waiting at the station.

Nanjing

At one of the Nanjing metro stations, I noticed a billboard that showed luscious houses with tiled roofs. Quickly typing the text into the translator, I found out that the place is called “Venice City Residential Complex”, and it is located on the other side of the Yangtze River from the center of Nanjing. Moreover, one of the new metro stations was located in this area, so it was only two or three stops in a straight line from my hotel. Doesn't it seem like something interesting?

Actually, the advertisement appeared on the left of this frame.

Having traveled many thousands of kilometers across China, I somehow did not come across a single block copying European architecture, although, for example, there are several of these in Shanghai. Nanjing Venice is not known at all to the outside world, I could not find a single story about this place in English.

"Venice" is a fairly large area, built in the same style. If you look at the map, it will be labeled “first quarter of Venice”, “fifth quarter of Venice” and so on. It probably all started with one thing, and then it went from there. Most of the area is not particularly similar to Venice, ordinary modern houses with a small number of floors by Chinese standards.

Quite cozy and nicer than Mao Zedong-type blocks.

In the first quarter, the canals are dug, in the center there is a tower, like in the central square of Venice.

At first glance it looks good, but there is clearly not enough life in the area.

Here is the main pedestrian area. Do you see people here? I don't see them either. Of course, the weather is not great, but the cafe is also a bit empty. True, perhaps the neighborhood only comes alive in the evening, when people come home from work.

But in truly vibrant areas in China, nothing stands still during the day.

On the tower it is written "Water City Venice".

The bridge is two-level, for cars and trains, while on the shore the tracks diverge, and the railway goes into the distance.

The Yangtze Bridge is notable for two things. Firstly, this is the first large infrastructure facility that the Chinese built themselves after the severance of relations with the USSR. At the entrances to the bridge there are glorious sculptural compositions with workers and collective farmers.

There are red banners on the towers.

Secondly, this bridge is the most popular place in the world for suicide, more popular than the Golden Gate Bridge in San Francisco.

This is my village, this is my home. In this cozy area of Nanjing, where I managed to find a simple hotel for 80 yuan, I lingered a little.

It’s not difficult to get here - from the station you go west and southwest along the highway (there’s nowhere to turn anyway) to the intersection, there to the right, after the railway bridge there will be a small pedestrian bridge - that’s what you need. To the right of the exit from the hotel there was this stall where they prepared Peking duck for takeaway. It was weighed, but in fact each duck costs about 19-20 yuan ($3). This, I tell you, is heavenly pleasure. I would go to Nanjing specifically for this duck.

Chopped duck. It comes with sauce and onion.

And besides duck there are many good restaurants.

Here there are good noodles, large portions, very tasty, low prices - maximum 10 yuan. Three Michelin stars.

In the neighboring stalls you can buy whatever your heart desires. Even snakes.

What can I say about Nanjing? The name of the city means “Southern capital” (Beijing is northern capital), at different periods it was the main city of China, most recently under Chiang Kai-shek, until 1937, when, due to the Japanese occupation, the government moved to Chongqing, and in 1946-1949 during the civil war. When the Japanese captured the city, the Nanjing massacre occurred - an event for which the Chinese especially want to make the Japanese repent, and the Japanese pretend that nothing particularly beyond the scope of the war happened.

It is not clear where such bitterness came from that the Japanese soldiers slaughtered and shot everyone indiscriminately. The Chinese did nothing bad to the Japanese, and the military clashes that preceded the capture of Nanjing can hardly be called long and difficult for the Japanese army.

The museum is built on a Chinese scale, with many reconstructions and panoramas.

An article in a newspaper about a competition “who will be the first to kill 100 Chinese with a sword.”

Books of memory.

By the way, one of the very few places in China where you can see the flag of the Republic of China (now the flag of Taiwan).

The Japanese do not agree with the estimate of 30,000 victims.

The second place where you can see the Taiwanese flag in Nanjing is the memorial to Sun Yat-sen, the first president of China.

Probably the most famous landmark of Nanjing.

It is typical for this region to have many canals.
