14-17 November 2013

Toulouse

Let's see what a higher education institution in France looks like.

France has communism, so education is free. There are three universities in Toulouse, according to French tradition they are called Toulouse 1, Toulouse 2 and Toulouse 3, and there are also many higher educational institutions called “schools” (ecole). Toulouse-3 includes faculties of natural sciences, Toulouse-2 - most of the humanities, Toulouse-1 - legal and economic sciences.

This building houses the Toulouse School of Economics (TSE), a highly respected institute, one of the best in economics in the world. TSE administratively joined the University of Toulouse 1 in 2011. By the way, education there is in English, so fluency in French is not necessary, but the admission process is very strict, and a significant portion of students do not graduate.

TSE is located in a former tobacco factory building; there is even an inscription on the facade.

At the entrance to Moscow State University, you will be greeted by a redneck security officer who will closely examine your pass, so that God forbid the enemies of the Motherland do not find out the super-secret nanodevelopments of spiritual bonds at the faculty of who knows what. There are no checks when entering TSE; anyone can enter.

The main difference between the approach to teaching in France and in Russia is the following. In Russia, students are traditionally given such a volume of information that it is obviously impossible to study, and even less necessary. Often, exam papers give meaningless questions on encyclopedic knowledge that require memorizing textbooks rather than understanding the subject. The result of this is deplorable: cheating is considered the norm of life, and even teachers turn a blind eye to it, nothing is left in the head, and 99% of graduates don’t even really know the first-year materials (calculus, hello!). In France, cheating is considered a mortal sin; those found guilty are excommunicated from higher education for various periods, and the court record is posted on a special board of shame.

For the discovery of a telephone or crib sheets, they are excommunicated for six months, but if the culprit also managed to use these things for their intended purpose, then one and a half to two years. The sentences are highlighted with a felt-tip pen.

After studying, it’s not a sin to have some fun. Toulouse is considered a city of youth and students. They sell marijuana here (Arnaud Bernard Square). Muddy types in the distance near the entrances - this is it.

I would like to make special mention of social housing-commieblocks. The idea of such structures appeared in France (and was implemented in Russia). In the turbulent 20s and 30s, leftist architects talked about how great it would be if, instead of separate isolated houses, all people lived in a common house and walked together along the corridors. There were revolutionary ideas like to demolish the entire center of Paris and build it up with such blocks. The idea didn't work out.

Now Arab and African migrants live in such houses.

Road trip

When you hear the word “village” in Russia, you immediately imagine hell: dilapidated huts, broken roads, life in a heap of dirt and manure, garbage thrown right at your feet, the population is aggressive, lazy and has lost the remnants of humanity. There is nothing like this in France. Visually, the French village differs from the city only in size - both streets quickly end up in a field. Of course, a significant role in this is played by the fact that in France there is work not only in Paris.

It should be noted that 99% of travelers with cars, although proud of their “freedom” from train schedules, hardly use it, moving mainly between large cities, where it is inefficient and financially unreasonable (if there are less than four of you traveling in one car). The car should be used to travel to places where public transport cannot reach. For example, you can, in principle, get to each of the villages described below by bus, but it runs 1-2 times a day (the schedule is tailored to those who go to school), and you probably won’t be able to visit several places at once in a day.

Carcassonne

Carcassonne is a fairly popular place, a castle town. The weather, to put it mildly, was not favorable for tourism.

Unlike small villages, everything here is for tourists, with souvenir shops at every turn. It's probably not worth going here in the summer.

In winter it looks very authentic.

You can climb walls and fortifications.

The modern part of the city is around.

Narbonne

Stations in French cities are often very similar to each other; apparently, many of them were built according to standard designs.

It was raining and the streets were empty.

Water flows down gutters laid in the middle of the street.

On one of the streets you could see the back side of the sidewalk.

The sold out crowds are unusual.

Narbonne is interesting because it is actually the end of the Canal du Midi, a 17th-century shipping canal connecting the Bay of Biscay and the Mediterranean Sea, bypassing Spain. A World Heritage Site.

The hotel in Narbonne cost 25 euros. Everything would be fine, but it was clearly not designed for such cold weather.
