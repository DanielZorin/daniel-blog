United Kingdom: Edinburgh, Glen Coe, Loch Ness, Glasgow

6-8 October 2019

Edinburgh

Scotland occupies the northern mountainous part of the main British island. The Romans were unable to conquer this region, and for defense they fenced off from it with Hadrian's Wall. Scotland remained independent for a long time, despite an attempted English conquest in the late 13th century. Eventually, in 1603, King James VI of Scotland inherited the English throne and the two countries were united under a single government, albeit with different laws. A hundred years later, the formation of the United Kingdom was officially proclaimed, which exists to this day. In 2014, Scotland held a referendum on independence, but 55% voted against it.

The Caledonian Canal runs here, connecting the east and west coasts of Scotland. Most of the route goes along long, elongated lakes located on the site of glacial valleys; between them, pieces with locks were only completed so that you could pass through.

The most famous of these lakes is Loch Ness.

On the shore of the lake rise the ruins of Urquhart Castle.

The castle was blown up in 1692 during the civil war between supporters of the Catholic King James II, overthrown in 1688, and the new King William of Orange.

A popular legend says that the Loch Ness monster lives in the lake.

Glasgow

Glasgow is the largest city in Scotland and very from the capital. If in Edinburgh there is solemn architecture, monuments, a palace, a lot of tourists, in Glasgow at first glance there is none of this, it is such a working class city.
