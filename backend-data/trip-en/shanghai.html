China: Suzhou, Hangzhou, Shanghai

13-23 May 2015

Suzhou

In the region where the Yangtze flows into the sea, from ancient times there were many cities and villages on canals, which were needed for agriculture. Now around Shanghai there are several such old cities that have been turned into tourist attractions with paid entrance to the territory (like Fenghuang). Suzhou is on a different level, because it is a very large city, and even the central part, which is a square island between the canals, is so huge that it takes an hour and a half to walk from one end to the other. There are canals everywhere here, and not just in the small boating section.

Usually people visit Suzhou (and Hangzhou) from Shanghai on a day trip, but I decided to be original and leave Shanghai for the very end. There are direct trains from Suzhou to Hangzhou, although non-high-speed ones transit through Shanghai.

Hangzhou

People travel to Hangzhou from Shanghai to see the West Lake. In the program "Heads and Tails" this city was even called the outskirts of Shanghai. Well, of course, it only takes 40 minutes by high-speed train, but Hangzhou is also a metropolis of ten million people, there’s not only the lake there, but I’ll start with it.

West Lake has been a source of inspiration for Chinese poets and artists since ancient times. Gradually, a canonical list of the 10 most beautiful views from different parts of the lake at different times of the year emerged. The lake is quite large; it will take three to four hours to walk around it.

This is where those strange tentacle-like things that are depicted on the 1 yuan bill are located, although you can only see them up close from a boat.

The rules for renting boats for foreigners are very simple (bottom right).

I was also incredibly unlucky with the weather.

On the other hand, the views of clouds over the lake are also quite good.

Also, because of the rain, I stayed in Hangzhou for three days instead of two and found a couple of pretty nice spots.

The road partly follows the shore, partly goes along two long roads of bridges and small islands, somewhat shortening the path across the lake.

The city of Hangzhou is on the east bank, the panorama is quite modest for a city of its size.

A huge kitsch ship that can be rented for an event.

To the east of the lake there are tea plantations where Longjing tea is grown.

Tea plantations look similar to rice fields, and bushes are also planted in terraces.

I've never seen tea grow. This is a bush with green leaves. The leaves at the top ripen in the sun and turn light green, which is when they are harvested.

We picked a little (not on the plantation, but in a nearby park), then drank very strong longjing for several days.

The Great Canal of China begins in Hangzhou! I have been to several cities through which it passes, but everywhere it was somewhere on the outskirts, and here I just lived nearby. This is an outstanding engineering structure that runs from Hangzhou all the way to Beijing. It was built long before the Panama and Suez Canals - back in the 13th century (and construction began 2000 years before that).

Due to the fact that I did not walk around the city on foot at all, but moved around all the time by transport, the impression of Hangzhou turned out to be somewhat not very integral. It seems like I’ve been to many places, but they are perceived separately, not lining up into a whole continuous picture, like when you walk around the city on foot. Many photographs were taken somewhere on intermediate transfers.

Of all the Chinese cities, Hangzhou turned out to be the most “American” - not pedestrian friendly and strict. This city is definitely worth a visit, and not just for the lake.

Shanghai

Shanghai was the 50th city I visited in China. It's hard to surprise with anything in this situation. Indeed, a lot was familiar: multi-storey residential complexes, slums, street trading, copies of European architecture, tourist streets with newly built pagodas, multi-storey overpasses. Shanghai takes everything that China has and makes it perfect.

Yes, it’s hard to resist taking a photo like this. By the way, for a long time Pudong (the area with skyscrapers) was a ghost town with half-empty offices, and 20 years ago there was nothing there at all.

Honestly speaking, Shanghai “on average” looks something like this:

Or, everyone’s favorite overpasses.

Until the 19th century, Shanghai was a small city; it began to develop only when it became a European colony. Therefore, there is no special antiquity here. At most, the ruins are a hundred years old.

What remains from the Europeans is the well-known Bund embankment with pompous mansions and several more quiet neighborhoods, which are now filled with boutiques and trendy cafes.

This area is called the "French Concession".

In the center of Shanghai, the world's largest network of souvenir shops has been built, located in a huge complex of buildings with a Chinese antique design.

I have never seen such a crowd of tourists as here anywhere in China.

Tour groups are brought here because in the middle there is the Yuyuan Garden - a good opportunity to see a real Chinese garden for those who do not have enough time to travel to Suzhou.

There are an order of magnitude more foreign tourists in Shanghai than in other cities. Souvenirs for them, such as magnets, are also sold here (magnets are not sold in an ordinary Chinese city, even at souvenir stalls). I wonder who buys magnets with Saddam Hussein?

This is also the only place where wheels for boots are sold. Andrei Bednyakov once went astray, buying it for as much as 90 yuan, then he lowered the price to 30, but even 30 is an inflated price, because they easily offered me for 20. They also sell selfie sticks and flying dolls.

The main idea of this area is that you should come here in the evening to watch the illuminations.

I reached Pudong somewhere on the fifth day. The tallest building - Shanghai Tower, the second tallest building in the world - has not yet been opened, although the outside has already been completed. You can climb the two adjacent ones, although it’s not cheap.

But we found a way to see a little for free.

There are cities in which the panorama is beautiful due to the relief (for example, Rio and Chongqing). Shanghai is completely flat, but here there are 20 floor buildings all the way to the horizon, something like this is not found anywhere else, even in New York.

There is a circular overpass here that people love to photograph, although there are plenty of them in other places in China.

I love Shanghai.

In Shanghai, as in Beijing, it is very difficult to find cheap housing. Foreigners are categorically not accommodated anywhere. Again I had to resort to the help of agoda.com, however, this time the reception did not agree to accommodate me at the online price just like that, I had to book directly in the lobby from my phone. The room cost 120 yuan ($20), the most expensive in China. They even gave out vouchers for breakfast! In expensive hotels, breakfast is usually free, but I find this very rarely. Breakfast turned out to be very strange - there was a lot of spicy vegetarian food, and no meat, no fresh vegetables, no fruits. Who might like to eat spicy salads at 9 am, I don’t know. The hotel façade was full of kitsch.

In terms of futuristic views, Shanghai is perhaps somewhat inferior to Chongqing.

It’s enough to say two facts about Shanghai’s transport. Firstly, when you take the metro, they show fish behind the glass doors.

Secondly, there is the fastest train in the world, reaching a speed of 400 kilometers per hour, but not always: at certain times (which, of course, I was in) the speed is only 300, like standard Chinese high-speed trains.

Since the fare is 40 yuan, this is not a very practical way to get to the airport unless you are a business person for whom half an hour is a matter of principle. The Maglev station is not in the center, you still need to get to it by metro, but exactly the same metro line ultimately goes to the same airport.

And at the airport the counters are numbered right up to the letter O.

Laptop department. The prices seemed very attractive to me even when converted into rubles, but still I decided not to change my trusty laptop, bought during my last visit to China in Chengdu .

I made two purchases, both at the standard 100 yuan fee - a camera lens repair and a new laptop battery.

Repair anything quickly and inexpensively.

This is electronic heaven.

In addition to equipment, all sorts of small accessories are also sold.

If you dig around here, you can find a lot of interesting things.

Here, even shoes for 10 yuan.

For shoes you have to go to the shopping center that is just around the corner from my Lianbang hotel.

Here I bought shoes for 70 yuan, and they served me faithfully for three months on the rough roads of the Philippines and Cambodia.

To buy clothes you need to go to Qipu lu Street.

I have never seen such a density of clothes displayed for sale before, even at the Laleli Bazaar in Istanbul.

An interesting feature is that in China you are not allowed to try on clothes. So here women shop just like men, at random.

And in some places they were even too lazy to sort it out and just put everything in bags.

In 2001, the “One City, Nine Towns” project was launched in Shanghai. The idea was to construct areas that copy the architecture of different countries. I have already been to Nanjing “Venice” and Hangzhou “Paris”, and in Shanghai the choice is even greater: London, Amsterdam, Stockholm, Germany, Canada, Italy - this is what seems to be complete. Unfortunately, all these areas are very far from the center, and not all of them even have metro access, and traveling two hours one way in one direction with transfers is too long, so the program had to be significantly limited. The most popular of these areas is Thames town, which copies London, but it is the farthest from the center. To start with, I chose Amsterdam, which is located far in the north, but within walking distance of the metro. Chinese Amsterdam is called Gaoqiao.

Having already seen various similar places, I can say that Amsterdam did not turn out very well. Firstly, it doesn’t look very similar, it pales in comparison. And in real Amsterdam, air conditioners are not installed on facades.

The central square is still well done.

Nearby there is a park with a mill and some type of canals.
