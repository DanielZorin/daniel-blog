Brazil: Brasilia, Chapada dos Veadeiros

24-30 April 2021

Brasilia

The capital of Brazil is a city built from scratch according to a strict plan, similar to the capitals of Australia, Malaysia, Indian state of Punjab as well as the capitals of Pakistan, Myanmar and Kazakhstan, where I have not yet been.

Brasilia is shaped like a bird, or a longbow. All government agencies, shopping centers, hotels, museums, etc. are located on the axis/body, and the “wings” on the north and south are residential.

I lived in a hotel, and they are all located in two hotel blocks, one north of the axis, one south.

The city was originally built for cars, so there are long distances, but in principle you can walk. Crossings are not everywhere, but it is easy to cross the road while a red traffic light is on somewhere.

The hotel district is almost in the very center, the residential wings begin just above it, and there are also intersections of the axis with the avenues passing through the wings.

Alto Paraiso de Goiás

To visit Chapada dos Veadeiros National Park, I rented a car for the first time in Brazil. The park is located in the state of Goiás, from which a rectangle of land was allocated for the capital. The drive is approximately 250 kilometers. The first stop was at the border of the state of Goiás and the capital district. The metropolitan area is quite large and there is no longer any development on the outskirts, so the sign is simply on the road.

The center of the capital is 60 kilometers away.

In Brazil, the markings are yellow, like in the USA.

There are fields around where they grow something.

There is a strange tradition in Brazil - pots, pans and basins are constantly sold along the highways.

On the 250-kilometer route to the national park, there are only three settlements: São Gabriel de Goiás, São João d'Aliança and Alto Paraiso de Goiás, the rest of the road is absolutely empty, there are no villages or gas stations/recreation areas. The population density is comparable to the desert state New Mexico. This is the first city of São Gabriel.

Looks similar to American cities along the roads - also a lot of shops on the wide main street.

São Gabriel is small, but truckers seem to stop there, so there are more restaurants. On the way back we had dinner there, and not in the larger São João d'Aliança, where everything was closed at 7 pm.

Chapada dos Veadeiros

The part of the Chapada dos Veadeiros National Park accessible to tourists is located next to the village of São Jorge. In principle, you can get there without a car by taking a bus or Blablacar, but not during the coronavirus, when there is much less transport. São Jorge is such a village that there are not even asphalt roads, you waddle through puddles.

It’s also quite expensive there, for one night I paid 180 reais even without air conditioning (in Brasilia in the very center I paid only 100), and I couldn't find a place right away - some are closed, some require a minimum of 2 nights. Restaurants are also twice as expensive as in ordinary cities.

The entrance to the national park is about a kilometer and a half away. Admission costs R$36, but I passed for a local (thanks to CPF) and got a half-price ticket. The trek takes about 5-6 hours. Moreover, most of the route goes through the forest, and there is even a road there (gravel), I’m sure that in the USA they would definitely cut the route in half, making parking closer to the waterfalls.
