Kyoto

Kyoto is the previous (before the 17th century) capital and main historical city in Japan. It was not bombed during the war (due to its historical value), but still the city suffered greatly from a fire in the 19th century, plus in the 20th it was extensively rebuilt and modernized, as a result it has a very unusual structure for a tourist city: all the sights are located on the outskirts, so you need to constantly travel by bus. However, a ticket for the whole day costs only 500 yen.

There are also two subway lines in Kyoto, but they pass through the center and are not close to attractions, so it is more useful for tourists to take the bus.

The real center of Kyoto from a tourist point of view is the Gion district.

Nara

The oldest capital of Japan is best known for the fact that deer live in the city center.

Deer were considered sacred animals here until the 1950s, but now they delight city guests.

All the deer roam in the area where the temples are concentrated; they are not found in the densely built-up parts of the city around the station.

Kashihara

Not far from Nara there is a small town that has preserved the wooden architecture of the 17th century - Imai-cho. Now this is the Kashihara area, the nearest train stations are Unebi (JR Line) or Yagi-Nishiguchi (Kintetsu Line).

The line from Nara towards Unebi is so local that there are no turnstiles at the stations; the fare is shown on a board, like on buses. The ticket is supposed to be thrown into a box, but I took the opportunity to keep it as a souvenir.

Osaka

Osaka is Japan's second largest city, but is not as popular as the neighboring ancient capital of Kyoto. In fact, Osaka, Kyoto and many neighboring cities already constitute one large city (like Tokyo, Yokohama and the surrounding area) - the Kansai region. In Japan, Kansai is famous for its cuisine and recognizable dialect. Osaka has always been a trading city; there are no super-famous palaces or temples here.

Osaka has its own castle, the most visited in Japan. The main tower was reconstructed in 1931.

One evening in Osaka I walked around a Japanese sex shop. While in Europe 95% of goods in sex shops are intended for women, here it’s the other way around - sex shops are mainly for men.

Three floors of this shopping center out of 6 are allocated for sex shops. T-shirts are sold on the first floor, regular DVDs are sold on the second, porn films are sold on the third, sex toys and other devices are sold on the fourth and fifth.

The hardest thing to understand is how large stores selling DVDs can exist in the 21st century; before, I only saw this in countries like Myanmar or the islands of Oceania, where not all people still have the Internet. But for some reason this industry is alive in Japan. Torrents have long been banned here (although everything is available for those who know, there are even ultra-anonymous Japanese networks such as Perfect Dark), and streaming services have not yet appeared, and apparently the Japanese are so law-abiding that the majority buy CDs the old fashioned way.

In addition to draconian laws on copyright infringement, Japan also has a funny law on pornography - in all porn, the genitals are covered with blurred squares. But boobs are not genitals, they can be shown.

At the same time, the law has nothing against the most severe perversions, which would be difficult to find even on the Internet. There is even a video in the “copro” genre quite openly sold there, lying on the counter as if it's nothing special (I took a photo, but decided not to post it anyway).
