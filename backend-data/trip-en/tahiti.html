French Polynesia

1-4 February 2020

Tahiti

The last country in Oceania is French Polynesia. Further east there is only the lonely Pitcairn Island, which is several days away by boat, the Chilean Easter Island and the shores of South America. From Tahiti to Paris the distance is 15,700 kilometers, which is 1,000 kilometers less than from Paris to New Caledonia, and the shortest route is through America, not through Asia. Flights from France land in Los Angeles, and tickets from the US West Coast are inexpensive.

We are approaching Tahiti. The beginning of February is the rainy season, the number of tourists is low.

You can enter Tahiti if you have a Schengen visa issued by France (as well as other French colonies around the world). The French have their own idea of ​​what kind of jobs there are, their questionnaires always confuse me about which item to choose. In most countries, you need to enter a profession in words, rather than choosing from a list.

The capital of French Polynesia is called Papeete. It is the third largest city on the islands of Oceania after Noumea and Suva. Here in the center is densely built, with many shops and shopping centers, however, on Sunday they were all closed, and the streets were empty, as if the coronavirus had already arrived in the city.

One day I went for a ride around the island of Tahiti. Tahiti consists of two touching circular isles, in the center of each one there is a volcano: large Tahiti Iti and small Tahiti Nui. All life is centered on Tahiti Iti, the city of Papeete is on the western shore, and on the opposite side there is an isthmus connecting Tahiti Nui and the city of Taravao. In Tahiti Iti there is one large road around the entire (half)island, in Taravao two roads diverge from it along the edge of Tahiti Nui, and both quickly end, the far part of Tahiti Nui is impassable.

Public transport in Tahiti runs more or less regularly, but only on weekdays, and at 4 o'clock it stops running. Buses run across the entire island to Taravao approximately once an hour.

The buses are surprisingly good, some even have USB chargers.

At the bus station in Papeete, where the timetables should be, there is a piece of paper promising that things will soon be much better.

The middle of the island is occupied by high, impassable mountains. All life is mainly along the coast.

Like other islands in Oceania, the mountains are covered with lush green tropical forests.

Moorea

Near Tahiti, the large island of Moorea is the only island that is reasonably accessible by ferry rather than by plane (although there are 10-minute flights from Tahiti too). Unlike buses, ferries at least run on Sunday.

Although the trip is only about 20 minutes, the waters between the two islands are rough, and there is terrible rocking. I crawled out of the ship very pale, even though it was a huge ferry. I’m afraid to imagine what it would be like on a small boat.

Public transport, extremely rare to begin with, did not work on Sunday, so I went for a walk. Moorea is a fairly large island, and, like Tahiti, the roads all go around the perimeter, and in the center there are mountains. There are much fewer people here, there are no cities, only small villages with small streets perpendicular to the highway.

Locals live in spacious houses on small stilts (to prevent flooding).

They really like the “Private Property” sign here; it hangs in every second yard. The passage to the sea is also fenced off in some places.

Although there is little traffic here, the main road is lovingly lined with road markings; they were not too lazy to draw an intermittent piece opposite the exit from each yard.
