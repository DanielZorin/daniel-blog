21-26, 30 June 2014

Zagreb

I have already written about the history of Croatia. The most interesting events there took place in remote corners of the country, which are far from tourist centers. The capital of Croatia is also not a popular destination because it is located far from the sea. Although there is nothing special in Zagreb, I liked the city. It looks like other cities of the good old Austria-Hungary, and due to its capital status, it has business atmosphere.

The new city center with the station and administrative buildings looks as if it were designed in the USSR - it’s rare to see such a grid of streets and wide avenues anywhere in Europe. In Turin the avenues are narrower. In reality, this area, of course, is older than the state of Yugoslavia, because there are buildings here that were built in the 19th century.

Rijeka

The city of Rijeka greatly disappointed me, perhaps because I expected more from it than from Zagreb. Rijeka has a rather epic history: after the collapse of Austria-Hungary, it was claimed by Italy and Yugoslavia. While they were negotiating, the Italian poet Gabriele d'Annuzio staged a putsch in Rijeka and seized power. When the League of Nations decided to make Rijeka an independent state like Danzig, he created the Republic of Fiume there and declared war on Italy, but lost. A few years later, the artificial “free city of Fiume” was annexed to Italy. "Rijeka" and "Fiume" mean "river" in Croatian and Italian respectively.

I expected to see an Italian city, and in a sense that's what I saw.

Zadar

One thing really surprised me about Croatia. Here, just like on the Russian and Ukrainian coasts of the Black Sea, old ladies who rent out rooms to vacationers meet you at the station. You don’t have to book accommodation in the coastal cities of Croatia, since there may be very few offers on Booking.com, or they are located inconveniently, and at the station you can always rent a room for 20 euros for one person or for 30 euros for two people.

Zadar was a semi-exclave of Italy in Yugoslavia between the world wars. The Venetian tower also stands here.

The lion on the gate has not gone away.
