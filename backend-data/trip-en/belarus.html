Belarus: Minsk, Babruisk, Mogilev

9-10 April 2012

Minsk

Belarus is a very calm and cultural country. Rumors of a total dictatorship are greatly exaggerated. In any case, if there is a dictatorship in Belarus, then even more so in Russia. After all, in Russia there are ten police officers at each metro station, half the streets are patrolled, as if there is a war going on. In Minsk, police officers can be seen at the train station (two at the entrance) and in the metro (one at the turnstiles: they probably appeared after the terrorist attack in 2011). Opposition rallies are banned here - well, it’s not just here. In elections, the president receives a lot of votes - in our country, too, Putin will gain a lot without fraud, and in the past he got even more. The political culture may not be up to high American standards, but let's not forget that this is a post-Soviet country. ((This text did no age well - greetings from 2023)
Let's move on to the details. Belarus is officially bilingual, so signs can be seen in both Russian and Belarusian. At the same time, you can only hear the Belarusian language at an opposition rally or on TV: some of the news reports are in Belarusian. Belarusians are very fond of the letter y with a "shortener".

Traffic lights advise you to be careful, and some wish you happiness.

Prices by Moscow standards are very low. The exchange rate of the Belarusian ruble is approximately the same as that of the Indonesian rupiah, so further prices are in Russian rubles. Gasoline - 24 rubles (yes, this is a country that buys oil from Russia). Metro ticket - 6 rubles. I bought a book at the store for 30 rubles. Half a liter of cola - 15 rubles. Furniture is several times cheaper than in Russia. There is only one minus - the average salary in Belarus is about 6,000 rubles, and with it these prices will not seem so low.

The Soviet style is constantly visible in the design. The emblem of the Ocean fish store could have looked like this, but here they took English words. By the way, the word “universam” can be seen very often in Belarus. Moreover, in every corner store, even somewhere in Bobruisk, you can pay with a bank card (how often do you see this in Moscow?).

Bobruisk

Bobruisk has one foot in the bright Soviet past.

And the other - in a bright democratic future. A bright, boldly decorated store.

Biker bar.

The modernist building was painted in such rich colors relatively recently.

Excellent street sign.

There is an old fortress in Bobruisk, built shortly before the war of 1812. At first glance, these are nondescript ruins.

But you can go inside and admire the centuries-old brickwork and wander through the underground tunnels. The tunnels are completely dark; you can navigate by photographing the path with a flash.

Unfortunately, there are few tourists here, and the fortress is not very well looked after. Some buildings seem to be being tidied up, but one, the most easily accessible from the road, is in complete chaos.

By the way, Bobruisk stands on the Berezina River, the same one where Napoleon suffered a crushing defeat in December 1812.

Overall the city is very pleasant. Silence, grace, purity, tranquility.
