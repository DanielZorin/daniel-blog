Laos: Don Det, Don Khon, Champatsak, Pakse, Bolaven Plateau, Savannakhet

23-29 December 2015

Four thousand Islands

On the border of Laos and Cambodia, the Mekong River breaks into many small streams, between which lie islands. Some of them are inhabited, in particular the island of Don Det, on which a recreational area with hotels and restaurants is organized.

I've heard claims that the crowd moved here after Vang Vieng closed, but that's not true. At night the village is quiet and calm, there is no discotheque, except that the presence of "lucky" menu items hints at fun.

There is also tubing, a signature Laotian entertainment. This is a ride along the river on a big wheel or lifebuoy with a beer. Although it is more interesting to go kayaking.

In terms of infrastructure, the village has many hotels and restaurants. Restaurant prices are high (as in other tourist places), but if you go further from the pier, prices drop to normal. I found a hotel for $3.5 with the slogan “Our rooms are modest, but we have a big heart.”

This is currently a record for the cheapest hotel for me. For three and a half dollars, the conditions here are quite decent: a small house, a large bed, a mosquito net (a useful device that is often missing), and an attached shower. There is no hot water, but during the day it is quite warm as it is heated by the sun. Electricity works normally (there were problems with this on the island before, because the supply came from generators). There is a hammock hanging at the entrance, where you can catch the Wi-Fi of the neighboring restaurant.

There are no ATMs on the island, but you can make a cash advance with a decent commission (5 percent). Cash currency exchange is also at a not very favorable rate, 5 percent lower than the real one. I didn’t find SIM cards for sale, I bought them later in Pakse. Stores generally do not offer a large assortment. Another disadvantage is the high prices for travel from the islands. You most likely won’t be able to leave on your own, because the boats depart only at one time, when tourists are taken out of the island. For example, I bought a ticket from Banlung to Don Det, including a boat, for $12, which I considered very expensive for a three-hour journey. On Don Det, a ticket to Banlung sells for $18. The two hour journey to Pakse costs almost $9.

Don Det is connected by this bridge to the neighboring island of Don Khon.

Old rails lie nearby. This is no accident.

The fact is that until recently the only railway in Laos was located here. (They recently built a couple of kilometers of extension of the rails from Thailand across the border near Vientiane, without reaching Vientiane itself). Why do they need a railway on some island in the middle of the river? The Mekong's 4,000 islands have high rapids, making it impossible to sail up or down the river. The only option is to drag the boat (or its cargo) overland through Don Khon. To facilitate this task, the French built a railway. Now all that remains of it is a rusty locomotive and the remains of rails, because the cargo can be transported along the road along the Mekong.

Champatsak

Once the capital of the region, Champatsak today is just a village: one street and wooden houses. It differs from any other village only in the presence of several hotels, which are there because the Khmer era temple, Wat Phu, has been preserved nearby.

Tuk-Tuk wants as much as 10 dollars for a 10-kilometer round trip. Actually, there is no other way for a tuk-tuk driver to earn money here; who else in the village would need a taxi? I thought about offering to split the price with a couple of Europeans who checked into the same hotel, but they somehow left very quickly (and I thought they were poor, since they checked into a room with a fan for 5 dollars, and I with an air conditioner for 10). The solution to the problem was simple - rent two bicycles for a dollar and ride on your own.

Pakse

Pakse is the first semblance of a city in Laos. Although there is less than 100 thousand population, the city is one of the three largest in Laos.

At least there is not just one street, but a city center where there is a grid of streets intersecting at angles. And then, anyway, one street stretches for ten kilometers, along which are all the buildings. And there are buildings with more than one floor.

There are bus stops in Pakse, but this is a foundation for the distant future - there is really no public transport in the city.

You can finally buy a SIM card in Pakse. There is a Beeline branch in Laos.

Bolaven Plateau

In the case of the Bolaven Plateau, I took the easy route and took a guided tour. In all Pakse agencies it costs 150 thousand kip ($18), this is for travel everywhere by minibus and entrance tickets. You can go on your own by motorcycle, but the cheapest one in Pakse costs more than $10 a day, and by motorcycle the route is more likely to take two days than one, because there are many stops. The first point of the program is two high waterfalls located nearby.

Then a coffee plantation. The Bolaven Plateau is known as the place where the grains for Lao's signature drink are grown. I've never seen coffee grow before.

Savannakhet

Savannakhet stands out in Laos for three reasons. Firstly, it looks like a city, which is rare for Laos. Secondly, architecture older than 50 years has been preserved here, which is even more rare. Thirdly, this is the least touristy of the cities (not villages) in the country; people mainly come here for a day to get a Thai visa at the consulate.

In this city (and in this house) Kaysone was born - the communist dictator of Laos, whose portrait is depicted on all banknotes.

I wanted to take a photo with the monument to him, as with Lenin, but there was no monument. There was only a monument to some other figure.

In general, the cult of personality in Laos is weakly expressed, and there are also fewer communist paraphernalia than in Vietnam and China. Only the pioneers wear red ties en masse.
