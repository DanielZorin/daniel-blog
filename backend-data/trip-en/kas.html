Kaş

Kaş is a small city in the very south of Antalya province. It is not close to the nearest airports (Antalya and Dalaman) - about 200 kilometers each, so, probably, there are no large all-inclusive hotels here, and in this way it resembles a small European resort town. For some reason, many expats and IT workers come to Kaş to work remotely (especially in the spring of 2022), so the cost of monthly rent here is noticeably higher than in other cities.

The road to Kaş is long and winding.

Just before the city it descends sharply to the sea.

The city is very multi-level. I probably wouldn't want to live here permanently. First of all, it's a little expensive. Secondly, it’s far from the airport, making travel even around Turkey inconvenient. There are no big stores either, you will have to use delivery for everything. Thirdly, because of the terrain, you will be tortured by walking uphill (surely most people do not live right next to the port, but in residential areas on the hills).

But, of course, the city is simply wonderful for tourism, one of the most beautiful in Turkey.

Central square and monument to Ataturk.

Streets of the old city. Kaş resembles the towns on the islands of Greece, and it is not surprising - before the First World War it mainly had Greek population.

Lycian tomb in the rock above Kaş. Lycia is the historical name of the area between modern Antalya and Fethiye. Once upon a time there was a state of the same name, where they spoke the Lycian language (now dead). The Lycians assimilated with the Greeks around the beginning of the Middle Ages. The most characteristic remains of Lycia are these rock tombs, they can be found in many cities on the coast.

Kınık

The last city you can get to by local bus from Antalya is Kınık, located slightly inland, exactly on the border with Muğla province.

The town is small and not touristy.

Bus station. Minibuses go to Kaş approximately every hour, to Antalya several times a day.

Monument to Ataturk. From the back I thought that he was with children as usual, but no, this is a worker and a collective farmer, they are just dwarfs compared to Ataturk.
