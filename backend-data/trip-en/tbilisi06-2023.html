Continuing the tradition established in Turkey, I visited to the main museums of Georgia in Tbilisi. The first one was the National museum.

At the entrance, the first thing that greets visitors is a hall with many skulls of prehistoric people.

Behind it there is a hall with skeletons and stuffed animals.

The top floor displays artifacts from antiquity.

Pieces of a table.

Later periods of history (the Middle Ages, the Modern Age) are not particularly represented. There is a hall with weapons and paintings depicting different Caucasian peoples.

So from antiquity we move straight to the Soviet and Russian occupation.

The most important part of the exhibition is on the basement floor: gold and jewelry.

The Golden Fleece.

Cup.

This ancient lion is easily recognizable from the Bank of Georgia logo, which is everywhere on the streets. I had to look for it for some time, because it’s sooooo small.

The National gallery is on Rustaveli Avenue, not far from the museum

It is quite small. The main hall is dedicated to the most famous Georgian artist - Niko Pirosmani.

There were fewer paintings than usual, since some were exhibited temporarily abroad (including a portrait of the actress Marguerite).

The second half was occupied by paintings by Guram Kutateladze, an artist of the second half of the 20th century.

Constructivist-impressionist still lifes.

Some sculptures.

On the lower floor there were two exhibitions of artists with similar surnames - Gela Patiashvili and Maka Batiashvili. The first reflects on the Last Judgment due to environmental pollution.

The second has characteristic faces, reminiscent of the cartoon "12 oz Mouse".

Tom kha gai costs 18 lari ($7).

There is another unusual landmark of Tbilisi in this area.

On maps it is referred to as "Tbilisi Skybridge".

To get there, I had to deal with the infamous Tbilisi paid elevators. To go up, you need to toss a 20 tetri coin ($0.08). This is a relatively expensive elevator; usually in high-rise buildings elevators cost 5 or 10 tetri. However, the problem is not paying 8 cents, but getting a hold of the right coin. I had to go to a nearby grocery store to get the change.

This bridge connects three neighboring high-rise buildings and goes out to the street along the mountainside at the same level as their upper floors.

There were a lot of people taking pictures on the bridge.

Telavi

Kakheti is the easternmost part of Georgia. I had never been there before, as it was off the main road. The region is famous primarily for winemaking. There are no large cities in Kakheti; the largest one is Telavi with a population of only 20 thousand. You can get to Telavi from Tbilisi either by a longer road along the main highway through the Alazani Valley, or by a shorter road through the Gombori Pass.

Rustavi

Rustavi is a large city 10 kilometers south of Tbilisi, the capital of the Kvemo Kartli region. English-language guidebooks recommend coming here if you want to experience the atmosphere of a typical Soviet industrial city, but what originally brought me here was the desire to get a Georgian driver’s license (you can’t take exams in Tbilisi, everyone goes to Rustavi).

The police department issuing licenses is located near the largest car market in Georgia on the outskirts of Rustavi, close to the highway to Tbilisi.

Panorama of new Rustavi.

There are hills nearby. Judging by the map, a cable car used to go to the cross at the top.

Rustavi is divided into old and new (on different sides of the river). Old Rustavi was built during Stalin era, the new one during Brezhnev era.
