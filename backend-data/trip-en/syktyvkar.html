Russia: Totma, Veliky Ustyug, Kotlas, Solvychegodsk, Koryazhma, Mikun, Syktyvkar

17-21 September 2020

Totma

A cluster of three historical cities at the junction of the Vologda and Arkhangelsk regions was the last region where I could visit several cities at once. The logistics were a little tricky: I had to find a cheap plane ticket from Syktyvkar, match the polar railway train schedule, and also figure out how to get around the Vologda region. Here, for the first time, I booked a ticket for a bus (or rather a minibus) across Russia via the Internet, it turned out to be good, they even called me the day before and clarified which stop to pick me up from. Having arrived by train to the already familiar Vologda I spent the night in a familiar hotel, and went east to the city of Totma, where the drive took about three hours. The roads got worse as we approached Totma, and the bus began to shake. Passengers in Totma were dropped off directly opposite the main cathedral.

The naked eye can see that the churches here are different from churches in other cities of Russia. This is a peculiar high elongated shape, and relief decorations on the facade, called cartouches.

Kotlas

The nearest railway station with regular passenger traffic to Veliky Ustyug is in Kotlas, the third largest city in the Arkhangelsk region after Arkhangelsk and Severodvinsk. The bus dropped me off right at the foot of the Lenin monument on the square in front of the station.

A steam locomotive that once ran on the Northern Railway. One of the northernmost railways in Russia passes through Kotlas: Moscow - Vorkuta with a branch to Labytnangi beyond the Urals.

Opposite the railway station there is an unused river station, behind which is the Northern Dvina River. Kotlas stands next to the confluence of the Northern Dvina and Vychegda.

Solvychegodsk

Solvychegodsk is located very close to Kotlas, but on the other side of the Vychegda River, so getting there requires adventure. First, 20 kilometers along a normal road, then 10 kilometers through the forest on a bad dirt road, and then by ferry across the river. Or 40 kilometers along a normal road to the city of Koryazhma, where there is a pontoon bridge, and then 20 kilometers along a broken dirt road. As I understand it, only the second option is available in winter, and in summer I tested both. Buses run both to the ferry and across the pontoon bridge, but not very often - about 3 times a day.

The bus stops on a wide sandy beach.

The car ferry runs every hour.

On the other side rises the Annunciation Cathedral.

And also an inscription, like in Hollywood and Barnaul.

The Annunciation Cathedral is the main reason why the city is among the best historical cities. This is a cathedral from the late 16th - early 17th centuries with frescoes, so even in the USSR they realized its value, and did not set up a warehouse or factory here.

Mikun

In the city of Mikun there is the intersection of the Moscow-Vorkuta highway and its branch to Syktyvkar. I got here from Solvychegodsk on the Vorkuta train, because the direct Syktyvkar train was at an inconvenient time. I didn’t want to drive another two hours in the evening to Syktyvkar, so I stayed overnight at the station hotel. The hostess was very surprised that I was not on a business trip.
