4-12 August 2016

Bialystok

The city of Bialystok is located near the border with Belarus. In 1939, it was even annexed to the USSR after the division of Poland along with Brest, Grodno, Lviv, but after 1945 it was transferred to the newly created Polish state.

The border with Lithuania is also nearby, I was seriously considering whether to spontaneously take a ticket to Kaunas instead of the Belarusian border.

They sell hardcore omelettes near the station.

The station faces the residential area, to get to the center you need to go in the opposite direction. Because of this, I even got lost.

Kuznica

Along the way we passed the town of Sokówka with its fabulously broken down train station. Most of the passengers got off here, only a few went to the border.

The police received us immediately upon exiting the train. For some time they examined our passports and even ran them through some database using a tablet, while at the same time asking several times questions about what I was doing in Poland and what my home address was in Moscow. You cannot cross the border on foot, although some time ago there was such a great opportunity, and you can even see a closed passage for pedestrians. At the border, the Russians also aroused a lot of interest; every border guard and customs officer considered it his duty to ask when I entered, and why I didn’t have a Polish entry stamp (although I had a fresh Spanish visa and a Spanish bold stamp right next to it; it seems that the concept The Schengen zone is not fully understood here). It felt like I was crossing the border of Congo and Burundi, where a Russian passport, or indeed any foreign one, had never been seen.

Kuznica is quite a village.

But there is still one monument here.

In general, people come here for two purposes. Firstly, you can order goods to be delivered to the local post office, so as not to wait for them to be checked at postal customs.

Secondly, a large “Bedronka” store was built near the border, where Belarusians go to shop.
