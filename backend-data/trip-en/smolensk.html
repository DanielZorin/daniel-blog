Russia: Smolensk, Talashkino, Flyonovo, Roslavl, Bryansk

24-25 August 2013

Smolensk

Smolensk was unlucky with its location. In addition to the fact that the city became a battle arena during all the invasions of Russia from the west, the city is located at a fairly large distance from Moscow: it’s a five-hour drive, it’s too far for a day trip. In addition, Smolensk is located away from the main tourist routes. And the city is more than worthy of a visit.

The road to Smolensk is of good quality.

So.

There are many steles in honor of the battles in 1941-1945.

It turns out that Russia also has its own piece of the Dnieper, which I have already seen in Ukraine (Kyiv) and Belarus ( Mogilev). The historical center of Smolensk, surrounded by walls, stands on the banks of the Dnieper. The uniqueness of Smolensk is that only here in Russia the fortress walls enclose a very significant residential part of the city, whereas usually kremlins are small and consist of churches and museums. It’s almost as if the walls of Kitay-Gorod were completely preserved in Moscow.

The newlyweds go out to take pictures against the backdrop of the fortress.

The main street.

The street goes across the bridge. At the intersection, when we arrived, there was a hellish traffic jam and confusion, like in some Asian metropolis. In general, drivers in Smolensk are interesting; they also take off a couple of seconds before the green light turns on.

There are stands around the city showing the nearest attractions, and even with text in English. Just like in Europe!

By the way, this church has miraculously stood here since the 12th century. Surviving the Mongol invasion is a rarity.

The walls were built at the turn of the 16th and 17th centuries. Not all are still standing, but many are.

The most famous monument in Smolensk is to the heroes of 1812. I wondered for a long time what it symbolizes. In fact, there are two eagles here fighting off a French soldier with a sword together.

Obelisk with monograms of different emperors from different sides.

Monument to children - prisoners of concentration camps.

A monument to an interesting personality - Nikolai Krylenko, Minister of Justice of the USSR, one of the organizers of the mass executions of 1937, who himself was executed in 1938.

Flyonovo

I found out about Flenovo by accident - I saw a board with souvenir magnets in a bookstore, one of them depicted a church that does not exist in Smolensk. Getting here is easy; local buses run along the southbound highway to nearby villages. You need to get to the village of Talashkino.

Talashkino, like any village, is very depressing, but in Talashkino they are unexpectedly polite, apparently, the presence of tourists is understandable to them and does not cause hatred, as in an ordinary village.

The drunkard sleeps right in the rain. A couple of people stood nearby and loudly condemned him.

Balcony decoration.

The "Products" store and the main store in any village - "Funeral services".

More than 100 years ago, they decided to turn Flenovo into a tourist village - a center of folk crafts. Now, due to the remoteness of Smolensk, there are not very many tourists here, but there are some excursions. We met a bus with the inscription La Valle for the fourth time: first it passed by when we were hitching on the highway, then we overtook it in one of the cars, and then we met it in the center of Smolensk.

Teremok itself.

What's worth coming here for is the church. Completely non-standard architecture, breaking all the patterns of Orthodoxy so much that they didn’t even want to consecrate it for a long time.

Roerich's paintings inside, apparently, have practically not survived. The doors are closed, through the crack you can see that inside there is a bare floor and bare walls. From the outside you can still look into the basement, where there is also nothing.

Roslavl

One of the most disgusting places I've ever been.

There's nothing here.

There is a very big difference between medium-sized regional centers and such outlying cities.

The people here are completely different - embittered, unfriendly. There are a lot of stereotypical gopniks with knocked out teeth, black eyes, and in tracksuits.

There is no life here, no prospects. All reasonable people from here left long ago for Smolensk.

I left here with great relief.

The road sign at the exit seems to indicate the direction.

Bryansk

If Bryansk stood by the sea, it could be called Russian Rio de Janeiro. But without the sea the picture is a little different, so it would be more correct to say that Bryansk is a piece of Belarus on Russian territory.

Firstly, the cleanliness and neatness here are at their best, and secondly, the architecture here is quite new, since the Bryansk region was thoroughly destroyed in 1941-1944.
