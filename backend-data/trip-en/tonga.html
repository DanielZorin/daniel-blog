Tonga

9-11 April 2019

I finally reached the Pacific Islands! Tonga is one of two dozen countries located on atolls in this part of the world. Tonga is in the Western Hemisphere, but the international date line goes around it to the right, so the time zone here is “Eastern”, 13 hours from London. The main island is called Tongatapu, and 70% of the 100 thousand population live there. The capital in some places looks like a small city in Russia.

As in Russia, in Tonga they make flower beds from old tires.

And they decorate them with mushrooms!

But what is characteristic of Tonga is the love of state flags. On many houses the fences are densely decorated with them. By the way, the flag of Tonga is similar to the flag of Switzerland, where they also love to use their flag in decoration.

There are very bad roads in Tonga with huge potholes.

And here is the cemetery. This is the main feature of Tonga - here the cemeteries are in the most visible place, and there are bright posters and richly decorated graves.

Every small village has a cemetery, and there are many of them in the capital.

Some cemeteries in the back streets may look more modest - there are heaps of stones and sand, and flowers on top.

The most important cemetery in the center of the capital - the kings of Tonga are buried here. This is a huge area, the graves are in the center, but the area is fenced around the perimeter.

The two main churches of Tonga stand on the same square. The first one appears to be abandoned.

Another square in the center surprises with its emptiness and abundance of advertising for the Digicel operator, which operates in many countries in Oceania. I recommend it, my SIM card from Tonga worked in roaming in Fiji at the home rate, even the Internet package worked.

At the end of the street you can see the royal palace. Tonga is the only monarchy in Oceania (not counting those countries where the Queen of Great Britain is formally considered the queen). Chickens graze near the royal palace.

On the other side, you can take a closer look at the palace.

Gate and initials of the king (George Tupou).

The king's portrait is on Tongan banknotes (called pa'anga, but everyone just says "dollar").

Opposite the palace stands the largest building in Tonga - the treasury.

The embankments in Nuku'alofa have recently been improved in appearance.

It looks like volcanic lava here.

But still, Tongatapu is a coral atoll, which is clearly visible from the white fragments of coral instead of sand.

Nuku'alofa Central Market.

Prices are not high. $3 is one and a half US dollars.

Many Chinese live in Tonga. They, as I understand it, run the vast majority of supermarkets and roadside stalls. I don’t know why they prefer Tonga (in neighboring Fiji it’s not so obvious). Perhaps Tonga has a liberal migration policy by regional standards. Even some Russians manage to live there for a long time.

Several years ago there were even ethnic clashes. Maybe that's why the stalls are barred?

There are also Chinese eateries in Nuku'alofa. You can eat here for 2-3 dollars! As a snack, in addition to rice, you can take cassava - this is a local analogue of potatoes.

For three dollars they gave me four rather large pieces of chicken.

Tongatapu Island is small, approximately 40 by 10 kilometers. In principle, you can get around it by public transport: from Nuku'alofa there are buses to a dozen different villages, including the far edges of the island. Traffic intervals are unpredictable, from 30 minutes to 2 hours, to the second largest town of Lapaha - every 15 minutes. You can also hitchhike in Tonga, and I can definitely recommend this method - hitchhiking here is better than in the Caucasus, I got picked up even before I managed to raise my hand.

Let's go around Tonga. The lady in the minibus wears a straw skirt. This appears to be local formal wear.

At the cemetery at the wake, everyone is wearing skirts like these.

The villages along the route alternate between churches and Chinese supermarkets.
