India: Cochin, Goa, Mumbai

28 June - 11 July 2017

Cochin

Welcome to South India! It has its own atmosphere: a little cleaner, a little richer, a little less people. Most Indian emigrants are from these parts (it’s for a reason that one of the official languages in Singapore is Tamil, and not Hindi). While in the north one empire replaced another, in the south goods were transported between China and Europe. The southernmost provinces became part of India very late, only under the British; before that they were not part of even the largest empires in the region. The languages of the south (Malayalam, Tamil, Telugu and Kannada) are not related to Hindi, Sanskrit and other Indo-Aryan languages, and the letters are also completely different. Cochin (or Kochi) is also a historical city; it was founded by the Portuguese back in the 15th century, when they first opened the route to India.

Goa

Goa is not a city, but a small state of India, stretching over 100 kilometers from north to south. There is also the city of Goa (now called Old Goa) - a Portuguese colony, but when they say “Goa”, they usually do not mean it. For backpackers, lovers of yoga, drugs and enlightenment, Goa is the village of Arambol on the northern edge of the state; For tourists arriving on a package tour for a beach holiday, Goa is most likely the village of Morjim south of Arambol, or some less popular place on the coast. By the way, Goa as a resort is famous only in Russia; there are no more Western tourists (except for the relatively small category of backpackers) there than in other places in southern India, that is, very few. Tourists in Goa are approximately 90% Indian, 8% Russian and 2% all others combined; There are only about 800 thousand foreigners per year (of which quite a few are, in fact, Indian emigrants). For comparison, the island of Phuket of comparable size receives 8 million tourists a year. The first place to visit in Goa is the old town - a World Heritage Site.

Mumbai

Mumbai is India's largest city, richest and most economically important. This is the #1 city to visit if you come to India, like New York in America: not the capital, but essentially the main city. Under the British, it was also not the capital (which was located in Calcutta, but as the main port of the west coast it was actively developing, and received its share imperial buildings. Mumbai is located on a long and narrow peninsula, with the city center in the south, but not at the southernmost point, as one might expect. The reason is that until the 19th century, instead of a peninsula it was a group of islands, the straits between which later were filled with land, and the city center remained in place of the island where it was originally located.

The most famous building, which is even a world heritage site, is the Mumbai CST station, which was previously named after Queen Victoria.

In addition to the old, shabby trains, Mumbai also has newer public transport systems: the metro and monorail. The Mumbai monorail is very strange: the line goes from nowhere to nowhere somewhere on the outskirts, is very poorly connected to other modes of transport, does not go in a straight line, but in a large loop. For several years now they have been promising to finish the line to the city center, they announced the opening in the spring of 2017, in the summer of 2018, but things are still slow.
