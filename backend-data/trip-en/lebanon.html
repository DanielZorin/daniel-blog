Lebanon: Beirut, Jounieh, Baalbek, Byblos, Batroun, Tripoli, Saida, Sur

16-21 November 2022

Beirut

Lebanon is not just another Arab country in the Middle East. Here, historically, the majority of residents were Christians, hence more liberal morals: alcohol, casinos, etc. are allowed. Lebanon was the center of trade and entertainment in the region, but in 2019 a debt crisis came, and in 2020, in addition to Covid, saltpeter exploded in the port of Beirut, causing colossal destruction. In four years, Lebanon's GDP fell by 50%, and the Lebanese pound fell from 1,500 to 40,000 per dollar (and this is the black market rate, that is, you cannot pay by card in Lebanon). Now the center of Beirut is practically a ghost town.

The evening after my arrival, I went to see the city center and buy something, but it turned out that the busy area with boutiques was no longer lively. The streets are empty, all restaurants are closed.

Surprisingly, the boutiques themselves are open, there are guards in jackets at the entrance, but there are no visitors in sight.

There was a checkpoint at the entrance to the central area, so I didn’t go there.

The next day I explored this place in more detail and found a passage not blocked by fences, although I still had to exit through the checkpoint on the other side. It turned out that the center, in principle, is not closed to the public, bored soldiers with machine guns let you inside, it’s just that everything there is closed, and almost no one goes there. And it is cordoned off because the parliament building is located there, and the authorities do not want protests near it (it was no longer possible to approach the building itself).

Jounieh

North of Beirut on the coast begins the city of Jounieh, once the center of tourism and entertainment in Lebanon, where there were many luxury hotels, casinos and restaurants. Due to the crisis, the city now looks shabby, but nevertheless it deserves a visit at least for the Jeita Cave and the cable car.

First of all, I went to Jeita Cave. This is one of the most popular and famous caves in the world. More precisely, there are two whole caves here - in the upper one you can walk on foot, and in the lower one everything is flooded, and visitors are taken on a boat.

For some strange logic, photography is prohibited in the caves. At the bottom they took away my entire bag, and at the top only my phone, so I clicked once on the DSLR.

Baalbek

The city of Baalbek is located in the Bekaa Valley, which runs north to south between the mountains of coastal Lebanon and the border with Syria. This region is predominantly inhabited by Shia Muslims, and is dominated by the notorious Hezbollah group (which is not considered terrorist in Russia). Baalbek is famous for its ancient Roman temples that have few competitors in Europe in terms of scale and preservation.

Byblos

Tripoli

Tripoli is the second largest city in Lebanon, located in the very north. This is a Muslim city, and Sunni, not Shia (like Baalbek and Sur), so there are no Hezbollah posters here. Sur

The city of Sur (historical name: Tyre) is located in the very south of Lebanon, near the border with Israel. The border areas are controlled by Hezbollah and you cannot go there, Sur is the only safe place. The Roman ruins at Sur are listed as a World Heritage Site.

The bus station is quite far from the city center, there are areas near it that look like slums. There is a checkpoint on every lane, so I ended up taking pictures there.
