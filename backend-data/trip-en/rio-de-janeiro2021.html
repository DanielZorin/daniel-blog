Rio de Janeiro

The historic center of Rio de Janeiro is considered a relatively dangerous area. It’s better not to walk there on weekends, because almost everything is closed. On a weekday it’s basically normal in crowded places. There is a lot to see here, you can imagine what it was like here in the 1930s, during the time of Ostap Bender, when Copacabana was still a village on the outskirts, and the main streets of the city were not inferior to the best cities in the world in terms of the splendor of their buildings. The sharp growth of favelas and crime began in the 40s and 50s, so in the 30s it should have been quite good.

Rio de Janeiro train station. To the right on the hill is Providencia - the very first favela in Brazil. It was formed back in the 1890s, when soldiers returning from the “War of Canudos” (the suppression of an uprising in the state of Bahia) settled on the hill. "Favela" is the name of a shrub with acrid sap that grows in northeastern Brazil, where the uprising was.

I had already been to the favelas of Rio de Janeiro in 2013 and promised to go there again then. And so, I fulfilled my promise. Not that much has changed over the years. Groups involved in drug trafficking are still based in the favelas, and the government periodically tries to clean them up there with gunfire in all directions (at the beginning of May 2021, 25 people died in one large shootout). I didn’t go to the northern part of Rio, but I did visit the south, where the largest but less dangerous favelas are.

As I already mentioned in the story about Copacabana, there are two favelas nearby on the northern and southern edges. I was just in the southern one, it’s called Pavão-Pavãozinho, or Cantagalo (after the name of the mountain). * * *

Rio de Janeiro is probably the most picturesque of the world's megacities. Here, quite high hills rise among the densely populated central areas, and also next to the sea. It is the landscapes and panoramas of Rio (and not the architecture) that are included in the World Heritage List. There are two famous high viewpoints in the city center: the Jesus Statue and the Sugar Loaf. But there is another way to see Rio from above, although it is not guaranteed - a flight through Santos Dumont Airport. This airport is located in the very center of the city on the bay. Most domestic flights fly through it rather than through the distant and inconvenient Galeão. To have a good view, you need to guess which side to sit on. If you're lucky, you can see the entire city center and more. This is more likely to happen if you sit on the right side when boarding.

I've flown to or from Rio de Janeiro six times, two of which were after sunset, so it was dark. The first time, I checked in late and was sitting on the left side, but suddenly the plane made a U-turn over downtown and Sugar Loaf at the end before landing.

Traditional review of a supermarket in Brazil. There are plenty of grocery stores here, including European chains such as Dia and Carrefour. In the city center there are usually smaller stores, and on the outskirts there are always a bunch of hypermarkets. This is the Pão de Açucar chain, I would say that it is an analogue of Russian Perekrestok in terms of price level and assortment. Prices in Brazilian reals, 1 USD = 5.3 BRL, 1 BRL = 14 RUB

In the fruit and vegetable department there is a whole huge table of the seasonality of different fruits. Even I haven’t tried four from this list, I tried a few more for the first time on this trip (acerola, caju, cupuaçu).
