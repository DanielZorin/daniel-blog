Moscow

A new line appeared in the Moscow metro in 2019, and a couple of days before the quarantine, new stations were opened on it, bringing it close to the center. The new line leads to the Nekrasovka district, which is considered one of the worst places to live due to air pollution. Nekrasovka was originally a settlement at a wastewater treatment plant, and was part of Moscow as an exclave, but in 2011, the Lyubertsy Fields wasteland was added to Nekrasovka, and it connected with the rest of Moscow through the Kosino-Ukhtomsky district located outside the Moscow Ring Road. Lyubertsy fields were built up with high-rise buildings, and now there is a metro here.

The Butovo district has historically had a bad reputation, probably because in the 90s it was almost the most remote area (along with Solntsevo) with massive development (unlike other areas extending beyond the Moscow Ring Road). In reality, Butovo is now a fairly civilized and settled area; by today’s standards, it is no longer particularly new. Transport accessibility is much better thanks to the Butovskaya metro line, although the journey to the center is still quite long. Initially, the Butovskaya line was an “addendum” to the gray line, and only 10 years later, in 2014, it was extended to also intersect with the orange line - at the Novoyasenevskaya station, which was formerly called “Bitsevsky Park”.

In the summer of 2020, I came up with a challenge - to visit all districts of Moscow. This task is not so simple. Moscow is divided into 12 administrative okrugs, and those into as many as 125 districts (rayon) and 21 settlements (in New Moscow - territories annexed in 2012). I have been to all administrative okrugs in one way or another, but at the district level everything is not so simple. Having looked more closely at the districts and their borders, I discovered that I was in less than half, and this is even taking into account all the adventures with visiting Lenin monuments in industrial zones. I think the vast majority of Moscow residents have been to a maximum of 40-50 districts. In some areas I was passing through: for example, only at the metro station; I have been to some places, but a very long time ago (for example, in Zelenograd). I planned to take a walk in such places again. The only okrug where I was in all 10 districts is, of course, the Central. It is clear that there are many attractions there, and you can talk about each district at length and in detail. So I went through the rest of the districts, divided by cardinal directions, and started with the North-East, since I live there myself. NE okrug is the top of the orange and gray metro lines (In Moscow metro, lines have lengthy official names, but all the locals call them by colors, maybe except for a few new lines that appeared only in the last 5 years), as well as a piece of light green, which then goes to the Northern District. There are 17 districts in the North-East - more than in any other okrug. Among the famous attractions here are the territory of VDNKh and the Ostankino TV tower.
