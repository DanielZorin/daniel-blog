USA: Maui, Hawaii, Kauai, Honolulu

11-19 February 2020

Maui

The Hawaiian Islands are a piece of Polynesia in the USA. They are much further north than most countries in Oceania, but were settled by the same Polynesians who sailed to New Zealand in the south. They were discovered for Europeans by Captain James Cook at the end of the 18th century, around the same time the independent islands were united into one kingdom under the rule of King Kamehameha I. At the end of the 19th century, the islands were captured by the Americans and were for some time an unincorporated territory (as now Guam), and eventually became a full-fledged state. There are eight islands, but four are actually visited: Oahu, Hawaii, Maui and Kauai. There are two more small islands with a small population and no particularly interesting places, and two islands that are completely closed to visitors. Hawaii

The largest island of the Hawaiian archipelago is also called Hawaii, or in everyday life - the Big Island. Its eastern and western coasts are very different in climate: all the resorts are on the sunny western side, and in the east there are mountains, which make the coast rainier. There is an airport on each coast. I flew into the much busier Kailua-Kona airport on the west and lived in the east near Hilo, so it was a round trip. There are two roads: a long one along the southern coast and a twice shorter one straight through the mountains. For a long time, the short road was of very poor quality, but about 5 years ago it was finally completed, and you can cross the island in about two hours if you wish. But initially, upon arrival, I had almost the whole day to spare, so I drove along the southern road, seeing the sights along the way.

Kailua-Kona Airport is unusual in that it is partially open-air, with canopies covering the check-in and security counters, but much of the area is exposed to rain.

Kauai

Kauai is the smallest island I've visited. You can travel around it by car in a day. After flying in from the Big Island, I rented a car at Alamo one last time and drove south. The main reason I included Kauai on the itinerary in the first place was Waimea Canyon.

Canyons occupy almost the entire central and western part of the island. It is surprising that this island was the only one of the entire group with such a relief.

The first small observation point where I stopped to catch my breath after leaving the airport and passing through the populated part of the island.

My car is a Chevrolet. It was simply incredibly huge, it seems to me that in Russia it is very rare to see such a long passenger car - parking in it would be very inconvenient.

On the southern coast of the island of Kauai is the Russian Fort Elizabeth. This fortress was built in 1816, in an attempt to colonize the Hawaiian Islands and annex them to the Russian Empire. At that time, Kauai was the only island that did not obey King Kamehameha I, and the local ruler was not averse to collaborating with Russia, but under pressure from other Hawaiians and Americans, the Russians were forced to leave within a year. Around the same time, news reached St. Petersburg, and the central government decided not to persist in Hawaii, so as not to worsen relations with the United States.

Honolulu

The main island of the Hawaiian archipelago, not in size but in population, is the island of Oahu, where the capital Honolulu is located. It is a large city (400k population), and quite dense by American standards. Getting around the city is easy and inexpensive (about $5 a day) by public transport, and there is not much natural beauty on Oahu compared to the previous islands, so I didn’t rent a car, and finally relaxed - you can go anywhere without thinking about parking .

Honolulu has two main areas - the business center and the tourist area around Waikiki Beach. 99% of tourists are in Waikiki, where all the hostels and most hotels are located. While in Honolulu, I couldn't help but go to the site of one of the battles of World War II - Pearl Harbor. On December 7, 1941, Japanese planes carried out an air raid on the American naval base in Honolulu. By destroying the American ships, the Japanese hoped to gain time to capture maximum territories in the Pacific Ocean, so that they could then be held along the perimeter, preventing the enemy from getting close to their territory. I used to think that the results were more convincing for the Japanese, but after studying this history more closely in the museum, I learned that the damage was not so catastrophic - 4 battleships and several smaller ships were sunk, and only two were sunk irrevocably. The aircraft carriers were generally in a different place and were not damaged, and they played an important role in the war, where air supremacy was most important. Just six months later, the Japanese lost the decisive battle for Midway Island west of Hawaii, the capture of which would have given them a chance to control this part of the Pacific Ocean, after that they retreated for a long time all the way until 1945.

Pearl Harbor has the most absurd security system in the world: all bags, even plastic ones, are prohibited. You need to put everything in your pockets. Carrying a camera in your hand is not prohibited. At the same time, there is no X-ray at the entrance, which means it is theoretically possible to carry a grenade in your pocket.

Pearl Harbor is an active base, and the vast majority of it is closed to the public.
