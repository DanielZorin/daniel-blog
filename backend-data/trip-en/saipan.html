Northern Mariana Islands

16-17 June 2019

The Northern Mariana Islands are a US overseas territory in Micronesia. Until the end of the 19th century, they belonged to Spain, then Germany bought them, after Germany’s defeat in World War I, the islands were transferred to Japan, which developed them well, but lost them in 1945 to the United States. As in Puerto Rico and Virgin Islands, local residents have US citizenship, but do not have representation in federal government bodies and do not participate in presidential elections. When flying from the mainland US territory (there are direct flights from Hawaii), there is no passport control, but there is customs control. It’s a long way from the USA, and it’s expensive to fly, so to develop tourism, practical Americans sacrificed the sacred inviolability of borders and abolished visas on the islands for residents of all four nearby large countries: the Japanese and Koreans don’t need it anyway, and the Russians and Chinese have a special visa-free regime. There are many Northern Mariana Islands, but only three are inhabited, and 90% of the population lives on the largest - Saipan.

I arrived in Saipan from Hong Kong at 5 am. There is almost no public transport on the island. It seems like there is one line in the capital area, opened only in 2018 and with a frequency of once every 2 hours. I thought, why not kill a lot of time before checking into the hotel and walk to the city? Fortunately, my backpack was not particularly heavy, and early in the morning it was not hot outside. I covered the distance to the city of about 12 kilometers in three and a half hours. At the same time I saw virtually the entire southern populated part of the island. The airport is built in the shape of the traditional houses of the islands' indigenous population.

Stores accept food stamps. For a long time, migrant labor was exploited in Saipan because US minimum wage laws did not apply there. To see the northern part of the island, I needed to buy an excursion, because taxis in Saipan are prohibitively expensive. I walked around Garapan twice and found only one place that offered tours, and only in Chinese. Well, why not in Chinese? The excursion cost only $25 and was conducted in a roomy eight-seater passenger car. I also saw large 50-seater buses in parking lots; you can probably get on them through guides in large hotels.

The flight from Saipan to the island of Guam (200 km, 40 minutes travel time) is operated by the American airline United. Surprisingly, on such a short flight they use a huge Boeing 737 aircraft. That's because that flight to Guam is part of a chain of flights connecting all the main islands of Micronesia: Saipan, Guam, Chuuk, Pohnpei, Kwajalein, Majuro and finally the Hawaiian Islands. There are also flights from Guam to the islands of Yap and Palau. There are simply no other flights to the mainland from this region (except for Palau, where there are flights from Korea), so all residents of Micronesian countries fly this way. This flight is popular among airline enthusiasts who at times travel from Honolulu to Guam without even stopping at the intermediate islands. A ticket to Guam costs $120, and then each subsequent flight costs 400-500 each, so you can fly relatively inexpensively only with bonus miles, although I don’t know how to get so many United miles without living in the USA.
