Czech Republic: Brno, Austerlitz

23-25 May 2012

Austerlitz

On the Pratzen Heights, where he had fallen with the flagstaff in his hand, lay Prince Andrew Bolkónski bleeding profusely and unconsciously uttering a gentle, piteous, and childlike moan. Toward evening he ceased moaning and became quite still. He did not know how long his unconsciousness lasted. Suddenly he again felt that he was alive and suffering from a burning, lacerating pain in his head. “Where is it, that lofty sky that I did not know till now, but saw today?” was his first thought. “And I did not know this suffering either,” he thought. “Yes, I did not know anything, anything at all till now. But where am I?” He listened and heard the sound of approaching horses, and voices speaking French. He opened his eyes. Above him again was the same lofty sky with clouds that had risen and were floating still higher, and between them gleamed blue infinity. He did not turn his head and did not see those who, judging by the sound of hoofs and voices, had ridden up and stopped near him. The Austerlitz adventure began when I took a train from Brno to the village of Blažovice. The next plan was to reach Prace, where the Battle of Austerlitz memorial is located, and then walk to Slavkov u Brna, the city formerly called Austerlitz. However, the plan failed due to the fact that I chose the wrong direction across the field, towards the village of Krenovice. Having found out the way from the local residents, I had to make a detour and go to Prace through Zbysov, as a result there was no time or energy left for the ten-kilometer path to Slavkov through Krenovice. Fortunately, the bus driver took us a couple of stops to Slavkov and even allowed us not to punch the ticket.

And here it is, the memorial to the Battle of Austerlitz! Near it there is civilization in the form of a small cafe. There I also got a detailed map of the roads of Moravia, which, in principle, can be used to figure out how to get here.

Prace is the final stop of this route, and after carefully studying the schedule at the stop, I came to the conclusion that there are no other buses here, which means it is impossible to get directly to Slavkov. We had to take the old road to Krenovice.

Brno

I went to Brno for a conference, so I didn’t have much time to explore the city, and after Austerlitz I had almost no energy left. Therefore, I limited myself to the central part of the city and the main attractions. However, the hotel where the conference was held was located on the farthest outskirts, where it took about 20 minutes to get from the center by bus, so I went around there a little. The outskirts look something like this.

Almost one-story America in the center of post-socialist Europe. Two signs on the porch - a house number with a street name and something unclear. Zebetin is the name of the district, but I don’t understand the principle by which numbers like 876 are displayed. The odd/even number does not depend on the side of the street; neighboring houses may have numbers that differ by several dozen.

Mailbox, without the traditional chimney sign.

Here and there in the backyards there are pastoral landscapes, vegetable gardens and barbed wire.

Public transport in Brno, represented by trams and buses, is very well organized. The buses have an electronic display that shows the current stop, the next few, and even whether you need to press a bell button to get off at the stop.

The schedule is accurate to the minute; there is a website through which you can plot a route between any two points in Brno and the surrounding area for several tens of kilometers. Fares depend on zones and time; one ticket can be used to transfer as many times as you like within the zone and time of its validity. The only thing that is not taken into account is that ticket machines only accept cash in coins, and it’s inconvenient to carry a pocket full of 50 CZK coins. Plus, ATMs only issue banknotes, and drivers, on the contrary, may not sell a 35-crown ticket if given a 100-crown banknote. Because of this, on the evening of arrival I missed my bus to the outskirts, and having bought a ticket at a tobacco kiosk, I relaxed at the stop for about 30 minutes.
