Taman Negara

There is only one national park in continental Malaysia and it is simply called "National Park", in Malay: Taman Negara. From Kuala Lumpur you first need to drive three hours inland to the city of Jerantut, then either an hour by bus or by boat on the river. I checked into a hotel in Jerantut and arrived by the first bus at 7 am.

It was still cool and foggy at this early hour. The bus arrives at the village of Kuala Tahan, the park starts on the other side of the river, where you can take a boat for 1 ringgit. The entrance fee to the park is a nominal 1 ringgit (1/4 dollar).

Near the entrance there are many bungalows for rent; luggage is lifted to them on a special trolley on rails.

The park's territory is many square kilometers, the vast majority of which is inaccessible jungle. The original idea was that the park preserves the rainforest, unlike other areas, which in Malaysia have largely been converted into palm plantations. Don't expect any spectacular scenery, but if you've never been to a tropical rainforest, this is one of the most accessible places in Southeast Asia. If you are not ready to go on a multi-day hike with a tent, then you can walk along several equipped routes through the forest.

For the most part, these are pathways like this. There is a thick layer of wet leaves on the ground, making it uncomfortable to walk on.

At 7 am there were no people at all. After a couple of hours, groups arrived, but only to the main path along the river, from the entrance in an easterly direction. If you go from the entrance along the track to the north, or turn off the main path deeper towards the hill, there is no one there.

In some places there are gazebos, even two-story ones, made for shelter in case of rain and for overnight stays.

Cicadas sing loudly in the forest and you can periodically hear monkeys jumping in the upper levels of the trees.

In general, there are various animals in the park, even hornbills, but it is unlikely to meet anyone on a short walk. I only met a flying lizard.

Johor Bahru

In Malaysia, prices are on average three times lower than in Singapore, so it is not surprising that there is a large flow of pedestrians at the border. The most desirable things - cigarettes and alcohol - cannot be brought back to Singapore without customs duty in any volume, but no one bothers you to drink in Malaysia and at the same time buy food. The border terminal is a whole multi-level city with shops, a bus station, and a railway.

Car parking is very important for Malays. You shouldn't go to Singapore by car. Interestingly, bus travel across the border is three times cheaper if you buy a ticket in Malaysia for Malayan currency - if you are coming from this side, you should buy a round-trip ticket.

Johor is certainly not Singapore. In general, this is probably the most unpleasant and dirty city in all of Malaysia - easy money corrupts. The entire center is an endless construction site and a heap of unknown things. There is really no public transport.

Shopping centers are being built and rebuilt here.

Seremban

In Malaysia, nine state monarchies take turns delegating their ruler to the position of head of state. Eight of them are ruled by dynasties, and the ninth is Malaysia in miniature, the state consists of nine parts, each with its own influential families, and the head of the state is chosen from among them. The state is called “Nine States”, Negeri Sembilan. The largest city in this state is called Seremban.

Ipoh

Like Kuala Lumpur, Ipoh grew out of a small settlement where tin was mined in the late 19th century.

Perak was the last state in Malaysia that I visited. I soon moved to Indonesia, so I couldn’t stay in Ipoh, but in general this city is well worth a few days - in addition to the historical center, there is a lot of beautiful karst around, which can be seen from the highway.

Where there is no karst, palm trees grow along the highway throughout Malaysia. Palm oil is made from them.

The Perak state flag looks suspiciously like the flag of Russia that we lost. Only upside down.

There is quite a lot of colonial architecture left in Ipoh - less than in Penang, but more than in the average Malay town. Here is the train station. Ipoh is almost the only city in Malaysia where it might make sense to go by train, however, the bus is still more convenient - 30% cheaper and runs to KL several times an hour, rather than once every two hours. Also, all tickets were sold out on the day of departure. The train is really faster, and the stations are in the city center.
