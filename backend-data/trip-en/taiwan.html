Taiwan: Taipei, Hualien, Kaohsiung, Tainan, Chiayi, Taichung

18-30 July 2015

Taipei

Once upon a time, the island of Taiwan was the ancestral home of modern Filipinos and Indonesians, then it was colonized by the Chinese from the mainland. In the 17th century, the island managed to receive the name “beautiful” (Formosa) from the Portuguese, to be a colony of The Netherlands, and then for 40 years it was controlled by the supporters of the overthrown Ming dynasty. History repeated itself two hundred years later: first the island was colonized by the Japanese, taking it from the Chinese following the war in 1895, and after the defeat of Japan in 1945 and the civil war in China, opponents of the communist regime moved there, and they still rule the island. Like Korea, Taiwan was ruled by dictators who brutally suppressed freedom, and democracy came only 30 years ago. It is curious that the Chinese National Party (KMT) has not disappeared, but is still one of the leading political forces, although in 2016 they lost the elections.

Taiwan is an unrecognized state with a unique status: formally they claim the entire territory of another state. You can only recognize one of the Chinese governments - Beijing or Taipei, and 90% of countries, of course, recognize Beijing, although about 20 countries that are very far from China recognize Taiwan. In Taiwan itself, of course, no one seriously believes in the possibility of conquering back all of China. The dispute is rather between supporters of unification with the mainland on some terms (in Beijing they want it to be like with Hong Kong, but in Taiwan not so much) and supporters of independence. Initially, Taiwan called itself nothing less than the “Republic of China”; under this name its representatives even worked at the UN until 1971 instead of representatives of mainland China. Now even the visa says “Republic of China - Taiwan,” but they tried to join the UN simply as the “Republic of Taiwan.”

Relations with Beijing are gradually improving, in particular, relatively recently, residents of the PRC were allowed to travel to Taiwan for tourism purposes (at the airport, a whole crowd of Chinese stood in line for control with some kind of special questionnaires in addition to passports). There are direct flights from many Chinese cities, although the most frequent flight connection is still with Hong Kong. Taiwanese tourists are quite privileged - they do not need visas to most developed countries, even to the USA, Canada and Australia. Interestingly, for visa-free entry to the USA, Schengen, etc. Taiwanese not only need a passport, but also their ID card. The fact is that since Taiwan formally claims all of China, any Chinese is, technically, a citizen of Taiwan. Therefore, only the owner of an ID card who has all the rights in Taiwan (in particular, the right to work) is considered a Taiwanese, and this card is not issued to just everyone easily.

Chiang Kai-shek ruled Taiwan for life, and now sits in bronze like Lincoln.

The honor guard, having marched in a dense crowd of spectators, goes into the elevator.

The controversial status did not prevent Taiwan from becoming one of the most developed and successful countries in the world. Try going to numbeo.com and find a city where Local Purchasing Power is higher than Taipei! I did not find. This means that although the absolute value of salaries in Taiwan is lower than, say, in the United States, you can live much better on this salary, because Taiwan is really cheap. I would say that prices for food, travel, hotels are only 30 percent higher than on the mainland, and the average salary in Taipei is about $2,000 per month.

Tainan

"Taipei" simply means "northern Taiwan". If there is a city in the north, there must be one in the south, and it is called Tainan. This was the capital before Taipei. There's also Taichung (in the middle) and Taitung (on the east coast).

Tainan is considered the cultural capital, there are many temples and some historical buildings.

For example, the remains of a Dutch fort.

A temple was built above. Students go there to pray before the exam, hanging appropriate signs with wishes.

The most important distinctive feature of Taiwanese temples is that they are integrated into the street development so much that they can be closely adjacent to neighboring houses, whereas in all other countries with different religions the temples stand separately.

Chiayi

If you look at the bus schedule to Alishan Park from Taipei, at first you are surprised at their inconvenience: they leave at 11 pm and arrive at 4 am. In fact, such a schedule is not accidental, but so that you can be on the top of the mountain exactly at dawn. I was not ready for such sacrifices, so I took a bus from the nearest city of Chiayi at around 7 am.

The morning was frosty and the coffee was just right.

The entrance of the 7-11 is decorated in Chinese style.

Previously, you could get right to the top via a narrow-gauge railway, which was an attraction in itself. However, several years ago it was damaged by a typhoon, and now you can only get to the top by bus. Trains run within the park itself. There are several departures to the observation deck early in the morning at dawn, and the rest of the time you can ride through the park, which I was planning to do, but it didn’t work out.

Monument to Chiang Kai-shek at the beginning of the trail to the summit.

The path looks something like this.

Empty terminal station of the railway.

And here it is, under the clouds, Mount Yushan - the highest point in Taiwan, more than 5000 meters.

View of the valley.

The observation deck is almost empty during the day.

Then the clouds came and covered the whole view. Then it started to rain, and we went down to a large two-story gazebo shelter.

After some time the rain stopped. The plan was to go down and then walk around the park and take the train. As soon as we walked about a kilometer and a half down, the real heavy rain began. There was nowhere to hide on the bare road in the forest. The umbrella, of course, saved the day - you can squat under it, hugging the backpack with the camera, and avoid getting your upper clothes wet. But when the boots were completely flooded with water, there was nothing to lose. When we got to the bus station, the rain stopped. All that was left was to dry off at the nearest hotel and go down.

And spend the evening in the city of Chiayi.
