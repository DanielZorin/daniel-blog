Cambodia: Siem Reap, Angkor, Kratie, Banlung, Stung Treng

11-23 December 2015

Roads

There are no passenger trains or domestic flights (except for rare flights from Sihanoukville to Siem Reap) in Cambodia, so to get around the country you will have to sit on buses. Locals still ride in the back of trucks, but it is much slower, uncomfortable, and for tourists the price is the same as a bus. The bus system in Cambodia is organized inconveniently, so it is difficult to understand it from the outside. There are a bunch of companies, all of them have their own offices and departure points at different ends of the city. There are bus stations, usually on the outskirts, even in small towns. The bus station is just a large field with buses, where sometimes there are folding tables with ticket sellers. With the exception of the Capitol company, whose station is located in the center of Phnom Penh, tourists can actually buy tickets at a travel agency or city ticket office with taxi to the bus station included.

I have no idea how transfers are organized. We drove from Siem Reap to Banlung all day from 8 am to 10 pm, with two transfers in Kampong Cham and Kratie. It was a regular bus to Kampong Cham, and then two minibuses, in which, fortunately, we managed to grab seats in the front row, where it was not so crowded. How money paid in Siem Reap is transferred to a company without a sign that owns minibuses on the other side of the country, I don’t know. However, I didn’t notice that locals paid anything at all on these minibuses. Although there seem to be tariffs at the Kratie bus station.

Roads in Cambodia 20 years ago were completely absent; travel was only possible along rivers, especially during the rainy season. Now there is virtually no river transport, and all major cities are connected by normal roads.

This is the highway from Phnom Penh to the Thai border via Battambang. Decent throughout.

But the road from Phnom Penh to Siem Reap north of Tonle Sap Lake let us down. There is a section of about 50 kilometers where the road is laid out in the best traditions of remote Russian villages. This section can be driven around along a normal road with a small detour, but the bus chose to drag along this dusty patch of potholes.

Cambodia Gate - border at Poipet.

The speed of travel in Cambodia is low - about 40-50 kilometers per hour.

Every self-respecting bus will have an entertainment program. It consists of the Cambodian comedy shows and a selection of music videos. Cambodian videos were made at the technical level of Russia in the early 1990s. The average plot is like this. The singer and his girlfriend are sitting in a street eatery, where they are enjoying fried noodles for a dollar. Then the girl meets a rich prince and leaves with him in a cool car, and the singer suffers and dreams of returning her. There is no happy ending. It is better not to travel on buses at night, because the entertainment program will disturb your sleep.

The buses stop every few hours for a pit stop at a roadside restaurant. It looks something like this. All restaurants in Cambodia have a bright red sign with a telephone number and the logo of one of the beer brands: Angkor, Anchor or Cambodia. In tourist places the name is written in English, in non-tourist places - only in Khmer.

Billboards advertising beer stand along all the roads.

I didn’t like the food at these roadside restaurants; I always took a supply of food with me. In general, in my opinion, it’s inconvenient to spend half an hour on these lunch breaks instead of getting there faster. In Europe, for example, buses do not stop except to drop off passengers. I suspect that the bus companies make a profit from these restaurants.

Moreover, the prices there are not low - eating rice costs $2.5. I only bought fruit at these stations, if they had it.

The rest of the time I walked along the highway.

Siem Reap

Siem Reap mostly consists of hotels and restaurants serving tourists coming to Angkor (which is 90 percent of all visitors to Cambodia). There is little of interest, it is all somewhere far from the center.

The average street in Siem Reap looks neater and newer than the average in Cambodia.

The main streets are filled with bars and pubs. In terms of the degree of westernization, Siem Reap is superior to Phnom Penh.

Elephant monument.

Since Siem Reap is very touristy, it is difficult to find food at reasonable prices in the center. I most often dined at one establishment where the prices were normal, $1.5-2 per dish. Steven Corner.

The signature dish is grilled ribs.

I came across an unknown fruit while walking around. Attempts to find out its name from sellers were unsuccessful. Much later, I learned the name from the Thais and found out that it was Limonia Acidissima, or elephant apple. The taste is strange, like cinnamon cake.

Travel agencies sell bus tickets. You can get to Phnom Penh for just $4.5. To Banlung (Ratanakiri), the cheapest I found was $16, the rest were asking for $22-25.

The New Year was approaching, Christmas trees were being placed near hotels.

A flock of flying foxes lives in one of the main squares of Siem Reap. These are animals similar to bats, only much larger.

They look impressive in photographs, but in reality they shy away from people, preferring to sit high in trees, where they can only be reached with a very big lens.

Angkor

Angkor is the most famous place in Cambodia, and throughout Southeast Asia. They often say and write “Angkor Wat”, meaning the entire archaeological park, but this is incorrect, because Angkor Wat is only one temple, although many people’s acquaintance with Angkor ends there.

In 90% of photographs, Angkor Wat appears to be standing over a lake.

When you first approach from Siem Reap, it’s not clear: there seems to be a wide ditch, with the temple itself in the distance.

In fact, the reflection is being filmed in this puddle.

Hundreds of people come to Angkor Wat at five in the morning to photograph the sunrise over this puddle in the terrible crowd and crush. If you wish, you can shoot here.

Entrance to Angkor costs $20. You can also buy a three-day ticket for $40, but I decided it was better to get up early and see everything in one day. All tickets are personalized and with photographs taken right at the ticket office, so you won’t be able to take someone else’s three-day ticket. Theoretically, if you use Photoshop, a ticket can be faked, since it is checked only with the eyes, the barcode is not read. But most of those who got to Angkor for free simply ran from the controllers. In principle, you can even enter the territory along a secondary road without control, not to mention walking through the forest, where there are no fences. The problem is that you need to somehow move around the territory: you can’t walk 40 kilometers in a day in the heat, and you can’t ride a bicycle either. All significant temples will also have to be accessed from the back entrance, so I think it makes sense to spend $20 here. As a means of transport, then you can take a tuk-tuk with a driver; you can move around quickly and inexpensively - within $15 for the whole day. You can rent a car, but it is much more expensive and inconvenient to drive on narrow roads. You cannot rent a motorcycle (this would ruin the tuk-tuk business), unless you rent it in another province and come on it.

First you need to decide on the route. If you are not an archaeologist, then it hardly makes sense to visit absolutely all temples, including those located 20-30 kilometers from the main ones. To consolidate knowledge about the Khmer Empire, you can later visit Khmer temples in Thailand (Phanom Rung, Phimai) and Laos (Wat Phu). Based on online reports and area surveys, most tourists go to the other extreme, visiting only three points - Angkor Wat, Bayon and Ta Prohm. You won’t be able to do more with the excursion, because you need a couple of hours for lunch and another couple of hours to wait for those who are late.

To better understand the situation, I will insert a map. The square surrounded by a moat in the middle is Angkor Thom, once the largest city in the world. To the south is Angkor Wat, the main temple. The center of Siem Reap is 6-7 kilometers to the south. Accordingly, first you come to Angkor Wat, then to Angkor Thom. From there you can move in two directions. If you go east, you get the so-called “Small Circle”. There will be the popular Ta Prohm temple and a couple more. The road will turn south and lead back to Angkor Wat. If you go north, you get the "Great Circle". Since in the “Small Circle” there are essentially only two temples (but large ones), it can be combined with a large one; the detour from the road to Ta-Prom is small. I would also like to note that if you have a ticket for the next day, you can visit Angkor Wat after 5 pm. There is definitely not enough time before sunset for a full visit, but it will still significantly save time on the main day. I visited Angkor Wat at the very end. You shouldn’t waste time on lunch, it’s still expensive to eat on the territory of Angkor, and going to the city is a waste of an hour of time. It’s wise to stop at a store and market on the way in the morning, stock up on high-calorie food, fortunately you don’t need to carry anything with you, you can leave it in a tuk-tuk.

Banlung

To explore the Cambodian hinterland, I went to the city of Banlung, which is located near the junction of the borders of Laos, Cambodia and Vietnam. This is practically the remotest corner of the region. Here you can even find such a rarity for Cambodia in modern times as off-road conditions - if you try to travel south to Sen Monorom.

I traveled from Siem Reap to Banlung all day - from seven in the morning to ten in the evening. The minibus stopped at the central square, where I checked into the first hotel for only 5 dollars. The hotel was very decent, there was Wi-fi and hot water.

But there was no air conditioning, that's why it was so cheap. And now the best part: in Banlung you don’t really need air conditioning! Vietnamese Dalat, by the way, is not far from here, the border is open, even buses run regularly.

Stung Treng

The city of Stung Treng is hidden on the northern outskirts of Cambodia near the border with Laos. Once upon a time it was possible to get there only by boat along the Mekong, but now the roads are quite normal, the city is developing and becoming similar to other cities.

People come to Stung Treng for one purpose - transit to Banlung, Kratie or Laos. The border with Laos and 4,000 islands are located about 50 kilometers away; there are no cities or settlements closer to the border. I stayed at the most luxurious hotel in Stung Treng, the Riverside, for $7.
