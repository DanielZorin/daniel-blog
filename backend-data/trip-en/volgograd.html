Russia: Volgograd, Elista, Astrakhan

2-5 August 2014

Volgograd

The first thing I saw in Volgograd was a sign next to which everyone takes pictures.

There is also a deputy in glasses with a suitable first and last name Artem Lebedev - sounds similar to Artemy Lebedev, who designed the sign above.

Volgograd airport, like most Russian airports, is small and cramped. There are 3-4 flights a day from the airport to Moscow and one to Turkey, but the waiting room is so small that even passengers of one flight are cramped. In Soviet times, airports did not have modern security systems, so there is less waiting space there than at a train station.

The main (and practically the only) attraction of Volgograd is Mamayev Kurgan.

First of all, this is, of course, the gigantic Motherland.

And also several interesting sculptural compositions.

Just like in the USSR, the rulers loved to praise themselves. Modern bureaucrats are not so vain, but more selfish.

By the way, it was the day of the Airborne Forces, the paratroopers were swimming in the fountains.

Another attraction of Volgograd is the Volga-Don Canal named after Lenin. You can look at the gateways.

On the arch it is written: “Glory to the great Lenin.”

At the entrance to the canal there is a monument to Lenin, notable because it is the largest monument to Lenin in the world. And before the Khrushchev era, an equally huge monument to Stalin stood on the other pedestal. On the day that Stalingrad was renamed Volgograd, the monument was demolished.

More attractions: Pavlov's House (it took many days for the Germans to storm it during the battle).

A colonnade was attached to it on the reverse side and a monument to Lenin was erected.

This is what Stalingrad looked like in 1943.

Field Marshal Paulus surrendered in the basement of this house.

There is a good embankment along the Volga.

But the biggest impression was made by Volgograd roads. Of all the regional centers I've been to, Volgograd has the worst roads.

A trip to the Lenin Canal is an hour of terrible shaking in a minibus, despite the fact that most of the way goes along a large highway, half consisting of potholes.

Panorama of the city.

The climate is arid, there is almost a steppe around the city.

In Volgograd, it is completely unjustifiable to construct underground passages even through small streets.

Transport is mostly minibuses, although there are buses and trolleybuses, and there is even a metro tram - a tram that runs underground in the city center.

The meme city of Uryupinsk is located in the Volgograd region, you can get there by bus, although it’s not close, it takes 5-6 hours.

Elista

It is unusual in the European part of Russia to come to a city where Asian-looking people live and profess Buddhism. Kalmykia can be said to be the westernmost region of Asia.

Pagodas and stupas have been built throughout the city.

You can often see residents walking around the stupas in a circle and praying. And you don’t need to go to India or China!

And there are drums.

Kalmykia is formally one of the poorest regions of Russia, but Elista looks good. The example of Ingushetia, however, has already shown that GDP per capita and economic conditions in Russia do not always correlate.

The roads in the city are decent everywhere.

Opposite the bus station there is a very good hotel for 1200 rubles per day.

Prices are low.

Traditional Russian art is present.

Stands with national patterns.

Lanterns too.

Bus stop.

In Kalmykia, Kirsan Ilyumzhinov was the president for a long time. The cult of his personality is impressive - probably in no other region are streets named after the head of the region during his lifetime, even in Chechnya .

Ilyumzhinov is no longer the president of Kalmykia, but still heads the International Chess Federation. Therefore, chess tournaments are held in Elista, and there is also a “chess city”, as if the embodiment of Ostap Bender’s fantasy about New Vasyuki (real Vasyuki, let me remind you, is Kozmodemyansk).

The scale of the chess city, of course, is far from cosmic.

By and large, this is a cottage community with a couple of restaurants.

After two streets the city ends and the steppe begins.

All sorts of sculptural compositions with a chess theme are placed on the streets.

And in the middle there is a chess palace.

You can also play a party in the central square.

I've never seen such an appropriate name for a mobile phone store before.

Kalmykia is a large and sparsely populated steppe.

Apart from Elista, there are essentially no cities there, only villages.

Here's one, for example.

But this is more of a picture.

Sometimes you can see salt marshes along the road.

But mostly - the road and pillars. The road from Elista to Astrakhan is dozens of kilometers without a single settlement, without a single tree. Conducive to thinking about the eternal.

Astrakhan

Far, far in the south, among the vast lifeless steppes, there is a city where for some reason they are very fond of vulgar jokes.

Astrakhan is the last large city on the Volga, for a long time it was actually the southernmost region of Russia, behind which Kalmyks and Dagestanis lived. The Astrakhan region is still sparsely populated; for the most part it is steppe.

The proximity of the Caucasus is felt here. Around the station there are authentic Dagestan canteens.

Many bus routes to the south, as well as trains towards Makhachkala and Baku. Here is a Eurolines bus going to Grozny.

And then the Nazis arrive.

A real village begins to the north of the station.
