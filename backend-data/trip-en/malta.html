Malta

15-16 March 2014

Valletta

I was in Malta for two days on the way from Marseille to Milan. Two flights on Ryanair cost around €80, which is roughly the same price as a bus ticket and much cheaper than a train. I expected Malta to be the second Cyprus, that is, a faceless gray small island country where people go to lie on the beach and learn English. It turned out to be exactly the opposite: Malta is an amazing, unique country in which you can spend time with great interest. I really regretted that there was so little time.

Prices contribute a lot to a positive impression of Malta. Malta is very cheap by European standards. For a measly one and a half euros you can buy a day pass for all buses on the island! The hotel cost 15 euros. There are a lot of negative reviews about it on booking.com, which were apparently written by clowns with inadequate expectations. In fact, it was one of the best hotels I've ever been to, for such a price, with a huge room that even had a kitchen, plus a good breakfast in the morning. In summer the price is 3-4 times higher, but there is no point in going to Malta in summer - it is very hot, lying on the beach there is simply criminal when there is so much beauty around. Spring or autumn is best. The only thing that was confirmed from the reviews is that the reception may not be able to cope with the flow of clients, sometimes I had to wait for 5 minutes, but this is expected, since the hotel has as many as 800 rooms (usually the hotels where I stay have no more than 20 rooms). This hotel is also located relatively far from Valletta, about 30-40 minutes drive. The place is called Paceville (c is pronounced as English "ch", the Italian way), this is the main party place in Malta, at night there were student celebrations there even in March. Administratively, Paceville is a district of the city of St Julian's, although the boundaries between the cities in Malta are often difficult to trace.

Coast at St Julian's. There is a small beach in Paceville. As far as I understand, the main beaches are located to the north.

The Maltese language is close to Arabic. It seems to me that Malta is what you get if you take some Arab city like Amman or Casablanca, and make Europe there.

Mdina

In the depths of the island is the old capital of Malta with the name Mdina, which is well understood by anyone who has been to Arab countries.

Mdina is even smaller than Valletta, but there is practically no population, and there are not a lot of shops and restaurants, so the city looks a bit empty.

But here, as in other places in Malta, it is very beautiful.

The sculptures in the corners are familiar from Valletta.

From the fortress wall you can see Valletta on the horizon.

There are also unusual door handles everywhere in Mdina.

In souvenir shops you can buy something for every taste.
