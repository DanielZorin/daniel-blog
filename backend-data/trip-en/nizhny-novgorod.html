Russia: Nizhny Novgorod, Gorodets

25-26 May 2013

Gorodets

There’s a lot you can learn by looking at bulletin boards in different cities. For example, in Moscow, riot police are busy breaking up opposition rallies, and in the city of Gorodets, Nizhny Novgorod region, riot police can take part in your birthday party.

Someone is selling manure.

Someone came to a very bad life, with three higher education degrees from England he became a tutor.

Tour offers in small towns are usually trips to monasteries in nearby areas or to the Black Sea coast. An interesting offer - a tour to Ivanovo for only 200 rubles, I am sure that a one-way regular bus costs more.

As usual, there are ubiquitous offers to take out a loan, with a whole week of grace period and 0.7% per day. But in cash, not by credit card.

Insightful advertising: "Let's meet on Sunday. God"

Someone thought of putting up street signs in Gorodets with Old Russian font. Now the names of revolutionaries are written in this style.

There are also old-looking signs on which the former names of some streets are mentioned.

The bus schedule is surprisingly clear.

Here and there there are fancy placebo buttons that turn on the green light. The road is not wide, and there are few cars on it.

The slogan of the Gorodets trash bins is “For a clean city!” It's written everywhere. The city is really not dirty.

Nizhny Novgorod

There are few places where you will find as much sparkling humor and creativity on the streets as in Russia. Can you imagine a funny advertisement or sign in, say, Germany? No. And in Russia they are at every step. So the story about Nizhny Novgorod will have nothing unnecessary, just like this poster says.

You're walking down the street and there are drugs advertisements all around. “Bookmarks” mean that the goods are not passed from hand to hand, but are placed in a hidden place, the location of which is communicated for non-cash payment. How this saves from provocations is unclear.

You go further - someone is making fun of the little piglet Peter. In principle, it is quite convenient to travel from Nizhny Novgorod. There are direct flights to Europe, there is a train to Moscow in 5 hours and 500 rubles.

In Nizhny Novgorod there is an oligarch who owns shawarma stalls painted in Pepsi colors.

The bus station is simply typical. By the way, bus stations in highly developed Hungary look approximately the same.

Oops - an athletics championship for babies!

I'm walking down the street and I hear retro music playing, Michael Jackson. The cassette recorder is standing right in the window. Back in 1994, when I lived in a residential area, hits from bands like “Dune” were constantly playing near my entrance, and for this, people brought tape recorders with them. I also remember well that on the wall of the entrance it was written “Yeltsin”.

In Nizhny Novgorod, political propaganda appears on the walls. A sign that the standard of living is high, people think about politics.

For some reason, Limonov's supporters (radical left National Bolshevist party) are especially active.

In general, the mood is optimistic.

More creative: "The law protects anyone who can hire a good lawyer".

Parking issues are very relevant in Nizhny Novgorod.

There are even traffic jams in the city.

Bor

Recently, a cable car across the Volga was built in Nizhny Novgorod.

I had only traveled by cable car in Rio de Janeiro before, so I couldn’t help but draw parallels.

Beauty.

On the other side of the Volga is the city of Bor. The road goes over picturesque islands.

Cows are grazing. Somewhere nearby a shepherd is lying on the grass.

The town of Bor is gloomy in places.

It seems that there was a shortage of paint in the USSR, so the houses were left the color of gray bricks. If they didn’t spare the red paint, life immediately would become more fun. Does anyone really like that the door from which the newlyweds come out looks like this?

Bor design.
