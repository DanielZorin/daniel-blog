Brazil: São Paulo, Santos

2-4, 25-27 March, 16 April, 1-9, 18-22 May, 1-4 June 2021

São Paulo

No less than eight years have passed since my first trip to South America. First, I methodically traveled around Europe, then Asia, then Oceania, with periodic visits to North America, which is easier and cheaper to get to. The time has come to begin a thorough study of this part of the world, and the closest and most accessible country is Brazil. All roads in Brazil lead to São Paulo, especially now that there are almost no international flights from other cities. As a result, I was in São Paulo a whopping 6 times in three months, for a total of almost 20 days.

I stayed at the Calstar Hotel, the price (100 reais for a double, 70 for a single), quality, excellent breakfast and location suited me completely, so I stayed there every time. This is the historical center - not the cleanest and safest area, but conveniently located and inexpensive.

On the street next to the hotel there were prostitutes every evening, and exclusively transsexual ones. I have not seen them anywhere else in São Paulo or in other cities, although I have been to other areas that are considered red light districts, in particular on Augusta Street, which leads directly from my hotel to the Paulista district.

I walked up and down the center of São Paulo. It can be roughly divided into two parts, separated by the former bed of the Anhangabau River, which now flows underground. On one side is the historical center with skyscrapers (Called Se = Cathedral), on the other is a shopping area with pedestrian streets and shops (Called Republica after Republica Square). This building houses the Museum of Art of São Paulo (MASP).

MASP has three floors - one underground and two above ground. On the underground floor there was an exhibition of the Brazilian artist Beatriz Milhazes.

On the ground floor there are sculptures by Edgar Degas.

And on the second floor there is the main exhibition. At first I thought I had missed something because the entrance was all modern art:

It turns out this is an unusual concept. Typically, in all museums, paintings are arranged in chronological order, from the early Renaissance to Cubism and Socialist Realism. But here, on the contrary, the farther from the entrance, the further you go into the depths of centuries. All the paintings are in one large hall in rows, and not hanging on the walls. So this one hall has a number of paintings equivalent to ten halls of an ordinary museum.

There is also no division by country; the works of Brazilian artists are on a par with foreign ones of the same era.

You might think that this is Aivazovsky, but no, this is a Brazilian artist.

In general, there are works of almost all famous artists, from Raphael and Bosch...

...to Modigliani and Dali.

And Van Gogh.

Here you can also see what is on the back of the paintings. And there are a lot of stickers that are attached when a painting is sent to another country for an exhibition.

In São Paulo, I traveled to some areas outside the center, but not as much as in other big cities (for example, Tokyo or London). It is convenient to travel in São Paulo by metro, which has many lines. Moreover, they belong to three different companies, plus the suburban trains are essentially also part of the metro system. Some lines run somewhere on the outskirts, allowing you only to transfer to the line leading to the center.

One day there was a strike in the metro: trains on three lines ran only in the center area, turning around 5-7 stations before the end of the line, and another line was closed. At the same time, the lines of other companies worked. The strike lasted one day.

There are three airports in São Paulo: international Guarulhos, domestic Congonhas and low-cost Viracopos (which I have not been to). Guarulhos is formally a separate city.

To Guarulhos, the main airport of the country, buses run once an hour, and trains also run once an hour (after the train you still need to take a shuttle to the main terminal). At the same time, train and bus schedules are such that there are 10 minutes between their departures, and if you are late, you have to wait 50-60 minutes.

Congonhas Airport is quite close to the city center. On takeoff you can see many high-rise buildings.

Evening landing in Congonhas.

Santos

The city of Santos is the closest port and beach to São Paulo. It only takes a little over an hour to get there by bus, and it is a very interesting and worthwhile trip.

The road itself is beautiful. It turned out that São Paulo is on a high plateau, and to go down to the sea, you need to drive along a steep serpentine road.

Panorama from the road.

Atlantic Forest, this landscape is listed as a World Heritage Site.

At the entrance to every Brazilian city there will be a Jesus, Santos is no exception.

Santos stands on a peninsula and has two centers - the old one on the northern shore near a narrow bay and the new one on the southern shore with access to the ocean. The bus arrives at the old center. The two centers are separated by a chain of high hills, or you can drive along the coast through the port and industrial zone.

There is a former prison on the square.

At first the center does not look remarkable.
