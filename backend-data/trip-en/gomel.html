Mir

There are two castles in Belarus, both included in the UNESCO World Heritage List, and both near Minsk. The only problem was visiting them together without returning to Minsk - for this you had to adapt to the schedule of the bus, which runs only once a day in each direction. The first was Mir Castle. It is formally located in Grodno region, almost on the border with Minsk region.

The village of Mir is quite small. It contains almost only private houses.

Veterinary hospital with street signs.

Bus station and small church.

Mir was a Jewish town (shtetl), and the largest building besides the castle was the Jewish Academy (Yeshiva). Now there is a hotel there.

The neighboring buildings housed synagogues and schools.

Central church.

Residential area.

Panorama of the town.

A small river separates the town from the castle.

Mir Castle belonged to the Radziwills - a very rich family of the Grand Duchy of Lithuania.

The castle was reconstructed quite substantially after the war.

Courtyard.

In the basement of the castle you can sit in a small cafe.

Interestingly, part of the castle is given over to a hotel - you can stay in the castle for only 60 dollars.

Men fish under the ice in the castle park.

The temperature was around zero, the snow was perfectly molded, and I couldn’t deny myself the pleasure of making a snowman.

Nesvizh

Nesvizh is a much larger city in the Minsk region than Mir. Here is the main castle of the Radziwills.

The main entrance is across the bridge over the moat.

Built in 1583.

Restored in 2010. The castle belonged to the Radziwills until the revolution of 1917, then it was destroyed, but a few years later Nesvizh came under Polish rule, and the prince returned the castle to himself. After the Second World War, the city became part of the USSR, and a sanatorium was located in the castle, as a result of which most of the decor was destroyed and restored only in the 21st century.

Gomel

The last regional center of Belarus where I had not been was Gomel, so on the way back I made a maneuver to go to Moscow by an indirect route: Minsk - Gomel - Novozybkov - Moscow. The center of Gomel is reminiscent of other Belarusian cities with Stalinist architecture in soft yellow and pink colors.
