One of the main attractions of Istanbul outside the center are the fortress walls that once protected Constantinople from enemies. Constantinople was a really big city, even now it’s about 10 stops by tram to get to the walls. However, in 1453, when the city was captured by the Turks, most of the territory inside the walls was just fields, as the city was severely depopulated as the Byzantine Empire declined, especially after the sack by the Crusaders in 1204.

Next to the walls there is a museum "Panorama 1453".

Almost like a panorama of the Battle of Borodino in Moscow.

The museum tells in detail about the fall of Byzantium. Unusually for us, the story is told from the Turkish point of view, which is very different from what is written in our school textbooks. Here are a couple of interesting perspectives on the situation.

The capture of Constantinople was in the long term a blessing for the city, since a dying city in a weak state turned into the brilliant capital of one of the greatest world powers.

The situation with religious tolerance improved with the arrival of the Turks, as the Sultan abolished the Union of Florence (the actual subordination of the Orthodox Church to the Pope, which the Byzantine emperor agreed to in an attempt to gain support from the West), and the inculcation of Catholicism, which caused protests from the local population, stopped.

In Istanbul there is one very interesting area very far from the center - the Princes' Islands. These are four small islands in the Sea of ​​Marmara, not far from the Asian coast, consisting of old villas, a sort of mini-resort in a metropolis. You can get there directly from the center, but ferries rarely run, the schedule is constantly changing, and you can’t rely on it, and it takes an hour and a half to sail. It is more convenient to take the Marmaray train to the pier in the Asian part, from which the trip is less than 30 minutes, and ferries run every hour. Well, to make it more interesting, I didn’t immediately take the train in the European part, but first moved to the Asian part by ferry to see one of the main local areas - Kadikoy.

Kayseri

I flew to the city of Kayseri to visit the famous Cappadocia. It takes about two hours by bus from Kayseri; usually no one stays in this city, but I, of course, did not fail to explore it, especially since it was the first non-capital city on this trip. Kayseri has almost a million inhabitants, in the top ten largest Turkish cities. The name, surprisingly, actually comes from the word Kaiser/Caesar, since it was once the Roman city of Caesarea.

Already on the way from the airport you can see how Türkiye is changing. This is how they lived before:

Göreme

Somehow it happened that it is in Cappadocia, and not anywhere else in the world, that you can easily fly in a hot air balloon. Usually you can fly in hot air balloons either at some festivals, or far from the city in splendid isolation, but here things are streamlined, there are a lot of tourists, therefore there are a lot of balloons at once, and the most beautiful landscapes below.

A hot air balloon flight costs approximately 60 euros. All the balloons take off near Göreme (the main city of Cappadocia, with the most hotels) early in the morning at dawn, some a little earlier, some a little later, thanks to this an epic picture is created with a large number of balloons.
