Nairobi

The capital of Kenya is quite an interesting city, but I was there at an unfortunate time. There were protests in Kenya against tax reform and against President William Ruto in general. About a month before my arrival, the protests were so violent that a couple dozen people died in battles with the police! The reform was cancelled, but the protests continued anyway. And on the day that I was going to Nairobi, a protest was planned. The day before, I was advised not to go to Nairobi, that it was not a fact that I would even be able to get to the center. But I did not want to waste a whole day, and it was also unclear how to bypass Nairobi, so I decided to go. At worst, I would just go somewhere to the outskirts and look for a hotel there.

Train ride

In Kenya, thanks to the efforts of Chinese investors, the railway was modernized, and now passenger trains run between Nairobi and Mombasa. I wouldn't say they are high-speed - they cover 500 kilometers in 6 hours. But it's definitely faster than a bus, not to mention the comfort. The only problem is buying a ticket, since payment is only through a Kenyan mobile phone via the M-Pesa system. But I managed, and in the morning I arrived at the train station. The station is very far from the center, even further than the airport.

Security measures are serious - before we got to the ticket office, we were all sniffed by dogs. In the ticket office, you can print out a ticket from a machine, I also bought a return ticket (you can pay by card here, but not cash).

The tickets look exactly like in China.
