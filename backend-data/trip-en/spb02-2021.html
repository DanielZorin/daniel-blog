15-18 February 2021

Saint Petersburg

Somehow, during so much time in St. Petersburg, I have never wandered onto the islands in the northern part of the Petrogradsky district. It's time to correct this mistake. These islands were a place for dachas for rich people in the 19th century (as the city grew, dachas moved further north to Sestroretsk and further to the Ozero cooperative Cooperative of Putin and his friends in the 90's). The closest metro station to Kamenny (Stone) Island is Chernaya Rechka. Lakhta Center is visible in the distance.

I walked across the pedestrian bridge to Elagin Island. This entire island is occupied by the Central Park of Culture and Rest. On weekends you even need to buy a ticket at the entrance (you can only get there via three bridges).

The snow lies beautifully on the bushes in the park.

Among the buildings, in addition to summer cafes and stalls, on this island there is the Elagin Palace and its extensions. The architect is the well-known Carl Rossi.

A pond on Elagin Island and a statue of Hercules in a box for the winter.

The development on this island is not particularly historical. Most of the island is occupied by a large Victory Park.

Krestovsky Ostrov metro station. At the far end of the island there is the Zenit Arena stadium, and a metro station. Since my last visit it was renamed from Novokrestovskaya to Zenit, but was never opened (as I understand, due to the coronavirus epidemic, it was closed in April 2020, since the station essentially serves only the stadium, and there are no residential buildings in its vicinity).

Lomonosov

The city of Lomonosov (formerly Oranienbaum) is the farthest piece of St. Petersburg on the southern side of the Gulf of Finland. Administratively, Lomonosov belongs to the Petrodvortsovy district, like neighboring Peterhof.

Central square of Lomonosov, here I got off the bus. In the center of the fountain is an orange tree, after which the city is named.

Popular sign "city of military glory".

Multi-colored houses. Architecturally, the city is quite solid and well preserved, although small.

The main attraction of the city is the Oranienbaum Palace. Peter's associate Menshikov began to build it; after his exile, the palace was not used for some time, then the future Emperor Peter III lived there with Catherine II.

Rows of fir trees in the park.

Rear view, across the yard.

The park is quite large and there are other buildings in it. Under Peter III, a wooden fortress that did not survive was built, of which a small palace and gate remained.

The only monument to Peter III in Russia (there is another one in Germany in his hometown Kiel)

Part of the garden is under renovation.

Under Catherine II, a couple more premises were completed: the Chinese Palace (only the interior design is Chinese).

On the way back, I stopped in the village of Strelna between Peterhof and Krasnoselsky district.

Here is the Putin Palace, also known as the Konstantinovsky Palace - the only palace in St. Petersburg that is used for state receptions and conferences.

You can only get there with a guided tour; there is no free entry to the territory.
