Bergamo

For the first time, I was late for a plane - due to a snowstorm, the departure of the Gdansk-Milan flight was delayed by three hours, and I literally missed the second flight from Milan to Brindisi by about 15 minutes. Since the delay was just under three hours, no compensation was due. I had to buy new tickets to Brindisi, fortunately, the next day they cost only 30 euros, that is, a little more than I paid a couple of months before. Though, the flight was inconvenient - 6 am, and as luck would have it, there were no cheap hotels and hostels. I had to stay in the city of Bergamo itself for more than 40 euros. By the time I figured out how to check into an automatic hotel without staff, it was already getting dark.

Bari

When I arrived in Bari it was already dark. I had to go through another check-in quest - people in Italy are so lazy that they don’t even want to sit at the reception during the day in a hotel with at least 15 rooms. In the morning, the following post-apocalyptic picture appeared before my eyes.

According to the plan, I was not supposed to hang around in Bari all day, but instead go to the beautiful city of Matera. But no. In general, trains go to Matera every hour or two, but on Sunday the trains have a day off (!), and on the official website of the railway company it was written that buses run instead. After standing at the bus stop for an hour, I gave up - apparently, working in bad weather is not part of the plans of the residents of Bari.

Naples

The Naples metro is famous for the fact that some of the stations there are decorated with various installations, like in Stockholm. Apart from these two cities, only in Moscow and St. Petersburg the metro can boast of the beautiful design of more than a couple of stations. The most famous station is Toledo.

The metro in Naples is relatively new, opened in 1993. The design of the stations was done less than 10 years ago.

Pompeii

For the first time I am writing about a city in which no one lives, and for almost 2000 years no less. Unlike Angkor and Pagan, Pompeii has preserved all the houses and streets, because they were made of stone and were not swallowed up by the jungle.

Pompeii starts from the train station. This reminds me of something... well, of course, India: where next to every attraction there are a dozen stalls with the inscription "Ticket office".

The central square of the city. Vesuvius can be seen from behind, the eruption of which covered Pompeii with ash.

The city is not that small, and the outlying streets in the residential areas are quite deserted.

These are small houses of poor people, and the alleys are even overgrown with grass.

The roofs of most of the buildings have collapsed, and of course the paint has peeled off, making the city look gray and red of bricks and stone. It didn't look like this in life.

Traces of chariots are visible on the roads.

The main streets have pedestrian crossings made of stones at sidewalk level to avoid stepping into the mud on the pavement.

The quality of the roads in Pompeii was not up to par in some places.

On the outskirts of Rome there is an area called EUR, where, under the rule of the fascists, it was planned to move the business center of the city. Everything is in the best traditions of totalitarian imperial architecture - wide avenues, pretentious palaces with columns, statues of pumped-up men, like in Ancient Greece. The city center was also partially changed to suit Mussolini's needs, for example, a wide and empty street leading past the forum to the Colosseum is his work.

There is a bazaar near the metro, they sell some second-hand goods for 2-3 euros.

Now it is an office area, but not that popular.
