India: Ahmedabad, Jodhpur, Pushkar, Ajmer, Jaipur

27 July - 6 August 2017

Ahmedabad

Ahmedabad is the capital of the state of Gujarat and one of the largest cities in India, almost unknown outside its borders. There are so few tourists there that I spent two hours looking for accommodation in the center, and they didn’t let me in anywhere. However, the historic center of Ahmedabad was recently inscribed as a World Heritage Site, the first city in India to receive such an honor (the rest are individual palaces/temples). Let's figure it out.

In Ahmedabad, a troublesome incident happened to me - I was supposed to leave by night train to Jodhpur, but when I arrived at the station, I received an SMS that my train was completely canceled. Some money was later returned to my card, but the question arose of what to do. Sitting on the floor of the station with a laptop, I quickly found out that there were no tickets for all of today's trains, even under the tourist quota. So I bought a ticket for the next day on the morning train, which I had to ride until the evening. I wanted to spend the night in the rest rooms at the station (in India, large train stations have them, just like in Russia), but there were no free places there. Then I found an inexpensive hotel for 500 rupees through Booking - it was not very far from the station, but not in the old city. * * *

The main feature of Ahmedabad is residential areas called "pol". A pol is a mini-district where some kind of community lives: a large family or people of the same caste or profession. It seems that this is an ordinary neighborhood with narrow streets, but in fact they all end in dead ends - the pol is isolated from neighboring areas, you can only enter through the main gate from the main street (or through the back doors). Many of Ahmedabad's pols retain rich wooden façades decorated with carvings.

It is clear that these places remember better times - now many wooden buildings are askew, the paint has peeled off the columns.

It’s not often that you see such sophisticated facades, carved doors and columns in ordinary houses.

The streets are clean - the whole area is like a common home, and the houses are not littered.

Sometimes there are entire multi-storey mansions in the alleys.

But more often than not, buildings have one floor, and the floor above is already owned by another person and with a different facade.

Balconies.

Outside there is already typical chaos.

Cows live here too.

Ahmedabad is perhaps one of the most interesting cities in India to walk the streets, there is not only trash and backward technology, but also beauty.

Jodhpur

Finally, I had a gorgeous view from the window in my hotel! The hotel itself was also the cheapest of the entire trip to India - only 180 rupees ($3), this is what a tourist city means, when the supply of housing is greater than the demand.

Jodhpur is not as famous as Jaipur because it takes longer to get there, but there are still more tourists here than in the 10 previous cities I visited combined. In the morning, looking out the window, I went to the fortress.

The weather was not good - it started to rain every now and then.

Pushkar

Pushkar is a very popular city among hippies and spiritual seekers. The concentration of tourists here is high, in addition, there are many pilgrims in Pushkar, because this city is also sacred, so much so that meat is generally prohibited in it: food is only vegetarian everywhere.

The main attraction is the lake, to which the ghats descend (like in Varanasi). The lake is quite small, you can walk around it in less than an hour, the city itself is not much larger.

Jaipur

Jaipur, one of the most popular cities in India, was for me about the fiftieth (and last) city in this country. I spent three days in Jaipur but didn't walk around the city very much.

Jaipur is the pink city.

The central streets are relatively clean.

Museum built by the British at the end of the 19th century.

With the exception of one central area, most buildings in Jaipur are light yellow rather than pink.

This also applies to the walls of palaces.

The most famous palace in Jaipur is the Palace of the Winds. The windows in it are made so that the rooms inside are ventilated by air flows.
