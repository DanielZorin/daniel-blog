St. Louis

St. Louis is an American city with a sad fate. The Olympic Games were held here in 1904. Why did they decide to spend them in such a hole, and not in New York or Los Angeles? It’s just that in 1904, St. Louis was not a hole, but the fourth largest city in the United States, and now it is 64th, and its population is two and a half times less than a hundred years ago. The St. Louis suburb of Ferguson was negatively noted for riots in 2014, and another suburb of East St. Louis recently topped the ranking of the most crime-ridden cities in the United States, knocking off Camden, New Jersey.

I thought that this arch had been here since the 19th century, but no, it was built in 1965.

It symbolizes the conventional border of the east and west of the United States - once St. Louis was the farthest city, beyond which unexplored territories and the wild west began.

There is an elevator inside that you can take to the top.

The arch stands on the banks of the Mississippi, the largest river in the United States, which I had previously seen at its mouth in New Orleans.

Embankment.

In St. Louis, I took a tour of a brewery.

The most popular American beer, Bud, is produced here. The plant was founded by German settlers Anheuser and Busch back in the 19th century. It was originally short for Budweiser (that is, beer from the city of Ceske Budejovice, which is called Budweis in German), but for English speakers it turned out to be too long, so the name was shortened.

The plant was built back in the days when factories were built to look like palace buildings.

The interior is also chic inside, at least in those rooms shown to the tourists.

Minneapolis

In Minneapolis (Minnesota), I had a layover at the airport for about 6 hours, during which time I managed to go to the city center, fortunately it's not far. Few people outside the United States have heard about this city, although it is quite large - the agglomeration has more than 3 million people.

From the airport to the center you can take a light rail, the lines of which cover Minneapolis and the neighboring city of St. Paul, the capital of Minnesota.

On the opposite side of the center, trams go to the Mall of America - the largest shopping center in the United States by total area.

Residential area.

The city center has its own skyline with skyscrapers.

Salt Lake City

Salt Lake City is the capital of Utah, famous for its national parks and the fact that Mormons live here. Mormons are followers of a heterodox Christian church founded in America in the 19th century. The 2002 Olympic Games were also held here.

The main attraction of the city is the Mormon Temple. I remember as a child I saw photographs of it in a book about world landmarks, and they were all from approximately the same angle as the photo above; it seemed that this temple was simply gigantic, but in life it turned out to be quite compact.

Non-Mormons are not allowed in, just as non-Muslims are not allowed into the mosque in Mecca.

Unlike most Christian churches (especially Gothic ones), this temple is almost symmetrical; at first glance, from the back it has the same façade, only the central tower is higher.

Meeting annex.

The Mormon Church administration building next door is no less impressive.

On the side is a map of the world.
