India: Delhi, Amritsar, Dharamsala, Shimla, Chandigarh

4-25 April 2017

Delhi

Almost all trips to northern India begin in Delhi. This city has a bad reputation among tourists, and usually no one stays long here, but due to circumstances, I spent almost 10 days in Delhi, so I had my fill of the Indian capital. It is believed that there are a lot of crooks on the streets here, but I didn’t notice anything: no one came up to me and tried to take me to a store or a fake railway ticket office, and they asked for alms no more than in Paris or Los Angeles. This is a very big city, and no one here cares about you, except tourists from the outback, who may ask to take a photo with them near famous attractions.

All guidebooks tell a fairy tale that the taxi driver taking you from the airport will probably say that your hotel has burned down, or that the area where your hotel is is all closed due to a holiday or rally, and will take you to a hotel that pays him a commission. I can’t imagine how dumb you have to be to fall for such a stupid scam. Besides, why spend two hours vomiting in a taxi in traffic jams, if you can get to the center in 20 minutes on a nice metro train for less than a dollar? The metro takes you to the New Delhi railway station, from where the Main Bazaar street begins, where cheap hotels are concentrated. I expected it to be something like Khao San Road and was immediately disappointed. Two-story gray houses, similar to Brazilian favelas, below are only shops with clothes and a couple of travel agencies with exchange offices. Not a single bar and only three or four restaurants on the entire street. Well, it's littered, of course. There are quite few foreign tourists in India, absolutely not comparable with Southeast Asia.

There is only one corner, which in a photograph may look like Khao San with its shining signs.

And then it is better to cut it from above, and not from below.

Quite quickly I found a hotel room for 800 rupees ($13) with air conditioning, which, as it turned out later, is about the standard price for India. Without air conditioning it's half the price, but in summer Delhi is still a little hot (40 degrees, but with low humidity). Towards the end of the trip, when I was traveling alone, I got used to it and began to stay in rooms without air conditioning. The whole area around Main Bazaar is like that.

On the last day before my flight, I was in Delhi again, I decided to go to a castle that I had never been to before - Feroz Shah Kotla, a 14th century castle.

At the top of the ruins stands the pillar of Ashoka. Such pillars were erected throughout India in the 3rd century BC, during the reign of Ashoka, when India reached its largest size in history (it did not include the current southernmost provinces, but did include modern Pakistan, Afghanistan and Bangladesh).

To this day, 20 such pillars have survived, mainly in the northern part of India, and the laws of the empire were written on them.

Amritsar

Amritsar is the main city of Indian Sikhs. Let's get a little education on the topic. Indians are citizens of India (of any religion and nationality). Hindus are followers of the Hindu religion. Sikhs are followers of the Sikhism religion; this relatively little-known religion appeared about 500 years ago in these lands. The stereotypical appearance of an Indian with a thick beard and a turban is based primarily on the Sikhs, because not shaving is one of their main traditions. Sikhs also do not eat meat out of principle. Ultra-budget travelers love and respect the Sikhs for another unusual tradition - organizing free lunches for everyone every day in the temple (gurudwara).

Sikhism is a monotheistic religion; we can say that it appeared in opposition to Islam, which was then dominant among the rulers. Thanks to the unity of the Sikh community, they were able to successfully resist the Mughal Empire, and in the 18th century achieved independence, which lasted until the capture of India by the British. During the partition of India, half of the state of Punjab, where there were more Muslims, went to Pakistan, while the Sikhs were harshly expelled from there. It was an incredibly cruel time: immediately after the British left and the independence of India and Pakistan was declared, tens of millions of people were expelled and hundreds of thousands, if not millions, were killed because they had the “wrong” religion. In Indian Punjab, Sikhs now form the majority, and in the 1980s they even seriously fought for independence, which is why the Indian army stormed the Golden Temple in Amritsar in 1984; in retaliation the Sikhs killed Prime Minister Indira Gandhi. Now anyone can enter the Golden Temple; inside there are dazzling white floors and walls, which are even pleasant to walk on without shoes.

Chandigarh

Chandigarh is an unusual Indian city. It was built from scratch in the 1950s according to a plan, and was originally built as the capital for the state of Punjab (instead of Lahore, which went to Pakistan). After the separation of the new state of Haryana from the eastern part of Punjab, Chandigarh became the capital of these two states at once, and the city itself was allocated to a separate "union territory" (like Delhi). Of all the regions in India, it has the highest GDP per capita, more than Delhi.

Chandigarh was built according to the design of the well-known French architect Le Corbusier, the one who once came up with the prototype of the concept of the commieblock and built the first examples in France. It is not surprising that Chandigarh is very similar to a typical Soviet city with very wide streets and neighborhood development. From a typical street in Chandigarh, you can't see the buildings behind the trees.

The city is divided into more than 50 squares, numbered from the northern edge. In square 1 is the Capitol, the closer to it, the more elite. Almost all the squares are fenced around the perimeter, and you can only enter in a few places.

I can safely add the Rock Garden in Chandigarh to the collection of strange and unusual parks with sculptures. Its creator, Nek Chand, made it in secret for almost 20 years in a patch of forest in the very center of Chandigarh - between the lake and the Capitol. Apparently, then the level of paranoia and the number of police on the streets of India was noticeably less, so he was not noticed near the most guarded facility. When the garden was discovered, they decided not to demolish it, but to make it open to everyone; now it is one of the main attractions of the city.

The first half of the garden is located in the river valley, the path passes through gorges.

Everything here is made from construction waste that Nek Chand originally collected from all over the city.

There are even waterfalls.

Monumental.

In the second half of the park, on each square there is an army of sculptures in the style of primitivism.

This is most similar to the sculpture park in Finland.
