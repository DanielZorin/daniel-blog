France: Dijon, Amiens, Rouen, Paris

20-25 November 2019

Dijon

Dijon is the capital of the Burgundy region. In 2016, it was merged with neighboring Franche-Comté into one large province.

The city center was not damaged by wars and is well preserved. Almost the entire old part is pedestrian.

On the side of the cathedral chapel, a small statue of an owl has been preserved since the 15th century, which all passers-by loved to rub.

Now the owl has become a symbol of Dijon.

There are owls on street signs and manhole covers.

Amiens

The city of Amiens is the capital of the Picardy region in northern France, annexed to Pas-de-Calais in 2016. This is an atypical French city, as this region suffered greatly during the First World War.

The strict train station of Amiens.

Opposite the station there is a square skyscraper.

Paris

I have been to Paris several times already, but the last time was quite a long time ago, in 2013-2014. I like Paris, so I budgeted more days for it at the end. In addition to seeing the Champs Elysees and Montmartre more than once, I also took a ride through some places that were new to me. The first morning after arriving in Paris, I headed to the northwestern suburb, the La Défense district.

Previously, I always took packs of 10 single tickets, but now they went up to almost 17 euros, and this time I usually traveled with a day pass. This ticket is called Mobilis, and if you don’t know what it is, you won’t understand from the name that it’s a regular ticket for the day, available to everyone. It costs 7.50 (for two zones, but I didn’t go further), that is, it pays for itself on the fifth trip.

In Paris, I accidentally walked into a feminist rally taking place on the boulevards. As I understand it, it was just a rally without any specific topical reason, but a lot of people came to it, no less than there were at the rally for the independence of Catalonia.

Most carried posters prepared by the organizers with a purple background, but there were also many homemade posters.

The posters were mostly against domestic violence.

The homemade posters contained quite radical statements, for example, “Hating men = life.”

Women have blood between their legs, and men have blood on their hands.

The penis is a weapon of mass destruction.

A yellow vest passed by - people wearing such vests protested against inequality. For them, socialism is never enough.

The police are the dogs of patriarchy.

Dinner.

French food stamps. They are given by employers to their employees instead of part of the salary - it is beneficial for them, since they have to pay less taxes for payment in kind, and the state stimulates the demand for restaurant services and GDP growth. These coupons can be used to pay in most restaurants.
