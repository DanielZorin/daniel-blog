New Caledonia

14-17 January 2020

New Caledonia is the largest island in Oceania. It is an overseas territory of France that strives to secede - in 2018 there was a referendum on independence, in which they voted against, and in 2020 they are already promising a new one. The fact is that the local indigenous people - Melanesians - make up 45% of the population, while the French make up 55%, and all the French are against independence because they do not want to lose French subsidies and become another useless banana country in Oceania. The local airline Air Calin has a flight from Paris with a stopover in Tokyo, where I boarded the plane.

Caledonia is the Roman name for Scotland. Captain Cook named the island this way because of its similar mountainous terrain.

Most of New Caledonia consists of such mountains, almost all life is concentrated on the west coast, with only a few roads across the island.

I was unable to rent a car in New Caledonia, since all rental offices required at least two years of experience, so I was not able to travel around the island outside the cities.

The money of New Caledonia is called Pacific francs. It is also used in French Polynesia and the Wallis and Futuna Islands. Moreover, each of the three overseas territories mints coins with its own coat of arms, but they are also interchangeable, although for obvious reasons you can find New Caledonian coins in Tahiti much less often than Italian euros in France.

New Caledonia is a very expensive country, more expensive than Australia, New Zealand and the independent countries of Oceania. Chicken in the supermarket costs $13 per kilo with bones!

Supermarkets have a full French selection - there are many types of soft cheeses.

Fresh baguettes.

And hundreds of types of wine.

Eating in a cafe is even more pricey - sandwiches cost 6 dollars, chicken noodles cost 16.

Burgers are $10. At McDonald's, a combo lunch with a Big Mac also costs almost $10 (more expensive than in Switzerland, but cheaper than in some US states)
