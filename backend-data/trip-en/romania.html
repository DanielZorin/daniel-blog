Bucharest

Once upon a time there was a dictator, Nicolae Ceausescu. Somehow it happened that his name became almost a common noun to denote an extravagant tyrant. Perhaps the reason is the stark contrast between the scale of the personality cult of the man who ruled Romania for decades and how quickly and sadly his story ended. Just yesterday he could gather tens of thousands of slaves for a rally, today he is running from the roof of his palace in a helicopter, and tomorrow they will put him up against a wall and shoot him. A typical end for a typical dictator. One of the important results of Ceausescu's rule (as well as communist dictators in other countries) was the destruction of the country's cultural heritage. Ceausescu demolished half of Bucharest to build palaces for himself and his party cronies.

The most famous example is, of course, the Parliament, one of the largest buildings in the world. Why did communist Romania even have a parliament? All the same, deputies were elected on "elections" with one candidate and did not decide anything.

Brașov

In the places where the Ceausescu's destruction did not reach, Romania is incredibly, unimaginably beautiful. It is one of the two most beautiful countries in Europe, along with Portugal. If in some Venice or Dubrovnik there is a clear feeling that you are walking through a museum, then in Romania people live their lives in the scenery of fifteenth century, and they don’t even seem to pay attention to it.

Sinaia

One Romanian king decided to build himself a residence outside the city. Disney's "Sleeping Beauty" didn't exist yet, so the architects probably relied on illustrations in old books of children's fairy tales.

The result was one of the most fabulous-looking palaces in the world. It’s completely incomprehensible why everyone is rushing to Neuschwanstein, and not here?

The courtiers decided not to lag behind and began to build their palaces nearby.

And now there is a whole village of luxury buildings.

Sighișoara

Have you ever heard about the city of Sighișoara? Sighișoara is a fairy tale. It can be put on a par with such magical places as Porto, Genoa, Gdansk, Ouro Preto and Suzdal. Sighișoara is what Europe looked like 500 years ago (only with modern comfort levels). When you approach the center from the station, you think that the only thing missing here is a dragon circling over the rooftops.

The dragon must have already been killed by the brave knights, and the sun was shining over the city.

I was in Sighișoara on the last day (more precisely, the last night and two last days) of a big trip through the Balkans. Before this, I saved for 10 days, lived in hostels, however, in most cities it turned out that there was no one in the room but me, and it was like a four- to eight-bed room for the price of a bed. There is also a hostel in Sighișoara, but I decided to splurge and rent a separate room. It cost ridiculously little money by European standards, less than 20 euros. House in the historical center of Sighișoara.

Sighișoara was originally a German colony (or enclave, as you prefer). Now the cemetery in the old town reminds us of this, as many graves have German names.

Târgu Mureș

Târgu Mureș was the last Romanian city on my way. This is one of the most Hungarian cities in Romania (in Hungarian it is called Marosvászarhely, which also means “market on the Mureș River”). Transylvania was part of Hungary and passed to Romania under the Treaty of Trianon after the First World War. Then, during World War II, the Hungarians returned part of the territory (Hungary and Romania were allies of Germany, and Hitler arranged a special arbitration to divide the territory), but not for long. Now Hungarians in Transylvania are already a minority, but visually Târgu Mureș really looks like Hungarian provincial towns.

But there is still some Romanian beauty here.
