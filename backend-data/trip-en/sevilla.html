Spain: Malaga, Granada, La Linea, Seville

21-25 July 2016

Malaga

I came to Spain for the eleventh time, after a break of almost two years. For the visa I needed a direct flight, I chose Malaga because I had already been to Barcelona five times.

Malaga is a pleasant seaside city, albeit without top attractions.

Almost the entire center is pedestrian, and on a dry summer day the tiles glisten in the sun.

I understood why these canopies are needed over shopping streets - so that there is shade! I used to be in Spain all the time in winter or fall and didn’t feel the heat. Although after Southeast Asia, Spain is not that hot.

Granada

Granada is home to the famous Alhambra Castle, where the Arab rulers of Spain once lived before they were finally driven out at the end of the 15th century.

The statue of the Virgin Mary was placed directly in the arch with typical Muslim decor.

By the way, I was in Alhambra on my very, very first trip abroad in 1996 (I was 6 years old).

Since then, the number of tourists in Spain has grown greatly, and now, to get to the Alhambra in the summer, you need to buy a ticket in advance via the Internet. A ticket, however, is only needed to enter the Moorish palace; you can walk around the territory anyway.

View of the city from the Alhambra.

In addition to the Moorish castle, on the territory of the Alhambra there is a palace built later, under Charles V.

The palace was not finished; it stood without a roof until the mid-20th century.

From the Alhambra you can descend through gardens leading to the far exit into the city.

The hill where the Alhambra is and the one opposite are filled with narrow winding streets.

On the outskirts of the city, but this also had a plus: when I went further to Portugal in a Blablacar, it was very convenient, because the highway to the west passed right under the window. And in this house you could go out onto the roof to see a panorama of the residential areas of Seville.

Seville seems to be a big city, but even from this outskirts it was a 40-minute walk to the center.

There is a lot of beauty and glory in the center.

One of the most solemn cities in Spain. Maybe it seemed like that to me because there were no people on the streets at all?

The bell tower of the main cathedral looks a bit like a minaret.

In the center of one of the squares there was a canopy in the shape of futuristic mushrooms.

The only skyscraper in Seville.

In 1929, the Spanish government decided that there were still not enough cool architectural ensembles in Seville, and they built one more grandiose than all the others. That year, the international Expo took place mostly in Barcelona, and in Seville they placed expositions of Latin American countries in order to make their interaction with Spain closer.

This is what happened.

Under each arch there is a composition dedicated to one of the provinces of Spain.

We had dinner at a place that served “tapas” - Spanish snacks. A very popular place, although no one is invited from the street, apparently there are many regular customers.

Menu. Tapa portions cost 2-3 euros, you can take several dishes to try.

By Russian standards, Spanish “tapas” would be complete meals. Yes, all 4 dishes on the table are "tapas", everything together with a glass of wine cost about 13 euros.

Someone put a box of unnecessary things on the street. Among them you can see as many as two kilogram packs of Maria cookies, each of which contained four separately packaged cylindrical blocks. I took one pack for my stash, and then we ate these cookies for a long time. Three quarters reached Russia, then I took them with me on a trip to the Baltics and Scandinavia, and the last pack also went with me to America and the Caribbean, where I almost finished them, there were literally a few cookies left, which stayed at the bottom backpack, along with a supply of tea, and later also traveled around India and Southeast Asia. This is how a pack of cookies visited 30 countries.
