Cambodia: Phnom Penh, Kampot, Kep, Sihanoukville, Battambang, Poipet, Koh Kong

22 August - 6 September, 29 November - 10 December 2015

Phnom Penh

Cambodia had two stellar moments in history: a thousand years ago it ruled Southeast Asia, and 40 years ago the Khmer Rouge regime tried to raise the country from its knees with mass executions. When I first learned about Pol Pot about 10 years ago, it seemed to me that it was quite recently: my grandmother, for example, witnessed the Second World War, which was 30 years before the Khmer Rouge. But given the low life expectancy in Cambodia, it is now very difficult to meet someone who experienced those times at a conscious age.

Nazis, Khmer Rouge, ISIS - from reading history books and news it may seem that a small group of obvious (from the outside) maniacs seized power, 99% of the population were brutally oppressed by them, but did not even try to resist somehow. In reality, the regime could not have survived without the support of a large number of ordinary people. In the Genocide Museum, several rooms display ordinary people's opinions of the Khmer Rouge. For example: “Yes, I served the Khmer Rouge, but I don’t regret it. I take care of my wife and children. Let the tribunal judge the leaders of the Khmer Rouge, and they should not hold ordinary people responsible for everything.”

“I don’t regret serving the Khmer Rouge. Whoever is the criminal must answer” (Did he just follow orders?)

These rooms are the most interesting part of the museum because the rest look something like this.

By the way, the United States did not oppose the Khmer Rouge because they positioned themselves as Maoists and enemies of Vietnam, which was the main enemy of the United States in the region. Many officials worked under all regimes and have not gone away.

The Khmer Rouge Crimes Tribunal was not established until 20 years after the events, and in its nearly 20 years of operation it convicted only five people.

The tradition of burning wads of money for good luck is alive and well. You can buy a couple of packs at the market for a dollar. Interestingly, you can buy and burn real money - 100 riel bills. A pack of 100 of these bills costs about $2.5. Unlike the Thais, the Cambodians do not seem to be bothered by the presence of the king's portrait on the banknote.

The Cambodian riel is pegged to the US dollar and ranges from 3800-4200 riel per dollar. You can pay in any shops and markets in dollars (even small ones), in everyday life at the rate of 4000, although in a store with an electronic cash register the conversion is at the rate of 4100 or 4200. At the same time, as you can see, there are not only very high interest rates on dollar deposits (as in Russia), but in riel the rates are even higher.

Since Cambodia was destroyed by civil wars and began to develop only 20 years ago, and even less with Chinese help, this gives an unexpected bonus: all the infrastructure that exists is quite new. In particular, you will not find a hotel that has not been renovated for 30 years. The Capitol Hotel, for example, is a very good place for $10 with air conditioning. There is a network of laundries around the city with Wi-Fi and a sumo wrestler on the sign. Why Wi-Fi is in the laundry room is not very clear. At these laundries, you simply drop off your clothes in a bag and pick them up the next day, rather than loading the machine yourself.

Cambodia has not yet reached the heights of civilization, but is gradually moving there.

Kampot

Kampot is a regional center in the south of Cambodia. There is no sea, but nevertheless it is often visited by tourists, as they write in guidebooks, “for its relaxed atmosphere.” There is infrastructure in the form of numerous hotels and restaurants, you can live there. The main attraction of the city is the durian monument. Only in this part of Cambodia are durians grown; during the season you can buy them for $1.5 per kilogram.

In Cambodia, in all provincial cities and even in villages, sculptures are placed at intersections along the highway.

Sihanoukville

Of the many similar resort towns in Southeast Asia, Sihanoukville stands out due to the successful combination of two factors: it is much cheaper than the resorts of Thailand and the Philippines, and there is a lot of party, in contrast to the quiet, family-friendly, conservative cities of Vietnam and Malaysia. In particular, it has some of the best prices in the region for European-style food, which is a welcome change after months of Chinese and Korean food.

I have, of course, not visited all the restaurants and eateries in Sihanoukville (there are several dozen of them just near Serendipity Beach, and there are also other areas where I visited once). Many of them are quite ordinary for Asia, such as the eatery with noodles with meat for 7-8 thousand riel ($2) that is closest to my house and market. I will describe several well-remembered establishments that personally receive Michelin stars from me. The first is called Nice Foods, located near Serendipity Beach across the intersection from the well-known Utopia bar.

This place is good because it has the lowest prices in the area for non-refined dishes like noodles, soups, sandwiches. At the same time, the menu is very extensive. Noodles cost $1.5-2.

My favorite is the $2 mushroom soup. Shakes cost $1-1.5 everywhere.

Sandwiches are cheap - $1.25-$1.5.

If you want fried meat, then opposite, on the street parallel to the beach, there are places with a grill for $3, for example, “Sony and Friend”.

For $3 this is a very decent dinner.

Approximate prices can be seen on this menu. This is some kind of establishment on the beach, I haven’t been to it, but I took a picture of the menu because it was in Russian (found in some places in Sihanoukville). Prices here are average by city standards, but if you look, you can find cheaper ones, like at Nice Foods.

And there is such a menu.

Now all sorts of delights. In Sihanoukville, many Chinese run hotels and other businesses, and you can find authentic restaurants with truly Chinese menus. Prices are slightly higher than usual in Cambodia - noodles with egg for $2, with meat for $3.

But the tomatoes with eggs are authentic, I guarantee.

And dumplings too.

I didn’t see seafood directly from the nets, like in Kep, in Sihanoukville, but during the day sellers of shrimp and lobsters constantly walk along the beach. In the evening I somehow managed to get a whole mountain of lobsters for $7.

There is one famous place in narrow circles where they serve clams and scallops for $2. No, not per piece, for the whole plate.

Steaks (with different degrees of doneness, everything as expected) are prepared in the French restaurant Susaday, which is approximately opposite Khin's shack across the road on the second line from the beach.

This place is more for special occasions, although for a steak the price of $8.5 is inexpensive. The sauce for it is with a signature Cambodian spice - Kampot peppers.

Well, the next establishment is for real aesthetes.

In China and Vietnam, you get used to the fact that after ordering you have to wait for a maximum of 5 minutes, forgetting, like a bad dream, the Russian service where a cup of coffee is brewed for half an hour, then they bring the receipt for half an hour, and another half an hour the change. And the plus of the “kitchen” is that the food there is very tasty. Not just tasty, but very tasty. European dishes. Here's tomato soup for $2.

* * *

Sihanoukville is a fairly large city by Cambodian standards, and it has more than just hotels for beachgoers. For example, here at the entrance to the city there is a factory for the production of Angkor beer. The center of Sihanoukville is not on the seashore, but at a distance of about one and a half kilometers from it, but due to the terrain, it is a walk of three kilometers to the nearest Serendipity beach or you can take a motorcycle taxi for a dollar.

For tourists, the city center is interesting mainly because there is a market and the Sumadera supermarket - probably the most reputable supermarket in the city, where you can buy both imported delicacies and locally produced milk from Sihanoukville. It’s sad to live without a market, because fruits and vegetables are not sold on the beaches. And in the city center you can stay in a hotel, which is what I did all the time.

Zhongguo Guesthouse (China) is located near the Central Market and Capitol Bus Stop. It’s nice that seasonality does not affect the price - a room costs $10 in both August and December. This is a truly Chinese hotel - there are even amulets hanging on the doors.

The rooms are spacious, have air conditioning, hot showers, and decent Wi-fi.

The room where I lived in August.

In the center you always have all kinds of fruits at hand; in the morning you can buy crispy French bread for 700 riel ($0.18). Other services are also available, for example, you can get a haircut for $1. But you won’t be able to wake up and go to the beach; it’s a 40-minute walk or a motorcycle ride. But if this is not important to you, then this hotel is the right option, because you won’t find accommodation of this quality for $10 near the beach, especially in high season.

Panorama of the city from the top floor of the hotel.

From here you can watch sunsets.

The party in Sihanoukville is on Serendipity Beach. The rest are more suitable for meditation, and Serendipity consists entirely of bars.

It all starts at the crossroads with the lion statue.

There are many casinos around. People come to Cambodia from Thailand and Vietnam, where gambling is prohibited. Unlike Macau, you can play poker here.

Advertising in Russian. There are a number of Russian-speaking residents in Sihanoukville, because living here for a long time is easier than anywhere else: you pay $300 a year, and your business visa is extended.

On the first line of the beach there are only bars, soft chairs and tables next to them, so to sit comfortably on the beach (and not on the sand), you need to buy a shake or coffee for $1. Very convenient, because you still need a cool drink, and when sun loungers belong to hotels, getting them is difficult or expensive.

Happy hour is here 24 hours a day. Cocktails for $2, beer for $0.5.

Further along the coast, part of the beach is popular with Cambodians and visitors from other Asian countries, mainly Chinese.

People take selfies here rather than swim, and kebabs and other Chinese snacks are actively sold on the beach.

A typically Cambodian relaxation area with hammocks.

But sunsets on this beach are inconvenient to watch; hills and trees get in the way.

The Serendipity area is where Sihanoukville's nightlife centers. You shouldn’t expect something like Full Moon Party - there are much fewer people, but nevertheless, this is a great rarity for the region - a European-style disco, where people party on the dance floor, rather than sitting at tables with beer.

The oldest bar in Sihanoukville is Utopia. It's more of a pre-party place in the evening. You can live there in a hostel for $2 per bed in a room without air conditioning or $3 with air conditioning.

Beer pong game - if you hit a cup with a ball, your opponent drinks it.

After 11, the party on the beach starts to pick up, sometimes they put on a show with fire.

Or they set a bar under which you can pass.

You walk through and they give you strong drinks. By the way, flyers are handed out on the streets during the day (and you can find them in the evening), with them you can get free cocktails or beer at certain hours, or get four beers for a dollar. In general, if you want, you can get drunk in Sihanoukville for one dollar or even for free!

There are 2-3 discos on the beach, usually JJ's and Dolphin Shack.

The party goes on for a long time, by about 2 am it is just warming up properly.

The fact is that the well-known Russian businessman Sergei Polonsky wanted to develop this island when he was in Cambodia. In 2014, they wanted to hold the Kazantip festival (Techno music festival, held annually in Crimea before it was occupied by Russia in 2014) on the island, but local authorities banned it at the last moment (were the Cambodian police really confused by drugs?). Since then, the island has been abandoned, although from the shore it is clear that some hotels have already been built there.

Behind Serendipity Beach is Otres - a place where those who want a quiet but civilized holiday go.

This is the most beautiful beach in Sihanoukville.

The downside is that it is far from the center: you can walk in an hour to Serendipity along the coast, but the road for motorcycles and cars makes a long detour, about 8 kilometers, a motorcycle taxi will cost 2 dollars one way. Apart from the main road, everything else on Otres is great.

Less than half of the beach has been developed; in other places there is nothing except the road.

In the nearest part of the beach everything is fine, there are cafes and sun loungers.

The water is not as muddy as on Serendipity, and there are fewer boats and jet skis.

And you can see the sunsets quite well from here too.
