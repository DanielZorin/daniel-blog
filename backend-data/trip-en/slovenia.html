Slovenia: Piran, Koper, Ljubljana

19-21 July 2014

Hitchhiking

So, Slovenia, the country that I wanted to get to for so long, and didn’t know how. The situation was not very good: in Hungary the road was of local importance, but right after the border there is a toll highway on which you cannot stand. The highway is a continuation of the Hungarian highway, coming from Budapest itself, and not far from the border it divides into two roads: to Croatia and Slovenia, and this border crossing is sort of a side one. However, it is a good 15 kilometers to walk to the town of Lendava, where the main highway begins. In fact, at this border (in the photo in the distance you can see the empty customs terminal that operated in pre-Schengen times) there is a whole truck stop. However, only an hour later we were picked up by a Slovak truck driver who was traveling to Spain through Slovenia, Italy and France. I started to get used to speaking a mixture of words from different Slavic languages, mainly Russian and Polish, which I know better.

In Europe, unlike Russia, truckers do not use walkie-talkies to inform each other of the situation on the road, since here it is not customary for the police to set up ambushes. The truck is equipped with a laptop with a map of Europe, some kind of special one, not Google Maps. There is also a system installed there that forces the driver to stop on a parking lot every N hours to sleep for 45 minutes; we don’t have that either.

In Slovenia, as in Hungary, you cannot stand on the motorway. However, here on the highway there are often pockets for an emergency stop, while in Hungary, on the roads that I saw, essentially everywhere there was a shoulder the width of a full-fledged lane.

In Hungary and Slovenia, when entering a toll road, you do not need to pay directly at the toll booth, unlike most countries. You need to buy a special sticker on your windshield, called "Vignette" in French, which gives you the right to drive on toll roads for a certain period of time. The exit to the toll road is indicated by a sign with the image of this very vignette. However, this does not apply to trucks; there is a special lane for them at the entrance to the highway. The driver has a special card that he touches to the reader. You can also refill it at the booth.

I have never seen anything like this before. From Koper in half an hour by bus you can get to the final point of the route in Slovenia - the city of Piran.

Piran

Piran is an old Italian city that ended up in Yugoslavia and then in Slovenia after Italy's defeat in World War II. Few people know that for more than 10 years after the war, Trieste was an independent city-state, like West Berlin or earlier Gdansk. De facto, the territory was half controlled by the American army, half by the Yugoslav army. Then it was divided in two, the city of Trieste itself returned to Italy, and the southern part went to Yugoslavia, now these are the cities of Koper, Izola, Piran in Slovenia and Umag in Croatia.

The Italian population has mostly left Piran, but signs in Italian still hang, although there is Lenin Street.

After the hilarious hitchhiking, the first thing I wanted to do was eat. The dish on the right is called "cevapi" or "cevapcici", Bosnian, although found throughout Yugoslavia. The restaurant was called "Sarajevo". The dish on the left is “pljeskavica”, a pork chop that I already tasted when I was in Serbia. Prices in Piran are lower than in Western Europe and Russian restaurants; the entire dinner cost about 10 euros.

Ljubljana

They say that many people confuse Slovenia and Slovakia. Hint: in Slovak “Slovakia” is Slovensko, and “Slovenia” is Slovinsko. In Slovenian, “Slovakia” is Slovacka, and Slovenia is Slovenija. So if you see a euro coin with the inscription Slovensko, it is from Slovakia, although Slovenia also has euros. Ljubljana is a city of expressive sculptures.

This is without comment.
