San Marino

San Marino is the last of the European microstates that I haven’t written about since I was there back in 2009 and the battery on my camera died. San Marino is surrounded on all sides by Italian territory, and the only public transport to get there is the bus to the nearest large city of Rimini, which runs quite rarely, once every 1.5-2 hours. The fare is 6 euros one way. San Marino is located on top of a fairly steep cliff called Monte Titano, which made the city impregnable in the Middle Ages. In the 15th century they successfully allied with the Papal States against the Duchy of Rimini, giving San Marino the modern territories around Monte Titano. Later, the Papal State tried to capture San Marino several times, but failed. In the 19th century, first Napoleon and then Garibaldi maintained the independence of San Marino out of respect for the fact that it is the oldest state with a republican form of government.

On the descent from San Marino you can see a carriage standing near the tunnel. Now San Marino is one of two countries in Europe without railways (the other one is Andorra), but once upon a time there was a railway, and it reached right up to the capital at the top, which was very impressive at that time. The road was built in 1932, but it was destroyed during the Second World War.
