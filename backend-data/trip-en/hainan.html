China: Guilin, Yangshuo, Nanning, Zhanjiang, Haikou, Sanya

18-30 April 2015

Guilin

Many pass through this city without stopping, heading straight to the neighboring town of Yangshuo, but there is also something to see in Guilin. There are karst hills everywhere in this area, only in Guilin they are among a more urban landscape, since it is still a rather large city.

As in all popular tourist cities, in Guilin they can easily accommodate foreigners in any cheap hotel. Near the reception there is a funny poster calling for checking guests' passports and not letting bearded Uyghur terrorists in.

As in many cities, in Guilin there are old five-story buildings in the center, and modern high-rise buildings on the outskirts.

Let's move on to the sights. This is not easy in Guilin. Since there are a lot of tourists here, the prices are too high, so don’t play around. They charge 60-80 yuan for entry anywhere, which means if you go everywhere you can simply go broke. It’s good that you can at least look at the pagodas from the outside for free.

The main attraction in the city center is a karst hill that looks like an elephant's trunk. Entrance 70 yuan. This is the elephant. They took a thorough approach to fencing the area: trees were planted on the embankment opposite to make it inconvenient to take selfies.

The area is tightly fenced with barbed wire.

In general, in China they usually don’t bother with fencing that much. Apparently, they’ve become quite impudent here in charging 70 yuan for viewing the rocks.

When the wire runs out, you can watch it for free, albeit from afar.

There are also several hills to climb. Yangshuo

Yangshuo is lovely. The city is small, sandwiched between all these karst mountains, you can admire the scenery up close on any street. Directly from the bus station itself.

It’s best to check in near the bus station: transport is nearby, the center is a 20-minute walk, exit from the city is nearby (if you go by bicycle), an excellent room with no problems with check-in and no haggling for 80 yuan, whereas in the center you will be charged 50 yuan for a bed in a hostel. In general, in China you can live in a hostel only if it is vital for you to drink beer every day with backpackers.

In Yangshuo there are noticeably more attempts to translate signs into English.

In general, the center is quite rotten: an unreal crowd and generally no place to eat properly.

Usually on tourist streets they at least sell some goodies from stalls, but not here.

Here they sell German sausages for two euros. Well, in Germany they cost about the same, of course, but here it’s strange.

For 30-38 yuan you can get two excellent meat dishes or something completely exotic and very tasty, rather than one dry toast with scrambled eggs and 100 grams of fruit. But the second announcement is very interesting. It turns out that an English teacher receives more than $1,000 a month and also free food and housing, that is, he lives better than 99% of the Russian population (except for top programmers, directors and Putin’s friends). Moreover, they only work 15-20 hours a week! In my opinion, if there is no way to make money online, this is a very good option, much better than working for similar money in expensive Australia picking fruit, as Americans and Europeans do. However, no one needs Russian language teachers. But if you know English well, then you can also get a job, just not in popular tourist cities.

In general, you can skip the center, especially since Yangshuo is not a historical city; there is no ancient architecture here.

Looks nice though.

There are not many attractions in the city itself. Usually from here people float along the river on rafts (very expensive) or ride around the surrounding area on bicycles (very cheap). There is one mountain in the very center that you can climb for a lot of money, but there is a better alternative: a hill with a TV tower. It is close to the center, quite high (it takes 30-40 minutes to climb the stairs). They let you in there not entirely officially; the gatekeeper collects 20 yuan without any tickets. Finding the road is also not very easy: the beginning of the stairs is located deep in a residential area with narrow streets. In some places there are signs (in this photo there is a white A4 piece of paper).

Upstairs, Chinese students were sitting on the roof of an abandoned booth, drinking beer.

From here you have a stunning view of the center of Yangshuo and the surrounding area.

By the way, the surrounding area of Yangshuo is depicted on the 20 yuan banknote. There is an exact place where this photograph was taken, this is the village of Xingping, to which you need to drive 30 kilometers to the north. I've been to all the cities with Russian banknotes, and now I've seen almost all the sights on Chinese ones: West Lake in Hangzhou (1 yuan), Taishan (5 yuan), Yangtze Gorges (10 yuan), Yangshuo (20 yuan) and Parliament Building (100 yuan). The only thing left is the Dalai Lama's palace in Lhasa (50 yuan).

The most convenient way to explore the beauty around Yangshuo is by bicycle. Walking distances are too long, and you can rent a bicycle for only 20 yuan per day (three dollars). In the city center it is 3-5 times more expensive, so the best place is near the bus station. Plus, it’s just close to the exit from the city, so you don’t have to go through the center, where there are quite a lot of cars. Since I got on a bicycle for the second time in my life, riding on a busy road was scary; in this regard, a rural road is much more convenient.

Nanning

Nanning is the largest city closest to the border with Vietnam. The proximity of Vietnam is evident in the huge number of motorcycles on the streets.

In many big cities, motorcycles are banned on big streets, but not here.

If you are in Nanning, then most likely you are traveling in transit. From here you can go directly to Guilin, Kunming, Guangzhou and Hanoi.

All the benefits of civilization are here for transit.

Sale of jackfruits. It’s a delicious fruit, but I have a severe allergy to it, my throat burns, like if you eat the pineapple pulp near the peel.

In general, there is nothing special to see in Nanning, but you can take a walk for one day during transit.

Zhanjiang

Getting to Hainan Island is not so easy. Planes there are not very cheap. There are trains that load entire cars directly onto ferries. It would be interesting to travel, but there are very few of these trains, they run at inconvenient times, and are very expensive even for a short segment of the journey between the mainland and the island. After a long meditation on the railway map, I discovered the city of Zhanjiang in Guangdong Province, which is close to Hainan and has many trains.

Zhanjiang was a French colony in the 19th century, but this is not particularly noticeable now. There are a couple of seemingly colonial buildings on the waterfront.

And so, an ordinary city.

Haikou

The ferry to Hainan looks very impressive. It even transports trucks, but the entire upper deck is given over to passengers.

Haikou City slowly emerges from the fog on the horizon.

It looks about the same as other Chinese cities.

Only the avenues are lined with palm trees.

And there are coconuts on the palm trees.

Coconuts are sold everywhere here. One costs seven yuan (~$1), for that price you can get it, although I don’t particularly like coconuts: there is not enough water and it is sour, and there is also little pulp, it is a chore to peel it off, and it is also quite tasteless. Maybe it was the comparison of the taste of real coconut with the taste of Bounty chocolate that caused me such disappointment?

Finding a hotel was not easy. At some point, out of desperation, I went to a very reputable hotel with prices starting from 150 yuan, but even there they refused. But in reality, everything is not so bad: you can stay in the private apartment, it’s just that the Chinese word for “rent a room by the sea” in hieroglyphs does not look like the word “hotel”, and at first I simply did not notice such signs. In the private sector you can rent a room for the standard 80 yuan.

Residential areas are like everywhere else.

Grilles on all balconies.

Commieblock.

I wanted to relax for a week on the beach in Hainan, but somehow it didn’t work out. On the very first day, both Alena and I, having spent about 30 minutes on the beach in the open sun, got so burned that we spent the entire evening and the next day in bed (instead of going to the city of Sanya). I didn’t take my camera, so there were no photographs left of the ill-fated Haikou beach. More precisely, there are night ones left, which I did when I was able to get up.

In fact, the area near the beach is quite interesting: it is built in a Mediterranean style, very similar to the resorts of Spain.

During the day it looks exactly the same - white mansions with orange roofs. Very high quality styling.

In the central park near the beach there are signs in English and Russian with funny mistakes.

All that remained was to taste different dishes. Once I randomly pointed at a cheap dish (less than 15 yuan) on the menu, being too lazy to translate its name. It turned out to be a sweet, fluffy corn pie.

These are small lobsters. They look cool, but they contain even less meat than crabs, so their value is questionable.

Carefully putting on my backpack, I was able to get to the bus station, from where I could leave for Sanya.

Sanya

Direct charters fly to Hainan Island from Russia, I even remember advertising in the Moscow metro, and for Siberia this is generally an analogue of Turkey. If you fly to Hainan directly, you don’t even need a visa to China. Based on the reviews, I expected it to be something like Cyprus, but no. Sanya is a real Chinese city with all the attributes, low prices, a huge beautiful empty beach. Overall, this is a very good, comfortable resort, I was pleasantly surprised.

Finding a hotel is not a problem at all. Right next to the bus, as in Crimea, you are greeted by grandmothers who rent out rooms with all amenities for 80 yuan. The beach is 5 minutes away. Here's the view from my window at night.

Gastronomy is top notch. My favorite restaurant street is near secondary school number 2.

Before I had time to enjoy the beautiful beach in Sanya, having just recovered from my burns, it was time to leave. Hainan is a logistical dead end; traveling from there by train towards Guangzhou is very long, tedious, and not particularly cheaper than flying. So I bought a plane ticket a couple of weeks in advance. In general, flights from Haikou are cheaper, but you still need to get to Haikou, it’s at least 80 yuan by bus, or more than 100 by train. Choosing between Haikou-Nanchang for 450 yuan and Sanya-Xiamen for 600 yuan, I preferred the second option so that I could see Xiamen and not have to travel back to Haikou. The flight to Xiamen was operated by Shandong Airlines, based in Jinan (after Xiamen the plane flies there). The route map of this small regional airline looks very impressive.

On the plane on TV they showed a Chinese humor show. I remember the scene about how a hard worker sits on a bench with a Chinese hipster in a yellow jacket and starts arguing with him: the hipster takes out a Big Mac, the hard worker takes out a sausage sandwich, and so on.
