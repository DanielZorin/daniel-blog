Puri

Puri was the first coastal beach town on my trip. In general, in India, as you might guess, the beaches are very bad: like in other poor countries, they are mainly used as a landfill. On the coast of the Bay of Bengal there are no well-known resorts for thousands of kilometers around.

It is not customary for Indians to swim; everyone comes to the seashore to take selfies.

The water in the sea is muddy, but you can live.

The beach is sandy and very wide. Moderately clean.

The most striking feature of Puri, which distinguishes it from all cities in the world, is that all hotels here have check-in and check-out at 8 am, maximum at 8-30. This is tough, I tell you.

There is no resort infrastructure in this city at all. I lived on the second line from the beach; there were at most three restaurants within a radius of two kilometers from me. They also don’t work during the day, somehow I didn’t find anything except the vegetarian canteen at the Hare Krishna temple, where it was quite tasty, but in the hall they played “Hare Krishna, Hare Rama” on repeat. Basically, life is concentrated not near the station and the beach, but a little further, next to the Jagannath Temple. By the way, here restaurants are called “hotels”, as in Sri Lanka, which you will not find in northern India (but everywhere in the south).

Calcutta

Calcutta / Kolkata is a special city. Such image cannot be found in other cities of India; Although in small towns the standard of living is lower, there is no such contrast between a huge majestic city with elements of modern infrastructure and the Middle Ages going on in it.

Imagine that a herd of goats is being driven along the main street of New York - this is Calcutta.

From where to where? What does this herd even eat in the city center? Are they really nibbling grass in the parks?

Kolkata is the only city in India that still has pedestrian rickshaws. An absolutely inexplicable phenomenon: it would seem that the market should have solved them.

The speed of the “trip” on such “transport” is the same as on foot, even on a bicycle it is many times faster. The fare is only slightly lower than that of bicycle rickshaws, which are plentiful in Kolkata. Again, the price of the bike is not prohibitive, the maintenance costs are low. How do they even survive?

Kolkata was the capital of India during British colonial times. Not surprisingly, the city center was largely built in Victorian style, no less than Colombo and Yangon. Before colonization, Kolkata was a village, so everything was built from scratch in the 19th century. After the city lost its significance, everything began to collapse. Now here you can feel what life was like on the ruins of the Roman Empire one and a half thousand years ago.

The building on the left is still in good order, but the building on the right is no longer there.

The less solemn part of the city fell into severe decline. All buildings are in patches.

The particularly plight of Kolkata is connected with the history of the city: first, during the partition of India, Hindus fled here from the territory of Bangladesh (Muslim East Bengal), and then 20 years later, during the war for the independence of Bangladesh, a second stream of migrants came from Pakistan.

Now the city is being revived, they are even building luxury housing on the outskirts.

There will soon be a skyscraper like this in the center.

In Kolkata you can visit Mother Teresa's grave. This name is associated with charity, but it seems that everything is not clear cut. Even on Wikipedia, the “criticism” section is twice as long as the biography - it talks about the ineffective use of huge amounts of money, and about the insignificant scale of activity, artificially inflated by PR, and about low-quality medical care in shelters, which were essentially places where the dying were dumped, not trying to treat in a special way.
