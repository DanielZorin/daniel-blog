Kutna Hora

Kutna Hora is a small ancient city an hour's drive from Prague.

The center is very small and in itself is not particularly beautiful by Czech standards.

City well-fountain.

Minimarket.

The main thing in the center of Kutna Hora is the Gothic cathedral of the 14th century, one of the most significant and largest in the Czech Republic.

A gallery leads to it, on one side of which there is a Jesuit college, and on the other, a gallery of statues, like on the Charles Bridge.

Panorama of the city.

I came to Kutna Hora by bus, and decided to go back by train, and the station is far from the center, it took an hour to walk.

Outside the center, Kutna Hora turned out to be a typical Soviet city.

Stalls and shops are just like in Russia.

Czech panel buildings.

People's Theater ("divadlo").

There is a small church on the outskirts of Kutna Hora.

Well, just an ordinary church-chapel in a rural cemetery.

If you don't know in advance, you might not realize that this is one of the most unusual and impressive places in Europe. At the end of the 19th century, Prince Schwarzenberg (one of the main noble families of Bohemia/Czech Republic) ordered the ossuary (bone storage) in the basement of this church to be restored. The storage of bones in itself is not a rare phenomenon, although usually they are not public (but it happens the other way around, for example, in Paris), but the local carving master Frantisek Rint showed remarkable creativity and used bones to decorate the church.

There are similar places in Europe, at least the chapel of bones in the Portuguese city Evora, which I did not get to due to restoration, and the Capuchin crypt in Rome.

Here the author went a little further, since not only the frame of the altar and the “drawings” on the walls, but even giant chandeliers were made from bones.

In the most honorable place is the coat of arms of the Schwarzenberg family.

Autograph of the author.

Once upon a time, this outskirts of Kutná Hora (Sedlec) was an independent important city, so it also has its own Gothic cathedral.

Cesky Krumlov

Cesky Krumlov is one of the most beautiful cities in the Czech Republic; It takes three hours to drive from Prague, but it’s worth it (especially since bus travel in the Czech Republic is not particularly expensive).

On the steep bank of the river stands the Schwarzenberg castle.

You can walk right through all the courtyards and exit on the opposite side.

Many of the walls have brick patterns painted on them.

At the end there will be a gallery with statues.

And then the castle ends, the princely stables, fields, etc. begin.
