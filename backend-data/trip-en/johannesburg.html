Pilanesberg National Park

In Africa, of course, I wanted to see the wildlife. Other continents do not have as many large animals as Africa. In the vast majority of countries, you can only go on a safari with a guide in a special open-top jeep, but South Africa stands out here in that there are parks where you can drive independently in a regular car. This way you can spend the night in the city, and not in the park, where it is usually much more expensive. The largest and most famous park is Kruger, but it is far from everything, and Pilanesberg was just along the way. Entry costs only about $8.

Johannesburg

Johannesburg is the largest city in South Africa, with an extremely dubious reputation. To be more precise, the crime rate there is incredibly high; according to all ratings it will be in the world tops. Although in the latest ranking for the number of murders per capita, it only ranks 34th, and among the cities of South Africa, the seemingly calm Cape Town is in the lead. Durban and Port Elizabeth are also higher. But statistics are one thing, feelings and people’s memory are another. Even if crime is now much lower compared to the 90s and 00s (and this is undoubtedly the case), locals still take the usual enhanced security measures. I, too, was very careful and walked around the city a little, but there was no way I could miss such a large and important city.

Johannesburg Airport is the main transport hub for the entire region. Until 2017, when South Africa abolished visas for citizens of Russia, even for a transfer you needed a visa, like in the USA, and this made traveling around southern Africa very problematic, since a South African visa was very hard to get.

Johannesburg from above. It looks decently like America - many private houses, areas for cars.

But in the distance there is chaotic construction - this is a poor area (township), where blacks lived under apartheid.

Wealthy areas have a lot of green space.

Here is the ideal view: on the right is the piece with high-rise buildings - this is Sandton, the modern business center. On the left is a large gray spot - this is the Alexander district, the most famous township.

In the distance you can see the high-rise buildings of the old center.

The old center is the historical core of the city, where skyscrapers were built during apartheid until 1994. After the fall of apartheid, the center was populated by former residents of Bantustans due to lawlessness, whites left for the outskirts (much like in the USA, only this all happened much more abruptly), and the center quickly fell into decay. The whites made a new business center in the north in the Sandton area. I checked into a hotel in Sandton. The hotel area is fenced around the perimeter, entry is through a barrier.

Pretoria

South Africa officially has three capitals: Cape Town has the parliament, Bloemfontein the supreme court, and Pretoria the office of the president and government. But of course, the office of the president is more important than the court, and besides, Pretoria is very close to the economic capital - Johannesburg. Statistically, Pretoria is not much safer than Johannesburg, but the central areas here still do not have such a bad reputation, so I walked right through the city, albeit carefully.

The fastest and most convenient way to travel from Johannesburg is by taking the Gautrain. It is not entirely clear where one city ends and another begins.

Emalahleni

From Eswatini I had to return to Johannesburg before flying home. I decided to drive overland through the province of Mpumalanga. In this province all the most interesting things are located on the eastern edge (Kruger National Park and Blyde Canyon), and the vast area west of Eswatini is mainly industrial and mining. I wanted to break the road so as not to spend the night in Johannesburg, for which the city of Emalahleni was best suited.

The city of Emalahleni turned out to be a typical South African city of average attractiveness.

Bus station. Often, both in everyday life and on signs, the old name of the city is used - Witbank. By the way, the modern name on some of the signs is written with a capital letter inside: eMalahleni. This is a feature of the spelling of the Zulu language, where e is a prefix denoting the nominative case of a certain gender (there are about 20 “genders” in Zulu).

Witbank is the Afrikaans name. This city is about 45% white, but I didn't see any whites. City center:
