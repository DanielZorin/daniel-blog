Cook Islands

31 January - 1 February 2020

The Cook Islands are a state in free association with New Zealand, and of all the not-fully-independent countries, one of the closest to independence. It has its own legislative and executive powers, and New Zealand is responsible only for defense and partially for foreign policy. The currency used is New Zealand dollars with locally minted coins, residents have New Zealand passports, but cannot vote by default. Entry to the Cook Islands is visa-free for everyone.

The main island of the country is called Rarotonga, and has a population of only 13 thousand (for comparison Tongatapu has 70 thousand, Upolu has 145 thousand). The second largest island is Aitutaki with a population of 2000 and most of the huge luxury hotels. There are a couple of dozen other islands with a population of no more than a few hundred, and flights once a month. Large planes from New Zealand and Australia fly to Rarotonga quite often.

Rarotonga is very close in shape to a circle with a radius of about 5 kilometers; accordingly, the road around the island has a length of 2π*5 = 30 kilometers. If you push yourself, you can even walk in a day, but it’s easier by bus. There are two routes: clockwise and counterclockwise. They run every hour, one at :00 minutes, the other at :30 (although, according to my observations, the counterclockwise route sometimes misses its turn). The fare is $5 (NZD), a day pass costs $16.

The capital of Cook Islands is called Avarua. Essentially this is the place where government agencies are concentrated, and locals' house stand along the main road just as throughout the rest of the island.
