Cuando lo iban a enterrar Ah, Mexico. Tequila, Mayan calendar, salsa, sombrero, bandito-gangsterito, jalapeno, gold, tacos, Pancho Villa, mariachi, tortilla, Aztec pyramids, Las Zetas, mezcal, wow... And even Juan without land, about whom Victor Jara sang , and in whose honor my pseudonym was taken, was also from Mexico. This is one of the most significant (tm) countries in the world, so I couldn’t help but cross it from end to end.

For most tourists, Mexico begins in Cancun, the easternmost point of the country, but I started in Tijuana, the most northwestern city. The northern half of Mexico consists of deserts and mountains (much like the US states on the other side of the border), and is not very densely populated. Tijuana is the largest city near the American border, with several million inhabitants. On the other side is San Diego, a million-plus city, and an even larger Los Angeles, so there is plenty to do for everyone. Of course, business here is tailored to the needs of those who travel across the border. This is the queue for pedestrians, or rather, its tail. The border crossing at Tijuana ranks first in the world in the number of people who pass through it (and in terms of cargo volume, Laredo, another crossing between the United States and Mexico, ranks first in the world).

Why do Americans go to Tijuana? First of all, drinking. In Mexico you can drink from the age of 18, and in the USA - from 21, plus alcohol in Mexico is much cheaper. Secondly, medicine. America is famous for its exorbitant prices for medical services, so half of Tijuana consists of hospitals and pharmacies, to the point that dental offices are opened in the same format as a hair salon.

Other services, consumer goods, food - all this is 5-10 times cheaper in Mexico than in America, therefore, what is not prohibited by customs is a sin not to bring. There is also this consideration: let’s say you damaged the car. If you repair it under insurance in the USA, it will spoil the “insurance history” and you will have to pay more for insurance next year. In Mexico, they will repair it for cash, no questions asked.

Only souvenirs in Tijuana cost the same as in the USA, and not like in the rest of Mexico.

Hotels in Tijuana are slightly more expensive than the average in Mexico. I settled at the very bottom for $10.

Guadalajara

The “baby duck syndrome” applies very well to travel. It is important which city you visited first in the country, because you subconsciously compare all the others with the first. In this regard, the first city in Mexico for me was not Tijuana, where I was essentially passing through, but Guadalajara.

Guadalajara is the capital of the state of Jalisco. The state has a strange motto, “Jalisco is Mexico!”, as if someone would dispute that.

The state of Jalisco produces the famous Mexican drink, tequila. Unfortunately, I didn’t have time to go to the city of the same name, but blue agave, the basis of tequila, is even planted along the roads as a decoration.

The main attraction of Guadalajara is this building. The hospital is called Hospicio Cabanas.

About half of the complex is empty corridors, courtyards and locked doors.

The other half houses an exhibition of contemporary art. Here, for example, is a room with saucers.

A yard filled with balloons in the shape of a globe. When I was at school, we had one like this in the gym. My favorite ball.

And here are applications made from old banknotes.

But the main thing here is the painting of the chapel. All walls and ceilings were painted by José Clemente Orozco. In the chapel, I secretly took only one photograph, which only partially conveys the artist’s unique style. This is probably the most interesting church painting I have ever seen. Even in the Sistine Chapel, I spent less time than here, looking at everything around me.

Near the hospital there are strange humanized pieces of furniture.

There are four main squares in Guadalajara, adjacent to the main cathedral on four sides. The entire city, like most other Mexican cities, is built on a grid.

I wonder why in Latin America they began to build cities on a grid, if this was not the case in Spain itself during colonization?

Guadalajara, by the way, was founded in 1542, around the same time as most major Latin American cities.

Most cities in Mexico look like this: houses with 1-2 floors, painted in bright colors.

And a lot of wires.

If you are tired of the noisy city, there is a solution in Guadalajara: take the bus and go to the final stop.

San Miguel de Allende

In Mexico they love to name streets and cities after political figures. In terms of the uniformity of urban toponymy, Mexico can easily compete with Russia: we have Lenin, Kirov, Soviet streets, they have Hidalgo, Juarez, Madero, Obregon streets. Unlike Russia, where cities were given new names, in Mexico the name was prologated: for example, Oaxaca is officially called Oaxaca de Juarez (in honor of the famous native). The city named after the Archangel Michael became known as San Miguel de Allende.

Queretaro

In the city of Queretaro, the hotel room was very poor, although cheap; to get Wi-Fi you had to go to the main square, fortunately it was a 5-minute walk; The situation was saved only by Jesus, who was watching what was happening.

The main difference between Queretaro and other Mexican cities is the new buses. In general, the city center is pleasant, it’s not for nothing that it was included in the UNESCO list, but not stunning. Therefore, I will take this opportunity to tell you a little about the history of Mexico, since two very important events took place in Queretaro.

The first attempts to declare Mexico's independence from Spain began when Spain was defeated and captured by Napoleon. The father of Mexican independence is Miguel Hidalgo from the town of Dolores in the state of Guanajuato. A street in every Mexican city is named after Hidalgo. Hidalgo was a priest who raised a popular uprising of peasants and miners, but they did not know how to fight, and the Spaniards defeated them, and Hidalgo himself was executed. The next fighter for independence was Jose Maria Morelos, in whose honor one of the streets near Hidalgo Street is named (and two small states near Mexico City are also named after the two fathers of the nation). Morelos also did not achieve success. Finally, the third attempt, led by Agustin de Iturbide, ended in victory in 1821, Mexico became independent.

Immediately after the victory, as usual, there was strife: Iturbide turned out to be a dictator, proclaimed himself emperor and arranged a coronation for himself, copying that of Napoleon. The emperor was dethroned a year later, after which Mexico was declared a republic in 1824. Taking a cue from the United States, the country was called the “United States of Mexico.” By the way, that’s what it’s officially called even now. Over the next 50 years, there was an unbroken chain of victories and revolts in Mexico: presidents changed on average every 9 months.

Particularly interesting in this regard is Antonio Lopez de Santa Anna, who was president 11 times. He started out as a soldier of the Spanish Empire, dispersed the Mexican rebels, but then went over to their side. When the Spaniards tried to take Mexico back by force, he defeated them and thereby became the hero of the nation. Well, the heroes of the nation, of course, have the moral right to stage a coup.

Santa Anna's friend Valentin Farias, who was a noble liberal at that time, became the president; for example, he took away lands from the church. But a year later, the junta decided to change policy, adopted a new undemocratic constitution, dispersed parliament, and in fact Santa Anna established a dictatorship.

It must be said that at that moment Mexico was not a single nation, but was a huge territory where different peoples (mostly Native Americans) ended up in one state because the Spaniards drew the border that way. Mexico was then twice as large as it is now, and included the territory of the modern American states of California, New Mexico, Arizona, Colorado, Utah, Nevada and Texas. It is not surprising that half of the states distant from the capital did not appreciate Santa Anna's government and decided to secede. In response, Mexico City sent troops there and carried out a punitive operation.

At that time, leading troops across the entire country was very long and expensive, so one state still managed to defend its independence. This was Texas. Texas by that time was half populated by English-speaking Americans who also knew how to fight. When Santa Anna's army reached Texas, they stormed the Alamo Monastery (located in the modern city of San Antonio), which was defended by 200 people, for two weeks. In the end, all the defenders were killed, but the Mexican army eventually fell into the cauldron, and Santa Anna himself was captured, so he signed the surrender and recognition of the independence of Texas. Returning back to Mexico, he immediately retracted this recognition.

At this time, the French landed an army in Mexico to force the Mexicans to pay their debts. In the battle, Santa Anna lost his leg and then walked on a piece of wood. However, they still lost the war. Santa Anna, however, again staged a coup and tried to carry out a punitive operation in Texas, as a result of which Yucatan separated, and in Texas they began to think about joining the United States. Having destroyed the country and plundered its budget, Santa Anna fled to Cuba. A year later, in 1846, things finally came to war between Mexico and the United States.

The war with America was a complete disaster for Mexico. The country was already dilapidated, but the Americans had a good army. Santa Anna from Cuba promised to yield everything to the Americans if they allowed him to return to power. Needless to say, he cheated everyone? In the end, Mexico still lost, the Americans took Mexico City and took away half of the country's territory, and Santa Anna again went into exile.

However, a few years later he staged a coup again and returned to power. This time he was no longer content with the title of president, but called himself “dictator for life” and “Your Excellency.” Santa Anna said he needed money to raise an army and take revenge on the Americans, so he sold more territory along the border to the US for $5 million. After that, he stole the money and went for a trip to Cuba for the last time. Twenty years later, having failed to raise the money in America to return to power in Mexico, he took advantage of the amnesty and came to Mexico City, where they paid no attention to him.

The wheel began to turn without Santa Anna. A civil war began in the country between liberals and conservatives. The leader of the liberals was the new hero of the nation, Benito Juarez, after whom a street is now also named in every city. In the end, the liberals won, but they once again bankrupted the country, and France once again demanded payment of debts.

France was ruled at that time by Napoleon III, who really wanted the glory of his uncle (in the end, this ended in failure for him in the war with Prussia). He decided to take over Mexico and place his man, the Austrian Prince Maximilian of Habsburg, on the throne. The French were successful, and in 1862 Mexico became an empire for the second time.

Emperor Maximilian turned out to be a very liberal ruler, a supporter of a constitutional monarchy. This alienated the strong-arm conservatives from him, and the liberals in Mexico already had their own leader in Juarez, who did not accept defeat. The only reason why the emperor remained in power was the presence of French troops and the absence of American troops, who at that time were busy in the American Civil War (1861-1865).

As soon as the US war ended, French troops left. Maximilian turned out to be a romantic and decided to stay, to show himself as a Mexican patriot. The Mexicans did not appreciate it, Maximilian's army capitulated in Queretaro, and the emperor was arrested and kept in this monastery:

The court sentenced the former emperor to death, and the sentence was soon carried out. Now on this site there is a chapel built with Austrian money.

Initially, the emperor was buried there, but later the body was transported to Vienna and buried in the same church where the Austrian emperors are buried.

Benito Juarez returned to power in Mexico and finally separated church and state.

After Juarez, Porfirio Diaz came to power in 1876 and ruled the country for 34 years. With him began the era of Stability (tm), which ended with a revolution and another civil war.

And as a result, in Queretaro, in this theater, the Mexican Constitution was signed, which is considered the end of the period of revolution, but that is a completely different story.
