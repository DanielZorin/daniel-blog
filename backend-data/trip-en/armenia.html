Armenia: Yerevan, Ethcmiadzin, Garni, Geghard, Noyemberyan, Sevan, Goris, Gyumri

3-6 May 2014

Noyemberyan-Sevan

Before arriving in Armenia, we had not actually been to the mountains; they were only visible on the horizon during the trip through Grozny and Derbent. Almost immediately after crossing the border of Armenia, Switzerland begins.

The first stop is in a town called Noyemberyan.

It was so steep here that we specially stopped the car driving back so we could go out at the pass to take photos.

If in Russia the church stands in the center of the village, then in Armenia they like to put the church on some mountain away from houses. This also applies to most large monasteries (Noravank, Tatev, Geghard).

Let's move on.

From the border with Georgia (Sadakhlo-Bagratashen, the most popular crossing) to Yerevan you can go in two ways - through Vanadzor or through Dilijan. The second road is a little longer, but incredibly beautiful.

The quality of roads in Armenia is mediocre; in Russia, the main roads are usually better.

Below this road through the pass is the village of Voskepar (Askipara), which in Soviet times was an exclave of the Azerbaijan SSR in Armenia. During the Karabakh War, all such small enclaves were actually annexed to Armenia (and one Armenian exclave came under the control of Azerbaijan).

In these places, the road de jure crosses the border of Armenia and Azerbaijan several times.

The road to the left of the lake is formally Armenia, actually Azerbaijan.

Yerevan

The capital of Armenia lags somewhat behind Tbilisi and Baku in terms of modernization. In general, Yerevan is the most Soviet of the three Transcaucasian capitals, perhaps because there is no money here from oil or grants. On the first day we lived in a residential area, which is officially called Nor-Nork, but in everyday life it is called “Massive”.

But in good weather you can see Ararat from the window.

And here on the horizon are the mountains in the area of Lake Sevan.

In the center they have prepared one ideally European street.

The more typical architecture is easily recognizable.

There are also surprises; this city hall building seems to have come here from America.

In general, it’s not boring in Yerevan.

Etchmiadzin

Armenians are very proud that Armenia was the first country to adopt Christianity. I visited two of the most famous monasteries located in close proximity to Yerevan. The first one is called Etchmiadzin.

The city in which it is located was also previously called Etchmiadzin, but in 1992 it received its older name Vagharshapat.

Vagharshapat urn.

There are churches here from the 5th-6th centuries.

Everything is built from typical Armenian bricks of different shades of brown.

Cemetery.

The Armenian Church is not Orthodox, unlike the Georgian one, since it separated from other churches much earlier. The image style here is unique.

Armenians also have crosses that are unusual for us.

Garni and Geghard

The second famous monastery is located in the mountains.

It is called Geghard. The spear of Longinus was kept here (hello Hideaki Anno!), or rather one of three: there is another one in the Vatican and one in Vienna. Now the spear is kept in the Etchmiadzin Museum.

A spear is attached to the cross.

Cell and Armenian crosses.

Inside this monastery is a very dark, ominous place.

In the semi-darkness you can hear the sounds of a stream flowing across the floor.

The light barely breaks through from above.

And crosses are everywhere in huge quantities. No less than on the Hill of crosses.

Nearby is the town of Garni, famous for the fact that a pagan Armenian temple has been preserved there, i.e. a temple built before the adoption of Christianity in the 3rd century.

But the main thing is not the temple itself, but what is around it.

Goris

The second big hitchhiking in Armenia was from Yerevan to Goris (and further to the border). Beautiful weather, and for the first hour and a half the road overlooks Ararat. Then the road turns into the mountains and goes entirely along serpentines, so that the distance from Yerevan to Stepanakert, nominally equal to 300 kilometers, is covered in a total of six hours.

Typical Armenian village. This one is called Areni.

Not far from here in the mountains is the famous Noravank monastery, but it’s not in the photo yet.

Gyumri

I decided to try a new genre: imagine that I am Artemy Lebedev and photograph the city from the car window. As it turned out, you can shoot quite a lot, although a better camera is desirable so that it focuses quickly. So, we are entering the city of Gyumri, the second largest city in Armenia. It used to be called Leninakan (and some people in Armenia still use this name in everyday life), and was almost completely destroyed by an earthquake in 1988.

Since then, houses of more than 3-4 floors have not been built here.

Gyumri is famous in Armenia for its beer "Gyumri", the logo of which (similar to the logo of the London Underground) hangs everywhere.

For the Internet, they still use the IE logo, not Chrome.
