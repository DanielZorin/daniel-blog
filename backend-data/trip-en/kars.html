Turkey: Doğubayazıt, Iğdır, Kars, Ani

1-5 September 2022

Doğubayazıt

Doğubayazıt is the closest city to the Iranian border, but also interesting in itself, since here was one of the most important fortresses in the east of the empire, which was besieged during almost every Russian-Turkish war. Historically, the city and fortress were located on the mountain, but were destroyed in 1930 during the suppression of the Kurdish uprising, which made Bayazit the capital of the proclaimed Kurdistan. After this, a new city was built in the valley and named Doğubayazıt (eastern Bayazit).

Iranian border. Mount Ararat can be clearly seen from here.

Bayazit turned out to be a rather large and rather lively town.

It is interesting that during the Russian-Turkish War of 1877-78, Russian veteran soldiers, who also witnessed the Crimean War of 1853-56, noted that Bayazit had become greatly impoverished during this time. The reason for this was the opening of the Suez Canal in 1869, after that the flow of goods by land fell sharply.

Iğdır

Iğdır is a regional center in the very east of Turkey. It’s also close to the tiny but very important border between Turkey and Azerbaijan for two allied countries. This city became Turkish only in 1921 under the Treaty of Kars; before that, for almost 100 years it was part of the Russian Empire, and until 1828 it belonged to Persia. Thus, Iğdır was Russian much longer than neighboring Kars, but nothing remains from this period, apparently because it was a very small county town in the Yerevan province.

Kars

Kars is one of the largest and most interesting cities in the far east of Turkey. Its peculiarity is that from 1878 to 1918 it was part of the Russian Empire, and unlike Iğdır, it was the capital of the Kars region, and was built in accordance with a master plan similar to other Russian cities of that time. Therefore, in Kars you can see a lot of things that will remind you of a provincial city in central Russia.

In the center of Kars there is a picturesque castle on a hill. As I understand it, the historical Turkish part of the city was on the slopes of this hill, as well as on the site of that large square where the inscription “Kars” stands, but it has not been preserved.

Ani

Ani is an ancient Armenian city, the capital of the Armenian state of the 9th-11th centuries. In my opinion, this is one of the most interesting archaeological zones, even by the standards of Turkey that is filled with ancient Roman and Greek ruins.

Ani is an hour's drive from Kars, right on the border with Armenia (which has been closed for 30 years). Once a day a bus runs there from the center of Kars especially for tourists. The road is on a plain, this is the Armenian highland.

Near Ani is the modern village of Ocakli.

Interesting fence.

The fortress wall at the entrance. The city has a triangular shape, it is surrounded on two sides by the Akhuryan River, along which the border runs, and on the third by this wall.

When you pass through the walls, you find yourself in the territory of the former city. It is a rare case that the outer walls, and not just the military citadel, have been preserved, so that one can appreciate the size of the ancient city (another similar example: Diyarbakir). According to historians, 100-200 thousand people could live in the city.

In the middle of everything stands a colossal cathedral.

Ani was destroyed in the 14th century and completely abandoned in the 18th. During the Armenian-Turkish War of 1918-1921 they even wanted to completely demolish it and caused great damage, and for decades the condition of the monuments was poorly monitored for obvious reasons. Due to the proximity of the border, entry was limited for a long time; only in 2004 Ani was opened for tourism, and it began to be restored with the help of UNESCO.
