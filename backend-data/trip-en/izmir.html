Ephesus

Ephesus is probably the most famous archaeological site in Turkey. Surprisingly, I explored it almost alone. Of course, it was winter, but in Italy or Ireland there was still someone, while here most of the time I was completely alone, only towards the end several people came towards me.

I arrived from Selçuk by taxi, the taxi driver took me to the upper entrance, from where the main road descends into the main part of the city. By public transport you can go up to the lower entrance (but you need to walk another kilometer from the highway).

The main street of Ephesus is well preserved.

Even the mosaic remains.

Ephesus was a major city during the times of Ancient Greece and Ancient Rome. Then it stood on the seashore, which is now 5 kilometers away. When the sea left, the city began to empty due to the decline of trade, and was finally abandoned only in the 15th century.

Izmir

Izmir is the third largest city in Turkey. Before the First World War, the population of the city was mainly Greek (and it was called Smyrna), and after the defeat of Turkey, Greece annexed the region, but in 1922 the Turks recaptured it, and the Greek population was killed or evicted. At the same time, there was a fire in the city that destroyed most of the historical center.

From the center of Çanakkale, ferries run regularly to several towns on the other side, on the Gallipoli Peninsula. On March 18, 2022, they promised to open the first bridge across the Dardanelles, 20 kilometers to the north.

The essence of the Battle of Gallipoli was that England and France wanted to land troops at the Dardanelles, capture Istanbul and take Turkey out of the First World War. Their force was landed, but could not advance anywhere, and was eventually evacuated with heavy losses. Troy

An important attraction of Çanakkale are the ruins of Troy - the city about the assault of which the Iliad was written. The ruins are located near the village of Tevfikiye, which is about 30 minutes by minibus. I was the only visitor that morning. There is a Trojan horse at the entrance.

Troy itself.

For a long time it was believed that the Iliad was purely fiction, but in the 1860s this site was found to correspond well with the descriptions from the legends.

The city was destroyed several times, different layers on the diagrams are called numbers (Troy-1 - Troy-9). The most central fortified part is Troy-2.

Heinrich Schliemann, who found these ruins, considered this citadel to be the site of the Trojan War, but it was later discovered that it dates back to an even earlier era.

The Troy of the Iliad is Troy 7, destroyed around 1180 BC.

Here the layers are clearly indicated.

Entrance to Troy.

The Trojan War was followed by a period known as the "Dark Ages" or "Late Bronze Age Catastrophe" - a period of severe cultural and technological decline comparable to the Middle Ages.

The latest Troy existed already during the Roman Empire.
