Myanmar: Mandalay, Monywa, Bagan, Inle Lake

3-12 February 2016

Mandalay

I thought Mandalay was a village with one-story sheds, but it turned out to be quite a decent city.

Finally there was a normal hotel where I could wash myself with hot water. They even served a breakfast of fruit and omelet with coffee, just like the good old days.

I was a little tired after a week in Myanmar, so I didn’t waste energy exploring the many local temples, especially since they are scattered over long distances, and the public transport system in Mandalay is very unclear. Well, now to the main thing. The king of Myanmar at the end of the 18th century suffered from gigantomania (a common disease in this region) and decided to make the largest pagoda in the world, 150 meters high.

Then the money ran out and a legend was invented that the king would die when the pagoda was completed, so construction stopped.

And 20 years later there was an earthquake, and cracks began to appear in the rather large building.

That's how it stands.

Someone leaves autographs.

Near the passage to the top there is a sign that says this is at your own peril and risk.

But everyone climbs.

The only inconvenient thing is that you have to do it barefoot.

But the apocalyptic views at the top are worth it.

There is no insurance, no guardrails, you can easily fall into a crevice.

The white building is what the big pagoda was supposed to look like in the plans.

Opposite for some reason is a nursing home.

In only one case did the king's megalomania reach its end - he made a giant bell. He probably just didn’t know that in far northern Moscow there was an even larger bell, so the Mingun one became only the second in the world.

Monywa

The city of Monywa (pronounced "mown - you - are") has not yet been included in the main tourist routes in Myanmar. In practice, in addition to the complete absence of foreigners, this means big problems with finding accommodation - those few hotels that have a license to serve tourists are hesitant and ask up to $30 for rooms, the real price of which is $7-8. You can visit Monywa for two reasons - to break the long road from Mandalay to Bagan in half and at the same time to look at the giant Buddha statue built outside the city.

The city of Monyua looks something like this.

To get to the Buddha statue it’s about 25 kilometers one way (a motorcycle taxi, by the way, cost only $8 round trip). Halfway you can visit another attraction - the Thanboddhay Temple. It was founded in the 14th century, but the current building was built in the 1930s.

This is the most glamorous temple in the world. I've never seen so much pink and gold before.

Mini pagodas look like cream cakes with flowers.

There is a small tower nearby from which you can look at the temple from above.

It’s interesting that women are not allowed to go up there, because according to Buddhist concepts, when a woman’s legs are higher than a man’s head, it’s a problem.

There is already domestic tourism in Myanmar, many Burmese take selfies at this temple.

Bagan

It's time to talk about the most famous landmark of Myanmar.

First, some joke advice on how to save $20 without buying an entrance ticket. Unlike Angkor, in Myanmar they decided that hiring a bunch of guards to stand at the entrance to each temple was too expensive. It is enough to place a ticket office at the main entrance, and 99% will not bother bypassing it, especially since the majority come anyway on organized tours. The ticket office is located on the road leading from the airport, train and bus stations to the center of Nyaung U town, where inexpensive hotels are located. All transport from afar goes through train stations and the airport (there are also ships on the river from Mandalay, but it seems like you have to buy a ticket immediately at the exit from the ship), so unless you have your own motorcycle, you won't be able to arrive from other directions. You need to go around the ticket office on the side; if you go on foot, there is only one difficulty - to walk of about 4 kilometers (it can be hot during the day). From the main road I turned right along the path marked on maps.me. First you walk along the airport fence, then through a residential area, then through a field (there are even a couple of pagodas there), then another residential area and the city center. Is my conscience tormenting me because I didn’t buy a ticket? No, because this money is still going to someone's pocket. Collecting $20 from each of hundreds of tourists, they did not even manage to hire normal cleaners, which is why the territory of Bagan in some places is the same garbage dump as the rest of Myanmar.

More precisely, here, near the main entrance, garbage is collected and dumped in the field behind the pagoda.

You have never seen such a view of Bagan!

Neither at the hotel nor on site did they check my ticket. I read that they can check in the evening near the temple where everyone goes to watch the sunset, so I went there in the afternoon. The city of Nyaung U is not similar to Siem Reap, apparently there are much fewer tourists here, plus the rich live in another town of New Pagan, so it is an ordinary residential city. There are different ways to get around Bagan. Most travel on buses with a guide. There are no tuk-tuks, like in Angkor, in Pagan, instead there are carts with horses. This is the worst way: the most expensive, the slowest, and the most uncomfortable due to the dust on the roads. It is best to take a bicycle ($1.5 per day) or an electric motorbike ($5 per day). Bagan is more compact than Angkor, only 5 kilometers long, and the terrain is flat, so the bike is quite comfortable. There are jugs of water strategically scattered around the area to keep you from getting too thirsty. And in several temples there are even coolers with ice-cold water.

Inle Lake

Inle Lake is, as they say in America, a tourist trap. Tourists are taken on boats to souvenir shops and all sorts of workshops. Those who have been to Egypt and Turkey will understand. However, I still think that Inle Lake is worth a visit.

What do we have? In the morning, you can charter a boat at the pier for 20,000 kyat ($16). Those with an organized tour obviously pay much more and travel in crowds, but Alena and I got a boat for two. If you don’t go to Inthein (and many don’t!), then the price is even lower - 15,000, but Inthein is exactly the most interesting thing.

The trip begins in Nyaung Shwe town.

There are several large villages on Inle Lake, where houses are on stilts, and travel is mainly by boats along canals. Local fishermen are known for attaching an oar to their leg to keep their hands free.

No one appears to be catching fish, but simply posing for the boats passing by.
