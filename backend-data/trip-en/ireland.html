Ring of Kerry

In my opinion, there is nothing to do in Killarney unless you like to hang out in a souvenir shop all day. Therefore, the main reason to be here is trips to the southwestern coast of Ireland: the Dingle Peninsula and the Iveragh Peninsula. Iveragh is a fairly large peninsula, and the road along the coast circles, starting and ending in Killarney, the capital of County Kerry, which is why the route is called the "Ring of Kerry".

The road is for the most part quite narrow, with one lane in each direction. It is recommended to drive counterclockwise by car, because buses travel in the opposite direction, clockwise. The bus tour is surprisingly cheap, only 20 euros.

Limerick

You can get from Killarney to Galway with one change in the city of Limerick, after which the genre of humorous five-line poems is named.

On the way we came across some ruined castle near the river.

The main dominant feature of the city is a huge medieval castle.

Near it is a long bridge over the Shannon River - the largest in Ireland.

Classic view from the opposite side.

Cliffs of Moher

Galway is primarily the gateway to visit the Cliffs of Moher. In principle, you can get there from Dublin in one day, but then the journey will take more than three hours one way, and from Galway you can still see a lot of interesting things along the way.

The area south of Galway is called the Burren. Here you can often find such an unusual landscape - fields covered with what look like stone slabs.

It's limestone, so maybe in a few million years there will be a new Stone Forest.

Entire hills are covered with these stones.

Finally, the main course is the Cliffs of Moher. An interesting tourist center is disguised as an extension of the hill.

It is located at the most popular place where the cliffs reach their maximum height - more than 200 meters.

It’s interesting that the official path here is laid as much as 5 meters from the edge (on the left you can see how it is fenced off with stone slabs), so if you walk along it, you can’t see anything at all.

The well-trodden paths confirm that almost everyone does not walk behind the fence.

There are quite a lot of people in the summer, but, as in all other attractions in the world, 90% of them are concentrated in 10% of the area near the entrance, the further you go, the freer it is around.

The cliffs stretch south for another 8 kilometers, gradually decreasing. Unfortunately, I didn’t have time to go that far, so I only got to the first big turn.

Many have seen the Cliffs of Moher in the sixth Harry Potter film - this is the place where Voldemort hid the locket.

View down.

View in the opposite direction - there are just fields there. If you look from there, you won’t guess that there is a cliff 200 meters high beyond the edge.

I managed to go in the opposite direction from the entrance. There is a tower built for tourists as an observation tower in the first half of the 19th century.

And the views there are similar.

Sligo

Initially, after Galway, I thought of going to Northern Ireland by the fastest route - through Dublin, where transport runs every hour. But then I figured that there was nothing stopping me from driving along the west coast and spending the night in Londonderry, and so I would see more of Northern Ireland, and at the same time I would also visit the north-west of the republic. Only one change - in the city of Sligo.

In general, this is an ordinary provincial center, like others, it has its own classic Irish churches, pubs and so on. There aren’t many tourists, but that’s not a big deal, life is in full swing as it is.

The weather, however, was notably bad - it rained all day, varying from drizzling to torrential.

The main attraction is the ruins of the abbey, abandoned in the mid-18th century.
