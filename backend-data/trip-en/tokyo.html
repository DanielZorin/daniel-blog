Japan: Tokyo, Yokohama, Kamakura

28-30 November, 1-2, 17 December 2017

Tokyo

Tokyo is a prime example of a city with a complex structure, where there is no obvious main central part, but interesting areas and places are scattered throughout the territory. Therefore, I will describe it sequentially by district. In terms of administrative divisions of Japan, the city of Tokyo consists of 23 wards, and another 26 less densely built-up municipalities in the hills to the west, which used to be a separate prefecture. In fact, the entire Kanto region (Tokyo and 6 neighboring prefectures) is one very, very large city, but I will still consider cities in other prefectures, like Yokohama, separately. Of the 23 districts of Tokyo, about 8 are of greatest interest to a traveler. Let's start with the place where I lived - the Taito district.

Tokyo has an extensive subway and train system, which is also unusual for its incredible liberalization: within the city's administrative boundaries alone there are about four different subway providers, plus a monorail, plus several dozen companies that operate commuter trains to neighboring prefectures. However, it is very easy to pay as you go - the Pasmo pass is accepted by all operators, and not only in Tokyo, but also in all other big cities of the country.

Within Tokyo, I traveled almost exclusively by Tokyo Metro, a private subway with 9 lines. There is also the state-owned Toei subway, which has 4 lines. One trip costs somewhere from 200 yen, but it is obviously cheaper to buy a day ticket for 600 yen on the Tokyo metro or 900 yen on both subways. However, I only used the first one; this only lengthened some of the routes a little.

Except for rush hour, the metro is quite free.

Yokohama

Yokohama is the second largest city in Japan; in fact, it and Tokyo have long merged. You can get to Yokohama via several different railway lines, including the nationwide JR. The cheapest option is the Toyoko Line (TOkyo-YOKOhama) from Shibuya Station. Yokohama is not just a residential area, but an important historical city: in the period after the discovery of Japan in the 19th century, it was home to foreign diplomats and merchants, close to the imperial capital of Tokyo, but still separate. A parallel can be drawn with Beijing and Tianjin in China.

I arrived at Yokohama Main Station. Many lines from different companies in all directions depart from different platforms.

There are bas-reliefs at the entrance to the station.

This is the modern part of the city, there are multi-storey office buildings and shops.

Kamakura

The city of Kamakura, famous for its ancient temples, is just half an hour's drive from Yokohama.

The city itself is quite ordinary.

Someone's yard.

There are mainly three temples visited in Kamakura. The first of them is Hase-dera.

You are not allowed to take photographs inside; there is a huge statue of the goddess Guanyin (Kannon in Japanese).

There are also many smaller statues here.

And a small cave with statues.

The temple is on a hill, so from there you can look down on Kamakura and the ocean shore.

And of course a beautiful garden with a pond.

The second important temple is Kotoku-in, where a 13th century bronze Buddha statue sits.

There are windows behind the Buddha.

You can even go inside the statue.

Back to the station I took a circuitous route through the hills. There are residential areas with typically Japanese houses.

Garbage collection is complex. There is a schedule for which days what type of garbage is taken out, and it needs to be put in these collecting boxes by morning. The poster says that you should not throw away garbage in areas other than your own.

A rickshaw carries tourists. Yes, Japan was once poor, like Calcutta is now, and foot rickshaws were common here.

Hidden in the hills is the Zeni-arai-benten temple.

It is interesting because, according to legend, the local spring helps to multiply money, so people put coins or even banknotes in special bowls with a handle and lower them under water.
