Albania: Sarandë, Gjirokastër

27-29 October 2017

Sarande

I was in Albania in 2014 and wrote a lot about this beautiful country. This time I visited the southern part of Albania - it is very easy to get to Sarandë by ferry from Corfu, and then I went back to Greece by land.

Sarandë is an Albanian resort town. The embankment is well maintained.

In Albania, tree trunks are painted white, and palm trees are no exception.

The beach in the city looks like this. There is also the resort village of Ksamil, 15 minutes drive south of Sarandë, which seems to have a larger beach.

Embankment.

Remains of the fortress.

Bell tower.

A little far from the embankment there are old apartment buildings, apparently from communist times.

Clothes on the balcony.

Santa Claus hanged himself.

Albanian flag.

On the streets they sell from upside down boxes, just like in Asia.

Albania is cheap, almost like Thailand. The hotel costs 10-15 euros. Restaurant dishes cost 2-3 euros (1 euro = 120 lek).

Not far from Sarandë there are the ruins of the ancient Roman city of Butrint - a World Heritage Site.

It is unusual here that the city remained abandoned in the forest; no new settlement arose in this place.

Amphitheater.

Temple ruins.

There are forests and picturesque bays around Butrint.

Gjirokastër

The road from Sarandë to Gjirokastër leads through a beautiful mountain pass.

When leaving Sarandë, you immediately go up, drive along serpentines for a while, then go down on the other side of the hills into a long and narrow valley, and there the road is straight.

Outskirts of Sarandë.

Beauty.

View of the valley.

The road here is straight, and the towns are located on the hillsides.

There is emptiness in the middle.

If you look closely, you can see the famous Albanian bunkers in many villages, built during the communist dictatorship.

* * *

Gjirokastër is one of the two most beautiful ancient cities in Albania (the other is Berat). Gjirokastër stands on the slopes of a steep hill.

At the very top there is a powerful fortress.

There is a long pedestrian tunnel under the fortress to the other side of the hill.

You can go inside for a couple of euros. Most of the buildings there have not survived.

Empty hall.

Traces of torn ivy.

Stage and clock tower.

Some decommissioned combat aircraft are also stored here.

Monument to the unknown soldier and gallery of old artillery.

And also empty bottles in the corner.

But the main thing here is the view.

The old town has classic Ottoman architecture.

There are many walls made of simple stone, and the same sidewalks underfoot.

Drainage goes through the center.

An abandoned house where someone decided to store old things.

At the same time, here and there there are communist monuments.

Gjirokastër is the hometown of the Albanian dictator Enver Hoxha, so it is the most left-wing city.

"Enver" is written on the wall.

George Soros Street.

The modern part of the city. In my hotel you could go out onto the roof, from where you could clearly see everything.

Strange ornate building.

Spiny flowers.

In the mornings, they sell fresh milk in cola bottles right on the streets.

Dinner in Gjirokastër. Like everywhere else in Albania, it’s cheap here, each dish cost 2-3 euros.
