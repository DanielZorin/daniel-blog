Truth or Consequences

Less than seven years have passed since my first trip to the USA, and finally I was able to do what I had long wanted - a road trip across America. The choice fell on the state of New Mexico because 1. It is not as cold in February as in the north 2. It is not so far to fly from the West coast 3. There is a lot to see. I arrived in the evening in Albuquerque, the largest city in the state, rented a car at the airport and checked into a nearby motel. Having thought through it, I decided not to explore Albuquerque the next day, but to immediately go south: I didn’t want to immediately drive a car into the center of a big city in an unfamiliar country, but I wanted to experimentally find out how far I could drive in a day. The maximum goal for the first day was to get to the White Sands National Park (about 450 kilometers), but it was possible to stay at a motel in any of the cities along the road.

The motel is really accessible only by car - there are large highways on three sides, and a river on the fourth.

White Sands

White Sands State Park is New Mexico's most visited attraction. In the middle of the steppe there is a small area strewn with snow-white sand (particles of gypsum), as if it were a desert with dunes, only white.

A national monument differs from a national park in that architectural/sculptural structures can also be added to the monuments, but in this case there is not much difference. White Sands was raised in status from a monument to a park at the very end of 2019; not all signs had time to change at that time.

This national park is only accessible by car, you can also move around inside it only by car, there are no shuttles.

From the entrance, drive about 5 kilometers to the most interesting part of the park; along the way, from time to time there are small paths several hundred meters deep.

Carlsbad Caverns

Carlsbad Cavern was long considered the largest cave in the world (until the discovery of larger ones in Malaysia and more recently in Vietnam). It was described in the book “One-Story America”; Ilf and Petrov were amazed at how well everything was equipped here, even with an elevator. The place is really remote, the nearest more or less large airport (El Paso) is 250 kilometers away. But I don't regret crossing all over New Mexico on the way here.

The entrance is at the top of the hill.

View of the surrounding area.

You can go down on foot (an hour and a half along the stairs) or by elevator. I didn’t feel well - I had a cold the day before, and had spent five hours driving in the morning, so after drinking paracetamol, I went to the elevator.

And here is the cave. The part open to the public is the “Great Hall” - the widest place, but in general the cave goes deeper and further down.

The path through the cave to the end of the large hall and back is 2-3 kilometers, it took me about an hour and a half to two hours.

There is very good illumination here, so all the numerous stalactites and other stone formations can be clearly seen up close.

And there is a lot to see here.

I have never seen such a number and variety of forms in other caves before.

The place is truly grandiose.

I took pictures without a tripod, but thanks to the bright light, it turned out more or less fine handheld.

Bandelier

The Rocky Mountains begin in northern New Mexico. To the east of Santa Fe is the Sangre de Cristo massif, to the north is the Jemez massif. In the same region there are many territories where Native Americans live. They have their own atmosphere and laws there. In the USA it is generally not customary to walk anywhere, every piece of land is fenced off and hung with the inscriptions “No Trespassing” and “No Loitering”, but the Indians went even further in this regard. In their villages, not only is it forbidden to take photographs (this is not surprising), but it is also forbidden to use a mobile phone (otherwise you might record the sound) and even... to draw. The only exception is the large community of Pojoaque, north of Santa Fe.

It's right off the highway heading north into Colorado, at the junction with the road heading west into the mountains.

There are many shops and casinos here - usually casinos are prohibited in the USA (except Las Vegas), but in Indian territories it is possible, for many of them this is the main source of income.

Santa Fe

The capital of New Mexico, Santa Fe is one of the most unusual cities in the United States. Let's start with the fact that it was founded in 1610 - it is the second oldest city in the States, after the small city of St. Augustine on the shores of Florida (and San Juan, if it is also considered as the USA).

The old part of Santa Fe is numbered from the central square, as in any Mexican city, which is not typical for American ones (usually the streets there are numbered from the river or sea). There is an obelisk in the center.

Kasha Katuwe

On my way from Santa Fe to Albuquerque, I took a short detour to visit the small and relatively little-known Kasha-Katuwe Park.

Admission is only $5 (annual passes for all national parks are also accepted). A few years ago the road to the park was very bad, but now everything seems to be in order, only the parking lot is small, so there may be queues on weekends in the summer, but in winter everything was empty.

A path leads from the parking lot to the top of this mountain.

The full name is Kasha Katuwe Tent Rocks.

It's not hard to see why.

The road up goes between the rocks, at some point it becomes very narrow.

And it turns out to be a slot canyon.

The most famous slot canyon is located in the neighboring state of Arizona - Antelope Canyon.

Due to snowfall, the last and steepest part of the climb was closed, the road was covered with ice and too slippery.

So I had to go back without looking at the rocks from above.

It was only possible to climb higher to the foot of the rocks on the other side.

The nearest village is called Cochiti. This is also Indian territory, where photography and drawing are prohibited, so I did not go deep into it.

Albuquerque

Albuquerque is the largest city in New Mexico. Outside of America, it is best known as the setting for the TV series Breaking Bad. I decided to go through some iconic places, and since I came from the northeast, I immediately got to the most important one - Walter's house.

Tourists come here most often, so the owners of the house even put up a notice asking them to take photographs from afar (and not to throw pizza on the roof).

The area around is a typical American residential area.

What’s interesting is that it’s not exactly a street grid; I had to pay close attention to the map to get the correct route.

There are mailboxes with a flag at every home.

When you get out of the residential area, you find yourself on a large street for cars with a high speed limit; along a network of such streets you can get to the center.

A couple of minutes' drive from Walter's house there is a plaza - it's not exactly a shopping center, but an area with a bunch of businesses.

It is easy to recognize the office of lawyer Saul Goodman in this place.

Also five minutes' drive away is Walter's Car Wash - formerly Octopus Car Wash, now part of the Mister chain.

From here I went to the city center, it took about 30-40 minutes, since the city area is quite large. At some point, I was surprised to discover that the middle of the road along which I was traveling was a designated area for buses - a system of high-speed buses with separate stops had been organized here.

After crossing the railroad over a humpback bridge, I reached downtown Albuquerque. The city’s official website kindly posted a map of the center indicating where parking is and how much it costs, so I immediately knew that on the fourth street down it was free.

It’s as if it’s not really a downtown area anymore; it’s a residential area, not a business one. The downtown skyscrapers are visible in the distance; it takes about 15 minutes to walk there.
