Russia: Yuzhno-Sakhalinsk, Korsakov, Novosibirsk

6-9 April 2014

Yuzhno-Sakhalinsk

Crimean latitude, Kolyma longitude

I expected that it might be cool on Sakhalin, but I didn’t think it would be this cold. There was a snowstorm for several days before my arrival, no planes were flying, so I was lucky. The most important thing was skiing and snowboarding, and this was after I walked around in Vladivostok in just a T-shirt.

They are going to make a base on Sakhalin to prepare the Russian team for the next Olympic Games, which will be held in Korea.

You can ride the ski lifts without equipment; there is a special excursion ticket for this.

At the top it was simply hellishly cold; in Moscow in January it is usually warmer.

Because of the snowstorm, everything was up to our ears in snow. Cafe.

As we know, the southern half of Sakhalin belonged to Japan from 1905 to 1945. Yuzhno-Sakhalinsk was originally called Toyohara and was built next to the former Russian capital of Sakhalin, Vladimirovka. As in Kaliningrad, almost nothing remains from the previous owners. The fighting on Sakhalin, of course, was not as intense as in East Prussia, but almost all the buildings of the Japanese period were wooden and simply burned down. Since I had more than enough time, I found all three buildings left from the Japanese. One - a local history museum.

Two - an art museum.

Three - the military prosecutor's office.

This building is the hardest to find, it is in a courtyard in a residential area.

There is one more thing left from the Japanese: the railway. Even the inexperienced naked eye can see that there is a narrow gauge here. After Sakhalin returned to the USSR, they did not change the rails, but continued to build a road on the northern part of the island with the same track. Now, for the most part, there are few passenger railroads, in particular, the famous Yuzhno-Sakhalinsk - Kholmsk section, where you can look at the Japanese “Devil's Bridge” if you walk ten kilometers on foot (not in this weather, of course), has been abandoned. There is a daily train across the island to the city of Nogliki. It seems that the rails are going to be changed to standard Russian gauge.

Otherwise, there is still a noticeably increased number of Japaneseized names of shops and cafes.

Modern Sakhalin is considered a fairly prosperous region because there is oil there. Oil is extracted in the harsh conditions of the north of the island, where they go on shifts, and everyone rests in the South. Due to the oil business, the city has a community of expats, whose cottages can be seen if you drive along the highway to Korsakov. There are also a certain number of shopping centers on the outskirts, and all signs with street names are duplicated in English.

There are expensive hotels on Sakhalin, but there is one cheap one - "Dalnevostochnik" on the station square.

For just 550 rubles I got into a double room. The hotel is a real museum of the Soviet Union.

But from the window there is a gorgeous view of the Lenin monument.

Everything is expensive on Sakhalin, much more expensive than in Moscow, which, however, is not surprising: delivery by sea or by plane is expensive. But here you can taste authentic Japanese instant noodles relatively cheaply (30-60 rubles), after which Doshirak and Rollton are no longer needed.

Despite the presence of foreigners and English-language signs, in general Sakhalin has a distinguished culture.

Korsakov

The name of the city of Korsakov is pronounced with an accent on “A”, in contrast to the surname of the famous composer. Korsakov (Otomari) was the first capital of Japanese Sakhalin, and now it is the dirtiest city I have ever met, worse than Jakarta, Brussels, Casablanca and other garbage dumps.

Of course, the weather was not very good, but a simple comparison with neighboring Yuzhno-Sakhalinsk shows that in Korsakov there is no street cleaning as a fact.

Novosibirsk

After walking along the streets of Novosibirsk, you need to wash off a thick layer of dust from your face, picked up from cars on the local 9,000-lane highways.

A day earlier, the problem of dust was irrelevant, but the problem of puddles was very relevant.

The courtyards of Novosibirsk are not conducive to walking in the rain.

And the streets, in fact, too.

It's not all that bad at all. There is a small historical center in Novosibirsk, about a hundred years old.

You can walk normally here.

Beautiful balconies.
