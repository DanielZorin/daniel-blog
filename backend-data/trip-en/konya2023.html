Çumra

Near Konya there is one of the few places on the World Heritage List that I have not previously visited - the excavations of Çatalhöyük. The nearest city to them is called Çumra, it is about 40 kilometers from Konya. Recently, the high-speed railway line was extended from Konya further to the regional center of Karaman, and an intermediate stop was made in Çumra. Therefore, now you can get there in just 20 minutes comfortably.

From Çumra itself there are still 10 kilometers to the excavations. If minibuses even go there, they are very rare, so I spent money on a taxi. According to the meter, including the wait, it came out to about $15 (400 liras).

Çatalhöyük is a Neolithic city. It existed from approximately 7500 to 5500 BC (that is, not as ancient as Göbekli Tepe, but much older than the Egyptian pyramids). Now only one piece of the excavation is open to the public.

You can see the walls of the houses tightly adjacent to each other.

It was in these holes in the floor that the residents of Çatalhöyük buried their dead. Then, periodically, the roof of the house was torn down, a new one was built higher, and this created layers of burials.

It sounds strange at first glance, but for someone who has been to the Pacific Islands and seen graves there right at the entrance to every house, it is not so unusual.

In the distance you can see a “layered” wall - in fact, these are layers of garbage that have been thrown there for decades. The fact is that in this city there were no streets familiar to us; people walked mainly on the roofs of houses, and the spaces between houses were used for garbage. The houses were entered through a hatch in the roof.

Archaeologists at work. There were also paintings on the walls, and many different artifacts, but all this is now in museums, in Ankara and Konya.
