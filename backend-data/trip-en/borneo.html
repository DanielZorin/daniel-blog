Kuching

Once upon a time, the entire northern coast of Borneo belonged to the Sultan of Brunei. But the inhabitants of the rivers and jungles had no strong desire to sponsor his treasury, and they were constantly in opposition. A certain James Brooke, an English businessman, successfully suppressed the uprising in 1841, for which he received as a reward the land where the city of Kuching is now. Brooke became rajah and gradually annexed almost the entire territory of Brunei to himself, leaving the Sultan only that pitiful piece, which to this day is called Brunei. Brooke and his dynasty of "White Rajahs" ruled Sarawak until the Japanese occupation in 1942, after the war the British abolished their autonomy.

Kuching means "cat" in Malay. There are other versions of the origin of the name: from the Chinese language or from the Malay name for the longan fruit - mata kucing (cat's eye). The cat is the symbol of the city, so it is depicted here together with the hornbill, the symbol of the state of Sarawak.

Kapit

Kapit is a very unusual city: it is one of the few cities that cannot be reached by road, only by river or plane. The largest of these cities is Iquitos in Peru, but Kapit is also not a village - there are 20 thousand people there. You can practically go to Kapit in two hours by high-speed ship from Sibu along the Rajang River.

The fare is 25 ringgit one way ($6).

Kapit is a rare opportunity to travel deep into Sarawak. All major cities are located near the coast.

Bridge at the exit from Sibu. Apparently there will be a new highway.

The interior of Borneo is being developed. Forests are being cut down slowly.

Kapit, like other large cities, is inhabited mainly by Chinese. Borneo's indigenous people, the Dayaks, historically lived along rivers away from the coast.

Their housing is called a “longhouse” - a communal apartment with a long corridor and small rooms.

Labuan

Labuan is a small island off the coast of Borneo. This is a separate region of Malaysia (federal territory like Kuala Lumpur) and a duty-free zone. Interestingly, when arriving in the states of Sabah and Sarawak, they put stamps in the passport, even if you fly from mainland Malaysia. But on Labuan they don’t put it, and if you leave there by ferry, they don’t put it either.

A plane ticket from Miri to Labuan cost only $20.

Ferries operate from Kota Kinabalu and Brunei to Labuan.

The airport is located a couple of kilometers from the capital of the island, minibuses go past.

The cheap hotels in Labuan are not that great, but I still found a place for $10 with air conditioning from a Rooms for Rent sign at the entrance of a residential building. There was no need to check into the abandoned Labuan Hotel.

Labuan is a special customs zone, here you can drink inexpensively. The main streets are lined with bars where residents of the conservative state of Sabah and the even more conservative Brunei go to party.

I bought a can of Guinness at the store for just $1. In other cities of Malaysia it costs three or four dollars.

Near Kota Kinabalu there is Tunku Abdul Rahman National Park. These are five islands: Gaya, Sapi, Manukan, Mabutik and Sulug. Boats often go from the port to these islands (except for Sulug, they go there only individually). A trip to one island for two costs about $20; you can go to 2-4 islands for a relatively small surcharge of $5 each. The largest island by far is Gaya, it has a population, but most of it is covered in forest.

Kinabalu National Park

In the depths of the island of Borneo is the highest point of Malaysia - Mount Kinabalu.

That day the top of the mountain was difficult to see because of the fog.

It is believed that the ascent to this peak (>4000 meters) is simple and does not require any preparation. However, it takes two days and is not cheap, because the park has a kind of monopoly, and spending the night in a hostel halfway up the climb costs a lot of money. I didn't go up, limiting myself to walking through the jungle and the views.

There are several tracks through the forest in the park; in principle, you can walk around them all in a day.

Unlike Taman Negara, here you walk everywhere on the ground.
