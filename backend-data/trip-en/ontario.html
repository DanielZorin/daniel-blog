Ottawa

The history of Ottawa becoming the capital is easy to guess: they did not want to give victory to any of the largest cities (and in Canada they also speak different languages), and made a Solomon decision - to move the capital to a small city located exactly on the border of English- and French-speaking lands , with a mixed population. So Ottawa became the capital immediately after the unification of Ontario and Quebec into one country.

Toronto

Toronto is the largest city in Canada. Unlike Montreal, there is no French language restriction, so migrants mostly settle here (or in Vancouver). At first glance it is a typical American square city with skyscrapers, but on the other hand there is a fairly compact center by the lake and a large tram network.

There are only two metro lines, one goes from west to east, and the second makes a loop, passing along the perimeter of the city center.

Transport System of the Year 2017.

I lived in a remote residential area, in one of the houses in the photo (via airbnb). The nearest metro station was a 30-minute walk, or 5 minutes by bus, which ran every 15 minutes until late in the evening. Since transfers are free, I always took the bus.

This is what the area looks like. Interestingly, if you pay cash for the bus, the transfer is also free - you are given a paper ticket, torn at the current time, and it is valid for several hours. In that area, the transfer from bus to metro was without turnstiles at all, because the bus terminus was adjacent to the metro station, and everything there was organized with a through passage.

Next to my house there was also this high-rise building - local social housing.

Toronto's main station is huge and pompous.

Toronto's main attraction is C.N. Tower, the tallest structure in the world from 1975 to 2007.

The tallest structure and the tallest building should not be confused (as Taipei 101 and the Petronas Towers were at one time) - buildings must have successive habitable floors. Since 2007, the tallest building has also become the tallest structure - the Burj Khalifa in Dubai.

Niagara Falls

Canada's top attraction by far is Niagara Falls. There are several of them nearby, but the main one is a powerful waterfall in the shape of a horseshoe. The Niagara River borders Canada and the United States. The opposite shore is in the States, but the visibility from it is worse, because it is slightly beyond the line of the waterfall. Since most tourists who can get here do not worry about visa issues, Canadians earn much more from the spectacle, and only Russian tourists often look with sadness only from the shore where it is easier to get a visa (not the one on which I am standing at this moment) .

The horseshoe seems to be turned with its inner side towards Canada, so this embankment has the best view of it.

If you go further, you can stand just behind the waterfall.

The view from the American side is something like this, only mirrored, of course.

The second waterfall is located entirely in the United States.

You can get closer by boat from both sides, but I didn’t really want to be doused with water from head to toe.

Unlike Iguazu, visiting the waterfall here is free, because it is actually in the city. But to generate income, countless entertainment facilities have been built here. Starting from the zipline.

The two countries are connected by the Rainbow Bridge over Niagara.

Here you can cross the border. According to North American tradition, there is no passport control at the exit, only at the entrance on the other side of the bridge. At the same time, exit from Canada to the USA is paid, it costs 1 dollar, which you need to throw into the turnstile in the form of four coins of 25 cents, and both Canadian and American ones are suitable (but Canadian ones are better, they are cheaper now).
