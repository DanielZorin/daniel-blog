Cape Town

I started my journey to Africa from the most non-African part of it - Cape Town. This is the only corner of sub-Saharan Africa where there were few indigenous people, and white colonists were in the majority, as in Australia, the USA or Argentina. The key factor here was the climate - it is not tropical, but more like southern Europe. There is no malaria that prevented the colonization of tropical Africa until the end of the 19th century. The Portuguese, who initially explored the route to India around Africa, did not like these places because of the dangerous winds, and preferred to make bases further (where Angola and Mozambique are now), and the Dutch began to settle in the Cape Town area from the 17th century. After the Napoleonic Wars, the territory became a colony of England, but the locals already spoke their own language, which over 400 years of evolution diverged from Dutch, and is now called Afrikaans, and the inhabitants are Afrikaners, or Boers. From Cape Town, the Boers moved north overland, establishing new colonies on lands not subject to other European powers. This is how the Boer republics of Orange and Transvaal appeared, but after the Anglo-Boer War in 1902 they also came under British rule, forming the Union of South Africa that we have now.

Stellenbosch

The town of Stellenbosch is located about 40 kilometers from Cape Town. It is known for its colonial architecture and wine production. But the most interesting and educational thing was how to get there.

I naively plotted the route on moovit and google maps, which suggested taking a local train, even the schedule was indicated. But when I arrived at the Cape Town station, I learned that trains to Stellenbosch had not been running for a long time. There was also a bus station nearby, I went out to a huge field with minibuses, found out which one was going to Stellenbosch (it was written in small letters on the side below, and not on the windshield) and got in there. And then I waited for two hours until there were enough passengers for us to go. It was somehow strange for such a seemingly popular route (two large neighboring cities). Empty Stellenbosch railway station:

After walking around the city, I returned to the Stellenbosch bus station to find a minibus back. There weren't any. They told me it might be in an hour or something. At the same time, there were a bunch of minibuses nearby that departed constantly. After Googling the names of their destinations, I realized what was going on. Some went to neighboring villages, and some of them went to Cape Town. But not to the city center where I came from. But to those areas with slums in the east that we passed on the highway. By the way, in Stellenbosch there are a lot of white people on the streets, but I didn’t notice a single one at the bus station. And that’s how I understood what apartheid was.

Durban

Durban is the third city of South Africa, located in the southeast on the coast of the Indian Ocean. The province is called KwaZulu-Natal, Natal is apparently a Portuguese colonial name (meaning "Christmas" in Portuguese) and KwaZulu is "Land of Zulu" in the Zulu language. South Africa has many official languages, and Zulu is the most common of them, quite a few people speak it as a second language. After the “white” Cape Town, where Afrikaans and English are spoken, Durban was for me the first city in the “other” half of South Africa.
