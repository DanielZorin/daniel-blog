Ukraine: Kyiv

15-18 May 2012

Kyiv

Beautiful May days, end of the week. People poured out into the streets to enjoy the warmth and sun.

Girls wearing pioneer ties walked along Khreshchatyk.

And meeting them are activists of the MMM that has not yet collapsed.

Above them hung red stars and a neon hammer and sickle.

Nearby, on the Maidan, a strange man preached through a loudspeaker, talked about how the government was destroying the country and called for repentance.

I don't know why he chose to do it on the Maidan.

On the other side, people demanded the freedom of Yulia Tymoshenko.

She was portrayed as Joan of Arc.

They put up leaflets.

They made fun of Profesor (president Yanukovych).

I also decided, to the best of my modest strength, to participate in Ukrainian politics. An activist who came up from the tent city shook my hand and advised me to also sign “May he die!”

Completely different personalities hung out near the St. Sophia Cathedral.

Orthodox activists and religious activists protested against the gay pride parade.

They promised to prevent the sin of Sodom.

They think that homosexuality = AIDS. They, like any Orthodox activists, clearly lacked culture and education.

To the left is Mazepa Street.

To the right is Petlyura Street.

A little further away is a monument to the victims of the Holodomor.

But someone has already transferred 1941-1945 to 1932-33.

In general, life in Kyiv is fun!

* * *

Kyiv is a city of not only political, but also visual contrasts. In the city center there are modern buildings, a lot of cars, advertising...

They are adjacent to historical buildings in excellent condition.

Maidan, which I have wanted to see since the Orange Revolution in 2004.

Basseynaya Street, where an absent-minded person lives (according to a children's poem).

I remembered St. Andrew's Descent during the reconstruction process.

A university with a very distinctive wall color.

Stall.

Other districts of Kyiv remind us of the good old nineties.

This is Podil, a beautiful, but not very well-groomed place.

Soul.

Even more soul.

If you go further, you can find yourself in the eighties. Here is the residential area from afar, it looks solid.

And here is another residential area nearby.

Here is the institute where the conference I took part in was held. Inside is a museum of the past.

Once upon a time there were fountains near it, but alas.

If you wander around the outskirts, you can find many fragments of the past. Here is a modern supermarket "Selpo".

Opposite, there is a Soviet font.

Bathhouse, hairdresser.

Fence.

In some places they have already begun to paint the walls of commercial blocks.

And somewhere there is no lighting in the underground passage, and it seems that you are walking in some kind of bunker.

There are old bus schedules on the streets.

And the buses are already new, on par with the old minibuses.

The militia in English have already become the police.

This is how the city lives, moving from the past to the future.
