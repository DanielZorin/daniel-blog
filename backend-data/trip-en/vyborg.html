Russia: Saint Petersburg, Sestroretsk, Vyborg

13-20 July 2013

Saint Petersburg

Moscow consists of 12 districts, of which I have not been to only one (Troitsky). St. Petersburg consists of as many as 18 districts, of which I have not yet been to four: Krasnoselsky, Kolpinsky, Kirovsky and Frunzensky. In July, I spent most of my time at the conference, but one day I was able to set aside time after lunch to visit one of the remote areas of the city - Kurortny district ("Resort district"). In addition, there are many historical places associated with the 1917 revolution and Lenin.

Sestroretsk is a resort and cottage community. Even the train station I got to is called “Resort”. Theoretically, you can sometimes swim on the beach if it is not very cold. But not that day.

A cold wind was blowing and there was almost no one on the beach.

The city of Sestroretsk is practically St. Petersburg Rublyovka (Moscow elite suburb). It doesn't take long to get to the center by car from here.

Just like on Rublyovka, here mere mortals still live side by side with the rich.

There are two monuments to Lenin in Sestroretsk. One is standing near some sanatorium. Painted white, which is rare.

The second one is located on the main square. Looks the same as in Dmitrov, only with fingers.

From Sestroretsk I walked on foot through the villages of Razliv and Tarkhovka. Naturally, there is nothing to see in the villages in terms of architecture, but there are plenty of monuments to Lenin there. This one is located near the Razliv railway station.

This is where Lenin's Barn is located. Lenin hid in Razliv in the summer of 1917 after the so-called July Days, when he tried to overthrow the Provisional Government. After the failure of the coup, Kerensky banned the Bolshevik party, and Lenin himself was wanted, so he fled to Razliv, where he lived under the guise of a Finnish fisherman. Later, Lenin went even further, to Finland, and returned back closer to the October revolution.

The barn is quite easy to get to following road signs. Near the barn there is a cute miniature monument to Lenin.

Let's go ahead. Suddenly, the Curonian Spit and sand dunes are right in St. Petersburg.

Here is Lake Razliv itself.

In these places, near the village of Tarkhovka, walking along the shore is problematic, there are swamps. I had to get out onto the road through a labyrinth of some sections, and once I even had to climb over a fence. Near the Tarkhovka station there is a chic monument, where instead of the word “Hollywood” it is written “Lenin”.

Opposite is the hammer and sickle. The road between these two monuments will eventually lead to the Lenin's Hut Museum, but this museum doesn't show up on Google Maps when searched, so I had no idea exactly how long to walk.

So, it's a very long walk. About an hour. At some point, I realized that I would no longer be able to catch the train I was interested in, but I decided not to give up and still reach my goal.

Apparently, the museum is popular among foreign tourists who are interested in the life of this legendary criminal.

The last march - and here it is! Lenin lived in this hut with Zinoviev, but when history was rewritten by the Ministry of Truth under Stalin, this fact was seemingly forgotten.

The hut was immortalized in granite.

Lenin's head is nearby.

And this place is called the "Green Office". Eh, probably in Soviet times people viewed this museum with serious faces and reverence. It must have been a funny sight.

I was completely exhausted from the hike of about 20 kilometers, but, fortunately, opposite the hut there is a pier from which ferries run across the lake to Sestroretsk, and just one of them was leaving.

On weekdays they run approximately once every two hours (only during the day), on weekends - every hour.

St. Petersburg is crammed with places associated with Lenin. In those days when Lenin was a substitute for God, there were even more of them. In many areas, maps were drawn on the walls, on which all Lenin’s places were marked; now they, apparently, have disappeared (in Petrogradka they were painted over just a few years ago). There were dozens of monuments to Lenin in St. Petersburg; now some have been demolished, but many still stand. Most of the monuments are located on the territory of factories, where you cannot go, but about 20 of them are in public places (including cities like Pushkin and Sestroretsk that are technically part of St. Petersburg). I rented a room in the house where Lenin met with Krasin twice.
