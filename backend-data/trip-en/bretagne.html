France: Mont Saint-Michel, Rennes, Brest, Quimper, Nantes

4-7 April 2024

Mont Saint-Michel

I’ve been to France 8 times, but there was still something missing: it seems like I’ve been to almost all regions, but it was like one city in each. On my new journey, I first headed to the west coast, to Brittany (the only region on the mainland I had not been to) and further south. First of all, I stopped by Mont Saint-Michel - one of the most famous attractions in France. Although it is a very famous place, it is quite difficult to get there. The nearest major city is Rennes (the capital of Brittany). From there there is a bus three times a day, twice in the morning and once in the evening, and back once in the morning and twice in the evening. There are also buses from the nearest railway station Pontorson, 4 times a day, and trains from Pontorson run less frequently (but the bus is coordinated with them). And all this is only on weekdays, and not in winter. At the same time, there are a huge number of tourists there, apparently 99% come by tour buses (considering the prices of trains in France, taking an excursion can be cheaper, although it’s a bit far from Paris).

Mont Saint-Michel is located on a small island, and long-distance buses go to the mainland; then you need to walk about 30 minutes along a long bridge. You cannot cross this bridge by car; you must also park on the mainland. Only buses to Pontorson arrive on the island itself. If you don't want to walk, you can also take a shuttle bus.

There is nothing particularly interesting on the mainland, cafes, shops, hotels.

This is what the shuttle looks like. It has a steering wheel on both sides, the driver just gets out, sits on the other side, and drives back. Although, in principle, there is a place where you can turn around.

There are strong tides here and at high tide the bridge can flood, so the island becomes isolated and there are no buses. But this happens somewhere at night; during the day the bridge works.

Sheep are grazing.

At low tide you can see the seabed - this is such quick mud. It is not safe to walk there, although the guides somehow lead groups of people through.
