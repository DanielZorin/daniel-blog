Peshawar

I started my trip to Pakistan from the city of Peshawar. It is the capital of the Khyber Pakhtunkhwa region, home to mainly Pashtuns, the same people as neighboring southern Afghanistan. For a long time, this region was famous for lawlessness; part of it was even allocated to a separate territory, the “Tribal Zone,” where there was de facto no central authority. Now all this is in the past, the region is more or less calm.

On Booking.com there were only three hotels in all of Peshawar (there are dozens in other cities), and I definitely needed to book something for the visa. In the end, I was not accommodated in the hotel I had booked; they said that foreigners were not allowed. Islamabad

Islamabad is the capital of Pakistan. Islamabad was built from scratch in the 1960s to move the capital there from Karachi. It is similar to other capitals founded at that time - Brasilia and Chandigarh. Here, too, the city is divided into square sectors, between which wide highways are laid. The distances in Islamabad are huge, so I almost didn’t walk, I went everywhere by taxi.

Taxila

Not far from Islamabad, near the city of Taxila, there are the ruins of an ancient city, listed as a World Heritage Site. For a small fee, I chartered a tuk-tuk and toured the main local attractions.

The attractions are scattered over a fairly large area; there is no way to get around on foot. Near the city center there is only a museum and small ruins nearby.

The museum is very rich; all kinds of artifacts are collected here.

Some stupas were moved here.

Buddha statues.

Taxila was the capital of the Gandhara kingdom (1000-500 BC), then part of Persia, and for some time in the empire of Alexander the Great. After Alexander, the Greco-Indian state of Bactria existed in this region for another 200 years, where the Greek dynasty ruled, and Buddhism and Hellenism were intertwined. The city was abandoned around the 5th century.

Gilgit

A trip to Pakistan would be incomplete without the northernmost region - Gilgit-Baltistan. If you look at the map, you won’t immediately understand where the border is, since it is drawn with a dotted line: the entire north is disputed between India and Pakistan, and China also claims some parts. In 1948, as a result of the war, Pakistan occupied all of Gilgit-Baltistan and a small western part of Kashmir, while India retained most of Kashmir and Ladakh. Later, Pakistan signed a border treaty with China, as a result of which a small strip of the mountains in the north passed to China, but India did not recognize neither this, nor the China’s occupation of Aksai Chin. It takes a long time to get here by bus, but there are two airports: Gilgit and Skardu. Gilgit can only accommodate small planes, which make it especially convenient to look out the window at the surrounding landscapes.
