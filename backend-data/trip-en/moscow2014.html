Russia: Moscow

4 March 1990 - 28 February 2015

Moscow

Having seen enough monuments to Lenin during my numerous travels around Russia, I thought that I could look for them in Moscow. In the end, this turned out to be a great way to see various unusual places outside the city center where I simply would not have gone otherwise, and I adopted this technique for other cities. More monuments to Lenin were erected in Moscow than anywhere else, in the catalog there are about a hundred. Many of them, however, are difficult to access, as they are located in closed areas, but about 60 can be seen in public places. The search for the Lenins slowly continued from 2014 to 2020. The creators of the sign confused the Ъ letter and the ѣ. The letter ѣ (yat) existed until the reform of 1918, it was pronounced as [e], by the 19th century the difference in pronunciation between ѣ and e disappeared so using this letter became obsolete. The letter Ъ ("hard sign") is not pronounced. Before the reform, this letter was written at the end of all words ending with a consonant, so it was very common. Now this letter is rarely used.

In Moscow, the vast majority of people associate districts with metro stations. When you need to give a reference point, the nearest metro is the number one choice. It happens that some areas are nearby, but since they are on different metro lines, they are perceived as distant. If you start going on long walks, after a while you begin to perceive the location of various places differently, you can already mentally imagine a map. A separate unusual way to look at the city is from the water, here the distance is felt completely differently. There is no river transport in Moscow, only recreational transport, so I think that the vast majority of Muscovites have never seen the city from the water.
