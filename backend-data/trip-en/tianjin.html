Tianjin

There is a city called Tianjin, next door to Beijing, which is one of the largest in China in terms of population, but no one goes there and no one knows what’s there. I thought, just for fun, to take a one-day trip from Beijing, but it turned out that from Dalian it was cheaper to take a plane ticket to Tianjin airport than to Beijing, and so I settled in a hotel near the Tianjin train station. And then it turned out that Tianjin is an incredibly cool city, for which not only one, but even two days are not enough.

In the mid-19th century, China, which had previously been a very closed country, lost two “opium” wars and was forced to allow trade with European countries. Some territories were given actual ownership to foreigners. By the way, all modern large Chinese seaports: Dalian, Shanghai, Hong Kong, Xiamen, were founded by Europeans, while the Chinese themselves always built their cities in the interior of the mainland (it seems the Chinese understand how to live comfortably). Tianjin, located in close proximity to the capital and controlling the route from the sea to Beijing, was of strategic importance, so part of the city’s territory was given over to “concessions.”

There are many buildings left from those times that were built by concessionaires. Now in Tianjin, between the typical Chinese skyscrapers, you can find real Europe.

Beijing

I was in Beijing for three whole weeks in 2008, shortly after the Olympics, but then I never went to the local working-class areas, which in Beijing are called “hutongs,” or simply slums. This time, in order to correct this oversight, I settled right in the middle of one of the hutongs.

There are a huge number of cheap hotels in the hutongs located south of Tiananmen Square, but the chances of a foreigner staying in them are absolutely zero. Everyone will send you to nearby Chinese chains like Super 8 and Motel 99, where room prices are around 250 yuan ($40). When it was 1200 rubles, I would not have minded, but now I preferred to look for something cheaper. The solution was found on agoda.com for only 80 yuan, with adventure we even managed to agree to check in directly without a reservation, so as not to pay a commission to the site. The hotel turned out to be very good.

Beijing is not a city where it’s pleasant to walk along beautiful, cozy streets or look at shining skyscrapers, but there are many different attractions here.

The city is very large, and interesting places are located at a distance from each other, so you constantly need to travel. Until recently, the Beijing metro was the cheapest in China, since the fare was fixed and equal to two yuan (in other cities this is the cost of the shortest trip). But this was recently fixed, and now the Beijing metro is the most expensive, with tickets starting from three yuan. They haven’t had time to rebuild the system yet, which is why half of the stations don’t have ticket machines or they don’t work. You have to go to the ticket office and tell the cashier which station you need a ticket to. In addition, the metro is designed very strangely, many lines do not intersect with each other, because of this, you can travel a very short distance with two transfers. Therefore, it is often faster and cheaper to take a bus, which costs 2 yuan, and routes can be viewed on Baidu maps. The Beijing Subway is the oldest in China, and it still has stations that are visually different from other Chinese subways built in the last 10 years.

Taishan

There are different ways to climb Mount Taishan. The easiest and fastest way is to take a bus to the middle of the mountain (30 yuan or $5 one way), and from there take the cable car (100 yuan or $16 one way), then go down the same way. The entrance ticket costs another $20 or 120 yuan, so in total a speed visit to Taishan will cost a little more than $60. You can walk any of the sections for free. The part covered by the cable car takes about 2.5 hours (up and down faster), the part covered by the bus takes 5-6 hours. It would be strange to save on a bus in such a situation, if not for the fact that Taishan has sacred significance, and going up there on foot is an important pilgrimage for every Chinese. Therefore, both during the day and late at night, the stairs are full of people going up, even old people and small children. We decided to go even further and go up the mountain on the other side of the stairs, at the same time bypassing the ticket office. That's what came out of it.

It all starts simply: it’s a 20-minute walk from the city center to the entrance to the mountain. More precisely, to the nearest entrance from which buses run. There is a second entrance further away, from which the stairs for walking begin.

From the footpath you can turn up, there is a path that eventually goes down to the road anyway.

Here is the bed of a river that dried up for the winter.

Before the bridge we turned off the path sharply up the slope. It is not difficult to climb there, and there is even some semblance of a path. However, after some time an obstacle arose.

Fortunately, it is quite possible for a thin person to slip between the two lower bars. But even if it is possible to climb over the top, it is very difficult.

Then a barely noticeable path goes through the forest and descends into a ravine. There are absolutely no people here, only sometimes buses drive along the road above the ravine (passenger cars are not allowed on that road). There were two gravestones in the ravine.

In the ravine there is a narrow bed of a dry river (it looks like the same one that looks much wider at the entrance), it is convenient to go up it up to a certain point.

Suddenly the climb over the rocks in the riverbed becomes too steep. You need to go around the side, making your way through dry thorny bushes along the way. However, there is a path here too.

At some point you emerge from the bushes onto a large rock rising above the forest. At this moment you realize that it was not in vain that you took such a roundabout and difficult route here.

Then the bushes begin again.

Hengshui

In China, you can buy a train ticket without a seat if there are no seats left. I've already traveled like this on a high-speed train, it's not scary: there are few people without seats, you can sit in the vestibule on an upside-down bucket. But on regular trains there can be very, very many people without seats, so that all vestibules, toilets, and aisles are clogged. It’s uncomfortable to ride for more than a couple of hours in such conditions, to put it mildly, but the Chinese are able to ride standing or sitting on a folding chair all night long. Xian

Xian is probably the third most popular city among tourists in China after Beijing and Shanghai. People come here to see the Terracotta Army. Is it worth it?

From this angle in the photographs it looks gigantic, but when you actually go there, you realize that it is not that big, because the back rows are empty. It’s just that the platform in front is high, so you get such a perspective. The platform is lower on the side, giving a better view.

Here's another note for photographers - a good angle from behind.

In my opinion, this place is overrated, I can’t say that a visit to China would be incomplete without the Terracotta Army.

Maybe because the tickets are expensive - $25?

Most of the soldiers were carefully restored from the wreckage. At the far end of the hall is the restorers' workshop.

New soldiers are preparing to go into the army.

The Chinese approach to restoration is somewhat different from the European one. If two fragments of a Greek vase are found during excavations in Europe, two fragments will be exhibited in the museum. The Chinese will glue them together, adding the missing parts. Initially, soldiers are in this state.

Next to the main hall there are two more, where excavations have just begun.

Of course, there is also a souvenir shop where you can buy a terracotta Mao Zedong.

The territory of the three pits with soldiers is small, but the territory of the tourist complex around is gigantic, it is easy to get confused in it. At first glance, it may seem that Xi'an is a city for a day, like small European old towns. Actually this is not true. Xi'an is a city with a population of millions, and its charm, like the charm of other Chinese megacities, lies not in a couple of pagodas, but in a million little things that catch your eye while you walk the streets here. Now I will show you what you can see here if you spend more than one day traveling from one attraction to another.

* * *

I like Chinese cities; at every step you can stumble upon something funny or strange. In Europe, in most cities, everything is quite predictable: beautiful, houses in the Baroque style, pavement, funny sculptures in the corners, outdoor cafes (the food in all countries in such places is the same, except for the most expensive Michelin-starred restaurants). No matter how much you walk around Dubrovnik, you won’t see anything unusual. But in China there is freedom. For example, here, in my humble opinion, is my most successful photo from Xian: a girl in an orange coat seems to say: “Selfie? DO NOT WANT!”

Tourists from Malaysia are taking photos nearby.

For some reason, many people try not to include other people in their photographs, but it can be much more interesting with people than without them. Where else can you see the latest hipster fashion if not near landmarks?

Oba Mao is in fashion now.

Let's go for a walk around the city. At first glance, Xian is a dusty, foggy city with very wide streets and gray high-rise buildings.

Especially if you walk along the main streets.

Although even on polluted highways the Chinese manage to plant trees between the lanes.

In China, there are sometimes huge circular overpasses at large intersections.

From here it is easy to observe how chaotic the movement is.

But this is not all of Xian. I lived in a workers' and peasants' area, where there were cheap hotels, canteens and barbecues on the street.

They even dry their panties on the street here.

Cute.
