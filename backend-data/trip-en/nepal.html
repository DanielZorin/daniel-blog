Nepal: Birganj, Pokhara, Annapurna, Kathmandu

14-28 May 2017

Birganj

The border of India and Nepal is not as contrasting as the borders of Russia and Estonia, USA and Mexico or Thailand and Myanmar. But still there are many differences. For starters, the Nepalese city is much cleaner than the neighboring Indian one (although still dirty).

There are no horse-drawn carts here, but there are many tuk-tuks of a slightly different model than in India.

However, this may be explained by the fact that Birganj is a rather large city compared to the Indian Raxaul.

There is even some nice architecture.

Nepal was not a British colony, but there is a clock tower here too.

Civilization is present here - you can buy a SIM card (I doubt that a SIM card would be easily sold to a foreigner in a small Indian city). There are more restaurants than in India.

Pokhara

Pokhara is the starting point for Annapurna treks. Here you can relax cheaply before and after the trek - I rented a room for 5 dollars close to the center, but it can be cheaper if not so close. Among the attractions in Pokhara there is a lake.

And mountains around.

Close to the lake there are a huge number of restaurants and shops for tourists. There you can buy souvenirs, hiking equipment, and cashmere scarves.

Restaurants on the main street are not cheap. Even at KFC (yes, they have it!) pieces cost two dollars, much more expensive than in Russia (to convert rupees to dollars, you can divide by 100).

Annapurna

Nepal is famous primarily for trekking in the mountains. The region around Mount Annapurna is the most popular because it can be reached by bus in a few hours from Kathmandu, while the second most popular region - around Everest - requires a flight (or 10 days on foot). The main trek around Annapurna with one pass higher than 5000 meters takes 2-3 weeks. Unfortunately, I didn’t have that much time, so I only walked along the initial sections of this trek, up to a maximum altitude of only 3200 meters.

Entrance to the track costs $40 - 20 for a ticket and another 20 for a booklet, which is stamped at checkpoints in certain places along the way. Hiking in Nepal is not what we are used to in Russia, with tents and a backpack full of canned food that will last for two weeks. Here the entire path actually passes through a populated area, from one village to another (except for the small highest section of the full track, which goes at an altitude of more than 4500 meters, where a person physically cannot stay for a long time without a supply of oxygen). In every village there are modest hotels where you can buy food. The higher, the more expensive, but in general you can spend 10-15 dollars per day per person. Travel agencies can organize all this much more expensively by assigning a guide and porter to you. The meaning of the guide is not particularly clear; it is difficult to get lost there even with a paper map, because there is essentially one road, with only a few global forks. In most places, even the Internet can be reached. A porter can be helpful if you are not very fit. I left most of my luggage at the hotel in Pokhara, so I was almost light.

The beginning of the trek is in the village of Nayapul, you can get there by public bus from Pokhara for a couple of dollars.

Hotels receive their main income from food, which is why the cost of a room is so low. Prices are the same everywhere, set by the local tourism committee.

Kathmandu

Kathmandu is the only truly big city in Nepal; it is a huge multi-million agglomeration. Contrary to expectations, Kathmandu turned out to be quite a colorful and interesting place, better than most cities in India. Even despite the consequences of the powerful earthquake that destroyed the city in 2015.

The central square has been partially restored, but even in this state the scale is visible.

There is an area in Kathmandu where many tourists live - Thamel, north of the central square. Thamel is full of hotels, decent shops and restaurants.

You can rent a room for 5 dollars or even cheaper if you bother searching. The most noticeable disadvantage of Kathmandu is the very poor water supply. This is the only city in the world where I was hesitant to even brush my teeth with tap water, let alone drink it.

It is also terribly dusty in Kathmandu, especially on the wider streets (the ring road is pictured in the photo). All the dust flies down into the valley here.

Public transport in Kathmandu consists of large buses that travel along the ring road and other highways, and small public tuk-tuks, as in the photo. The routes of small tuk-tuks are difficult to understand, but going around the circle road is not difficult, and most of the attractions are within walking distance from it.

A bus ride costs 25-30 rupees.

Here and there in Kathmandu there are really nice, clean streets:

There is a former royal palace nearby. The monarchy in Nepal was abolished in 2008. In 2001, the heir to the throne staged a Columbine in the palace, killing his father, the king, and his entire family, and then shot himself. His uncle, who had previously been king in early childhood in 1951, became king. The monarchy was abolished because it was one of the conditions for ending the civil war and peace with the Maoist rebels who controlled large parts of the country.

Portraits of the royal family, however, are still sold in stores.
