Russia: Gorno-Altaysk, Chemal, Artybash, Srostki, Biysk, Barnaul

13-17 August 2020

Gorno-Altaysk

I wanted to visit Altai for a long time, but I put it off for two reasons: it’s expensive and inconvenient to get there, and it’s not clear how to get around. The first problem was resolved over time: air tickets across Russia are steadily becoming cheaper, and since 2019, Pobeda began flying directly to Gorno-Altaysk, the capital of the Altai Republic, which is more convenient than the more usual route through Barnaul, the capital of the neighboring Altai Krai, from which it is still almost 5 hours away by bus. The second problem remained. The vast majority of tourists in the region are either on a group tour or in their own car. You can come by your own car from Barnaul, well, maybe from Novosibirsk (450 km), but not from the western part of Russia, from which the journey will take 5 days. Car rental in Altai is in its infancy, and for me it was basically impossible due to the requirement of 5 (!) years of driving experience. I also rejected group tours, since they are tied to certain dates and have a too sparse program. I wouldn't mind buying day trips, as I have done in many countries from Ireland to New Zealand, but this approach does not work in Altai either: the only company I found has tours in each direction, at best, once a week, and not every day. There was only one thing left to do - board a plane and plunge headlong into the unknown, as when traveling through outback Indonesia.

Chemal

One main road passes through Altai - the Chuisky tract from Biysk to the border with Mongolia. The highest mountains are located closer to the border, but getting there is very difficult - many places have no roads, others can only be reached by SUV: you need to have a lot of time. The northern part of the region is relatively accessible, and most hotels and good roads are concentrated there. Basically, it’s all along the Katun River, parallel to which the Chuysky tract runs, and then the Chemalsky tract, which branches off from it. Buses and shared taxis go to Chemal several times a day.

Lake Teletskoye

Lake Teletskoye is one of the largest lakes in Russia and a UNESCO World Heritage Site (together with other protected areas of Altai). You can get here only once a day by bus, which arrives at the lake in the evening and leaves in the morning for Barnaul with a stop in Gorno-Altaisk, or by rare collective taxis only in the morning, or by taxi for 5k rubles. There is a road to the lake from only two edges: in the north-west there is the village of Artybash, and to the far south-eastern shore there is a road from the Chulyshman Valley. There are no roads along the shores; you can only move by water.

The Biya River flows from Lake Teletskoye, in this place the villages of Artybash and Iogach stand opposite each other, connected by a bridge.

Biya.

True fans of Altai believe that Lake Teletskoye is a place for cattle and there are too many tourists there. This may seem so due to the fact that in the summer local hotels are well filled, especially on weekends, but there are not so many of them in total - I estimated the number of people on the lake at one time at one and a half to two thousand people, and later I checked that according to the official statistics there are about 1800 hotel beds in this entire area. For comparison, in the city of Queenstown in New Zealand, which is similar to Artybash as it's located in the mountains on the shore of a similar lake, there are up to 50 thousand tourists every day, but for some reason this does not destroy nature in any way.

While Artybash has room to grow in terms of infrastructure, there are already hotels of various levels, but roads have not yet been built (after all, they are made by the state, not private owners).

The tourism industry is also at an early stage. In principle, there is already a lot of entertainment here, but you can only find out about it by asking the hotel owners, like in the good old days.

It’s generally strange with food - judging by Yandex maps, in all of Artybash and Iogach there are no more than 4 restaurants, at least two of which are more like canteens or cafeterias at the store. I found only one restaurant - in the Artybash hotel, which fortunately was next to me, there is also at least one more restaurant, advertising for which I saw on the street (in the photo above), but it was far from me. The Artybash restaurant pleased me with both the food itself and the low prices - almost all dishes cost 200-350 rubles maximum.

In the Sadko canteen, near which the buses stop, I ate for only 160 rubles. At the same time, this is not to say that the restaurants are full, rather the opposite. So it turns out that fifteen hundred people mostly cook their own food.

Barnaul

Barnaul is a meme city where people are imprisoned for memes, and the capital of the Altai Territory. There is a meme comic strip with a dialogue: "Doctor, I am so worried about the leftists, BLM and political correctness in America" - "What's the deal with America? You live in Barnaul, Altai krai!" In 2019 a girl from Barnaul was prosecuted for posting some memes about the church in social networks.

Barnaul is located on the banks of the Ob River, as is Novosibirsk.

There is nothing beyond the bridge except Leroy Merlin. In 20 kilometers there will be the neighboring industrial city of Novoaltaisk, but in general this is the main route to Novosibirsk.

The inscription "BARNAUL" stands on a high hill above the river, like in Hollywood, Brasov and Keelung.
