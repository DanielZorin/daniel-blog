Hungary: Esztergom, Nagymaros, Gödöllő, Győr

25-30 November 2012

Esztergom

In Esztergom, when I arrived at the station, I found a room that looked like the reception room of some Soviet government agency, with the walls covered with some kind of documents on A4 sheets. The saleswoman at the ticket office explained to me that there are no trains from here and that I need to go somewhere by bus (indeed, on the railway website next to this route there is a silhouette of a bus, and if you hover your mouse over it, the words “replacement bus” will appear). However, my modest knowledge of Hungarian was not enough to understand where this bus would go and how to get from there to Budapest, so I had to urgently go to the bus station and look for a way to leave there. This turned out to be a non-trivial matter. The Esztergom bus station is an example of extreme decay. To the credit of the station, it is worth noting that in the waiting room (if you can call so a small room with two iron benches) there is a schedule printed on a printer, and after reading it a little, you can even find out the number of the desired platform. There are no announcements over the loudspeaker, and there is no ticket office either: in a room that resembled a ticket office, I was sent to buy a ticket for the bus itself. As a result of all these shenanigans, I barely made it to the bus and didn’t have time to take a proper photo of the station. All that remains is a photograph of the masterpiece sign that says “station.”

The Hungarian province as a whole gives the impression of complete ruin and depression.

The cities are mostly built up with dull panel houses surrounded by prison fences.

Every second house has a sign that it is for sale ('elado' in Hungarian).

The area near the bus station.

The city center looks civilized.

But individual houses are destroyed.

The walls are covered with graffiti.

Nice street sign.

A mailbox with a whole family of pipes.

Monument to the unknown warrior. As in Budapest, the hood is pulled over the face.

A strange screen for accessing the Internet (judging by the @ sign above). I saw two of these, neither showed signs of life.

A flock of birds gathered to bask on the roof.

The main attraction of Esztergom is the basilica, one of the largest in the world.

On the opposite bank of the Danube is Slovakia, I will talk about it later.

Nagymaros - Visegrad

On the road to Esztergom there are villages that are similar to each other: each will have a church of approximately the same design and many “for sale” signs.

The village of Nagymaros is notable mainly for the fact that the ferry for Visegrad runs here. I was late for the ferry, not finding the ticket office in time, so I had a whole hour to explore this village.

The street signs here resemble old Russian ones.

Column.

A typical window with a ventilation hole at the bottom.

The central alley is pleasing to the eye.

Among the many monuments is a memorial to those killed in three major national tragedies in Hungary: two world wars and the 1956 uprising.

The underground passage under the rails is nicely painted.

Memorial plaque in German.

In Visegrad you can climb to the castle on the hill, but November is not the best time for such activities. In addition to the castle, there is a monument with a signature in Spanish.

There is also abstract art.

And absolutely deserted streets. I didn’t see a single living soul in Visegrad.

Except for the man who came out of the door of this building, played a little drum, and went inside again.

Gödöllő

A Hungarian pedestrian crossing sign shows a robber carrying off a suitcase of money.

There's not much to see in Gödöllo. The palace has not yet been completely renovated.

The area around the central square is well kept.

Beyond its borders, familiar residential areas begin.

You don't have to go far

I experienced cognitive dissonance when I saw the writing at the bottom of the gong. It turned out that this is indeed the Indonesian language. After the terrorist attacks in Bali, Indonesians made it a tradition to give the “peace gong” to different cities. Gödöllö is one of them.

Preschoolers are taken on an excursion to the Christmas market.

Birdhouses will be installed in the park.

Locals drive antique cars.

The supermarket sells Soviet champagne, from Russia with love.

The main attraction of Gödöllo is the manhole covers with the coat of arms.

Győr

Győr is the most beautiful Hungarian city I have visited.

There is plenty of gilding here.

There are many pedestrian streets in the center.

Beautiful facades.

Parking is the same as in Moscow, 50 rubles per hour, even a little more.

Right in the center are archaeological excavations.

There are at least two Turul monuments in Győr. One sits on each of the bridge supports.

The other is opposite the station.

This is a monument not to Pushkin, but to some Hungarian figure.

Concrete obelisk on the arrow.

Monument to the boatman. A passerby tried to explain something to me about him, but I didn’t understand anything in Hungarian.

City Hall.

The street signs are similar to those in Budapest.

Memorial plaque in memory of the deceased in 1956. A Hungarian tradition is to hang several wreaths directly on the wall.

The station deserves special mention.

It preserves artifacts of the socialist era: panels.

And a mechanical display with a list of the nearest trains.
