Star City

In Russia there is such a thing as a "closed administrative-territorial entity" (shortened to ZATO). These are small cities that contain important military installations, most of them related to nuclear weapons. In fact, they are not much different from large military bases (for example, US Navy base in Guam), where military personnel live with their families, plus there is a certain number of civilians employed in the service sector. To get into the ZATO you need a pass, but in different ZATOs the rules differ - in some places they may not check anything in fact, but in others they allow you in only for work. In most ZATOs, it is enough for someone who lives there to order a pass for you; there is no need to explain the reason. There are five ZATOs in the Moscow region. The most famous of them is Star City, where the cosmonaut training center is located.

Dzerzhinsky

I at least knew about Kotelniki that it was a metro station, but I had never heard about the city of Dzerzhinsky. The city is small, and somehow not well-known, and there are no railway stations here; you can only get there by bus from Kotelniki or from Lyublino. It turned out to be a very interesting city that deserves a visit.

On the northern outskirts of Dzerzhinsky there is an old quarry filled with water.

Now you can go water skiing there.

Fountain in a residential area.

On the wall is the coat of arms of Dzerzhinsky. One might assume that the coat of arms of a city with that name would feature a Mauser and rusty barbed wire around the Gulag, but, oddly enough, it depicts the walls of a monastery.

The fact is that Dzerzhinsky was founded in 1380, when Dmitry Donskoy founded the Ugreshsky Monastery in honor of the victory in the Battle of Kulikovo. Until the 20th century, it was the village of Ugresha at the monastery, and then it became a penal colony for street children named after Dzerzhinsky. Monument to the founder of the city.

Lobnya

The city of Lobnya begins behind Dolgoprudny, this is the final station of the MCD-1. I was here once while passing through, when I needed to get to Shermetyevo Airport, terminal B - so as not to make a detour through Khimki with constant traffic jams. Terminal B is just closer to Lobnya, and buses run regularly from there. Quite recently it was reconstructed and now it is used not only for domestic flights, but also for international ones, so the likelihood of getting there has risen sharply (before 2018, I was there only once and was surprised by its emptiness and unchanged state since Soviet times). Domodedovo

In the city of Domodedovo there is DME Airport, which until 2014 was essentially the main airport of Moscow (now it is inferior in passenger traffic to Sheremetyevo Airport). In my rating it is still in first place in terms of the number of flights, although Vnukovo is gradually getting closer. As of August 2020, I have been to Domodedovo 61 times, and on the 62nd I finally looked at the city itself. Electric trains from Paveletsky Station go there regularly; before the advent of Aeroexpress trains, most of them also traveled two stops to the airport, but now such trains are quite rare.
