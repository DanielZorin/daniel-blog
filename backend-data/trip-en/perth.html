Perth

Perth is the capital of Western Australia and the most remote city in the world with a population of over 1 million people. Three quarters of the population of a state half the size of Europe lives in this city. Much of Western Australia is a hot desert, with only a corner of the southwest around Perth enjoying a refreshing Mediterranean climate.

In Perth I spent a lot of time getting to know the Australian fauna. To begin with, I went to Pinnaroo Valley Cemetery, which can be reached by train.

The cemetery itself is interesting. Instead of tombstones, there are flat memorial plaques located in rows along the paths.

But most importantly, there are just crowds of kangaroos living here.

The kangaroos here are wild, which means they are shy, but still not as wild as in other places.

If I stand, then the kangaroos also stand and watch.

And some even lie and watch.

But when you start moving towards them, they run away.

More precisely, they jump.

It is much more difficult to meet other living creatures right on the street, so I went to the zoo. Many native animals are rarely found in zoos in other countries, so you can only see them here (or in other Australian zoos). Here is the echidna - a long-nosed marsupial hedgehog that lays eggs.

In principle, it is possible to meet echidnas in the wild outside large cities, although they try to hide. The platypus, related to echidnas (but visually not similar at all), is much more difficult to find - not only does it live in water, it is also active mainly at night. Platypuses also do not live well in captivity; only in Sydney they were able to build a suitable environment for them at the zoo with an improvised river.

Emu. There was even a “war” with these birds in 1932, due to the fact that they greatly harmed farmers.

Fremantle

Rottnest Island

Rottnest Island, off the coast of Western Australia, was once a prison, then a military base, and is now a resort visited by 500,000 people a year. The ferry from Fremantle takes 40 minutes and costs 50 Australian dollars (35 USD), half of which is a fee for entering the protected area.

There is almost no permanent population on the island, except for hotel staff. In the village near the ferry terminal there are mainly restaurants and former administrative buildings.

Parish church.

Suddenly, a railway. It was built during the Second World War to transport ammunition to the fortifications on the southern coast of the island.

Bicycle rental costs 30 AUD per day. A bicycle is suitable here because almost all the roads are flat.

I walked half the island in a few hours. There are a lot of people near the terminal, but literally after a kilometer the crowd disappears; if you leave the road onto the pedestrian path, there will be no one at all.

Cemetery.

Path along the top of the hill.

The central part of the island is covered with a large number of salt lakes.

In the 19th century, when salt was in short supply, it was also mined at Rottnest.

I turned off the road onto deserted paths along the lakes.

Foam on the shore of the lake.

In all lakes the water closer to the shore is slightly pinkish, and in some the entire surface and bottom are pink.

This happens thanks to a special bacterium that lives in water.

This is the pinkest pink lake. You can come closer and leave footprints, just be careful so that your foot doesn’t drown.

There are beaches along the northern coast of the island.

They look very picturesque thanks to the steep cliffs and blue water. Probably not very good for swimming.

Parking for yachts.

Main beach.

But the main attraction of Rottnest Island are the quokkas. These are marsupial animals, similar to large tailed hamsters, jumping like a kangaroo.

Quokkas live only in this piece of Western Australia, there are a few on the mainland, but they are most numerous on the islands because there are no predators.

During all the time that I walked through the wild center of the island, I did not see a single quokka, but closer to the city there were more than enough of them.

Quokkas here are not afraid of people and can come close.

Children petting a quokka.

I found the most quokkas in the forest north of the city, towards the beach. There are dozens of them.
