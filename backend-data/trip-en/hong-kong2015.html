Hong Kong

30 July - 8 August 2015

Hong Kong

The most affordable housing in Hong Kong is located in the Chungking mansions residential complex. I already stayed there for one night in 2013, but this time I stayed in the "mansions" for 10 days, exploring everything, and I was even able to get onto the roof.

There is also cheap housing further north along Nathan Street near the Mongkok metro station, but Chungking mansions are simply ideally located - you can walk to Mongkok, or you can walk to the Star Ferry in 10 minutes and move to Hong Kong Island for just over 2 HKD (the metro is 6 times more expensive). I was there in the summer, it's low season, and the prices were lower than in the fall: a double room cost about 20 dollars (150 HKD). It's worth booking in advance at Chungking mansions if you want to check in on Friday or Saturday - there is an influx of weekend tourists from China, and on these days prices start at $30 (240 HKD). Downstairs at the entrance there are barkers who offer hotels at low prices. If you arrive on a weekday, ideally you need to agree several days in advance, including weekends, then the price on a weekend will be the same as on a weekday. The rooms in Chungking mansions are traditionally very small, but quite cozy.

Everything you need is there.

Above there was a room for the weekend, and we lived in this room most of the time. The bed has high legs so there is somewhere to put the luggage.

There is even a refrigerator, which is so lacking in many hotels. In general, everything you need for life is here.

The first room had only one advantage - it had a gorgeous view from the window.

For some reason, some people here don’t think it’s shameful to throw trash out of the window.

Theoretically, you can live in “mansions” without going outside at all. Food is sold on the ground floor, but mostly Indian, the portions are small, and the prices are very high. It's better to eat at the Chinese or at McDonald's.

Elevator. I have often heard complaints that there is always a queue for the elevator, but in 10 days of living in two different buildings I have never waited long at the bottom; at most I have to miss one elevator. It’s more problematic to go down - the elevator from above often arrives full, so I almost always went down the stairs on foot, from the sixth floor it's not that high. You can see the situation on the monitor.

Patios always look grand.

One day I went to explore the upper floors. In block D, one of the exits to the roof was not closed. A bare-chested European sat near the stairs and smoked a cigarette.

Neighboring buildings obscure the view somewhat.

The entire roof is dotted with communications.

The most interesting thing is to photograph downwards.

Below is not the ground, but the roof of the third floor. Above the third floor the blocks diverge.

Less than three minutes had passed when a man appeared on the roof of the next block and shouted at me to get off the roof.

While searching for a repair shop, I found myself in yet another old high-rise building. Walking along the street of Mongkok, you won’t even notice the nondescript sign.

If it weren’t for this, I would never have guessed that this was not the entrance to a residential building.

In the center of Hong Kong there are no grandiose traffic intersections as in Chongqing, but there are narrow dark alleys combined with tall old buildings and pipes everywhere, they create the right cyberpunk atmosphere.

A typical Hong Kong alley, most of them, of course, are in Kowloon, but there are also some on the island (this photo is from the island). You won't see this in new areas.

In such places you can find budget eateries, like this one - prices range from 1.5 to 3.5 dollars; usually it will be more expensive to eat in Hong Kong.

An interesting feeling from a walk in the center in the evening. In certain areas there are a lot of people, in Times Square, for example, and when you drive through the business districts, it seems that everyone has died out.

Yes, this time I did ride a double-decker tram.

The signs on Mongkok look shabby, despite the fact that except for them, everything on the main streets is new.

When you take the metro towards the airport, you can see an unusual hill to the north of the line.

It is almost entirely covered with crypts and graves.

I have not seen cemeteries in other places in Hong Kong. Perhaps the problem of lack of space extends to the dead too.

Does it look dark enough?

Some people really don't want visitors in their yard.

But on a sunny day in Hong Kong, everything is positive.

Life is in full swing.

The skyscrapers don't even block the sky.

Construction on the embankment continues as it was two years ago.

In the evening everything shines.

I went to Victoria Peak again.

We were unlucky with the view - it was foggy.

A visit to Hong Kong is incomplete without nature trips to all sorts of remote islands. To not to repeat myself, this time I went to the islands of Lamma and Lantau. It is better to travel around the islands on weekdays, because on weekends ferries are 50% more expensive.

Lamma Island is fairly close to the less developed southern coast of Hong Kong Island. There are no high-rise buildings here, only two villages on opposite ends of the island.

Behind the hills you can see the chimneys of the power plant, which dominate the landscape of the island.

The village in the north of the island looks something like this. Ferries from the center mostly go here.

Just a couple of streets and you're in the field.

There is a small beach towards the middle of the island.

Overlooking the power plant, but you can swim without problems.

The road to the south goes through the hills, it can only be overcome on foot, by bicycle or on this ATV. There are no cars.

How much more space is left idle!

View of the southern bay. In the distance is Hong Kong Island.

A group of rafts are floating in the bay.

By the looks of it, I would say that locals use them instead of balconies for storing things.

If you turn onto the side path, you can go to another very small and secluded beach.

Nearby there is a small village where for some reason there are many abandoned houses.

"Kamikaze Tunnels" where the Japanese parked their small kamikaze boats during the war, used for ramming.

There are several fresh seafood restaurants near the ferry terminal in the south. The prices are like this. 1 dollar = 8 HKD, that is, a set with lobsters for two costs about $70. Not even that expensive for a high-end dinner.

I took the ferry to the Aberdeen area.

Such a grandiose residential area. From here it is easy to get to the center of Hong Kong - the tunnel passes directly under Victoria Peak.

Less than two weeks had passed before I reached one of the most popular attractions in Hong Kong - the Big Buddha.

The cable car is a little expensive, but you can also get there by bus from the metro.

There is nothing particularly outstanding about the Buddha; it is unlikely to impress after a long trip to China. The views around are not bad.

There is a temple nearby with many, many small statues.

And under the big Buddha they put small plaques in memory of different people.

There are a lot of tourists, so much so that the owners of souvenir shops are against photographs in hats.

But I never expected to see a cow in densely populated Hong Kong.

From the Buddha statue, you can take a bus to the village of Mui Wo on the same Lantau Island, from where ferries go to the center.

This village has a beach that is very popular among the locals who grill kebabs.

And suddenly a whole herd of cows appears on the beach!
