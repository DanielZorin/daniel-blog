Brazil: Salvador, Aracaju, Maceió, Recife, João Pessoa, Natal

13-25 March 2021

Salvador

Salvador was the first new city for me on this trip to Brazil. I got there at an extremely unfortunate time: in the state of Bahia there was a lockdown, which meant that everything was closed on weekends (but not on weekdays). In general, in Brazil, most stores are closed on weekends, and in the center of large cities it’s empty, but there’s also the problem of eating - while on Saturday there was still something open for takeaway, on Sunday only Subway was open. It was also forbidden to go outside from 8 pm to 5 am, but this is not a big deal, because sunset was at 6, and after that normal people do not go outside anyway. Due to the lockdown, the city was deserted. In the historical center there is a police presence on at least every square, but on the neighboring streets only homeless people sleep. Otherwise, the city is very beautiful, and on a weekday without a lockdown it should be lively and atmospheric.

Walking along the streets with tightly closed shop windows, near which only young people hanging out, was really uneasy. The first time I decided to take out my camera only when I reached one of the central squares. There wasn't much life there - a couple of souvenir vendors, ice cream sellers and a few police officers.

Here is the top station of the Lacerda elevator, which connects the upper and lower city.

The elevator was built in the 19th century and renovated in the 20th century in the Art Deco style.

As you can see, the lower city consists mostly of boxes. There are only offices and shops in this part, so it is dangerous to go there on weekends.

It is also extremely dangerous to try to go down or up on foot instead of the elevator - it is clear that closer to the hill there are completely destroyed buildings, mostly squats and slums. The elevator ride costs only 30 centavos (5 cents), and in the evening, when it is not working, you will have to spend money on a taxi.

Recife

The day after my arrival in Recife, the “lockdown” began. It was possible to walk the streets, but non-food stores and restaurants were closed. More precisely, it seemed to be written in the news that restaurants would not be open, but in fact they were open for takeout, including trucks selling food (which always seem to be open for takeout). That, and having a full kitchen in a fancy two-bedroom apartment in a good area that I rented for less than $20 a day, made for a good time, although the closure of the beaches was a bit of a bummer.

Recife is one of the largest cities in Brazil, stretching along the coastline for tens of kilometers. Tourists are primarily interested in two places: the historical center and the beach area of ​​Boa Viagem (which means “Have a nice trip!”). Boa Viagem is also the location of Recife airport, which took me about 20 minutes to walk or take a taxi for 5-6 reais. I took the metro to the center and to the bus station from the Airport station. In theory, I was supposed to have one flight from Recife airport, but at the last moment my flight to Salvador was canceled, and instead of a direct flight, I flew with a short transfer in Recife, so I got to see a lot of the city from above.

João Pessoa

Many who have been to Portugal have probably traveled to Cape Roca - the westernmost point of Europe. In Brazil, I visited its sister city on the other side - the easternmost point of America, located on the outskirts of the city of João Pessoa.

There is a monument on a high cliff.

Actually, the real easternmost point is 500 meters further south (it's a small prominent piece of sand in the distance in the photo), but it is not so photogenic.

The monument was erected in the 70s and since then it seems to have been abandoned: everything around is being destroyed, the approach to the observation deck is fenced off, and there are rows of barbed wire throughout the territory, like in concentration camps.

João Pessoa is named after the governor of the state of Paraiba in 1928-1930. He was killed shortly before the revolution of 1930, and after the victory of the revolution and the establishment of the Getúlio Vargas regime, he became a kind of sacred victim, a fallen hero. Until 1930, the city was called the same as the state - Paraiba.

Natal

The city of Natal (the capital of the state of Rio Grande do Norte) is statistically one of the most dangerous places in Brazil and in the world - in 2019 it had the third highest number of murders in Brazil (and first among state capitals); the state as a whole ranks first in Brazil. In general, I have been to several cities that have higher murder rates: Tijuana, Cuernavaca and Acapulco (Mexico), San Juan and St. Louis (USA). The number of murders does not always correlate with the danger of walking around the city, since murders are most often the consequences of conflicts between criminal groups, and not a random street crime such as phone theft. Nevertheless, in Brazil I used increased precautions - I took some photographs with my phone instead of a DSLR (before the trip I bought a new phone with a fairly good camera), did not walk the streets after sunset, and avoided long walks through residential areas.

Interestingly, in the northeast of Brazil there are collective taxis, just like in Central Asia and some regions of Russia (such as Altai). Drivers hang out at bus stations.
