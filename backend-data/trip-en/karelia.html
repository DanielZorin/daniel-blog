20-24 June 2013

Petrozavodsk

Oddly enough, the name of the city of Petrozavodsk comes not from the word “factory” (zavod), but from the word “water” (za-vod'). The capital of Karelia is a fairly developed and civilized city. It is enough to note that bus travel here costs 20 rubles, it's more expensive only in St. Petersburg and Moscow. The buses advertise vacancies with salaries >20,000, so the standard of living here is not bad.

Although Petrozavodsk was founded around the same time as St. Petersburg, it cannot boast of chic architecture, although the city center is all quite clean, the houses in it are painted and are not falling apart. The beauty is concentrated on the embankment of Lake Onega.

Kizhi

To get to Kizhi Island from Petrozavodsk, you need to travel by hydrofoil for about an hour and 15 minutes. It costs as much as 2,300 rubles ( another 250 for entry to the island), which is quite expensive by Russian standards, although in Europe trips by ship are even more expensive. In general, in Russia, many famous places are expensive and difficult to get to because of their remoteness, but sometimes you can pamper yourself. But the most amazing thing is that ordinary people still live on Kizhi Island.

A ship sailing to Kizhi from Petrozavodsk (in the foreground).

The place itself is of amazing beauty, and in fact unique, since wooden churches are very rare.

Especially those that have been in place for 300 years.

From the water you can best see that the entire church is now raised above the ground for restoration. Naturally, you can’t go inside.

There is a cemetery around the church. Actually, that’s what it’s called - Kizhi churchyard.

Nearby there are houses with ethnographic exhibitions.

Judging by the signs, the houses were brought from different villages in Karelia. And even a chapel.

Scarecrow.

Unusual painting.

As I already said, there is a permanent population on the island. Tourists do not go to that part of the island very often, since most come on excursions and have no free time, but it cannot be said that no one goes there at all. By the way, at the entrance where they sell tickets, they advise against leaving the wooden platforms located in the tourist part of the island because of snakes. Vyshny Volochyok

From St. Petersburg to Moscow it takes from 4 (Sapsan) to 8 hours by train, and 11-12 hours by car. Hitchhiking is about the same, drivers pick you up pretty quickly. The longest wait was near Tver, but even then it was only about 20 minutes. In Vyshny Volochyok I made a break for the night. In my opinion, Volochyok is the funniest name of the city, even cooler than Vydropuzhsk, located 20 kilometers further along the highway.

The main attraction of Volochyok is the traffic light. As the truck driver who drove us to the city put it, this is a traffic light that fucked the whole of Russia. Due to the fact that the Moscow-St. Petersburg highway runs through the city center in zigzags and periodically stops at traffic lights, huge traffic jams form, starting another ten kilometers from the entrance to Volochyok.

There is an overpass across the highway, but people still run across.

There is also a McDonald's in Volochyok, which is also an important attraction.

The city is very dark at night. There is almost no lighting, a bunch of dilapidated houses with broken windows. The feeling that you are in the village, and now the village geeks will stab you in the heart.

During the day it just looks like a neglected, unkempt poor city, but the people don’t seem to be completely embittered.
